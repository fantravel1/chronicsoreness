NSAIDs Overview

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs

What Are NSAIDs?

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) reduce pain, inflammation, and fever. They work by blocking enzymes (COX-1 and COX-2) that produce prostaglandins, chemicals involved in inflammation and pain signaling. They're among the most commonly used medications worldwide.1

Common NSAIDs2

Over-the-Counter

  • Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)
  • Naproxen (Aleve)
  • Aspirin

Prescription

  • Celecoxib (Celebrex): COX-2 selective
  • Meloxicam (Mobic)
  • Diclofenac (Voltaren)
  • Indomethacin
  • Ketorolac: Short-term use only

How They Help

  • Reduce inflammation
  • Decrease pain
  • Lower fever
  • Work for many pain types

Best For

  • Arthritis (inflammatory)
  • Acute injuries
  • Headaches
  • Menstrual pain
  • Post-surgical pain
  • Inflammatory conditions

Risks and Side Effects3

Gastrointestinal

  • Stomach upset, heartburn
  • Ulcers
  • GI bleeding (can be serious)
  • Risk increases with age and duration

Cardiovascular

  • Increased heart attack risk
  • Increased stroke risk
  • Higher with longer use and higher doses
  • COX-2 inhibitors have higher CV risk

Kidney

  • Can affect kidney function
  • Risk higher in kidney disease, dehydration
  • Can raise blood pressure

Safe Use Guidelines

  • Use lowest effective dose
  • Shortest duration possible
  • Take with food
  • Stay hydrated
  • Don't combine different NSAIDs
  • Monitor for side effects

Who Should Be Cautious

  • History of GI bleeding or ulcers
  • Heart disease or risk factors
  • Kidney disease
  • Taking blood thinners
  • Older adults
  • Pregnant women (especially 3rd trimester)

Long-Term Use Considerations

  • Regular monitoring may be needed
  • Consider gastroprotection (PPI)
  • Discuss risks vs. benefits with doctor
  • May need periodic breaks
