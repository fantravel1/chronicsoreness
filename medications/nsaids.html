What Are NSAIDs?

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) reduce pain, inflammation, and fever by blocking COX enzymes that produce prostaglandins. They are first-line treatments for dysmenorrhea and inflammatory conditions.

Common NSAIDs

Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)

OTC doses: 200-400mg every 4-6 hours

Maximum daily dose:

  • Acute use: 3,200mg/day
  • Chronic use: 2,400mg/day
  • OTC limit: 1,200mg/day

Naproxen (Aleve, Naprosyn)

OTC doses: 220mg every 8-12 hours

Maximum daily dose: 1,250mg/day

Advantage: Longer-acting; twice daily dosing

Celecoxib (Celebrex)

Prescription only: 100-200mg twice daily

Maximum daily dose: 400mg/day

Advantage: COX-2 selective; less GI side effects

Topical NSAIDs

Topical formulations are preferred for localized musculoskeletal pain because they provide local relief with minimal systemic absorption.

  • Diclofenac gel (Voltaren): Now available OTC; apply 4x daily
  • Diclofenac patch: Apply 1-2 patches daily
  • Best for: Knee/hand osteoarthritis, localized muscle pain
  • Advantage: Much lower risk of GI and cardiovascular side effects

Safety Considerations

GI Risks

NSAIDs can cause stomach ulcers and bleeding. Risk is higher with:

  • Age over 65
  • History of ulcer or GI bleeding
  • Concurrent aspirin or anticoagulant use
  • Higher doses and longer duration
  • Alcohol use

Protection: Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole, etc.) can reduce GI risk if NSAIDs are necessary.

Cardiovascular Risks

All NSAIDs (except aspirin) increase cardiovascular risk slightly. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time.

Kidney Considerations

NSAIDs can affect kidney function, especially in:

  • Existing kidney disease
  • Dehydration
  • Use with certain blood pressure medications
  • Long-term use

Hormonal Contraception Interaction

Important for Women on Birth Control

Women taking NSAIDs with hormonal contraception have a small increased VTE (blood clot) risk, particularly with third/fourth generation progestins. This risk is still low overall, but discuss with your doctor if you take hormonal birth control regularly with NSAIDs.

Pregnancy Considerations

Avoid in Third Trimester

NSAIDs should be avoided in the third trimester due to serious risks:

  • Premature closure of ductus arteriosus (heart defect)
  • Oligohydramnios (low amniotic fluid)
  • Fetal nephrotoxicity (kidney damage)

Recommendation: Discontinue NSAIDs 6-8 weeks before term.

First and Second Trimester

Limited use may be considered with medical supervision, but acetaminophen is preferred when possible.

Breastfeeding Safety

Compatible with Breastfeeding

Ibuprofen and naproxen are considered compatible with breastfeeding. However, avoid use if infant has neonatal jaundice.

For Menstrual Pain (Dysmenorrhea)

NSAIDs are the most effective treatment for menstrual cramps. For best results:

  • Start early: Begin at first sign of cramps or day before period if predictable
  • Dose adequately: Higher doses work better than minimal doses
  • Take regularly: Around-the-clock dosing for first 1-2 days is more effective than PRN
  • Naproxen advantage: Longer duration may provide better overnight coverage

When NOT to Use NSAIDs

  • Allergy to NSAIDs or aspirin
  • Active peptic ulcer or GI bleeding
  • Severe kidney or liver disease
  • Third trimester of pregnancy
  • Immediately before or after heart surgery (CABG)
  • Uncontrolled hypertension

Alternatives When NSAIDs Aren't Right

  • Acetaminophen: Safer for GI system; doesn't reduce inflammation
  • Topical treatments: Capsaicin, lidocaine patches
  • Heat therapy: Especially effective for menstrual cramps
  • Physical therapy: For musculoskeletal conditions
