Gabapentin (Neurontin)

Understanding this commonly prescribed medication for nerve pain and other chronic pain conditions

What Is Gabapentin?

Gabapentin is an anticonvulsant medication originally developed for epilepsy, now widely used to treat nerve pain (neuropathic pain) and various chronic pain conditions. It's sold under brand names including Neurontin, Gralise, and Horizant.1

While gabapentin doesn't work for all types of pain, it can be effective for nerve-related pain that doesn't respond well to traditional painkillers like NSAIDs.

FDA-Approved Uses for Pain

  • Postherpetic neuralgia - Nerve pain after shingles

Common Off-Label Uses

  • Diabetic neuropathy
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic low back pain with nerve involvement
  • Sciatica
  • Cancer-related nerve pain
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  • Migraine prevention

How Gabapentin Works

Despite its name suggesting GABA activity, gabapentin's main pain-relieving effect comes from:2

  • Calcium channel binding - Binds to alpha-2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels
  • Reduces neurotransmitter release - Decreases excitatory signaling in pain pathways
  • Calms overactive nerves - Reduces abnormal firing that causes neuropathic pain

Effectiveness

What Research Shows

  • Works for about 30-40% of people with neuropathic pain3
  • Typically reduces pain by 30-50%, not complete relief
  • Most effective for nerve pain, less for other pain types
  • NNT (number needed to treat) of about 6-7 for substantial benefit
  • Better than placebo, but effects are modest

What to Expect

  • May take 2-4 weeks to notice benefit
  • Full effect may take 2-3 months
  • Dosage adjustments often needed
  • Not everyone respondsâ€”it's worth trying but not guaranteed

Realistic Expectations

Gabapentin works well for some people and not at all for others. If you respond, expect pain reduction rather than elimination. A 30-50% decrease in pain intensity is considered a good response. Combining with other approaches usually produces the best results.

Dosing

Typical Dosing Schedule

  • Starting dose - Usually 100-300 mg at bedtime
  • Gradual increase - Add dose every 1-7 days as tolerated
  • Typical effective dose - 900-3600 mg daily in divided doses
  • Maximum dose - 3600 mg daily (divided into 3 doses)
  • Three times daily dosing - Due to short half-life

Dosing Tips

  • Start low, go slow - Reduces side effects
  • Take with food - May improve absorption
  • Consistent timing - Divide doses evenly throughout day
  • Bedtime dose first - Can help with sleep, reduces daytime drowsiness

Absorption Note

Gabapentin has unusual absorptionâ€”higher doses are absorbed less efficiently. This is why frequent dosing is needed and why some people need high doses.4

Side Effects

Common Side Effects

  • Drowsiness/sedation - Most common, often improves with time
  • Dizziness - Especially when starting or increasing dose
  • Fatigue
  • Peripheral edema - Swelling in legs/feet
  • Weight gain - Can be significant for some
  • Cognitive effects - "Brain fog," difficulty concentrating
  • Coordination problems - Unsteadiness

Less Common Side Effects

  • Blurred vision
  • Dry mouth
  • Nausea
  • Constipation
  • Headache

Serious Side Effects (Seek Help)

  • Severe allergic reaction
  • Suicidal thoughts (rare but monitor)
  • Severe dizziness or drowsiness
  • Breathing difficulties

Important Warnings

Don't stop gabapentin suddenlyâ€”this can cause withdrawal symptoms or seizures. Always taper gradually under medical supervision. The FDA warns about increased risk of suicidal thoughts with anticonvulsants. Report any mood changes to your provider.5

Precautions and Interactions

Use Caution If You Have

  • Kidney disease - Dose reduction needed
  • Respiratory conditions - May affect breathing, especially with opioids
  • History of substance abuse - Has some abuse potential
  • Depression or suicidal thoughts
  • Elderly - Higher risk of side effects, falls

Drug Interactions

  • Opioids - Increased sedation and respiratory depression risk6
  • Alcohol - Avoid; increases sedation
  • Antacids - Reduce gabapentin absorption (take 2 hours apart)
  • Other CNS depressants - Additive sedation

Gabapentin vs. Pregabalin

  • Pregabalin (Lyrica) is related but different
  • Pregabalin has more predictable absorption
  • Twice-daily dosing vs. three times for gabapentin
  • Pregabalin may work faster
  • Both are scheduled controlled substances in some areas
  • If one doesn't work, the other might

Practical Considerations

  • Generic available - Much less expensive than brand
  • Give it adequate trial - 2-3 months at appropriate dose
  • Don't expect miracles - Part of multimodal approach
  • Communicate with provider - About effects and side effects
  • Don't drive initially - Until you know how it affects you
  • Taper slowly if stopping - Over 1-2 weeks minimum
