Duloxetine (Cymbalta)

Understanding this SNRI medication used for chronic pain, fibromyalgia, and nerve pain

What Is Duloxetine?

Duloxetine is a serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI) originally developed as an antidepressant, now FDA-approved for several chronic pain conditions. It's sold under the brand name Cymbalta and is available as a generic.1

Unlike medications that only target pain pathways, duloxetine addresses both pain and the depression/anxiety that often accompany chronic painâ€”making it particularly useful when both are present.

FDA-Approved Uses for Pain

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Diabetic peripheral neuropathy
  • Chronic musculoskeletal pain (including chronic low back pain and osteoarthritis)

Also Used For

  • Major depressive disorder
  • Generalized anxiety disorder
  • Stress urinary incontinence (in some countries)

How Duloxetine Works for Pain

Duloxetine affects pain through multiple mechanisms:2

  • Inhibits reuptake of serotonin and norepinephrine - Increases these neurotransmitters in the brain and spinal cord
  • Enhances descending pain inhibition - Strengthens the brain's natural pain-dampening pathways
  • Modulates pain processing - Changes how pain signals are interpreted
  • Addresses mood components - Treats depression and anxiety that worsen pain

Effectiveness

What Research Shows

  • Fibromyalgia - Reduces pain by 30% or more in about 40-50% of people3
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Effective for about 50% of patients
  • Chronic low back pain - Modest but significant benefits
  • Osteoarthritis - Some evidence of benefit
  • Effects independent of depression - Works for pain even without mood disorder

Timeline for Effects

  • Some pain relief may begin within 1-2 weeks
  • Full effect typically takes 4-8 weeks
  • Mood effects may take longer than pain effects
  • If no benefit by 8-12 weeks, reassess

Dual Benefits

Because duloxetine addresses both pain and mood, it can be especially helpful when chronic pain is accompanied by depression or anxietyâ€”which is common. Treating both simultaneously may produce better overall results than treating each separately.

Dosing

Typical Dosing Schedule

  • Starting dose - Usually 30 mg once daily
  • Target dose - 60 mg once daily for most pain conditions
  • Maximum dose - 120 mg daily (may use for depression)
  • Once daily dosing - Usually in the morning

Dosing Tips

  • Start at 30 mg - Reduces initial side effects
  • Increase after 1-2 weeks - To 60 mg if tolerated
  • Take with food - May reduce nausea
  • Swallow whole - Don't crush or chew delayed-release capsules
  • Morning usually better - Can cause insomnia for some

Side Effects

Common Side Effects (Often Improve)

  • Nausea - Most common; usually decreases over time
  • Dry mouth
  • Constipation
  • Drowsiness or fatigue
  • Decreased appetite
  • Dizziness
  • Sweating

Other Side Effects

  • Insomnia - For some; take in morning
  • Sexual dysfunction - Decreased libido, difficulty with orgasm
  • Weight changes - Can go either direction
  • Headache
  • Tremor

Serious Side Effects (Seek Help)

  • Suicidal thoughts (especially in young adults)
  • Serotonin syndrome (rare; with certain drug combinations)
  • Severe skin reactions
  • Liver problems (rare)
  • Severe increase in blood pressure

Black Box Warning

Like all antidepressants, duloxetine carries an FDA black box warning about increased risk of suicidal thoughts and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults (up to age 24). Monitor closely, especially when starting or changing doses. Report any concerning thoughts to your provider immediately.4

Discontinuation

Discontinuation Syndrome

Stopping duloxetine suddenly can cause withdrawal-like symptoms:5

  • Dizziness, vertigo
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Headache
  • Irritability, anxiety
  • Electric shock sensations ("brain zaps")
  • Insomnia
  • Flu-like symptoms

How to Stop Safely

  • Never stop suddenly - Always taper
  • Gradual reduction - Over weeks to months
  • Work with provider - They can guide tapering schedule
  • Slower is often better - Especially after long-term use

Precautions and Interactions

Use Caution If You Have

  • Liver disease - May need to avoid or reduce dose
  • Kidney disease - Dose adjustment needed
  • Uncontrolled glaucoma - Can increase eye pressure
  • Seizure disorder
  • Bleeding disorders - SNRIs can affect bleeding
  • Uncontrolled hypertension

Drug Interactions

  • MAOIs - Contraindicated; risk of serotonin syndrome6
  • Blood thinners - Increased bleeding risk
  • Other serotonergic drugs - Risk of serotonin syndrome
  • CYP1A2 inhibitors - Can increase duloxetine levels
  • Heavy alcohol use - Avoid; liver concerns

Practical Considerations

  • Generic available - More affordable than brand
  • Give adequate trial - 8-12 weeks at therapeutic dose
  • Don't stop abruptly - Plan for gradual discontinuation
  • Monitor blood pressure - Especially if pre-existing hypertension
  • Report mood changes - Especially worsening depression or anxiety
  • Good option if - Pain plus depression/anxiety present
