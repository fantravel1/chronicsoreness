Antidepressants for Pain

How medications for depression can help chronic pain

Why Antidepressants for Pain?

Certain antidepressants have independent pain-relieving effects separate from their effects on mood. They work by affecting neurotransmitters involved in pain processing, particularly serotonin and norepinephrine.1

Types Used for Pain

SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors)2

  • Duloxetine (Cymbalta) - FDA approved for fibromyalgia, diabetic neuropathy, chronic musculoskeletal pain
  • Venlafaxine (Effexor) - Used off-label for neuropathic pain
  • Milnacipran (Savella) - FDA approved for fibromyalgia

TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)3

  • Amitriptyline - Most studied for pain
  • Nortriptyline - Fewer side effects than amitriptyline
  • Desipramine

Conditions Treated

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Diabetic neuropathy
  • Chronic low back pain
  • Postherpetic neuralgia
  • Chronic headaches/migraines (prevention)
  • Neuropathic pain (various)

How They Work for Pain

  • Enhance descending pain inhibition
  • Increase serotonin and norepinephrine
  • Effects on sodium channels (TCAs)
  • Pain relief occurs at lower doses than antidepressant effect
  • Pain relief independent of mood effects

What to Expect

  • May take 2-4 weeks to see benefits
  • Start low, increase gradually
  • Side effects often improve over time
  • Don't stop abruptly (taper)

Common Side Effects

SNRIs

  • Nausea (usually temporary)
  • Dry mouth
  • Sweating
  • Insomnia or drowsiness

TCAs

  • Drowsiness (take at bedtime)
  • Dry mouth
  • Constipation
  • Weight gain
  • Use caution with heart conditions
