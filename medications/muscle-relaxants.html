Muscle Relaxants

Medications for muscle spasms and tension

What Are Muscle Relaxants?

Muscle relaxants are medications used to reduce muscle spasms and tension. They work primarily in the central nervous system rather than directly on muscles. They're often used for acute muscle pain and spasms, with more limited evidence for chronic pain conditions.1

Types of Muscle Relaxants2

Antispasmodics (Central Acting)

  • Cyclobenzaprine (Flexeril): Most commonly prescribed
  • Methocarbamol (Robaxin): Less sedating option
  • Carisoprodol (Soma): Controlled substance, abuse potential
  • Metaxalone (Skelaxin): Less sedation
  • Tizanidine (Zanaflex): Also used for spasticity

Antispasticity Agents

  • Baclofen: For spasticity (MS, spinal cord injury)
  • Dantrolene: Works on muscle directly

How They Work

  • Affect the central nervous system
  • Reduce nerve signals causing spasms
  • Sedative effects contribute to relief
  • Don't actually "relax" muscles directly (except dantrolene)

Common Side Effects3

  • Drowsiness (most common)
  • Dizziness
  • Dry mouth
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Constipation

Important Warnings

  • Avoid driving and heavy machinery
  • Don't combine with alcohol
  • Can interact with other sedating medications
  • Risk of dependence with some (carisoprodol)
  • Elderly more sensitive to side effects
  • Abrupt stopping may cause withdrawal

Appropriate Use

  • Best evidence for acute muscle spasms
  • Limited evidence for chronic use
  • Should be part of comprehensive treatment
  • Short-term use preferred
  • Not a substitute for physical therapy

When to Consider

  • Acute muscle spasms
  • Muscle tension contributing to pain
  • When other treatments insufficient
  • Short-term adjunct therapy

Alternatives to Consider

  • Physical therapy
  • Heat therapy
  • Stretching
  • Massage
  • NSAIDs for inflammation
