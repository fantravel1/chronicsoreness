Muscle Relaxants

Medications for muscle spasm and tension

What Are Muscle Relaxants?

Muscle relaxants are a group of medications used to reduce muscle spasm and tension. Despite their name, they don't directly relax muscles but work on the brain and nervous system to reduce muscle-related pain. They're typically used short-term for acute muscle problems but sometimes prescribed for chronic conditions.1

How They Work2

  • Most work on the central nervous system
  • Reduce signals that cause muscle spasm
  • Sedating effect may contribute to relief
  • Don't directly act on muscle tissue

Common Muscle Relaxants

Cyclobenzaprine (Flexeril)

  • Most commonly prescribed
  • Structurally similar to tricyclic antidepressants
  • Very sedating

Methocarbamol (Robaxin)

  • Less sedating option
  • Available over-the-counter in some countries

Tizanidine (Zanaflex)

  • Works differently (alpha-2 agonist)
  • Often used for spasticity
  • Can lower blood pressure

Baclofen (Lioresal)

  • GABA agonist
  • Used for spasticity from MS, spinal injury
  • Must taper off slowly

Carisoprodol (Soma)

  • High abuse potential
  • Controlled substance
  • Generally avoided

Common Uses

  • Acute back pain with spasm
  • Neck pain and strain
  • Fibromyalgia (some)
  • Spasticity conditions
  • Tension headaches

Side Effects3

  • Drowsiness (most common)
  • Dizziness
  • Dry mouth
  • Blurred vision
  • Constipation
  • Difficulty urinating

Precautions

  • Don't drive until you know effects
  • Avoid alcohol (dangerous sedation)
  • Use caution with other sedating medications
  • Elderly more sensitive to effects
  • May be habit-forming
  • Usually prescribed short-term

For Chronic Pain

  • Limited evidence for long-term use
  • May be helpful at bedtime
  • Should be part of broader plan
  • Discuss risks and benefits with doctor
