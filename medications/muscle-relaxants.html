Muscle Relaxants

Understanding medications used to treat muscle spasms, tension, and related chronic pain

What Are Muscle Relaxants?

Muscle relaxants are a group of medications used to reduce muscle spasms, tension, and associated pain. Despite their name, these drugs don't act directly on musclesâ€”they work through the central nervous system to reduce muscle activity.1

These medications are commonly prescribed for acute musculoskeletal conditions like back strains, though their use in chronic pain is more limited and requires careful consideration.

Two Main Categories

  • Antispasmodics - Used for musculoskeletal conditions; reduce spasms from muscle injury
  • Antispasticity agents - Used for neurological conditions like multiple sclerosis or spinal cord injury

Common Muscle Relaxants

Cyclobenzaprine (Flexeril)

Most commonly prescribed muscle relaxant:2

  • Structurally related to tricyclic antidepressants
  • Primarily affects brain stem to reduce motor activity
  • Usually taken 3 times daily; also available as extended-release
  • Most effective for acute muscle spasm (2-3 weeks use)
  • Common side effects: drowsiness, dry mouth, dizziness

Tizanidine (Zanaflex)

  • Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist
  • Used for both spasticity and muscle spasms
  • Shorter acting than cyclobenzaprine
  • Side effects: sedation, dry mouth, low blood pressure, dizziness
  • Requires liver function monitoring

Baclofen (Lioresal)

  • GABA-B receptor agonist
  • Primarily used for spasticity from neurological conditions
  • Can be given orally or intrathecally (via pump)
  • Side effects: drowsiness, weakness, dizziness
  • Must be tapered slowlyâ€”sudden discontinuation dangerous

Methocarbamol (Robaxin)

  • Central nervous system depressant
  • Available over-the-counter in some countries
  • May cause less sedation than some alternatives
  • Often used for acute musculoskeletal pain

Carisoprodol (Soma)

  • Metabolized to meprobamate (a controlled substance)
  • High potential for abuse and dependence3
  • Classified as Schedule IV controlled substance
  • Generally not recommended for chronic use

Metaxalone (Skelaxin)

  • May cause less sedation than other options
  • Mechanism not fully understood
  • Take with food for better absorption

Orphenadrine (Norflex)

  • Has mild anticholinergic properties
  • Also available combined with aspirin and caffeine
  • May help with pain as well as spasm

Important Safety Information

Muscle relaxants cause sedation and impair driving ability. They should not be combined with alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines, or other CNS depressants due to additive effects. Older adults are at higher risk for side effects and falls. These medications are generally intended for short-term use.

How Muscle Relaxants Work

Despite the name, these medications don't relax muscles directly:4

  • They act on the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord)
  • Reduce nerve signals that cause muscles to contract
  • Many have sedative properties that may contribute to perceived benefit
  • The exact mechanisms for several drugs are not fully understood

Effectiveness

Research shows:

  • Muscle relaxants are modestly effective for acute low back pain (short-term)5
  • Limited evidence for chronic conditions
  • May work partly through sedation rather than true muscle relaxation
  • Not superior to NSAIDs for acute back pain
  • Work best when combined with other treatments (physical therapy, activity modification)

Side Effects

Common Side Effects

  • Drowsiness - Most common; affects ability to drive
  • Dizziness
  • Dry mouth
  • Headache
  • Nausea
  • Weakness

Serious Concerns

  • CNS depression - Especially with other sedating drugs
  • Fall risk - Particularly in older adults
  • Dependence - Especially carisoprodol
  • Withdrawal - Baclofen requires slow tapering
  • Cognitive impairment
  • Liver toxicity - Rare; monitor with tizanidine

Who Should Avoid Muscle Relaxants

  • People with history of substance abuse (especially carisoprodol)
  • Older adults (increased fall and cognitive impairment risk)
  • Those with liver or kidney disease (dose adjustments needed)
  • People taking other CNS depressants
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women (most not recommended)
  • Those who need to drive or operate machinery

Best Practices for Use

  • Short-term use - Generally 2-3 weeks for acute conditions
  • Take at bedtime - If drowsiness is problematic, evening dosing helps
  • Combine with active treatments - Physical therapy, exercise, stretching
  • Start low - Begin with lowest effective dose
  • Avoid driving - Until you know how medication affects you
  • No alcohol - Dangerous combination
  • Regular review - Reassess need periodically

Alternatives to Consider

Non-medication approaches for muscle tension and spasm:

  • Heat therapy
  • Massage
  • Stretching and gentle exercise
  • Physical therapy
  • Stress management and relaxation techniques
  • Improved sleep habits
  • Topical treatments (menthol, capsaicin)

Long-Term Management

For chronic muscle tension, addressing underlying causes is more effective than long-term muscle relaxant use. This may include physical therapy, posture correction, stress management, treatment of underlying conditions, and lifestyle modifications. Muscle relaxants are generally best reserved for acute flares.
