Muscle Relaxants

Skeletal muscle relaxants for chronic pain with muscle tension or spasm

Medical Disclaimer: This information is for educational purposes only. Always consult your healthcare provider before starting, stopping, or changing medications.

Overview

Muscle relaxants are often prescribed for chronic pain conditions that include muscle spasm or tension as a component. They work primarily in the central nervous system rather than directly on muscles.

Evidence Overview

Evidence for muscle relaxants in chronic pain is limited. Most studies focus on acute pain and short-term use. For chronic conditions, they're often used as part of a multimodal approach, particularly when muscle tension is a significant component.1,2

Types of Muscle Relaxants

Cyclobenzaprine (Flexeril)

  • Structure: Related to tricyclic antidepressants
  • Mechanism: Acts on brainstem, reduces muscle hyperactivity
  • Dosing: 5-10 mg three times daily; extended-release 15-30 mg once daily
  • Duration: Generally for short-term use (2-3 weeks)3
  • May help: Fibromyalgia sleep (low dose at bedtime), back pain with spasm
  • Side effects: Drowsiness (most common), dry mouth, dizziness

Tizanidine (Zanaflex)

  • Mechanism: Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist (similar to clonidine)
  • Dosing: Start 2 mg at bedtime; max 36 mg/day in divided doses
  • Advantages: Less drowsiness than cyclobenzaprine for some; also reduces pain4
  • Monitoring: Liver function tests (rare liver toxicity)
  • Side effects: Drowsiness, dry mouth, low blood pressure, dizziness
  • Caution: Avoid abrupt discontinuation (can cause rebound hypertension)

Baclofen (Lioresal)

  • Mechanism: GABA-B receptor agonist
  • Dosing: Start 5 mg three times daily; increase gradually up to 80 mg/day
  • Primary use: Spasticity (MS, spinal cord injury, cerebral palsy)
  • Off-label: Sometimes used for pain conditions
  • Side effects: Drowsiness, weakness, dizziness, nausea
  • Important: Must taper slowly—abrupt withdrawal can cause seizures, hallucinations5

Methocarbamol (Robaxin)

  • Mechanism: Not fully understood; CNS depression
  • Dosing: 750-1500 mg three to four times daily
  • Advantages: May have less sedation than others; available OTC in Canada
  • Side effects: Drowsiness, dizziness, lightheadedness
  • Note: Less evidence than cyclobenzaprine6

Carisoprodol (Soma)

Caution: Abuse Potential

  • Schedule IV controlled substance in the US
  • Metabolized to meprobamate (addictive sedative)
  • High potential for dependence and abuse7
  • Generally not recommended for chronic use
  • Many providers avoid prescribing this medication

Common Side Effects

Most muscle relaxants cause similar side effects due to their CNS effects:

  • Drowsiness/sedation: Very common—often dose-limiting
  • Dizziness: Especially when standing quickly
  • Dry mouth: Common with cyclobenzaprine
  • Blurred vision: Occasional
  • Cognitive impairment: "Brain fog," difficulty concentrating
  • Falls risk: Increased in older adults

Important Considerations

Drug Interactions

  • Alcohol: Dangerous combination—enhanced sedation
  • Opioids: Increased sedation and respiratory depression risk
  • Benzodiazepines: Additive sedation
  • Other sedating medications: Including antihistamines, sleep aids

Older Adults

The Beers Criteria lists muscle relaxants as potentially inappropriate for older adults due to sedation, falls risk, and anticholinergic effects. If used, start with lowest doses.8

Pregnancy and Breastfeeding

  • Limited data for most muscle relaxants in pregnancy
  • Discuss risks and benefits with your OB/provider
  • Most pass into breast milk—caution with breastfeeding

Specific Conditions

Fibromyalgia

Low-dose cyclobenzaprine (5-10 mg at bedtime) has some evidence for improving sleep quality in fibromyalgia, though it's not FDA-approved for this condition.9

Chronic Low Back Pain

May provide short-term relief when muscle spasm is present. NICE guidelines suggest muscle relaxants as short-term option, not for long-term use.10

Myofascial Pain

Sometimes used as adjunct to trigger point therapy, stretching, and physical therapy.

Practical Tips

  • Start low: Begin with lowest dose, increase gradually
  • Take at bedtime: Drowsiness can be a benefit at night
  • Don't drive: Until you know how it affects you
  • Short courses: These are generally not meant for indefinite use
  • Combine with PT: Muscle relaxants work best alongside physical therapy and stretching
  • Taper when stopping: Don't stop abruptly, especially baclofen or with prolonged use

Questions for Your Provider

  • Is muscle spasm/tension contributing to my pain?
  • Which muscle relaxant is best for my situation?
  • How long should I take this medication?
  • Can I take it with my other medications?
  • What time of day should I take it?
  • How should I taper off when stopping?

Alternatives to Muscle Relaxants

Non-pharmacological options for muscle tension:

  • Heat therapy (for muscle relaxation)
  • Progressive muscle relaxation
  • Gentle stretching and yoga
  • Massage therapy
  • Physical therapy
  • Foam rolling and trigger point release
  • Topical treatments (menthol, capsaicin, magnesium)
  • Magnesium supplementation (for muscle cramps)
