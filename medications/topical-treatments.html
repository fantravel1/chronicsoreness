Topical Pain Treatments

Targeted relief applied directly where it hurts

Why Consider Topical Treatments?

  • Targeted delivery: Works at the site of pain
  • Fewer systemic side effects: Less GI, cardiovascular, and kidney concerns than oral NSAIDs1
  • Guideline recommended: NICE and ACP recommend topical NSAIDs for osteoarthritis2,3
  • Good for: Localized musculoskeletal pain, arthritis, neuropathic pain

Topical NSAIDs

Most evidence-supported topical pain treatments. Reduce inflammation locally.

Diclofenac (Voltaren Gel, Pennsaid)

  • Evidence: Strong for osteoarthritis (knee, hand); Cochrane review supports use4
  • OTC available: Voltaren Arthritis Pain 1% gel (US)
  • Prescription: Higher strengths available
  • Application: 2-4 grams, 3-4 times daily; rub in completely
  • Wait time: 10 minutes before covering; 1 hour before bathing
  • Side effects: Skin irritation at application site (most common)

Other Topical NSAIDs

  • Ketoprofen gel: Available in some countries
  • Ibuprofen gel: OTC in some countries
  • Piroxicam gel: Prescription

Note: While safer than oral NSAIDs, topical NSAIDs still have some systemic absorption. Use caution if you have contraindications to oral NSAIDs, especially with extensive application.

Capsaicin

Derived from chili peppers; depletes substance P (pain signaling chemical) from nerve endings.

Low-Concentration (0.025-0.1%)

  • OTC available: Zostrix, Capzasin, many generics
  • Evidence: Modest benefit for osteoarthritis, neuropathy5
  • Application: Apply 3-4 times daily for at least 4 weeks
  • Key point: Initial burning is normal and decreases with regular use
  • Dropout: Many stop due to burning sensation

High-Concentration Patch (8%)

  • Brand: Qutenza (prescription only)
  • Evidence: Good for neuropathic pain conditions6
  • Application: Applied by healthcare provider; single 30-60 min treatment
  • Duration: Relief may last 3 months
  • Initial reaction: Intense burning; local anesthetic used first

Lidocaine

Local anesthetic that numbs the area.

Lidocaine Patch 5% (Lidoderm)

  • FDA-approved: Post-herpetic neuralgia (shingles pain)
  • Off-label: Used for various localized neuropathic pain
  • Evidence: Moderate for neuropathic pain7
  • Application: Up to 3 patches at once; 12 hours on, 12 hours off
  • Advantages: Can be cut to size; well-tolerated

OTC Lidocaine

  • Aspercreme with Lidocaine: 4% cream/patch
  • Icy Hot with Lidocaine: Cream/patch combinations
  • Evidence: Less robust than prescription; may provide temporary relief

Menthol and Counterirritants

Create sensations (cooling, warming) that distract from pain. Limited evidence but widely used.

  • Menthol: Cooling sensation; Biofreeze, Bengay Cold
  • Methyl salicylate: Warming; Bengay, Icy Hot (original)
  • Camphor: Cooling/warming
  • Evidence: Weak; may provide short-term relief through distraction8
  • Safety: Generally safe; avoid on broken skin; don't use with heating pads

Methyl Salicylate Warning

Don't apply large amounts of methyl salicylate products or use with heating pads—can cause salicylate toxicity. Deaths have occurred from misuse. Follow package directions carefully.9

Prescription Topical Options

Compounded Creams

  • Custom mixtures from compounding pharmacies
  • May contain: ketamine, gabapentin, amitriptyline, baclofen, etc.
  • Evidence: Very limited; most lack rigorous study10
  • Cost: Often not covered by insurance
  • Use with caution: May be worth trying if other options fail

Topical Gabapentin/Pregabalin

  • Compounded formulations
  • Limited evidence; theoretical benefit for neuropathic pain
  • Not FDA-approved in topical form

By Condition

Osteoarthritis (Knee, Hand)

  1. First-line: Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel)
  2. Consider: Capsaicin (if tolerated)
  3. Adjunct: Menthol/counterirritants for temporary relief

Neuropathic Pain (Diabetic Neuropathy, PHN)

  1. Options: Lidocaine 5% patch, capsaicin 8% patch, capsaicin cream
  2. Often used alongside oral medications

Muscle Pain/Strain

  • Topical NSAIDs for acute strains
  • Menthol/counterirritants for symptomatic relief
  • Consider: Magnesium oil (limited evidence; anecdotal benefit)

Application Tips

  • Clean, dry skin: Apply to intact skin only
  • Wash hands after: Especially with capsaicin (avoid eyes, mucous membranes)
  • Allow to dry: Before covering with clothing
  • Don't combine with heat: Avoid heating pads over topical treatments
  • Patch test: Try small area first if concerned about sensitivity
  • Be consistent: Many topicals (especially capsaicin) require regular use for weeks to work

Cost Considerations

  • OTC options: $10-20 for most products
  • Voltaren gel (OTC): $15-25
  • Prescription lidocaine patches: Can be expensive; generics available
  • Capsaicin 8% (Qutenza): Very expensive; requires in-office application
  • Compounded creams: Highly variable; often $50-150+/month
  • Check insurance coverage; manufacturer coupons may be available
