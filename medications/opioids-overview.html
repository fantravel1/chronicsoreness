Opioids Overview

Understanding opioid medications for chronic pain

What Are Opioids?

Opioids are powerful pain medications that work by binding to opioid receptors in the brain and body. While effective for acute pain, their role in chronic non-cancer pain is controversial due to limited long-term efficacy and significant risks.1

Common Opioids

  • Hydrocodone (Vicodin, Norco)
  • Oxycodone (OxyContin, Percocet)
  • Morphine
  • Fentanyl (patches)
  • Hydromorphone (Dilaudid)
  • Methadone
  • Buprenorphine

Risks and Concerns2

  • Tolerance - Needing higher doses over time
  • Physical dependence - Withdrawal if stopped
  • Addiction/Opioid Use Disorder
  • Overdose risk - Especially with benzodiazepines
  • Opioid-induced hyperalgesia - Increased pain sensitivity
  • Hormonal effects
  • Constipation
  • Sedation, cognitive effects

Current Guidelines3

  • Non-opioid therapies preferred for chronic pain
  • If used, start with lowest effective dose
  • Use immediate-release before extended-release
  • Reassess benefits vs. risks regularly
  • Avoid with benzodiazepines when possible
  • Consider naloxone co-prescription

When Opioids May Be Considered

  • Severe pain unresponsive to other treatments
  • Clear functional improvement demonstrated
  • Benefits outweigh risks for individual patient
  • With appropriate monitoring

Alternatives to Consider First

  • NSAIDs, acetaminophen
  • Antidepressants (duloxetine, amitriptyline)
  • Anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin)
  • Physical therapy
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Interventional procedures
