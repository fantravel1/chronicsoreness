Capsaicin

Pain relief from chili peppers

What Is Capsaicin?

Capsaicin is the natural compound that makes chili peppers hot. When applied to the skin, it initially causes a burning sensation but then reduces pain over time. It's available as over-the-counter creams and prescription patches for various chronic pain conditions.1

How It Works2

  • Binds to TRPV1 receptors in pain nerves
  • Initially activates pain fibers (causes burning)
  • With repeated use, depletes substance P
  • Substance P is a pain neurotransmitter
  • Results in reduced pain signaling
  • Temporary desensitization of nerve fibers

Available Forms

Over-the-Counter

  • Creams (0.025% - 0.1%)
  • Gels and lotions
  • Patches
  • Apply 3-4 times daily

Prescription

  • High-concentration patch (8% - Qutenza)
  • Applied by healthcare provider
  • Single application lasts up to 3 months

Conditions Treated3

  • Postherpetic neuralgia (shingles pain)
  • Diabetic neuropathy
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Musculoskeletal pain
  • Some other neuropathic conditions

How to Use OTC Capsaicin

  • Apply thin layer to affected area
  • Use 3-4 times daily
  • Wash hands thoroughly after
  • Or use gloves during application
  • Avoid eyes, mouth, genitals
  • Don't cover with bandages unless directed

What to Expect

  • Initial burning sensation is normal
  • Burning typically decreases over days
  • Takes 2-4 weeks for pain relief
  • Must use consistently
  • Effect wears off if stopped

Side Effects

  • Burning, stinging at application site
  • Redness
  • Itching
  • Usually decreases with continued use
  • Coughing if inhaled

Precautions

  • Don't apply to broken or irritated skin
  • Avoid hot showers/baths right after application
  • Keep away from eyes
  • Wash hands after handling
  • Test on small area first

Tips for Success

  • Start with lower concentration
  • Apply consistently (don't skip doses)
  • Give it several weeks to work
  • Use gloves or applicator
  • Don't give up due to initial burning
