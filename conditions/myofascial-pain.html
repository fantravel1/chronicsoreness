Myofascial Pain Syndrome

Understanding trigger points and chronic muscle pain

What Is Myofascial Pain Syndrome?

Myofascial pain syndrome (MPS) is a chronic pain condition characterized by sensitive points in muscles called trigger points. These trigger points can cause local pain and refer pain to other areas of the body. Unlike fibromyalgia's widespread pain, myofascial pain typically involves specific muscles or muscle groups.1

Almost everyone experiences muscle pain at some point, but in MPS, the pain persists and may worsen over time. It's one of the most common causes of chronic musculoskeletal pain.

Key Concepts

  • Myofascia - The thin connective tissue covering muscles
  • Trigger point - A hyperirritable spot in a taut band of muscle
  • Referred pain - Pain felt in a different area than the trigger point
  • Taut band - Rope-like hardness in affected muscle

Trigger Points Explained

Types of Trigger Points

  • Active trigger points - Cause pain spontaneously or with movement
  • Latent trigger points - Only painful when pressed; can become active
  • Primary trigger points - Develop independently
  • Satellite trigger points - Develop in response to other trigger points

How to Identify a Trigger Point2

  • Palpable nodule or "knot" in muscle
  • Taut band of muscle containing the nodule
  • Pain reproduction when pressed
  • Local twitch response when palpated
  • Characteristic referred pain pattern
  • Restricted range of motion

Symptoms

Pain Characteristics

  • Deep, aching muscle pain
  • Pain that persists or worsens
  • Tender "knots" in muscles
  • Pain that refers to other areas
  • Pain with pressure on specific points

Common Affected Areas

  • Neck and shoulders - Trapezius, levator scapulae
  • Lower back - Quadratus lumborum, paraspinals
  • Jaw - Masseter, temporalis (can mimic TMJ)
  • Head - Can cause tension-type headaches
  • Hips and glutes - Piriformis, gluteus medius

Associated Symptoms

  • Muscle stiffness
  • Weakness without atrophy
  • Decreased range of motion
  • Sleep disturbances from pain

Referred Pain Patterns

A key feature of trigger points is that they refer pain to predictable areas. For example, a trigger point in the trapezius often refers pain up the neck and into the temple. This referred pain can be mistaken for other conditions. Understanding these patterns helps with diagnosis and treatment.

Causes and Risk Factors

Common Triggers

  • Muscle overuse or strain - Repetitive motions
  • Poor posture - Especially prolonged sitting
  • Trauma or injury - Direct or indirect
  • Stress and tension - Muscle guarding
  • Inactivity - Deconditioning
  • Other pain conditions - Can contribute to muscle tension

Perpetuating Factors3

  • Poor sleep
  • Nutritional deficiencies (vitamin D, B12, iron)
  • Thyroid dysfunction
  • Chronic stress
  • Poor ergonomics
  • Continued mechanical stress

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical:

  • Physical examination identifying trigger points
  • Reproduction of characteristic pain pattern
  • No definitive imaging or lab tests
  • Rule out other conditions

Treatment

Manual Therapy

  • Trigger point pressure release - Sustained pressure on trigger points
  • Myofascial release - Stretching and manipulating fascia
  • Massage therapy - Therapeutic massage targeting affected muscles
  • Spray and stretch - Coolant spray followed by stretching

Needling Techniques

  • Dry needling - Inserting thin needle into trigger point4
  • Trigger point injections - Injecting local anesthetic or saline
  • Acupuncture - May target similar points

Self-Care

  • Self-massage - Using hands, balls, or foam rollers
  • Stretching - Gentle, sustained stretches
  • Heat therapy - Relaxes muscles
  • Posture correction - Address contributing factors
  • Ergonomic changes - Workstation setup

Other Treatments

  • Physical therapy - Exercise and manual techniques
  • TENS - May provide relief
  • Ultrasound - Limited evidence
  • Medications - NSAIDs, muscle relaxants for flares

Addressing Root Causes

Treatment focused only on trigger points without addressing perpetuating factors often fails. Correcting posture, improving ergonomics, managing stress, addressing sleep issues, and treating nutritional deficiencies are essential for lasting relief.

Self-Treatment Tips

  • Find the trigger point - Locate the tender spot
  • Apply pressure - Firm but tolerable (pain 5-7 out of 10)
  • Hold 30-90 seconds - Until pain decreases
  • Follow with stretching - Gentle stretch of affected muscle
  • Apply heat - After treatment
  • Be consistent - Daily or every other day

Tools for Self-Treatment

  • Tennis or lacrosse balls
  • Foam rollers
  • Massage canes (Theracane)
  • Pressure point tools

MPS vs. Fibromyalgia5

  • Location - MPS is regional; fibromyalgia is widespread
  • Trigger vs. tender points - Trigger points refer pain; tender points don't
  • Taut bands - Present in MPS; not in fibromyalgia
  • They can coexist - Many people have both
  • Treatment differs - MPS responds well to local treatment
