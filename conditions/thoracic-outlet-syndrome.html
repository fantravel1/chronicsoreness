Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

Compression affecting arm and hand

What Is Thoracic Outlet Syndrome?

Thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS) refers to a group of disorders that occur when blood vessels or nerves in the thoracic outletâ€”the space between the collarbone and first ribâ€”become compressed. This can cause pain in the shoulder and neck and numbness or weakness in the arm and hand.1

Types of TOS2

Neurogenic TOS (Most Common)

  • Compression of brachial plexus
  • ~95% of cases
  • Pain, numbness, weakness

Venous TOS

  • Compression of subclavian vein
  • Arm swelling, discoloration
  • Risk of blood clots

Arterial TOS (Rarest)

  • Compression of subclavian artery
  • Cold, pale hand
  • Weak pulse

Symptoms

  • Pain in shoulder, neck, arm
  • Numbness or tingling in fingers
  • Weakness in hand
  • Symptoms worse with arms overhead
  • Dropping objects
  • Cold or discolored hand
  • Arm fatigue

Risk Factors

  • Anatomical abnormalities (extra rib)
  • Poor posture
  • Repetitive overhead movements
  • Trauma or injury
  • Pregnancy
  • Obesity
  • Certain occupations

Diagnosis3

  • Physical examination with provocative tests
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • X-rays (check for cervical rib)
  • MRI or MRA
  • Ultrasound
  • Often diagnosis of exclusion

Treatment

Conservative (First Line)

  • Physical therapy
  • Postural exercises
  • Stretching tight muscles
  • Strengthening weak muscles
  • NSAIDs for pain
  • Activity modification

Surgical Options

  • First rib resection
  • Scalenectomy
  • Reserved for failed conservative care
  • Or vascular complications

Living with TOS

  • Maintain good posture
  • Avoid prolonged overhead positions
  • Regular stretching breaks
  • Ergonomic workspace setup
  • Strengthening exercises
