Joint Hypermobility and Chronic Pain

When flexibility becomes a source of pain and instability

What Is Joint Hypermobility?

Joint hypermobility means having joints that move beyond the normal range of motionâ€”being unusually flexible or "double-jointed." While hypermobility alone is common and often harmless, in some people it causes chronic pain, joint instability, and other symptoms.1

When hypermobility causes significant symptoms, it may be diagnosed as Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS) or Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder (HSD), depending on specific criteria.

Key Terms

  • Joint hypermobility - The physical finding of excessive joint flexibility
  • Asymptomatic hypermobility - Flexible joints without problems
  • Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder (HSD) - Hypermobility with symptoms
  • Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS) - More severe form meeting specific criteria
  • Ehlers-Danlos Syndromes (EDS) - Group of connective tissue disorders (13 types)2

How Hypermobility Causes Pain

  • Joint instability - Excessive movement stresses joint structures
  • Muscle overwork - Muscles compensate for loose ligaments
  • Subluxations - Partial dislocations
  • Dislocations - Complete joint displacement
  • Early osteoarthritis - Joint wear from instability
  • Soft tissue injuries - Sprains, strains
  • Central sensitization - Nervous system becomes more pain-sensitive

Symptoms

Musculoskeletal

  • Chronic, widespread joint and muscle pain
  • Joints that "give way" or feel unstable
  • Frequent sprains, strains, or injuries
  • Dislocations or subluxations (some spontaneous)
  • Pain that worsens with activity
  • Clicking, popping joints
  • Early-onset osteoarthritis

Other Common Features

  • Fatigue - Often significant
  • Dysautonomia - POTS, dizziness, heat intolerance3
  • Digestive issues - IBS-like symptoms, reflux
  • Easy bruising
  • Soft, stretchy skin
  • Poor wound healing
  • Headaches
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Anxiety

The Beighton Score

A simple screening tool for hypermobility, the Beighton score assesses flexibility at 9 points (elbows, knees, thumbs, pinkies, and spine). A score of 5 or more suggests generalized hypermobility. However, diagnosis of hEDS or HSD requires additional criteria beyond just being flexible.

Diagnosis

hEDS Criteria (2017)

Requires meeting all three criteria:4

  • Criterion 1: Generalized joint hypermobility (Beighton score)
  • Criterion 2: Two or more of:
    • Specific systemic features of connective tissue disorder
    • Family history
    • Musculoskeletal complications
  • Criterion 3: Other diagnoses excluded

HSD Diagnosis

If symptomatic hypermobility doesn't meet full hEDS criteria, it may be classified as HSD. HSD can be just as impactful as hEDS.

Who to See

  • Rheumatologist
  • Geneticist (for EDS evaluation)
  • Pain medicine specialist
  • Physical therapist (preferably hypermobility-aware)

Treatment

Physical Therapy (Essential)

The cornerstone of management:5

  • Strengthening - Build muscle stability around joints
  • Proprioception training - Improve body awareness
  • Low-impact exercise - Swimming, cycling, Pilates
  • Avoid overstretching - Don't push into hypermobile ranges
  • Core stability - Essential for spinal support
  • Pacing - Avoid boom-bust cycles

Pain Management

  • NSAIDs - For acute flares (use cautiously long-term)
  • Topical treatments - For localized pain
  • Neuropathic pain medications - If central sensitization present
  • Heat/cold therapy
  • TENS

Joint Protection

  • Braces and supports - Use strategically, not constantly
  • Taping - For proprioceptive feedback
  • Activity modification - Avoid high-impact activities
  • Ergonomic adjustments

Addressing Other Symptoms

  • POTS/dysautonomia - Salt, fluids, compression, medications
  • Fatigue - Pacing, sleep hygiene
  • GI symptoms - Diet modifications, treatment as needed

Avoid Overstretching

Many people with hypermobility are drawn to activities like yoga or gymnastics, but stretching already loose joints can increase instability and pain. Focus on strengthening and stability rather than increasing flexibility. If you do yoga, focus on strength poses and avoid pushing into end ranges.

Living with Hypermobility

Daily Management

  • Pace activities - Rest before you have to
  • Strengthen consistently - Maintenance matters
  • Listen to your body - Pain is information
  • Protect vulnerable joints - During activities
  • Stay active - Despite pain (appropriately)

What Helps

  • Finding knowledgeable providers
  • Connecting with others who understand
  • Learning your limits
  • Advocating for your needs
  • Addressing mental health impacts
