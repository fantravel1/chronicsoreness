Plantar Fasciitis

Understanding and treating the most common cause of heel pain

Key Takeaways

  • Plantar fasciitis affects approximately 10% of people at some point in their lives
  • Conservative treatment is successful in 80-90% of cases within 10-12 months
  • Stretching and proper footwear are foundational to recovery
  • Patience is essential—healing typically takes several months

Understanding Plantar Fasciitis

Plantar fasciitis is the most common cause of heel pain, resulting from irritation and degeneration of the plantar fascia—a thick band of tissue running from the heel bone to the toes along the bottom of the foot.1 Despite its name suggesting inflammation ("-itis"), the condition is now understood to be primarily a degenerative process (plantar fasciosis) rather than inflammatory.2

Anatomy of the Plantar Fascia

The plantar fascia is a thick, fibrous band that:

  • Attaches to the heel bone (calcaneus) and spreads to the base of each toe
  • Acts like a bowstring supporting the arch of the foot
  • Absorbs shock during walking and running
  • Stores and releases elastic energy during gait

What About Heel Spurs?

Heel spurs (bony growths) are often found in people with plantar fasciitis, but they're not the cause of pain. Studies show many people have heel spurs without any symptoms, and treating the fascia itself—not the spur—resolves the condition.3

Symptoms

Classic plantar fasciitis symptoms include:1,4

Characteristic Pain Patterns

  • First-step pain: Sharp, stabbing pain with the first steps in the morning or after prolonged sitting
  • Location: Usually centered at the inside of the heel where the fascia attaches
  • Improvement with activity: Pain often decreases after walking around but returns after rest
  • Worsening with prolonged activity: Pain may increase after long periods of standing or walking
  • Pain after exercise: Rather than during activity

The Classic "First-Step Pain"

The hallmark of plantar fasciitis is intense pain with the first steps after waking up or sitting for extended periods. This occurs because the fascia tightens during rest and is suddenly stretched when you stand. The pain typically eases after a few minutes of walking as the tissue warms up.

When to See a Doctor

Seek medical evaluation if:

  • Pain persists despite several weeks of self-care
  • Pain is severe or getting worse
  • You have numbness or tingling
  • Pain is present at night or at rest (may indicate other conditions)
  • You have difficulty bearing weight

Causes and Risk Factors

Plantar fasciitis typically develops from repetitive stress and microtrauma to the fascia:5

Primary Risk Factors

  • Age: Most common between ages 40-60
  • Obesity: Excess weight increases stress on the fascia
  • Foot mechanics: Flat feet, high arches, or abnormal gait patterns
  • Occupation: Jobs requiring prolonged standing or walking on hard surfaces
  • Exercise: Running, jumping sports, or sudden increases in activity
  • Tight calf muscles: Reduces ankle flexibility and increases fascia strain
  • Footwear: Inadequate arch support, worn-out shoes

Women and Plantar Fasciitis

Women may face additional risk factors:

  • High heels can shorten the Achilles tendon and calf muscles
  • Flats and ballet-style shoes often lack arch support
  • Pregnancy increases weight and releases hormones that loosen ligaments
  • Higher rates of certain conditions that can contribute to foot problems

Conservative Treatment

Conservative measures resolve plantar fasciitis in 80-90% of cases, though full recovery often takes 6-12 months.6

Rest and Activity Modification

  • Reduce activities that aggravate symptoms
  • Avoid going barefoot, especially on hard surfaces
  • Consider low-impact activities (swimming, cycling) while healing
  • Don't push through significant pain

Ice

Apply ice to reduce pain:

  • Ice the heel for 15-20 minutes, 3-4 times daily
  • Rolling a frozen water bottle under the foot combines ice and massage
  • Never apply ice directly to skin

Over-the-Counter Pain Relievers

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) may help manage pain, though they don't address the underlying tissue changes. Use as directed and consult a healthcare provider for prolonged use.7

Stretching Exercises

Stretching is one of the most effective treatments for plantar fasciitis. A systematic review found that stretching programs significantly reduce pain and improve function.8

Plantar Fascia Stretch

The most important stretch for this condition:

  1. Sit and cross the affected foot over the other knee
  2. Grasp the toes and gently pull them back toward the shin
  3. You should feel a stretch along the bottom of the foot
  4. Hold for 30 seconds, repeat 10 times
  5. Perform before the first steps in the morning and 3x daily

Calf Stretches

Tight calf muscles contribute to plantar fasciitis:

Wall Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius):

  1. Face a wall with hands at shoulder height
  2. Step one foot back, keeping it straight and heel down
  3. Lean forward until you feel a stretch in the calf
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times each leg

Bent-Knee Stretch (Soleus):

  1. Same position as above, but slightly bend the back knee
  2. Keep the heel on the ground
  3. Feel the stretch lower in the calf
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times each leg

Towel Stretch

  1. Sit with legs extended in front of you
  2. Loop a towel around the ball of your foot
  3. Gently pull the towel toward you, keeping knee straight
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times
  5. Excellent for first thing in the morning before standing

Stretching Tips

  • Never stretch to the point of pain
  • Consistency is key—daily stretching is essential
  • Always stretch before getting out of bed
  • Continue stretching even after symptoms improve

Footwear and Support

Appropriate footwear is crucial for recovery and prevention:9

Shoe Recommendations

  • Arch support: Choose shoes with good built-in arch support
  • Cushioning: Adequate heel cushioning to absorb shock
  • Firm heel counter: The back of the shoe should be sturdy
  • Avoid worn-out shoes: Replace athletic shoes every 300-500 miles
  • Avoid going barefoot: Even at home, wear supportive slippers

Orthotic Inserts

Orthotics provide additional support:10

  • Over-the-counter insoles: Many find relief with quality prefabricated orthotics
  • Custom orthotics: May help those who don't respond to OTC options
  • Heel cups: Provide cushioning and slight elevation

Research suggests prefabricated orthotics work as well as custom orthotics for most people with plantar fasciitis.10

Night Splints

Night splints hold the foot in a stretched position during sleep:

  • Keep the plantar fascia gently stretched overnight
  • Can significantly reduce morning pain
  • Most effective for people with symptoms for more than 6 months
  • May take time to adjust to wearing

Physical Therapy

Physical therapy can be highly effective, especially for persistent cases:11

  • Individualized stretching and strengthening programs
  • Manual therapy techniques
  • Gait analysis and correction
  • Iontophoresis or ultrasound
  • Taping techniques
  • Education on activity modification

Strengthening Exercises

Strengthening the intrinsic foot muscles may help:

  • Towel scrunches: Curl toes to scrunch a towel on the floor
  • Marble pickups: Pick up marbles with toes
  • Calf raises: Strengthen the calf muscles
  • Toe walking: Walking on tiptoes to strengthen the foot

Advanced Treatments

When conservative measures aren't sufficient, additional treatments may be considered:12

Corticosteroid Injections

  • Can provide significant short-term relief
  • Effects typically last weeks to months
  • Limited number of injections recommended due to potential tissue damage
  • Risk of plantar fascia rupture with repeated injections

Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (ESWT)

  • Uses sound waves to stimulate healing
  • May help chronic cases unresponsive to other treatments
  • Evidence is mixed but generally positive
  • Usually requires multiple sessions

Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)

  • Injection of concentrated platelets from your own blood
  • May promote tissue healing
  • Evidence is still emerging

Surgery

Surgery is rarely needed (less than 5% of cases) and considered only after 6-12 months of failed conservative treatment:13

  • Plantar fascia release: Partial cutting of the fascia
  • Gastrocnemius recession: Lengthening the calf muscle

Prevention

After recovery, prevent recurrence with these strategies:

  • Continue daily stretching even when symptom-free
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Wear supportive footwear consistently
  • Replace athletic shoes regularly
  • Increase activity levels gradually
  • Vary exercise activities to avoid repetitive stress
  • Address tight calf muscles

Recovery Timeline

Understanding typical recovery helps set realistic expectations:

  • 2-4 weeks: Some improvement with stretching and proper footwear
  • 6-8 weeks: Significant improvement for many people
  • 3-6 months: Most people recover with conservative treatment
  • 6-12 months: Stubborn cases may take longer
  • 12+ months: Consider advanced treatments if not improving
