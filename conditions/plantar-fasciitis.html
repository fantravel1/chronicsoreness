Plantar Fasciitis

Understanding and treating heel pain

Key Takeaways

  • Plantar fasciitis is the most common cause of heel pain
  • Classic symptom: sharp heel pain with first steps in the morning
  • Most cases (90%) resolve with conservative treatment
  • Stretching and proper footwear are essential

What Is Plantar Fasciitis?

Plantar fasciitis is inflammation and degeneration of the plantar fascia, a thick band of tissue that runs along the bottom of the foot connecting the heel bone to the toes. It's the most common cause of heel pain, affecting approximately 2 million Americans each year.1

90%
of plantar fasciitis cases improve with conservative treatment2

The Plantar Fascia

  • Supports the arch of the foot
  • Acts as a shock absorber
  • Helps with push-off during walking
  • Connects heel to base of toes

Symptoms

Classic symptoms include:3

Pain Pattern

  • Sharp, stabbing pain at the bottom of the heel
  • Pain with first steps in the morning (most characteristic)
  • Pain after prolonged standing or sitting
  • Pain often decreases with activity but returns after rest
  • May worsen over course of day with prolonged standing

Pain Location

  • Usually at heel, where fascia attaches
  • May extend along arch
  • Typically one foot, but can be both

Aggravating Factors

  • First steps after rest
  • Prolonged standing
  • Walking barefoot or in flat shoes
  • Climbing stairs
  • High-impact activities

Causes and Risk Factors

How It Develops

  • Repetitive stress and microtears in fascia
  • Overuse and overloading
  • Now understood as more degenerative than inflammatory

Risk Factors

  • Age: Most common 40-60
  • Foot mechanics: Flat feet, high arches, abnormal gait
  • Obesity: Increased load on fascia
  • Occupation: Jobs requiring prolonged standing
  • Activity: Running, dancing, high-impact exercise
  • Footwear: Poor support, worn-out shoes
  • Tight calf muscles: Common contributor

Heel Spurs

Heel spurs (bone growths) are often found with plantar fasciitis but are usually not the cause of pain. Many people have heel spurs with no symptoms, and removing them doesn't necessarily relieve plantar fasciitis pain.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of characteristic symptoms
  • Physical examination
  • Tenderness at heel where fascia attaches
  • Pain with dorsiflexion (pulling toes back)

Imaging

  • Usually not needed for typical presentation
  • X-ray may show heel spur (not causative)
  • Ultrasound or MRI if diagnosis uncertain
  • May show thickened fascia

Treatment

Conservative treatment is effective for most people:4,5

Stretching (Essential)

  • Plantar fascia stretch: Pull toes back to stretch bottom of foot
  • Calf stretches: Wall stretch, step stretch
  • Stretch before first steps in morning
  • Stretch multiple times daily
  • Frozen water bottle roll under foot

Footwear

  • Supportive shoes with good arch support
  • Cushioned heel
  • Avoid flat shoes and going barefoot
  • Replace worn-out shoes
  • Consider shoes immediately upon waking

Orthotic Devices

  • Over-the-counter arch supports often helpful
  • Heel cups or cushions
  • Custom orthotics for persistent cases
  • Night splints (keep foot stretched during sleep)

Ice

  • Ice massage or ice pack on heel
  • 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Frozen water bottle roll particularly effective

Medications

  • NSAIDs: Short-term for pain relief
  • Topical anti-inflammatories

Physical Therapy

  • Stretching instruction
  • Strengthening exercises for foot muscles
  • Manual therapy
  • Activity modification guidance

If Conservative Treatment Fails

  • Corticosteroid injection: Can provide relief but risks include fat pad atrophy, fascia rupture
  • Extracorporeal shock wave therapy: Sound waves to promote healing
  • Platelet-rich plasma (PRP): Emerging option
  • Surgery: Rarely needed (last resort)

Home Treatment Plan

Daily Routine

  1. Before getting out of bed: Stretch plantar fascia and calves
  2. First steps: Wear supportive shoes or slippers
  3. Throughout day: Stretch every few hours
  4. After activity: Ice the heel
  5. Evening: More stretching, consider night splint

Key Stretches

Plantar Fascia Stretch

  • Sit and cross affected foot over other knee
  • Pull toes back toward shin
  • Should feel stretch in arch
  • Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times
  • Do before getting out of bed

Calf Stretch

  • Face wall, hands on wall
  • Step back with affected leg, keep heel down
  • Lean forward, keeping back knee straight
  • Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times
  • Also do with back knee slightly bent

Timeline for Recovery

  • Some improvement often within 2-4 weeks
  • Most cases resolve within 6-12 months
  • May take longer if chronic
  • Consistency with stretching is key
