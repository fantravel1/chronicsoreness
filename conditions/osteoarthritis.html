Osteoarthritis

Understanding and managing degenerative joint disease

Key Takeaways

  • OA is the most common form of arthritis, affecting millions worldwide
  • Exercise and weight management are cornerstone treatments
  • OA is not just "wear and tear"—it involves active disease processes
  • Many effective treatments exist to manage pain and maintain function

What Is Osteoarthritis?

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterized by breakdown of cartilage, changes in bone, and inflammation. It is the most common form of arthritis and a leading cause of disability worldwide.1

32.5 Million
adults in the US have osteoarthritis2

What Happens in OA

  • Cartilage breakdown: Protective cartilage wears down
  • Bone changes: Bone spurs (osteophytes) may form
  • Inflammation: Low-grade inflammation in the joint
  • Synovial changes: Joint lining becomes inflamed
  • Muscle weakness: Surrounding muscles may weaken

Beyond "Wear and Tear"

OA was once thought to be simple wear and tear, but we now know it involves active biological processes including inflammation, abnormal mechanics, and metabolic factors. This means it can potentially be modified, not just managed.

Symptoms

OA symptoms typically develop gradually:3

Common Symptoms

  • Pain: Aching during or after movement
  • Stiffness: Especially in the morning or after inactivity (usually <30 minutes)
  • Tenderness: When pressing on the joint
  • Loss of flexibility: Reduced range of motion
  • Grating sensation: Crepitus with movement
  • Bone spurs: Hard lumps around the joint
  • Swelling: Soft tissue inflammation around joint

Commonly Affected Joints

  • Knees (most common)
  • Hips
  • Hands (base of thumb, finger joints)
  • Spine (neck and lower back)
  • Feet (big toe)

Symptom Patterns

  • Pain typically worsens with activity, improves with rest
  • Symptoms may fluctuate day to day
  • Weather changes may affect symptoms
  • Symptoms often progress slowly over years

Risk Factors

Several factors increase OA risk:4

Non-Modifiable Factors

  • Age: Risk increases with age
  • Sex: More common in women, especially after menopause
  • Genetics: Family history increases risk
  • Previous injury: Joint injuries increase later OA risk

Modifiable Factors

  • Obesity: Major risk factor, especially for knee OA
  • Occupation: Repetitive stress on joints
  • Muscle weakness: Weak muscles provide less joint support
  • Joint malalignment: Abnormal mechanics

Diagnosis

OA is typically diagnosed through:5

Clinical Evaluation

  • Medical history and symptom assessment
  • Physical examination of affected joints
  • Assessment of range of motion and function

Imaging

  • X-rays: Show joint space narrowing, bone spurs, bone changes
  • MRI: More detailed view of cartilage and soft tissues
  • Note: Imaging findings don't always correlate with symptoms

Lab Tests

  • Blood tests mainly to rule out other conditions (RA, gout)
  • Joint fluid analysis if needed

Treatment: Lifestyle Modifications

Lifestyle changes are first-line treatment for OA:6

Exercise

Exercise is one of the most effective treatments:

  • Strengthening: Builds muscles to support joints
  • Aerobic exercise: Walking, cycling, swimming
  • Flexibility: Maintains range of motion
  • Aquatic exercise: Reduces joint stress
  • Evidence shows exercise reduces pain and improves function7

Weight Management

  • Weight loss reduces stress on weight-bearing joints
  • Even modest weight loss (5-10%) can significantly reduce pain
  • Reduces inflammation throughout the body
  • Combined with exercise is most effective

Joint Protection

  • Avoid prolonged positions that stress joints
  • Use larger joints for heavy tasks
  • Pace activities
  • Assistive devices when helpful

Treatment: Medications

Oral Medications

  • Acetaminophen: First-line for mild pain
  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen—effective but monitor for side effects
  • Duloxetine: May help with OA pain
  • Opioids: Generally not recommended for OA

Topical Treatments

  • Topical NSAIDs: Effective for knee/hand OA with fewer systemic effects
  • Capsaicin: May provide modest relief

Injections

  • Corticosteroid injections: Temporary relief, limited frequency
  • Hyaluronic acid: Mixed evidence, may help some patients
  • PRP (platelet-rich plasma): Emerging evidence, not yet standard

Supplements

  • Glucosamine/chondroitin: Mixed evidence; may help some people
  • Generally safe but benefits are modest at best

Physical Therapy

PT is highly recommended for OA:8

  • Individualized exercise programs
  • Manual therapy techniques
  • Education on joint protection
  • Gait and movement training
  • Modalities (heat, cold, TENS)
  • Assistive device recommendations

Surgical Options

When conservative treatment fails:9

Joint Replacement

  • Highly effective for severe knee and hip OA
  • Considered when pain significantly limits quality of life
  • Most people have significant improvement
  • Prostheses typically last 15-20+ years

Other Surgical Options

  • Arthroscopy: Generally not recommended for OA
  • Osteotomy: Realigning bones, sometimes used in younger patients
  • Joint fusion: For certain joints (spine, ankle)

Living Well with OA

Self-Management

  • Stay active—movement is medicine
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Use heat for stiffness, cold for swelling
  • Pace activities and rest when needed
  • Use assistive devices without shame

Emotional Well-being

  • Chronic pain can affect mood—address this
  • Stay socially connected
  • Consider support groups
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy may help
