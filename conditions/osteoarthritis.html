What is Osteoarthritis?

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease involving breakdown of cartilage (the cushioning tissue in joints), bone changes, and inflammation. Unlike rheumatoid arthritis, it's not primarily an autoimmune disease, though inflammation plays a role.

Commonly Affected Joints

  • Knees: Most common site of symptomatic OA
  • Hips: Major cause of disability
  • Hands: Particularly DIP/PIP joints (finger joints), thumb base
  • Spine: Neck and lower back
  • Feet: Big toe joint

Symptoms

  • Pain: Typically worse with activity, better with rest
  • Stiffness: After rest; usually <30 minutes (unlike RA)
  • Reduced range of motion: Difficulty moving joint through full range
  • Crepitus: Crackling/grinding sensation or sound
  • Swelling: May be bony enlargement or soft tissue swelling
  • Joint instability: Feeling of "giving way" (especially knees)

Women-Specific Considerations

Why Women Are More Affected

  • Estrogen decline: Cartilage has estrogen receptors; menopause accelerates OA3
  • Joint anatomy: Women have different hip/knee alignment
  • Hand OA: Particularly common in postmenopausal women
  • Pain experience: Women often report more pain at same disease severity

Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases over 50
  • Obesity: Major risk factor, especially for knee OA4
  • Previous joint injury: Increases OA risk at that joint
  • Genetics: Family history increases risk
  • Occupation: Jobs with repetitive joint stress
  • Gender: Women > men after age 50
  • Muscle weakness: Weak muscles increase joint stress

Diagnosis

OA is usually diagnosed clinically:

  • History: Characteristic symptoms
  • Exam: Joint findings (crepitus, bony enlargement, tenderness)
  • X-rays: May show narrowed joint space, bone spurs, but don't always correlate with symptoms5
  • Blood tests: Normal (used to rule out RA, gout)

Treatment: Evidence-Based Approach

ACR/NICE Guidelines Emphasize6,7

  • Exercise is strongly recommended for ALL OA
  • Weight management for overweight patients
  • Topical NSAIDs preferred over oral for knee/hand OA
  • Education and self-management

First-Line Treatments

  • Exercise: Aerobic + strengthening—proven to reduce pain8
  • Aquatic therapy: Excellent for OA—water supports joints
  • Strength training: Builds muscles to support joints
  • Weight loss: Reduces joint stress; even 5-10% helps significantly9
  • Physical therapy: Targeted exercises, manual therapy
  • Assistive devices: Cane, walker, braces as needed

Medications

  • Topical NSAIDs: Diclofenac gel—effective with fewer systemic effects
  • Oral NSAIDs: If topicals insufficient; use lowest dose, shortest duration
  • Acetaminophen: Limited evidence for OA; may help some
  • Duloxetine: May help chronic OA pain10
  • Capsaicin: Topical option for some

Injections

  • Corticosteroid injections: Short-term relief; shouldn't be repeated frequently
  • Hyaluronic acid: Evidence mixed; some patients benefit11
  • PRP, stem cells: Insufficient evidence to recommend routinely

Surgery

  • Joint replacement: Hip and knee replacement highly effective for severe OA
  • Consider when: Significant disability, failed conservative treatment, good surgical candidate
  • Arthroscopy: Not recommended for OA (no better than placebo)12

Self-Management Strategies

Joint Protection

  • Use larger joints when possible
  • Avoid sustained gripping (use jar openers, lever handles)
  • Maintain good posture
  • Change positions frequently
  • Use assistive devices without shame

Activity Modifications

  • Pacing: Alternate activity and rest
  • Low-impact exercise: Swimming, cycling, walking on level surfaces
  • Avoid: High-impact activities, deep squatting, kneeling (for knee OA)

Heat and Cold

  • Heat: For stiffness—before activity
  • Cold: For swelling—after activity or during flares
  • See full heat/cold guide

Supplements

Evidence is limited for most supplements:

  • Glucosamine/chondroitin: Large trials show minimal benefit; may help some13
  • Turmeric/curcumin: May have anti-inflammatory effects; evidence limited
  • Vitamin D: Check and correct deficiency; unclear if helps OA directly
  • Fish oil: May reduce inflammation; modest evidence

Prognosis

  • OA is progressive but rate varies greatly
  • Many people manage well with conservative treatment for years
  • Joint replacement is highly successful when needed
  • Exercise and weight management can slow progression
  • OA doesn't always get steadily worse—symptoms fluctuate
