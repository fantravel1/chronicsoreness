Baker's Cyst

Fluid-filled swelling behind the knee

What Is a Baker's Cyst?

A Baker's cyst (popliteal cyst) is a fluid-filled sac that forms behind the knee. It occurs when excess synovial fluid from the knee joint is pushed into a bursa at the back of the knee, often due to underlying knee problems.1

Causes

  • Knee arthritis (most common in adults)
  • Meniscus tear
  • Cartilage injury
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Other knee injuries
  • In children, may occur without underlying condition

Symptoms2

  • Swelling or lump behind knee
  • Knee stiffness
  • Pain behind knee
  • Difficulty bending knee fully
  • Symptoms worse after activity
  • Some cysts cause no symptoms

Complications

  • Cyst rupture - Causes sudden calf pain, swelling, redness
  • Ruptured cyst symptoms mimic deep vein thrombosis
  • Rarely, can compress nerves or blood vessels

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination
  • Transillumination (light through cyst)
  • Ultrasound (confirms diagnosis)
  • MRI (evaluates underlying knee problem)

Treatment3

Conservative

  • Often no treatment needed if asymptomatic
  • Treat underlying knee condition
  • Ice and rest
  • NSAIDs
  • Physical therapy

Procedures

  • Aspiration (draining the cyst)
  • Corticosteroid injection
  • Surgery (rarely needed, addresses underlying cause)

Prognosis

Baker's cysts often resolve when the underlying knee problem is treated. Some cysts come and go. Children's cysts usually resolve on their own.
