Piriformis Syndrome

When a hip muscle causes sciatic-type pain

What Is Piriformis Syndrome?

Piriformis syndrome occurs when the piriformis muscleâ€”a small muscle deep in the buttockâ€”irritates or compresses the sciatic nerve. This can cause pain, tingling, and numbness in the buttock and down the leg, similar to sciatica from a herniated disc. It's sometimes called "wallet sciatica" because sitting on a wallet can aggravate it.1

Anatomy

  • Piriformis muscle runs from sacrum to hip
  • Sciatic nerve passes under (or sometimes through) the piriformis
  • Muscle tightness or spasm can compress nerve
  • Anatomical variations exist

Causes2

  • Muscle tightness or spasm
  • Overuse (running, cycling)
  • Prolonged sitting
  • Trauma to buttock
  • Anatomical variations
  • Hip problems

Symptoms

  • Pain in buttock, often deep
  • Pain may radiate down back of leg
  • Worse with sitting
  • Worse climbing stairs
  • Pain with hip movements
  • Tenderness in buttock area
  • Tingling or numbness in leg

Diagnosis3

  • Clinical diagnosis (no definitive test)
  • Physical examination
  • FAIR test (flexion, adduction, internal rotation)
  • Tenderness over piriformis
  • MRI may show inflammation
  • Rule out lumbar spine causes

Treatment

Conservative (First-Line)

  • Piriformis stretching
  • Physical therapy
  • Avoid prolonged sitting
  • Remove wallet from back pocket
  • NSAIDs
  • Ice or heat

Other Options

  • Massage therapy
  • Trigger point release
  • Dry needling
  • Botox injections
  • Corticosteroid injections

Piriformis Stretches

  • Figure-4 stretch (supine)
  • Seated piriformis stretch
  • Pigeon pose (modified)
  • Cross-body stretch
  • Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times

Self-Care

  • Stretch regularly
  • Use cushion when sitting
  • Take breaks from sitting
  • Foam rolling (careful around nerve)
  • Tennis ball massage

Prognosis

  • Most cases improve with conservative care
  • May take weeks to months
  • Stretching and activity modification key
  • Surgery rarely needed
