Sacroiliac Joint Dysfunction

Pain where spine meets pelvis

What Is SI Joint Dysfunction?

Sacroiliac (SI) joint dysfunction occurs when the SI jointsâ€”where the lower spine connects to the pelvisâ€”become a source of pain. It's estimated to cause 15-30% of lower back pain cases and can be challenging to diagnose.1

Anatomy

  • Two SI joints, one on each side
  • Connect sacrum (base of spine) to ilium (pelvis)
  • Transfer weight between spine and legs
  • Have minimal movement
  • Strong ligaments provide stability

Symptoms2

  • Pain in lower back and/or buttock
  • Pain may radiate to hip, groin, or thigh
  • Usually one-sided (occasionally both)
  • Worse with sitting, standing from seated, climbing stairs
  • Difficulty sitting for long periods
  • Pain turning over in bed

Causes

  • Too much movement (hypermobility)
  • Too little movement (hypomobility)
  • Pregnancy and childbirth
  • Arthritis
  • Trauma or injury
  • Leg length discrepancy
  • Previous spine surgery

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination with provocation tests
  • Multiple positive tests increase confidence
  • Diagnostic injection (most reliable)
  • Imaging rarely helpful

Treatment3

Conservative

  • Physical therapy (core strengthening, stabilization)
  • SI belt for support
  • Activity modification
  • NSAIDs
  • Heat or ice
  • Manual therapy

Injections

  • SI joint corticosteroid injection
  • Can be diagnostic and therapeutic

Procedures

  • Radiofrequency ablation
  • SI joint fusion (severe cases)
