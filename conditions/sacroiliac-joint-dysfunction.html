Sacroiliac Joint Dysfunction

Understanding SI joint pain

Key Takeaways

  • SI joint dysfunction accounts for 15-30% of chronic low back pain cases
  • Diagnosis can be challenging—no single definitive test
  • Physical therapy focusing on stability is often effective
  • More common in women, especially during and after pregnancy

What Is the SI Joint?

The sacroiliac (SI) joints connect the sacrum (base of the spine) to the iliac bones of the pelvis. There are two SI joints, one on each side. They are strong, weight-bearing joints that transfer forces between the upper body and legs.1

SI Joint Function

  • Transfers load from spine to pelvis and legs
  • Acts as a shock absorber
  • Allows small amounts of movement (2-4mm)
  • Stabilized by strong ligaments and muscles
15-30%
of chronic low back pain cases may originate from the SI joint2

Symptoms

SI joint dysfunction can cause:3

Pain Location

  • Low back pain, usually one-sided
  • Pain in buttock or hip
  • May radiate into groin or upper thigh
  • Usually does NOT go below the knee (unlike sciatica)
  • Can feel like deep aching

Aggravating Factors

  • Prolonged sitting or standing
  • Climbing stairs
  • Getting in/out of car
  • Rolling over in bed
  • Walking, especially up hills
  • Standing on one leg

Associated Symptoms

  • Feeling of instability in pelvis
  • Stiffness in low back and hips
  • Pain with transitions (sit to stand)

Causes

Too Much Movement (Hypermobility)

  • Pregnancy (hormones loosen ligaments)
  • Postpartum
  • Generalized hypermobility
  • Trauma

Too Little Movement (Hypomobility)

  • Degenerative changes (arthritis)
  • Previous lumbar fusion
  • Ankylosing spondylitis

Other Causes

  • Leg length discrepancy
  • Gait abnormalities
  • Scoliosis
  • Muscle imbalances
  • Direct trauma or fall

Pregnancy and Postpartum

SI joint pain is very common during pregnancy due to hormonal changes (relaxin) that loosen ligaments, weight gain, and postural changes. It often continues postpartum and may need specific treatment.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis can be challenging:4

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of pain pattern
  • Physical examination
  • Provocative tests (multiple tests increase accuracy)

Common Tests

  • FABER (Patrick's) test
  • Gaenslen's test
  • Sacral thrust test
  • Distraction test
  • Compression test
  • 3 or more positive tests increase likelihood

Imaging

  • X-rays may show arthritis
  • MRI can show inflammation
  • Imaging often normal in dysfunction

Diagnostic Injection

  • Gold standard for confirmation
  • Local anesthetic injected into SI joint
  • 75%+ pain relief suggests SI joint source

Treatment

Conservative treatment is usually effective:5

Physical Therapy

  • Stabilization exercises: Core, gluteal, pelvic floor strengthening
  • Mobility work: If joint is stiff
  • Manual therapy: Mobilization or manipulation
  • Muscle energy techniques
  • Education: Posture, body mechanics

SI Belt

  • Provides external support and stability
  • Especially helpful during pregnancy/postpartum
  • Worn around pelvis, not waist
  • Use during activities, not constantly

Medications

  • NSAIDs for pain and inflammation
  • Acetaminophen
  • Topical treatments

Injections

  • SI joint corticosteroid injections
  • Prolotherapy (evidence limited)
  • PRP (emerging evidence)

Radiofrequency Ablation

  • For persistent pain after positive diagnostic block
  • Uses heat to disrupt pain signals
  • Effects can last months to years

Surgery

  • SI joint fusion—last resort
  • For severe, refractory cases
  • Minimally invasive options available

Self-Care

Helpful Strategies

  • Avoid prolonged one-sided positions
  • Keep knees together when rolling in bed
  • Use pillow between knees when sleeping
  • Sit with equal weight on both sides
  • Avoid crossing legs
  • Ice for acute pain; heat for muscle tension

Exercises

  • Pelvic tilts
  • Bridges (gluteal strengthening)
  • Bird-dog exercise
  • Clamshells
  • Gentle stretching (avoid overstretching if hypermobile)
