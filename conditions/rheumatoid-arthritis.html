Rheumatoid Arthritis

Understanding and managing this autoimmune condition

Key Takeaways

  • RA is an autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks joint tissues
  • Early treatment is crucial to prevent joint damage
  • Many effective treatments now available, including DMARDs and biologics
  • With proper treatment, many people achieve remission or low disease activity

What Is Rheumatoid Arthritis?

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic autoimmune disease in which the body's immune system mistakenly attacks the lining of the joints (synovium), causing inflammation, pain, and eventually joint damage.1

~1.3 Million
Americans affected by rheumatoid arthritis2

Key Facts

  • Affects about 0.5-1% of the population
  • 2-3 times more common in women
  • Can begin at any age, most commonly 30-60
  • A systemic disease—can affect other organs beyond joints
  • Different from osteoarthritis (wear-and-tear arthritis)

RA vs. Osteoarthritis

Unlike osteoarthritis (OA), which is caused by joint wear and tear, RA is an autoimmune condition. RA typically affects joints symmetrically, causes morning stiffness lasting over an hour, and can occur at any age. OA usually affects weight-bearing joints, causes brief stiffness, and is more common with age.

Symptoms

RA symptoms can vary in severity and may come and go:3

Joint Symptoms

  • Pain: Aching or throbbing, often worse in morning
  • Swelling: Soft, spongy swelling of joints
  • Stiffness: Morning stiffness lasting >30-60 minutes
  • Warmth and redness: Over affected joints
  • Symmetric pattern: Same joints on both sides affected

Commonly Affected Joints

  • Hands (knuckles, middle finger joints)
  • Wrists
  • Feet (ball of foot)
  • Knees
  • Ankles
  • Shoulders
  • Elbows
  • Cervical spine (neck)

Systemic Symptoms

  • Fatigue (often significant)
  • Low-grade fever
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weight loss
  • General feeling of being unwell

Extra-Articular Manifestations

RA can affect other body systems:

  • Rheumatoid nodules: Firm bumps under skin
  • Lungs: Inflammation, nodules, interstitial lung disease
  • Eyes: Dryness, inflammation (scleritis)
  • Heart: Increased cardiovascular risk
  • Blood vessels: Vasculitis (rare)

Diagnosis

RA diagnosis involves clinical evaluation and testing:4

Physical Examination

  • Assessment of joint swelling, tenderness, warmth
  • Range of motion testing
  • Examination for rheumatoid nodules
  • Pattern of joint involvement

Blood Tests

  • Rheumatoid factor (RF): Present in ~70-80% of RA patients
  • Anti-CCP antibodies: More specific for RA
  • ESR and CRP: Markers of inflammation
  • CBC: May show anemia

Imaging

  • X-rays: Baseline and to monitor for erosions
  • Ultrasound: Detects early inflammation and erosions
  • MRI: Most sensitive for early changes

Seronegative RA

About 20-30% of people with RA test negative for RF and anti-CCP antibodies. This is called "seronegative RA" and is still RA—it is diagnosed based on clinical features and imaging. Seronegative RA may have a different prognosis.

Treatment Overview

Modern RA treatment aims for remission or low disease activity. The approach has transformed outcomes:5

Treatment Goals

  • Achieve remission or low disease activity
  • Prevent joint damage
  • Maintain function and quality of life
  • Minimize medication side effects

Treat-to-Target Approach

  • Set a target (remission or low disease activity)
  • Monitor disease activity regularly
  • Adjust treatment until target is reached
  • Early, aggressive treatment produces better outcomes
Window of Opportunity
Early treatment (within 3-6 months of symptoms) leads to better long-term outcomes6

Medications

Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)

The foundation of RA treatment—slow disease progression:5

  • Methotrexate: First-line treatment, taken weekly
  • Sulfasalazine: Often used in combination
  • Leflunomide: Alternative to methotrexate
  • Hydroxychloroquine: Often used in combination

Biologic DMARDs

Target specific parts of the immune system:

  • TNF inhibitors: Adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, certolizumab, golimumab
  • IL-6 inhibitors: Tocilizumab, sarilumab
  • T-cell inhibitor: Abatacept
  • B-cell inhibitor: Rituximab

JAK Inhibitors

Newer oral medications:

  • Tofacitinib, baricitinib, upadacitinib
  • Target intracellular signaling pathways
  • Some safety considerations (discuss with rheumatologist)

Supportive Medications

  • NSAIDs: For symptom relief (do not prevent damage)
  • Corticosteroids: Short-term for flares or bridging to DMARDs
  • Analgesics: For pain management

Medication Monitoring

RA medications require regular monitoring through blood tests. Keep all appointments and report new symptoms to your rheumatologist. Vaccines may need to be updated before starting certain medications.

Living with RA

Exercise and Physical Activity

  • Regular exercise is essential—reduces pain and improves function
  • Low-impact activities: swimming, walking, cycling
  • Range of motion exercises to maintain flexibility
  • Strengthening exercises to support joints
  • Physical therapy can provide personalized programs

Joint Protection

  • Use larger, stronger joints when possible
  • Avoid positions that stress joints
  • Use assistive devices when helpful
  • Balance activity with rest
  • Maintain good posture

Managing Fatigue

  • Prioritize and pace activities
  • Take rest breaks
  • Good sleep hygiene
  • Address anemia if present
  • Exercise actually helps reduce fatigue

Diet and Nutrition

  • Anti-inflammatory diet may help
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) have some evidence
  • Maintain healthy weight to reduce joint stress
  • Mediterranean diet associated with benefits

Monitoring and Follow-Up

Disease Activity Measures

Your rheumatologist may use standardized measures:

  • DAS28 (Disease Activity Score)
  • CDAI (Clinical Disease Activity Index)
  • SDAI (Simplified Disease Activity Index)
  • Patient-reported outcomes

Regular Monitoring

  • Rheumatology visits every 1-3 months when adjusting treatment
  • Less frequent once stable
  • Regular blood tests for medication monitoring
  • Periodic imaging to assess for damage

Managing Flares

  • Have a plan discussed with your rheumatologist
  • May involve short-term corticosteroids
  • Rest but maintain gentle movement
  • Ice or heat as helpful
  • Contact provider if flares are frequent or severe

Associated Conditions

People with RA have increased risk of other conditions:7

  • Cardiovascular disease: Increased risk—manage risk factors
  • Osteoporosis: From disease and corticosteroid use
  • Infections: From disease and immunosuppressive medications
  • Sjögren's syndrome: Dry eyes and mouth
  • Depression and anxiety: Common with chronic disease
