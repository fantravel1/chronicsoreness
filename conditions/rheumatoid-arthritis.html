What is Rheumatoid Arthritis?

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks the synovium (lining of the joints), causing inflammation, pain, and eventually joint damage. Unlike osteoarthritis (wear and tear), RA is an inflammatory condition that can affect the whole body.

Symptoms

Joint Symptoms

  • Symmetric: Same joints on both sides affected (e.g., both hands)
  • Commonly affected: Fingers, wrists, feet, knees, ankles
  • Morning stiffness: Lasting >1 hour (a hallmark symptom)3
  • Swelling: Soft, boggy joint swelling
  • Warmth: Inflamed joints feel warm
  • Pain: Worse with rest, improves with movement initially

Systemic Symptoms

  • Fatigue (often profound)
  • Low-grade fever
  • Loss of appetite, weight loss
  • Rheumatoid nodules (firm lumps under skin)
  • General feeling of being unwell

Extra-Articular Manifestations

RA can affect organs beyond joints:

  • Lungs (inflammation, nodules)
  • Eyes (dry eyes, inflammation)
  • Heart (increased cardiovascular risk)4
  • Blood vessels (vasculitis)
  • Skin

Women-Specific Considerations

  • Hormonal influence: Often improves during pregnancy, may flare postpartum
  • Menopause: New onset or worsening may occur around menopause
  • Oral contraceptives: May slightly reduce risk5
  • Pregnancy planning: Some medications must be stopped before conception; work with rheumatologist
  • Breastfeeding: Many RA medications are compatible; discuss options

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is based on clinical criteria, blood tests, and imaging:

Blood Tests

  • Rheumatoid Factor (RF): Positive in ~70% of RA patients
  • Anti-CCP antibodies: More specific; can appear before symptoms
  • ESR and CRP: Markers of inflammation
  • Complete blood count: Anemia is common

Note: "Seronegative RA" (negative RF and anti-CCP) occurs in ~30% of cases.

Imaging

  • X-rays: Show joint damage, erosions (may be normal early)
  • Ultrasound: Detects early inflammation
  • MRI: Most sensitive for early changes

Treatment Goals

Treat-to-Target Approach

Modern RA management aims for remission or low disease activity. Early, aggressive treatment prevents irreversible joint damage. Regular monitoring and medication adjustment are key.6

Medications

Disease-Modifying Anti-Rheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)

These are the foundation of RA treatment—they slow disease progression and prevent joint damage.

Conventional DMARDs

  • Methotrexate: First-line treatment; weekly dosing; very effective7
  • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil): Often combined with methotrexate; requires eye monitoring
  • Sulfasalazine: Alternative or add-on option
  • Leflunomide: Alternative to methotrexate

Biologic DMARDs

For inadequate response to conventional DMARDs:

  • TNF inhibitors: Adalimumab (Humira), etanercept (Enbrel), infliximab (Remicade), etc.
  • IL-6 inhibitors: Tocilizumab (Actemra), sarilumab (Kevzara)
  • B-cell therapy: Rituximab (Rituxan)
  • T-cell modulator: Abatacept (Orencia)

Targeted Synthetic DMARDs (JAK Inhibitors)

  • Tofacitinib (Xeljanz), baricitinib (Olumiant), upadacitinib (Rinvoq)
  • Oral medications; effective for some who don't respond to biologics
  • Require monitoring for specific risks8

Other Medications

  • NSAIDs: For symptom relief; don't prevent damage
  • Corticosteroids: "Bridge therapy" while DMARDs take effect; long-term use has risks

Lifestyle Management

Exercise

  • Essential: Exercise improves function and doesn't worsen RA9
  • Low-impact: Swimming, water aerobics, cycling, walking
  • Strengthening: Builds muscle to support joints
  • Flexibility: Maintains range of motion
  • During flares: Gentle range-of-motion; rest inflamed joints

Nutrition

  • Mediterranean diet: Anti-inflammatory; associated with less RA disease activity10
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: Fish oil may reduce inflammation
  • Vitamin D: Deficiency common; check levels
  • Maintain healthy weight: Excess weight stresses joints

Joint Protection

  • Use larger joints when possible (push with palm, not fingers)
  • Ergonomic tools (jar openers, enlarged grips)
  • Splints during flares
  • Balance activity and rest

Managing Flares

  • Contact rheumatologist—may need short-term steroid
  • Rest inflamed joints but maintain gentle movement
  • Ice for acute inflammation; heat for stiffness
  • Prioritize essential activities only
  • Track triggers (stress, infection, skipping medication)

Working With Your Rheumatologist

  • Regular visits (typically every 3-6 months when stable)
  • Blood monitoring for medications
  • Disease activity assessments (DAS28, CDAI)
  • Discuss treatment goals and concerns
  • Advocate for yourself if not achieving targets

Prognosis

  • With modern treatment, many achieve remission or low disease activity
  • Early aggressive treatment improves outcomes11
  • Joint damage can be prevented with good control
  • RA increases cardiovascular risk—manage heart health
  • Life expectancy gap has narrowed with better treatment
