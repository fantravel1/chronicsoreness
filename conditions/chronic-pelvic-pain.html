Chronic Pelvic Pain

Understanding and managing persistent pelvic pain in women

Key Takeaways

  • CPP affects approximately 15% of women and has many possible causes
  • Often involves multiple organ systems and contributing factors
  • Requires a multidisciplinary, patient-centered approach
  • Effective treatments existâ€”finding the right combination takes time

What Is Chronic Pelvic Pain?

Chronic pelvic pain (CPP) is persistent pain in the area below the belly button and between the hips that lasts 6 months or longer. It is a common condition that significantly impacts quality of life, relationships, work, and daily functioning.1

~15%
of women of reproductive age experience chronic pelvic pain2

Characteristics of CPP

  • Pain lasting 6 months or more
  • Location below the umbilicus
  • May be constant or intermittent
  • May be cyclic (related to menstrual cycle) or non-cyclic
  • Often difficult to pinpoint exact location
  • May worsen with certain activities or positions

Impact on Daily Life

  • Difficulty with work and daily activities
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Sexual dysfunction and relationship strain
  • Mood changes (depression, anxiety)
  • Social isolation
  • Reduced quality of life

Possible Causes

CPP often has multiple contributing factors. Common causes include:3,4

Gynecological Causes

  • Endometriosis: Tissue similar to uterine lining grows outside the uterus
  • Adenomyosis: Endometrial tissue grows into uterine muscle
  • Ovarian cysts: Fluid-filled sacs on ovaries
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): Infection of reproductive organs
  • Uterine fibroids: Benign growths in the uterus
  • Vulvodynia: Chronic vulvar pain

Urological Causes

  • Interstitial cystitis/bladder pain syndrome: Chronic bladder pain
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections
  • Urethral syndrome

Gastrointestinal Causes

  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS): Common co-occurrence
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Chronic constipation

Musculoskeletal Causes

  • Pelvic floor dysfunction: Very common contributor
  • Myofascial pain: Trigger points in pelvic muscles
  • Hip joint problems
  • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction
  • Pubic symphysis dysfunction

Neurological Causes

  • Pudendal neuralgia: Nerve pain in the pelvic region
  • Nerve entrapment
  • Central sensitization: Nervous system amplifying pain signals

Multiple Causes Are Common

Research shows that women with CPP often have multiple conditions contributing to their pain. For example, many women with endometriosis also have IBS, interstitial cystitis, and/or pelvic floor dysfunction. Addressing all contributing factors leads to better outcomes.

Getting a Diagnosis

Diagnosing CPP can be challenging but thorough evaluation is important:5

Medical History

  • Detailed description of pain (location, timing, triggers)
  • Relationship to menstrual cycle
  • Bowel and bladder symptoms
  • Sexual function
  • Obstetric history
  • Previous surgeries or trauma
  • History of abuse or trauma

Physical Examination

  • Abdominal examination
  • Pelvic examination
  • Assessment for tender points and trigger points
  • Musculoskeletal examination
  • Single-digit vaginal exam to assess pelvic floor

Possible Tests

  • Ultrasound: Imaging of pelvic organs
  • MRI: More detailed imaging if needed
  • Laparoscopy: Surgical visualization of pelvis (for endometriosis)
  • Cystoscopy: Bladder examination
  • Blood tests: Rule out infections, inflammation
  • Urine tests: Screen for bladder issues

Seek Care If:

Severe sudden onset pain, fever, abnormal vaginal bleeding, pain during pregnancy, or signs of infection require prompt medical evaluation.

Treatment Approaches

Effective CPP management typically involves multiple approaches:6

Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

Often a cornerstone of treatment:

  • Assessment and treatment of pelvic floor muscles
  • Manual therapy for trigger points
  • Biofeedback training
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Home exercise programs
  • Strong evidence of effectiveness7

Medications

  • NSAIDs: For inflammation and pain
  • Hormonal treatments: Birth control pills, progestins, GnRH agonists
  • Neuromodulators: Amitriptyline, gabapentin, duloxetine
  • Muscle relaxants: For pelvic floor spasm
  • Topical treatments: Lidocaine, estrogen creams

Psychological Approaches

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT): Addressing pain-related thoughts and behaviors
  • Mindfulness: Managing pain and stress
  • Trauma-informed therapy: If history of abuse or trauma
  • Sex therapy: Addressing sexual pain and intimacy issues

Interventional Treatments

  • Trigger point injections: Into painful muscle points
  • Nerve blocks: For specific nerve pain
  • Botox injections: For pelvic floor spasm
  • Neuromodulation: Nerve stimulation techniques

Surgery

May be appropriate in selected cases:

  • Laparoscopic excision of endometriosis
  • Hysterectomy (carefully considered, not always effective)
  • Adhesion removal
  • Surgery should be last resort in most cases

Self-Management Strategies

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Exercise: Regular, gentle movement helps
  • Diet: Anti-inflammatory diet, avoid bladder irritants if IC
  • Sleep: Prioritize quality sleep
  • Stress management: Stress worsens chronic pain

Heat and Cold

  • Heating pad on lower abdomen or back
  • Warm baths
  • Some prefer cold for certain types of pain

Positioning

  • Pillows for support during sleep
  • Positions that reduce pelvic pressure
  • Cushion for sitting

Tracking

  • Keep a pain diary
  • Track relationship to menstrual cycle
  • Note triggers and relieving factors
  • Bring records to medical appointments

Finding the Right Care Team

Specialists Who May Help

  • Gynecologist (preferably with CPP expertise)
  • Pelvic floor physical therapist
  • Pain management specialist
  • Urologist or urogynecologist
  • Gastroenterologist
  • Pain psychologist

Questions to Ask Providers

  • Do you specialize in chronic pelvic pain?
  • What is your approach to CPP treatment?
  • Do you work with a multidisciplinary team?
  • What non-surgical options do you offer?
  • Do you have experience with pelvic floor evaluation?

Validation Is Important

Many women with CPP have experienced dismissal of their pain. A good provider will take your pain seriously, conduct thorough evaluation, and work with you to find effective treatments. If you do not feel heard, seek another provider.

Living with CPP

Relationships and Intimacy

  • Open communication with partners
  • Explore non-painful forms of intimacy
  • Consider couples counseling or sex therapy
  • Pelvic floor PT can help with painful intercourse

Work and Daily Life

  • Workplace accommodations may be available
  • Pacing activities
  • Ergonomic adjustments
  • Planning for flares

Emotional Support

  • Support groups (in-person or online)
  • Mental health care
  • Education and self-advocacy
  • Connect with others who understand
