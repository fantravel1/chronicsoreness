Ankylosing Spondylitis

Understanding and managing inflammatory spine disease

Key Takeaways

  • AS is an inflammatory arthritis primarily affecting the spine and sacroiliac joints
  • Exercise is essential—regular movement prevents stiffness and maintains function
  • Biologic medications have transformed treatment outcomes
  • Early diagnosis and treatment can prevent significant disability

What Is Ankylosing Spondylitis?

Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is a chronic inflammatory disease that primarily affects the spine and sacroiliac joints (where the spine meets the pelvis). Over time, inflammation can lead to new bone formation, potentially causing sections of the spine to fuse.1

Key Facts

  • Part of a group called axial spondyloarthritis
  • Usually begins in late teens to early 30s
  • More commonly diagnosed in men, but women also affected
  • Strong genetic association with HLA-B27 gene
  • Can affect other joints and organs beyond the spine

Symptoms

AS symptoms typically develop gradually:3

Hallmark Symptoms

  • Inflammatory back pain: Key distinguishing feature
  • Pain and stiffness in lower back and buttocks
  • Worse in the morning or after inactivity
  • Improves with movement and exercise
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Symptoms last more than 3 months

Inflammatory vs. Mechanical Back Pain

Improves with exercise Worsens with activity

Other Manifestations

  • Enthesitis: Inflammation where tendons attach to bone (heel, ribs)
  • Peripheral arthritis: Other joints like hips, shoulders, knees
  • Uveitis: Eye inflammation (red, painful eye—requires urgent care)
  • Fatigue: Often significant
  • Chest pain: From rib involvement
  • IBD: Association with inflammatory bowel disease
  • Psoriasis: Some patients also have psoriasis

Diagnosis

Diagnosis often delayed—average 7-10 years from symptom onset:4

Clinical Evaluation

  • Detailed history of back pain characteristics
  • Physical examination of spine mobility
  • Assessment for other features (enthesitis, peripheral joints)

Imaging

  • X-rays: Sacroiliitis, spine changes (may take years to appear)
  • MRI: Can detect earlier inflammation before X-ray changes

Blood Tests

  • HLA-B27: Present in ~90% of AS patients (but also in healthy people)
  • Inflammatory markers: CRP, ESR (may be elevated)
  • No single definitive blood test

Treatment

Treatment aims to reduce symptoms, maintain mobility, and prevent complications:5

NSAIDs

  • First-line treatment
  • More effective when taken regularly (not just as needed)
  • May slow radiographic progression
  • Monitor for GI and cardiovascular side effects

Biologic Medications

When NSAIDs are insufficient:

  • TNF inhibitors: Adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, golimumab, certolizumab
  • IL-17 inhibitors: Secukinumab, ixekizumab
  • JAK inhibitors: Tofacitinib, upadacitinib (newer options)
  • Highly effective for many patients
  • Can reduce inflammation and improve function

Other Medications

  • Conventional DMARDs (sulfasalazine, methotrexate) for peripheral arthritis
  • Local corticosteroid injections
  • Analgesics for breakthrough pain

Exercise: Essential Treatment

Exercise is crucial for AS—as important as medication:6

Why Exercise Matters

  • Maintains spine mobility and flexibility
  • Strengthens muscles supporting the spine
  • Reduces pain and stiffness
  • Improves posture
  • Improves lung function
  • Combats fatigue
  • Improves overall well-being

Recommended Exercises

  • Stretching: Daily spine mobility exercises
  • Strengthening: Core and back muscle strength
  • Aerobic exercise: Swimming, walking, cycling
  • Posture exercises: Maintaining upright posture
  • Breathing exercises: Chest expansion

Swimming

Often ideal for AS:

  • Full-body exercise with minimal joint stress
  • Maintains chest expansion and spine mobility
  • Warm water soothing for stiffness

Physical Therapy

A physical therapist experienced with AS can design an individualized exercise program. Regular PT assessment helps ensure exercises remain appropriate as the condition changes.

Living with AS

Posture Awareness

  • Practice good standing and sitting posture
  • Sleep on a firm mattress
  • Use a thin pillow or none
  • Avoid prolonged stooped positions

Work Modifications

  • Ergonomic workstation setup
  • Regular breaks to move and stretch
  • Adjustable desk options
  • Workplace accommodations may be available

Monitoring

  • Regular rheumatology follow-up
  • Monitor for eye symptoms (uveitis)—seek urgent care if red painful eye
  • Periodic spine imaging
  • Cardiovascular risk management
