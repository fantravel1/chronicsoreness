Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Understanding compression of the median nerve at the wrist

What Is Carpal Tunnel Syndrome?

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) is a common condition caused by compression of the median nerve as it passes through the carpal tunnelâ€”a narrow passageway on the palm side of the wrist. This compression causes numbness, tingling, and weakness in the hand and fingers.1

CTS affects about 3-6% of the general population and is one of the most common causes of hand pain and numbness. It's more common in women and typically develops between ages 40-60.

Anatomy

  • Carpal tunnel - Narrow passage formed by wrist bones and ligament
  • Median nerve - Provides sensation to thumb, index, middle, and half of ring finger
  • Flexor tendons - Also pass through the tunnel
  • Limited space - Any swelling compresses contents

Symptoms

Classic Symptoms2

  • Numbness and tingling - Thumb, index, middle, and ring fingers
  • Worse at night - Often wakes people from sleep
  • Hand weakness - Dropping things, difficulty with fine motor tasks
  • Pain - Can radiate up the forearm
  • Shaking hand helps - Temporary relief from shaking or hanging hand

Symptom Progression

  • Early - Intermittent numbness, mainly at night
  • Moderate - More frequent symptoms, daytime symptoms appear
  • Severe - Constant numbness, muscle wasting at thumb base, weakness

Which Fingers Are Affected?

CTS affects the thumb, index finger, middle finger, and the thumb-side half of the ring fingerâ€”these are supplied by the median nerve. The pinky and the other half of the ring finger are NOT affected (they're supplied by the ulnar nerve). If your pinky is numb, it's not carpal tunnel.

Causes and Risk Factors

Contributing Factors

  • Repetitive hand motions - Especially with wrist flexion or extension
  • Anatomy - Some people have smaller carpal tunnels
  • Female sex - Higher risk, possibly due to smaller tunnel
  • Pregnancy - Fluid retention; often resolves after delivery
  • Medical conditions - Diabetes, thyroid disorders, rheumatoid arthritis
  • Wrist injury or fracture
  • Obesity

Occupational Factors

  • Assembly line work
  • Keyboard use (disputedâ€”less impact than thought)
  • Use of vibrating tools
  • Forceful gripping
  • Awkward wrist positions

Diagnosis

Clinical Tests

  • Phalen's test - Holding wrists flexed for 60 seconds
  • Tinel's sign - Tapping over carpal tunnel
  • Carpal compression test - Direct pressure over tunnel
  • Clinical tests help but aren't definitive

Electrodiagnostic Testing3

  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) - Measures nerve signal speed
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Checks muscle response
  • Confirms diagnosis and severity
  • Often recommended before surgery

Imaging

  • Usually not needed for diagnosis
  • Ultrasound can show nerve swelling
  • MRI rarely needed

Treatment

Conservative Treatment4

Wrist Splinting

  • Neutral position splint (wrist not flexed)
  • Worn at night (symptoms often worse then)
  • Can wear during aggravating activities
  • Often first-line treatment
  • Most effective for mild-moderate CTS

Activity Modification

  • Avoid aggravating activities when possible
  • Ergonomic adjustments
  • Frequent breaks during repetitive tasks
  • Neutral wrist position

Corticosteroid Injections

  • Injection into carpal tunnel
  • Can provide weeks to months of relief
  • May be diagnostic (confirms CTS if helpful)
  • Not a permanent solution for most

Other Conservative Options

  • NSAIDs - May help but limited evidence
  • Physical/occupational therapy
  • Nerve gliding exercises
  • Ultrasound therapy - Limited evidence

Surgical Treatment5

Surgery is considered when:

  • Conservative treatment fails
  • Severe symptoms or muscle wasting
  • Significant nerve damage on testing

Carpal Tunnel Release

  • Cuts the transverse carpal ligament to enlarge tunnel
  • Can be done open or endoscopically
  • Outpatient procedure
  • High success rate (70-90%)
  • Recovery takes weeks to months

Don't Wait Too Long

Severe, prolonged nerve compression can cause permanent damage. If you have constant numbness or muscle wasting (visible loss of muscle at thumb base), don't delay seeking treatment. Nerve damage that has progressed too far may not fully recover even after surgery.

Self-Care and Prevention

At Home

  • Wear splint at night
  • Take breaks from repetitive tasks
  • Keep wrists neutral when possible
  • Ice for flare-ups
  • Gentle stretching and nerve gliding

Ergonomic Tips

  • Keyboard and mouse at proper height
  • Wrists should be neutral, not bent up or down
  • Consider ergonomic keyboard or mouse
  • Take micro-breaks frequently

Prognosis

  • Mild CTS often responds well to conservative treatment
  • Surgery has high success rate
  • Some recurrence possible
  • Earlier treatment generally means better outcomes
