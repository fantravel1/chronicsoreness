Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Understanding and treating median nerve compression

Key Takeaways

  • CTS causes numbness, tingling, and pain in the thumb, index, middle fingers
  • Women are 3 times more likely to develop CTS than men
  • Many cases improve with conservative treatment
  • Early treatment prevents permanent nerve damage

What Is Carpal Tunnel Syndrome?

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) occurs when the median nerve, which runs through a narrow passage in the wrist called the carpal tunnel, becomes compressed. This causes numbness, tingling, and weakness in the hand. It's one of the most common nerve compression syndromes.1

3-6%
of the general adult population has carpal tunnel syndrome2

Anatomy

  • Carpal tunnel: Narrow passageway in wrist formed by bones and ligament
  • Median nerve: Provides sensation to thumb, index, middle, and half of ring finger
  • Flexor tendons: Also pass through the tunnel
  • Limited space—any swelling causes compression

Symptoms

Symptoms typically develop gradually:3

Classic Symptoms

  • Numbness and tingling in thumb, index, middle, and ring fingers
  • Pain in wrist, hand, or forearm
  • Symptoms often worse at night
  • "Electric shock" sensations in fingers
  • Symptoms may wake you from sleep

Progressive Symptoms

  • Weakness in hand
  • Difficulty gripping objects
  • Dropping things
  • Clumsiness with fine motor tasks
  • Muscle wasting at base of thumb (severe cases)

Symptom Patterns

  • Often worse at night or upon waking
  • Aggravated by activities requiring wrist flexion
  • Worse with repetitive hand use
  • May be relieved by shaking hands
  • Often affects both hands (may be asymmetric)

Night Symptoms

Symptoms often worsen at night because many people sleep with wrists flexed, which increases pressure in the carpal tunnel. Waking with numb or tingling hands is a common early symptom.

Causes and Risk Factors

Contributing Factors

  • Anatomic factors: Smaller carpal tunnel
  • Health conditions: Diabetes, thyroid disorders, rheumatoid arthritis
  • Hormonal changes: Pregnancy, menopause
  • Inflammatory conditions: Cause tendon swelling
  • Fluid retention: Pregnancy, kidney disease
  • Wrist injuries: Fractures, dislocations

Risk Factors

  • Sex: Women 3x more likely
  • Age: More common 40-60
  • Occupation: Vibrating tools, assembly line work
  • Obesity: Increased risk
  • Family history: Genetic component

Work-Related Factors

  • Prolonged wrist flexion or extension
  • Repetitive hand motions
  • Use of vibrating tools
  • Forceful gripping
  • Computer work is controversial—less clearly linked than once thought

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of symptoms and pattern
  • Physical examination
  • Phalen's test: Flexing wrists to reproduce symptoms
  • Tinel's test: Tapping over nerve
  • Sensory and strength testing

Diagnostic Tests

  • Nerve conduction study (NCS): Measures electrical signals in nerve
  • Electromyography (EMG): Measures muscle electrical activity
  • These tests confirm diagnosis and assess severity
  • Ultrasound: Can show nerve swelling

When Testing Is Needed

  • Symptoms not clearly typical
  • Before surgery is considered
  • To rule out other conditions
  • Poor response to conservative treatment

Treatment

Many cases improve with conservative treatment:4,5

Splinting

  • Wrist splint keeps wrist in neutral position
  • Worn at night initially (when symptoms often worst)
  • May be worn during aggravating activities
  • First-line treatment for mild to moderate CTS

Activity Modification

  • Take frequent breaks from repetitive tasks
  • Adjust workstation ergonomics
  • Avoid extreme wrist positions
  • Reduce force and grip intensity

Exercises and Physical Therapy

  • Nerve gliding exercises
  • Tendon gliding exercises
  • Stretching
  • Strengthening (once symptoms improve)

Medications

  • NSAIDs: May help if inflammation present
  • Corticosteroid injection: Into carpal tunnel
  • Injection can provide temporary relief
  • Helps predict response to surgery

Surgery

Carpal tunnel release—considered when:

  • Conservative treatment fails after 3-6 months
  • Severe symptoms
  • Significant nerve damage on testing
  • Muscle wasting present
  • Can be done open or endoscopically
  • Success rate approximately 70-90%

Self-Care

At Home

  • Wear splint at night
  • Take breaks from repetitive activities
  • Keep wrists neutral during activities
  • Gentle stretching throughout day
  • Ice if swelling present

At Work

  • Adjust keyboard height and angle
  • Use ergonomic keyboard/mouse if helpful
  • Take microbreaks every 20-30 minutes
  • Stretch hands and wrists regularly
  • Keep wrists straight, not bent up or down

Nerve Gliding Exercise

  1. Make a fist
  2. Straighten fingers (thumb tucked)
  3. Bend wrist back, extend thumb
  4. Turn palm up
  5. Gently stretch thumb with other hand
  6. Hold each position 3-5 seconds
  7. Repeat sequence 5 times, several times daily

When to See a Doctor

Seek Evaluation If

  • Symptoms persist despite self-care
  • Numbness or tingling interferes with daily activities
  • Weakness in hand or grip
  • Symptoms spreading or worsening
  • Dropping objects frequently

Don't Delay Treatment

Prolonged nerve compression can cause permanent damage. If you have persistent symptoms, especially weakness or constant numbness, seek evaluation. Early treatment has better outcomes.
