Rotator Cuff Problems

Understanding shoulder pain from rotator cuff issues

Key Takeaways

  • Rotator cuff problems are the most common cause of shoulder pain
  • Range from tendinitis to partial and complete tears
  • Many cases improve with physical therapy—surgery not always needed
  • Even complete tears may heal or be managed without surgery in some cases

What Is the Rotator Cuff?

The rotator cuff is a group of four muscles and their tendons that surround the shoulder joint, holding the ball of the upper arm bone (humerus) in the shoulder socket. They provide stability and enable arm rotation and lifting movements.1

4.5 Million
visits to doctors annually for rotator cuff problems in the US2

The Four Muscles

  • Supraspinatus: Most commonly injured; lifts arm
  • Infraspinatus: External rotation
  • Teres minor: External rotation
  • Subscapularis: Internal rotation

Types of Rotator Cuff Problems

Tendinitis/Tendinopathy

  • Irritation or degeneration of tendons
  • Often from overuse or repetitive motion
  • Usually responds well to conservative treatment

Bursitis

  • Inflammation of the bursa (fluid-filled sac)
  • Often occurs with tendinitis
  • Causes pain with arm movement

Partial Tear

  • Incomplete tear of tendon
  • May occur from injury or wear
  • Many treated successfully without surgery

Complete (Full-Thickness) Tear

  • Tendon completely separated
  • From acute injury or progressive degeneration
  • Treatment depends on tear size, symptoms, patient factors

Impingement Syndrome

  • Tendons pinched between bones
  • Pain with overhead activities
  • Often related to tendinopathy

Symptoms

Common Symptoms

  • Pain on the outside/front of shoulder
  • Pain with overhead activities
  • Pain reaching behind back
  • Night pain (especially lying on affected side)
  • Weakness with lifting or rotating arm
  • Catching or clicking

Acute Tear Symptoms

  • Sudden, intense pain (often after injury)
  • Immediate weakness
  • Snapping sensation

Chronic/Degenerative Symptoms

  • Gradual onset of pain
  • Progressive weakness
  • May worsen over time

Causes and Risk Factors

Causes

  • Acute injury: Fall, lifting heavy object, pulling
  • Overuse: Repetitive overhead motions
  • Degeneration: Age-related wear
  • Impingement: Bone spurs or anatomic factors

Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases significantly after 40
  • Occupation: Overhead work (painters, carpenters)
  • Sports: Baseball, tennis, swimming
  • Smoking: Affects tendon healing
  • Family history: Genetic component

Tears Without Symptoms

Many rotator cuff tears cause no symptoms. Studies show increasing prevalence of asymptomatic tears with age—found incidentally on imaging. An MRI showing a tear doesn't mean it's causing your pain.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History and physical examination
  • Specific tests for rotator cuff (e.g., empty can test, lift-off test)
  • Assessment of strength and range of motion

Imaging

  • X-ray: Shows bone spurs, arthritis
  • Ultrasound: Can visualize tears, good for dynamic assessment
  • MRI: Best for detailed view of soft tissues

Treatment

Most rotator cuff problems improve without surgery:3,4

Conservative Treatment

  • Rest and activity modification: Avoid aggravating activities
  • Ice: For acute pain and swelling
  • NSAIDs: For pain and inflammation
  • Physical therapy: Essential—strengthening, stretching, posture

Physical Therapy Focus

  • Rotator cuff strengthening
  • Scapular (shoulder blade) stabilization
  • Posture correction
  • Flexibility work
  • Gradual return to activities

Injections

  • Corticosteroid injections may help short-term
  • Can allow participation in PT
  • Limit number of injections (may weaken tendon)

Surgery

Consider when:

  • Conservative treatment fails after 3-6+ months
  • Acute, large tears in active individuals
  • Significant weakness
  • Types: arthroscopic repair, open repair
  • Recovery takes months

Self-Care

During Acute Phase

  • Rest from aggravating activities
  • Ice 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Avoid sleeping on affected side
  • Take pain medication as needed

Basic Exercises (Once Acute Pain Subsides)

  • Pendulum exercises
  • Gentle range of motion
  • Isometrics (strength without movement)
  • Progress to resistance exercises under PT guidance

Prevention

  • Strengthen rotator cuff and shoulder blade muscles
  • Warm up before activities
  • Avoid repetitive overhead motions when possible
  • Maintain good posture
  • Take breaks during repetitive tasks
  • Don't ignore shoulder pain
