Episodic vs Chronic Migraine

  • Episodic migraine: <15 headache days per month
  • Chronic migraine: ≥15 headache days per month for >3 months, with ≥8 having migraine features3
  • About 3% of episodic migraine transforms to chronic annually

Symptoms

Typical Migraine Features

  • Pain: Usually one-sided, pulsating/throbbing
  • Intensity: Moderate to severe
  • Duration: 4-72 hours untreated
  • Photophobia: Sensitivity to light
  • Phonophobia: Sensitivity to sound
  • Nausea/vomiting: Common
  • Worsened by activity: Movement makes it worse

Aura (in ~25% of migraineurs)

  • Visual disturbances (zigzag lines, blind spots, flashing lights)
  • Sensory symptoms (tingling, numbness)
  • Speech difficulties
  • Usually lasts 20-60 minutes before headache

Women-Specific Factors

Menstrual Migraine

  • Timing: Occurs around menstruation (day -2 to +3 of cycle)4
  • Cause: Estrogen withdrawal triggers attacks
  • Characteristics: Often longer, more severe, harder to treat
  • Prevention: Continuous hormonal contraception, perimenstrual triptans or NSAIDs

Pregnancy

  • Many women improve during pregnancy (especially 2nd/3rd trimester)
  • Some worsen or experience new onset
  • Many preventives are contraindicated—work with specialist
  • Acetaminophen and certain triptans may be options

Menopause

  • Migraines often worsen during perimenopause (fluctuating hormones)
  • Many improve after menopause (stable hormones)
  • HRT effects variable—may help or worsen

Contraception

  • Migraine with aura + estrogen: Increased stroke risk—avoid combined hormonal contraception5
  • Migraine without aura: Combined methods may be used with caution
  • Progestin-only methods: Generally safe for all migraineurs

Common Triggers

Hormonal

  • Menstruation
  • Ovulation
  • Hormone changes

Sleep

  • Too little sleep
  • Too much sleep
  • Irregular schedule

Dietary

  • Skipped meals
  • Alcohol (red wine)
  • Caffeine changes
  • Dehydration

Environmental

  • Weather changes
  • Bright/flickering light
  • Strong smells

Stress

  • Acute stress
  • Letdown after stress
  • Anxiety

Treatment

Acute Treatment (Stopping an Attack)

Take early—most effective at onset:

  • Triptans: First-line for moderate-severe migraine6
  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen—effective for mild-moderate
  • Acetaminophen: Option if NSAIDs contraindicated
  • Gepants (CGRP antagonists): Ubrogepant, rimegepant—newer options
  • Ditans: Lasmiditan—alternative to triptans
  • Anti-nausea medications: Metoclopramide, ondansetron

Medication Overuse Headache

Using acute medications too frequently (>10-15 days/month) can cause medication overuse headache—paradoxically increasing headache frequency. Limit acute medication use and discuss prevention if needed frequently.7

Preventive Treatment

Consider if: ≥4 migraine days/month, attacks significantly disable you, or acute treatments don't work well.

Oral Preventives

  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol, metoprolol
  • Antidepressants: Amitriptyline, venlafaxine
  • Anticonvulsants: Topiramate (note: teratogenic), valproate
  • ARBs: Candesartan

CGRP Monoclonal Antibodies

  • Erenumab (Aimovig), fremanezumab (Ajovy), galcanezumab (Emgality), eptinezumab (Vyepti)
  • Monthly or quarterly injections
  • Well-tolerated; effective for many8
  • Often require prior authorization

Other

  • Botox: FDA-approved for chronic migraine (31+ injections every 12 weeks)9
  • Devices: CEFALY (supraorbital stimulation), gammaCore (vagus nerve stimulation)

Supplements (Some Evidence)

  • Magnesium: 400-600 mg daily (especially for menstrual migraine)10
  • Riboflavin (B2): 400 mg daily
  • CoQ10: 100-300 mg daily
  • Feverfew: Mixed evidence
  • Butterbur: Effective but safety concerns (hepatotoxicity)

Lifestyle Management

  • Regular sleep schedule: Same wake time every day
  • Regular meals: Don't skip meals
  • Stay hydrated: Dehydration is a trigger
  • Regular exercise: Aerobic exercise reduces migraine frequency11
  • Stress management: Mindfulness, relaxation techniques
  • Trigger management: Avoid known triggers when possible

When to Seek Urgent Care

  • "Worst headache of my life" or sudden severe onset
  • Headache with fever, stiff neck
  • New neurological symptoms
  • Headache after head injury
  • New headache pattern after age 50
  • Headache that's progressively worsening
