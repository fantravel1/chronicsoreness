Migraines

Understanding and managing this disabling neurological condition

Key Takeaways

  • Migraines are a neurological disease, not just a bad headache
  • Affect about 12% of the population, more common in women
  • Many effective treatments exist for both prevention and acute attacks
  • Lifestyle modifications and trigger management are important components

What Are Migraines?

Migraines are a complex neurological condition involving much more than head pain. They are caused by abnormal brain activity affecting nerve signals, chemicals, and blood vessels in the brain.1

~1 Billion
people worldwide affected by migraines2

Migraine Statistics

  • Affects about 12% of the population
  • 3 times more common in women than men
  • Often runs in families (genetic component)
  • Ranked as one of the most disabling conditions worldwide
  • Peak prevalence in ages 25-55

Migraine Symptoms

Migraines typically involve multiple symptoms:3

Head Pain

  • Usually moderate to severe intensity
  • Often one-sided (but can be both)
  • Throbbing or pulsating quality
  • Worsened by physical activity
  • Lasts 4-72 hours if untreated

Associated Symptoms

  • Nausea and/or vomiting
  • Photophobia: Sensitivity to light
  • Phonophobia: Sensitivity to sound
  • Osmophobia: Sensitivity to smells
  • Dizziness or vertigo
  • Difficulty concentrating ("brain fog")
  • Fatigue
  • Neck pain or stiffness

Migraine Phases

A migraine attack can have four phases (not everyone experiences all):

  1. Prodrome (hours to days before): Mood changes, food cravings, yawning, fatigue, neck stiffness
  2. Aura (if present, 5-60 minutes before or during): Visual disturbances, sensory changes, speech difficulties
  3. Headache phase: Head pain and associated symptoms
  4. Postdrome (after headache resolves): Fatigue, difficulty concentrating, mood changes

Types of Migraines

Migraine Without Aura

  • Most common type (~70-75% of migraines)
  • Headache with associated symptoms
  • No preceding aura

Migraine With Aura

  • About 25-30% of people with migraines
  • Aura symptoms precede or accompany headache
  • Visual aura: Flashing lights, zigzag lines, blind spots, shimmering
  • Sensory aura: Tingling, numbness in face or arm
  • Speech aura: Difficulty finding words
  • Symptoms typically develop over 5+ minutes and last under 60 minutes

Chronic Migraine

  • 15 or more headache days per month
  • At least 8 days with migraine features
  • For 3+ months
  • Significantly disabling
  • May need preventive treatment

Other Types

  • Vestibular migraine: Prominent dizziness/vertigo
  • Hemiplegic migraine: Temporary weakness on one side
  • Menstrual migraine: Related to menstrual cycle
  • Retinal migraine: Vision loss in one eye

When to Seek Emergency Care

Seek immediate care for: sudden severe headache ("worst headache of life"), headache with fever and stiff neck, headache with confusion or weakness, headache after head injury, or new headache patterns after age 50.

Migraine Triggers

Identifying and managing triggers can reduce migraine frequency:4

Common Triggers

  • Hormonal: Menstruation, ovulation, hormone medications
  • Sleep: Too little sleep, too much sleep, irregular schedule
  • Stress: During or after stressful periods
  • Dietary: Skipped meals, dehydration, certain foods
  • Environmental: Weather changes, bright lights, strong smells
  • Physical: Intense exercise, physical exertion

Dietary Triggers

Common food triggers (vary by individual):

  • Alcohol (especially red wine)
  • Aged cheeses
  • Processed meats (nitrates)
  • MSG
  • Caffeine (too much or withdrawal)
  • Artificial sweeteners
  • Chocolate (though often a prodrome craving, not trigger)

Trigger Diary

Keep a headache diary tracking potential triggers, symptoms, and timing. Over time, patterns may emerge. Note that triggers often combine—a single trigger may not cause a migraine, but several together might. Apps like Migraine Buddy can help with tracking.

Acute Treatments

Treatments for stopping a migraine in progress:5

Over-the-Counter Options

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin
  • Acetaminophen: Less effective than NSAIDs for migraines
  • Combination products: Excedrin Migraine (acetaminophen, aspirin, caffeine)
  • Most effective when taken early in the attack

Prescription Medications

  • Triptans: Sumatriptan, rizatriptan, etc.—very effective, target migraine-specific pathways
  • Gepants (CGRP antagonists): Ubrogepant, rimegepant—newer option
  • Ditans: Lasmiditan—for those who cannot take triptans
  • Anti-nausea medications: Metoclopramide, prochlorperazine
  • Ergots: Less commonly used now

Treatment Tips

  • Treat early—most medications work better at onset
  • Rest in a dark, quiet room
  • Apply cold to head or heat to neck
  • Stay hydrated
  • Caffeine can help if not overused

Medication Overuse Headache

Using acute migraine medications too frequently (more than 10-15 days per month) can cause rebound headaches (medication overuse headache). If you need frequent acute treatment, discuss prevention with your doctor.

Preventive Treatments

For those with frequent migraines (4+ per month) or significantly disabling attacks:6

Oral Medications

  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol, metoprolol
  • Antidepressants: Amitriptyline, venlafaxine
  • Anticonvulsants: Topiramate, valproate
  • CGRP antagonists: Atogepant, rimegepant (daily)

Injectable CGRP Medications

  • Erenumab (Aimovig)
  • Fremanezumab (Ajovy)
  • Galcanezumab (Emgality)
  • Eptinezumab (Vyepti—IV infusion)
  • Specifically developed for migraine prevention
  • Monthly or quarterly injections

Other Preventive Options

  • Botox: FDA-approved for chronic migraine (31+ injections every 12 weeks)
  • Nerve blocks: Occipital nerve blocks
  • Devices: CEFALY, SpringTMS, gammaCore

Supplements with Evidence

  • Magnesium: 400-600mg daily
  • Riboflavin (B2): 400mg daily
  • Coenzyme Q10: 100mg three times daily
  • Feverfew: Some evidence
  • Butterbur: Effective but safety concerns (liver toxicity with some preparations)

Lifestyle Management

Regular Routines

  • Sleep: Consistent sleep schedule, 7-8 hours
  • Meals: Regular meal times, don't skip meals
  • Hydration: Adequate water intake
  • Exercise: Regular moderate exercise (may reduce frequency)

Stress Management

  • Relaxation techniques
  • Biofeedback (good evidence for migraines)
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Mindfulness meditation

Work and Activity

  • Take breaks from screens
  • Good lighting and ergonomics
  • Manage workload and deadlines
  • Know workplace accommodations available

Migraines in Women

Migraines have unique considerations for women:7

Menstrual Migraine

  • Related to estrogen drop before menstruation
  • May benefit from "mini-prevention" around periods
  • Continuous hormone contraception may help

Contraception

  • Migraine with aura increases stroke risk with estrogen-containing contraceptives
  • Progestin-only methods often preferred
  • Discuss with healthcare provider

Pregnancy

  • Migraines often improve during pregnancy
  • Many medications not safe during pregnancy
  • Plan ahead with your doctor if trying to conceive

Menopause

  • Migraines may worsen during perimenopause
  • Often improve after menopause
  • HRT can affect migraines (may help or worsen)

Working with Healthcare Providers

When to See a Specialist

  • Migraines not adequately controlled
  • Need for preventive treatment
  • Atypical symptoms
  • Consider headache specialist or neurologist

Preparing for Appointments

  • Bring headache diary
  • List of all medications tried (what worked, what didn't)
  • Current medications and supplements
  • Questions written down

Questions to Ask

  • Am I a candidate for preventive treatment?
  • What acute treatments would you recommend?
  • Are there newer treatments I should consider?
  • What lifestyle changes might help?
