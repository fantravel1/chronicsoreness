Endometriosis

Understanding and managing this common cause of chronic pelvic pain

Key Takeaways

  • Endometriosis affects approximately 1 in 10 women of reproductive age
  • Average time to diagnosis is 7-10 years—early recognition is crucial
  • Multiple treatment options exist, from hormonal therapy to surgery
  • A multidisciplinary approach often provides the best outcomes

Understanding Endometriosis

Endometriosis is a chronic condition in which tissue similar to the lining of the uterus (endometrium) grows outside the uterus, most commonly on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, and tissue lining the pelvis.1 This tissue responds to hormonal changes during the menstrual cycle, leading to inflammation, scarring, and often significant pain.

Where Endometriosis Can Be Found

Common locations include:

  • Ovaries (endometriomas or "chocolate cysts")
  • Fallopian tubes
  • Pelvic peritoneum (lining of the pelvic cavity)
  • Uterosacral ligaments
  • Bowel and bladder surfaces
  • Cul-de-sac (space behind the uterus)

Less commonly, endometriosis can affect the diaphragm, lungs, or other distant sites.

Why Pain Occurs

Endometriosis causes pain through multiple mechanisms:3

  • Inflammation from monthly bleeding of endometrial implants
  • Adhesions (scar tissue) pulling on organs and nerves
  • Direct nerve invasion by endometriosis implants
  • Central sensitization (nervous system becomes hypersensitive)
  • Pelvic floor muscle dysfunction

Symptoms

Endometriosis symptoms vary widely—some women have severe disease with minimal symptoms, while others have mild disease with significant pain.4

Pain Symptoms

  • Dysmenorrhea: Painful periods, often more severe than typical menstrual cramps
  • Chronic pelvic pain: Pain occurring throughout the cycle, not just during menstruation
  • Dyspareunia: Pain during or after sexual intercourse
  • Painful bowel movements: Especially during menstruation
  • Painful urination: During menstruation
  • Lower back pain: May worsen with periods

Other Common Symptoms

  • Heavy menstrual bleeding: Or bleeding between periods
  • Infertility: Affects 30-50% of women with endometriosis
  • Bloating: Often called "endo belly"
  • Fatigue: Chronic exhaustion
  • Bowel symptoms: Constipation, diarrhea, or nausea, especially during periods

The Severity Paradox

The stage or severity of endometriosis (I-IV) does not always correlate with pain level. A woman with Stage I (minimal) disease may have debilitating pain, while someone with Stage IV (severe) disease may have few symptoms. Pain experience depends on location of implants, nerve involvement, and individual pain processing.

When to Seek Medical Attention

  • Pelvic pain that interferes with daily activities
  • Period pain that requires prescription medication or is not relieved by over-the-counter options
  • Pain during intercourse
  • Difficulty getting pregnant
  • Bowel or bladder symptoms with your cycle

Do not accept dismissal of your symptoms—severe menstrual pain is not normal.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing endometriosis can be challenging, contributing to the average 7-10 year delay from symptom onset to diagnosis.5

Diagnostic Process

Medical history:

  • Detailed symptom assessment including pain patterns
  • Menstrual history
  • Family history (endometriosis can run in families)
  • Impact on quality of life

Physical examination:

  • Pelvic exam to check for tenderness, nodules, or masses
  • May find tenderness but exam can also be normal

Imaging:

  • Transvaginal ultrasound: Can detect endometriomas (ovarian cysts)
  • MRI: May show deep infiltrating endometriosis
  • Imaging may be normal even with endometriosis present

Definitive Diagnosis

Laparoscopy (surgical visualization) remains the gold standard for definitive diagnosis, allowing direct visualization and biopsy of suspected lesions.6 However, recent guidelines suggest that clinical diagnosis based on symptoms may be sufficient to begin treatment, avoiding unnecessary surgical delays.

Treatment Options

Treatment goals focus on managing pain, slowing disease progression, and preserving fertility when desired. A multimodal approach is often most effective.7

Pain Medications

Over-the-counter options:

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) for inflammation and pain
  • Work best when started before pain becomes severe

Prescription options:

  • Stronger NSAIDs
  • Nerve pain medications (gabapentin, pregabalin) for neuropathic pain component
  • Other pain modulators

Hormonal Treatments

Hormonal therapy suppresses estrogen, which fuels endometriosis growth:8

Combined hormonal contraceptives:

  • Pills, patches, or rings
  • Can be used continuously to skip periods
  • First-line treatment for many women

Progestin-only options:

  • Progestin pills (norethindrone, dienogest)
  • Hormonal IUD (Mirena)
  • Depo-Provera injection
  • Nexplanon implant

GnRH agonists and antagonists:

  • Suppress estrogen production more completely
  • Lupron, Orilissa (elagolix), Myfembree
  • May require "add-back" therapy to prevent bone loss

Aromatase inhibitors:

  • Block estrogen production
  • Used for refractory cases

Surgical Treatment

Surgery may be recommended for:9

  • Symptoms not controlled by medication
  • Large endometriomas (cysts)
  • Infertility related to endometriosis
  • Bowel or bladder involvement
  • Diagnostic purposes

Laparoscopic excision:

  • Removes (excises) endometriosis implants
  • Considered more effective than ablation (burning) for deep disease
  • Best outcomes with experienced endometriosis specialists

Hysterectomy:

  • Removal of uterus, sometimes with ovaries
  • Not a cure—endometriosis can persist or recur
  • May be appropriate for completed childbearing and severe disease

Complementary Approaches

Many women find benefit from integrating complementary approaches with medical treatment:10

Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

Essential for many women with endometriosis:

  • Addresses muscle tension and dysfunction
  • Treats painful intercourse
  • Improves bladder and bowel function
  • Teaches relaxation techniques

Heat Therapy

  • Heating pads for pelvic pain
  • Warm baths
  • Can be used alongside other treatments

Dietary Approaches

Some evidence supports:11

  • Anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean-style)
  • Reducing red meat consumption
  • Increasing omega-3 fatty acids
  • Limiting alcohol and caffeine
  • Some women find relief eliminating gluten or dairy

Mind-Body Practices

  • Mindfulness meditation
  • Yoga (gentle, restorative practices)
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy for pain management
  • Stress reduction techniques

Acupuncture

Some studies suggest acupuncture may help with endometriosis-related pain, though more research is needed.12

Living with Endometriosis

Managing Flares

  • Keep pain medications readily available
  • Use heat therapy
  • Rest when needed—pacing is important
  • Have a plan for work or school accommodations

Tracking Symptoms

Keeping a symptom diary helps:

  • Identify patterns and triggers
  • Communicate effectively with healthcare providers
  • Monitor treatment effectiveness
  • Plan activities around your cycle

Communication and Relationships

  • Educate partners and family about the condition
  • Communicate about sexual pain and find comfortable alternatives
  • Set boundaries around activities during flares
  • Consider couples counseling if needed

Mental Health Support

Living with chronic pain impacts mental health:

  • Depression and anxiety are common with endometriosis
  • Counseling can help with coping strategies
  • Support groups connect you with others who understand
  • Do not hesitate to seek professional mental health support

Finding Expert Care

Not all gynecologists specialize in endometriosis. Consider seeking:

  • Gynecologists specializing in endometriosis or pelvic pain
  • Reproductive endocrinologists (if fertility is a concern)
  • Surgeons experienced in excision surgery
  • Multidisciplinary endometriosis centers

Questions to Ask

  • How many endometriosis cases do you treat annually?
  • Do you perform excision or ablation surgery?
  • What is your approach to fertility preservation?
  • Do you work with a multidisciplinary team?

Research and Hope

Endometriosis research is advancing:13

  • Non-invasive diagnostic tests are being developed
  • New medications with fewer side effects
  • Better understanding of pain mechanisms
  • Improved surgical techniques
  • Recognition of the condition leading to faster diagnosis
