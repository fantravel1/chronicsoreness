What Is Endometriosis?

Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the uterine lining grows outside the uterus—on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, bowel, bladder, and other pelvic structures. It affects 10% of reproductive-age women worldwide—approximately 176 million women globally.

While endometriosis is known primarily for pelvic pain, the condition causes body-wide symptoms through mechanisms that extend far beyond the pelvis.

Beyond Pelvic Pain: Whole-Body Effects

Endometriosis causes systemic symptoms through three key mechanisms:

Central Sensitization

The central nervous system becomes hypersensitive, amplifying pain signals from throughout the body—not just the pelvis.

Neurogenic Inflammation

Inflammatory signals spread through nerve pathways, causing pain and sensitivity in areas far from endometriosis lesions.

Viscero-Visceral Cross-Sensitization

Organs that share nerve pathways become sensitized to each other, explaining why bladder, bowel, and pelvic symptoms often occur together.

Overlapping Pain Conditions

Women with endometriosis experience significantly higher rates of other chronic pain conditions:

  • 6% have concurrent fibromyalgia—double the general population rate
  • Over 100 times higher likelihood of chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Higher rates of:
    • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
    • Interstitial Cystitis/Painful Bladder Syndrome
    • Temporomandibular Joint Disorders (TMJ)
    • Chronic Low Back Pain
    • Migraines
  • 25% report 3+ overlapping pain conditions compared to 12% of women without endometriosis

The Diagnostic Delay Problem

7-10 Years to Diagnosis

The average diagnostic delay for endometriosis remains 7-10 years from symptom onset. This happens because:

  • Symptoms are often dismissed as "normal period pain"
  • The only definitive diagnosis requires surgery (laparoscopy)
  • Pain severity doesn't correlate with lesion extent
  • Imaging often misses endometriosis

The Pain-Lesion Paradox

One of the most confusing aspects of endometriosis: the discordance between lesion severity and pain intensity. Some women with minimal visible disease have severe, debilitating pain, while others with extensive disease have mild symptoms.

This paradox is explained by central sensitization—once the nervous system becomes sensitized, pain can persist and spread regardless of what's happening at the original lesion sites.

Symptoms to Track

Beyond classic pelvic pain, watch for these endometriosis-related symptoms:

Pelvic Symptoms

  • Severe menstrual cramps
  • Pain during/after sex
  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Ovulation pain

Bowel/Bladder

  • Painful bowel movements
  • Diarrhea or constipation with periods
  • Bladder pain
  • Urinary urgency

Body-Wide

  • Fatigue
  • Low back pain
  • Leg pain
  • Widespread muscle aches

Other

  • Infertility
  • Heavy periods
  • Bloating ("endo belly")
  • Nausea

Treatment Approaches

Medical Management

  • Hormonal therapies: Birth control pills, progestins, GnRH agonists/antagonists
  • Pain management: NSAIDs, neuromodulators for central sensitization
  • Treating overlapping conditions: If fibromyalgia, IBS, or IC are present, treat those as well

Surgical Options

  • Diagnostic laparoscopy: The only definitive way to diagnose
  • Excision surgery: Removes endometriosis lesions (preferred over ablation)
  • Hysterectomy: May be considered in severe cases, but doesn't guarantee pain relief if central sensitization is present

Addressing Central Sensitization

For women with body-wide symptoms, treating the underlying endometriosis alone may not resolve pain. Additional approaches include:

  • Pain neuroscience education
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Medications targeting central sensitization (similar to fibromyalgia treatment)
  • Pelvic floor physical therapy
  • Mind-body approaches

Advocating for Diagnosis

If you suspect endometriosis:

  1. Track your symptoms carefully, noting relationship to menstrual cycle
  2. Document functional impact—missed work, activities you can't do
  3. Seek a specialist—gynecologists with endometriosis expertise or endometriosis centers
  4. Know that normal imaging doesn't rule out endometriosis—ultrasound and MRI often miss it
  5. Request documentation if a provider dismisses your concerns
