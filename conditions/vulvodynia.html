Vulvodynia

Understanding chronic vulvar pain and evidence-based management approaches

Key Takeaways

  • Vulvodynia affects approximately 8-16% of women at some point in their lives
  • It is a real medical condition, not psychological
  • Multiple effective treatments exist, often used in combination
  • Pelvic floor physical therapy is a cornerstone of treatment

Understanding Vulvodynia

Vulvodynia is chronic pain or discomfort of the vulva (the external female genitalia) lasting at least three months without an identifiable cause.1 This condition significantly impacts quality of life, affecting sexual function, daily activities, and emotional well-being.

Types of Vulvodynia

Vulvodynia is classified by location and triggers:1,3

By location:

  • Localized: Pain in a specific area (most commonly the vestibule—called vestibulodynia)
  • Generalized: Pain throughout the vulvar region
  • Mixed: Combination of localized and generalized

By trigger:

  • Provoked: Pain triggered by touch or pressure (intercourse, tampon use, sitting)
  • Unprovoked: Pain occurs spontaneously without touch
  • Mixed: Both provoked and unprovoked pain

Vestibulodynia

The most common form of vulvodynia, vestibulodynia causes pain localized to the vestibule (the area around the vaginal opening). It is typically provoked by touch and is a major cause of painful intercourse (dyspareunia).

Symptoms

Common symptoms include:1,4

Pain Characteristics

  • Burning (most commonly reported)
  • Stinging or rawness
  • Aching or throbbing
  • Soreness or irritation
  • Pain with intercourse (dyspareunia)

Impact on Daily Life

  • Difficulty sitting for long periods
  • Pain with tight clothing
  • Discomfort with exercise (cycling, horseback riding)
  • Pain with tampon use
  • Difficulty with gynecological exams

Vulvodynia Is Real

Vulvodynia is a genuine medical condition, not a psychological problem. While the vulva may appear normal on examination, the pain is very real and is now understood to involve sensitization of the nerve pathways. Many women have been incorrectly told the problem is "in their head"—this is not true.

Causes and Contributing Factors

The exact cause of vulvodynia is unknown, but research suggests multiple factors may contribute:5,6

Neurological Factors

  • Increased nerve fiber density in the vestibule
  • Hypersensitization of nerve pathways
  • Central sensitization (nervous system becomes overly sensitive)

Musculoskeletal Factors

  • Pelvic floor muscle dysfunction (tightness, spasm)
  • Chronic muscle tension

Inflammatory/Hormonal Factors

  • History of recurrent yeast or other infections
  • Hormonal changes (low estrogen)
  • Hormonal contraceptive use (may be associated in some cases)

Other Factors

  • Genetic predisposition
  • History of sexual trauma or abuse
  • Other chronic pain conditions (fibromyalgia, IBS, TMJ)

Diagnosis

Diagnosis involves ruling out other causes of vulvar pain:7

Medical History

  • Detailed description of pain characteristics
  • Timeline and triggers
  • Impact on function
  • Previous treatments tried

Physical Examination

  • Careful inspection of the vulva
  • Cotton swab test: Gently touching the vestibule to map pain
  • Pelvic floor muscle assessment

Tests to Rule Out Other Conditions

  • Testing for infections (yeast, bacterial, herpes)
  • Skin biopsy if needed to rule out dermatological conditions
  • Evaluation for hormonal changes

Conditions to Exclude

  • Infections (yeast, bacterial vaginosis, STIs)
  • Skin conditions (lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, contact dermatitis)
  • Hormonal changes (vulvovaginal atrophy)
  • Neurological conditions

Treatment Options

Treatment is multimodal, often combining several approaches:8,9

Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

Often considered first-line treatment:

  • Internal and external muscle release techniques
  • Exercises to relax tight pelvic floor muscles
  • Biofeedback to learn muscle control
  • Dilator therapy to desensitize and increase comfort
  • 70-80% of women report improvement with pelvic floor PT

Topical Treatments

  • Lidocaine: Numbing gel or ointment for temporary relief
  • Compounded creams: May include amitriptyline, gabapentin, baclofen
  • Estrogen cream: If hormonal factors contribute

Oral Medications

  • Tricyclic antidepressants: Amitriptyline, nortriptyline (low doses for pain)
  • SNRIs: Duloxetine, venlafaxine
  • Anticonvulsants: Gabapentin, pregabalin

Nerve Blocks and Injections

  • Local anesthetic injections
  • Botox injections for pelvic floor muscle spasm
  • Pudendal nerve blocks

Surgical Options

For localized vestibulodynia unresponsive to conservative treatment:

  • Vestibulectomy: Surgical removal of the painful tissue
  • Success rates of 60-90% in appropriately selected patients
  • Usually reserved for cases that do not respond to other treatments

Psychological Support

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Sex therapy
  • Mindfulness-based approaches
  • Treatment of anxiety or depression if present

Self-Care Strategies

Vulvar Care

  • Wear 100% cotton underwear
  • Avoid tight clothing
  • Use unscented, gentle products
  • Avoid douching
  • Use water or approved lubricant for intercourse
  • Rinse and pat dry after urination

Pain Management

  • Cold compresses or cool gel packs
  • Sitz baths
  • Topical lidocaine before activities
  • Cushioned seating when needed

Sexual Health

  • Communicate openly with your partner
  • Use plenty of lubricant
  • Try different positions
  • Focus on non-penetrative intimacy when needed
  • Consider working with a sex therapist

Finding the Right Provider

Specialists who treat vulvodynia include:

  • Gynecologists with vulvar expertise
  • Vulvovaginal specialists
  • Urogynecologists
  • Pelvic floor physical therapists
  • Pain medicine specialists

Questions to Ask

  • How many vulvodynia patients do you treat?
  • What treatment approaches do you use?
  • Do you work with pelvic floor physical therapists?
  • What is your approach to multimodal treatment?

Outlook

With appropriate treatment, most women experience significant improvement:10

  • Treatment often takes time and patience
  • Combination approaches tend to work best
  • Many women achieve substantial relief or resolution
  • Ongoing management may be needed for some
