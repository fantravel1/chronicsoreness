Vulvodynia

Understanding chronic vulvar pain

Key Takeaways

  • Vulvodynia is chronic vulvar pain without an identifiable cause
  • It's more common than many realize—affecting up to 8% of women
  • It's a real medical condition, not "in your head"
  • Treatment is available and can significantly improve quality of life

What Is Vulvodynia?

Vulvodynia is chronic pain or discomfort of the vulva (the external female genitalia) lasting at least 3 months without an identifiable cause such as infection, skin condition, or other medical explanation. It's a diagnosis of exclusion, made after other causes are ruled out.1

8%
of women experience vulvodynia at some point in their lifetime2

Types

  • Localized: Pain in a specific area (most commonly vestibulodynia—at the vaginal opening)
  • Generalized: Pain throughout the vulvar area
  • Provoked: Pain triggered by touch or pressure
  • Unprovoked: Constant pain without trigger
  • Mixed: Combination of types

Symptoms

Symptoms vary but commonly include:3

Pain Characteristics

  • Burning (most common description)
  • Stinging
  • Rawness
  • Soreness
  • Throbbing
  • Itching

Pain Triggers

  • Sexual intercourse (most common)
  • Tampon insertion
  • Gynecological exams
  • Tight clothing
  • Sitting for prolonged periods
  • Exercise (cycling, horseback riding)

Impact

  • Painful or impossible intercourse
  • Difficulty with daily activities
  • Relationship strain
  • Anxiety and depression common
  • Reduced quality of life

Causes

The exact cause is unknown, but contributing factors may include:4

Proposed Mechanisms

  • Nerve changes: Increased nerve fibers or hypersensitivity
  • Inflammation: Past or chronic inflammation
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction: Tight, spasming muscles
  • Hormonal factors: May play a role in some cases
  • Genetic factors: May affect pain processing
  • Central sensitization: Nervous system changes

Associated Factors

  • History of recurrent yeast infections
  • Other chronic pain conditions
  • Anxiety or depression
  • Early oral contraceptive use (research ongoing)

It's Not Your Fault

Vulvodynia is not caused by sexually transmitted infections, poor hygiene, or anything you did wrong. It's a complex pain condition with biological causes, even though we don't fully understand them yet.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis involves ruling out other causes:5

Evaluation

  • Detailed medical and pain history
  • Gynecological examination
  • Cotton swab test (Q-tip test) to map painful areas
  • Tests to rule out infections, skin conditions

What's Ruled Out

  • Infections (yeast, bacterial, STIs)
  • Skin conditions (lichen sclerosus, dermatitis)
  • Neurological conditions
  • Trauma or injury

Finding the Right Provider

Many healthcare providers aren't familiar with vulvodynia. If your symptoms are dismissed, seek a provider with expertise in vulvar pain—often gynecologists or urogynecologists with special interest in this area.

Treatment

Treatment is individualized and often multimodal:6

Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

  • Often first-line treatment
  • Addresses muscle tightness and spasm
  • Internal and external techniques
  • Dilator training
  • High success rates reported

Medications

  • Topical treatments: Lidocaine, estrogen cream, compounded creams
  • Oral medications: Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline), anticonvulsants (gabapentin)
  • Nerve blocks: For some cases

Self-Care Measures

  • Wear loose, cotton underwear
  • Avoid irritants (scented products, harsh soaps)
  • Use water-based lubricants
  • Cool compresses for flares
  • Rinse vulva with water only

Psychological Support

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Sex therapy for couples
  • Support groups
  • Treatment of co-existing anxiety/depression

Other Options

  • Biofeedback
  • Acupuncture
  • Surgery (vestibulectomy)—for specific cases, when other treatments fail

Living with Vulvodynia

Daily Management

  • Identify and avoid triggers
  • Use cushions or donut pillows for sitting
  • Communicate with partner about intimacy
  • Pace activities

Intimacy

  • Pain doesn't mean intimacy is impossible
  • Explore alternatives to penetration
  • Use plenty of lubricant
  • Topical lidocaine before activity (as directed)
  • Sex therapy can help couples

Support

  • National Vulvodynia Association (nva.org)
  • Online support communities
  • Consider therapy for emotional impact
