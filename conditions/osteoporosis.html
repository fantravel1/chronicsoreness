Osteoporosis

Understanding bone loss, preventing fractures, and managing related pain

What Is Osteoporosis?

Osteoporosis is a bone disease characterized by low bone mass and deterioration of bone tissue, leading to increased bone fragility and risk of fractures. The word means "porous bone" â€“ under a microscope, healthy bone looks like a honeycomb, but osteoporotic bone has larger holes and spaces.1

Often called a "silent disease," osteoporosis typically causes no symptoms until a fracture occurs. Approximately 10 million Americans have osteoporosis, and another 44 million have low bone density (osteopenia), placing them at increased risk.2

Bone Remodeling

Understanding osteoporosis requires knowing how bone works:

  • Bone is living tissue constantly being broken down and rebuilt
  • Osteoclasts break down old bone; osteoblasts build new bone
  • Peak bone mass is reached around age 30
  • After age 30, bone breakdown begins to exceed bone formation
  • Women experience rapid bone loss in the years following menopause

Risk Factors

Many factors contribute to osteoporosis risk:

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Age - Risk increases with age, especially after 50
  • Sex - Women are at higher risk, especially postmenopausal women
  • Family history - Having a parent with osteoporosis or hip fracture
  • Body frame size - Smaller, thinner people have less bone mass
  • Ethnicity - Caucasian and Asian women at highest risk

Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Low calcium intake - Inadequate calcium throughout life3
  • Vitamin D deficiency - Needed for calcium absorption
  • Sedentary lifestyle - Weight-bearing exercise builds bone
  • Smoking - Accelerates bone loss
  • Excessive alcohol - More than 2 drinks daily increases risk
  • Low body weight - Less weight-bearing stress on bones

Medical Conditions and Medications

  • Long-term corticosteroid use (prednisone)
  • Hyperthyroidism or excess thyroid medication
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Celiac disease and other malabsorption conditions
  • Cancer treatments
  • Eating disorders

Symptoms and Complications

Osteoporosis itself doesn't cause pain â€“ the complications do:

Fractures

The most serious consequence of osteoporosis:4

  • Vertebral compression fractures - Most common; can occur with minimal trauma
  • Hip fractures - Most serious; associated with significant mortality
  • Wrist fractures - Often the first sign of osteoporosis
  • Other fractures - Ribs, pelvis, upper arm

Spinal Changes

  • Height loss over time
  • Stooped posture (kyphosis or "dowager's hump")
  • Chronic back pain from vertebral fractures
  • Reduced lung capacity from spinal curvature

When to Seek Immediate Care

Seek medical attention for sudden, severe back pain (may indicate vertebral fracture), pain after a fall, or inability to bear weight. Hip fractures require emergency treatment.

Diagnosis

Bone Density Testing (DXA Scan)

The gold standard for diagnosing osteoporosis:

  • Measures bone mineral density (BMD) at hip and spine
  • Low radiation exposure, painless, takes about 15 minutes
  • Results reported as T-scores comparing your BMD to healthy young adults

T-Score Interpretation

  • -1.0 or above: Normal bone density
  • -1.0 to -2.5: Osteopenia (low bone mass)
  • -2.5 or below: Osteoporosis
  • -2.5 or below with fracture: Severe osteoporosis

Who Should Be Tested

  • Women 65 and older; men 70 and older
  • Postmenopausal women under 65 with risk factors
  • Anyone who has had a fracture after age 50
  • Anyone on long-term corticosteroid therapy

Prevention

Building and maintaining strong bones:

Nutrition

  • Calcium - 1,000-1,200 mg daily from food and supplements5
  • Vitamin D - 600-800 IU daily; many need more
  • Protein - Adequate intake supports bone health
  • Limit sodium - High sodium increases calcium loss

Exercise

  • Weight-bearing exercise - Walking, jogging, dancing, stair climbing
  • Resistance training - Builds muscle that supports bone
  • Balance exercises - Reduces fall risk
  • Posture exercises - Strengthens back muscles

Lifestyle

  • Don't smoke
  • Limit alcohol
  • Prevent falls through home safety modifications
  • Get adequate sleep

Treatment

Medications

Several classes of medications treat osteoporosis:6

Bisphosphonates

  • Most commonly prescribed: alendronate (Fosamax), risedronate (Actonel), ibandronate (Boniva), zoledronic acid (Reclast)
  • Slow bone breakdown
  • Oral or IV formulations

Other Medications

  • Denosumab (Prolia) - Injection every 6 months
  • Romosozumab (Evenity) - For very high-risk patients
  • Teriparatide (Forteo) - Stimulates new bone formation
  • Hormone therapy - May be option for some menopausal women
  • Raloxifene (Evista) - Selective estrogen receptor modulator

Pain Management

For pain from vertebral fractures:

  • Pain medications (acetaminophen, NSAIDs, sometimes opioids short-term)
  • Heat therapy
  • Physical therapy
  • Bracing
  • Vertebroplasty or kyphoplasty (cement injection into fractured vertebra)

Living with Osteoporosis

Fall Prevention

  • Remove tripping hazards at home
  • Install grab bars in bathrooms
  • Ensure adequate lighting
  • Wear supportive, non-slip footwear
  • Use assistive devices if needed
  • Have vision checked regularly

Protecting Your Spine

  • Use proper body mechanics when lifting
  • Avoid forward bending with twisting
  • Maintain good posture
  • Use a pillow to support spine when sitting

Monitoring Progress

Bone density testing is typically repeated every 1-2 years while on treatment to monitor response. Your healthcare provider may also check bone turnover markers through blood tests. Consistent medication adherence is crucial for treatment success.
