TMJ Disorders

Understanding temporomandibular joint dysfunction

Key Takeaways

  • TMJ disorders affect the jaw joint and surrounding muscles
  • Women are affected more often than men
  • Most cases improve with conservative, reversible treatments
  • Surgery is rarely needed

What Are TMJ Disorders?

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) are a group of conditions affecting the temporomandibular joint (TMJ), which connects the jawbone to the skull, and the muscles that control jaw movement. These disorders can cause pain, difficulty chewing, and clicking or locking of the jaw.1

5-12%
of the population is affected by TMJ disorders2

Types of TMD

  • Muscle disorders: Pain in the jaw muscles (myofascial pain)
  • Joint disorders: Disc displacement, arthritis, joint inflammation
  • Degenerative disorders: Arthritis of the TMJ
  • Many people have more than one type

Symptoms

Symptoms can vary widely:3

Pain

  • Pain in jaw, face, or around ear
  • Pain while chewing
  • Facial pain that may feel like toothache
  • Pain in temple area
  • Pain may radiate to neck and shoulders

Jaw Function

  • Clicking, popping, or grating sounds
  • Limited mouth opening
  • Jaw locking (open or closed)
  • Difficulty chewing
  • Change in bite (teeth don't fit together normally)

Associated Symptoms

  • Headaches
  • Ear pain or fullness
  • Tinnitus (ringing in ears)
  • Neck and shoulder tension
  • Dizziness

Clicking Without Pain

Jaw clicking or popping alone, without pain or limitation, is common and usually doesn't require treatment. Many people have painless clicking that never becomes problematic.

Causes and Risk Factors

Contributing Factors

  • Bruxism: Teeth grinding and clenching
  • Stress: Leads to muscle tension and clenching
  • Trauma: Injury to jaw or head
  • Arthritis: Osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis
  • Disc problems: Displacement of joint disc
  • Habits: Gum chewing, nail biting, jaw clenching

Risk Factors

  • Sex: More common in women (especially ages 20-40)
  • Stress and anxiety: Strong association
  • Other pain conditions: Fibromyalgia, chronic headaches
  • Poor posture: Head-forward posture
  • Hormonal factors: May play a role in women

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of symptoms
  • Physical examination of jaw, face, neck
  • Assessment of jaw movement and sounds
  • Palpation of muscles and joints
  • Dental examination

Imaging

  • Often not needed for initial evaluation
  • X-rays may show bone changes
  • MRI shows disc and soft tissue (if needed)
  • CT scan for bone detail

Treatment

Conservative approaches are first-line:4,5

Self-Care (Essential)

  • Soft diet: Avoid hard, chewy foods during flares
  • Heat or cold: Apply to jaw area
  • Jaw rest: Avoid wide opening, excessive chewing
  • Stress management: Relaxation techniques
  • Posture: Avoid head-forward position
  • Awareness: Notice and release jaw clenching

Physical Therapy

  • Jaw exercises and stretches
  • Manual therapy
  • Posture training
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Dry needling for muscle pain

Oral Appliances

  • Night guard or splint
  • Protects teeth from grinding
  • May relax muscles
  • Should be properly fitted

Medications

  • NSAIDs: For pain and inflammation
  • Muscle relaxants: Short-term for muscle spasm
  • Low-dose tricyclics: For chronic pain
  • Botox injections: For some muscle-related cases

Avoid Irreversible Treatments

Be cautious of treatments that permanently change your bite, teeth, or jaw. Start with conservative, reversible treatments. Most TMJ problems improve without surgery or permanent dental changes.

Self-Care Exercises

Relaxed Jaw Position

  • Lips together, teeth apart
  • Tongue resting on roof of mouth
  • Jaw muscles relaxed
  • Practice awareness throughout day

Gentle Stretches

  • Open mouth gently, don't force
  • Side-to-side jaw movement
  • Hold each position briefly
  • Never stretch to point of pain

Resistance Exercises

  • Place thumb under chin
  • Open mouth against gentle resistance
  • Place fingers on front of chin
  • Push jaw forward against resistance

Living with TMD

Daily Management

  • Check for jaw tension throughout day
  • Avoid resting chin on hand
  • Don't chew gum excessively
  • Cut food into small pieces
  • Support jaw when yawning

Stress Connection

  • Stress often worsens TMJ symptoms
  • Practice stress management daily
  • Consider counseling if stress is significant
  • Regular exercise helps

Prognosis

  • Many cases improve with time and self-care
  • May fluctuate with stress levels
  • Long-term management often needed
  • Most people manage well with conservative approaches
