Gout

Understanding this painful arthritis caused by uric acid crystals and how to manage it effectively

What Is Gout?

Gout is a form of inflammatory arthritis caused by the deposition of uric acid crystals in joints and surrounding tissues. It causes sudden, severe attacks of pain, swelling, redness, and tendernessâ€”most commonly in the joint at the base of the big toe.1

Gout affects approximately 4% of American adults and is the most common form of inflammatory arthritis in men. While historically called the "disease of kings" due to its association with rich foods and alcohol, gout can affect anyone.2

How Gout Develops

  • The body produces uric acid when breaking down purines (found in certain foods and naturally in the body)
  • Normally, uric acid dissolves in blood, passes through kidneys, and leaves in urine
  • When uric acid levels are too high (hyperuricemia), it can form sharp, needle-like crystals
  • These crystals accumulate in joints and cause inflammation

Symptoms

Acute Gout Attack

A gout attack typically comes on suddenly, often at night:

  • Intense joint pain - Often described as worst within first 12-24 hours
  • Swelling and redness - Joint appears inflamed and warm
  • Extreme tenderness - Even light touch can be unbearable
  • Limited range of motion
  • Duration - Untreated attacks last 7-14 days; treated attacks resolve faster

Commonly Affected Joints

  • Big toe (podagra) - Most common, affecting 50% of cases
  • Ankles
  • Knees
  • Wrists
  • Fingers
  • Elbows

Chronic Gout

Without treatment, gout can progress:

  • More frequent attacks
  • Multiple joints affected
  • Tophiâ€”chalky deposits of uric acid crystals under the skin
  • Joint damage and chronic pain
  • Kidney stones

When to Seek Care

See a doctor promptly for your first gout attack for proper diagnosis. Seek immediate care if you have fever with joint pain (could indicate infection) or if symptoms don't improve with treatment.

Risk Factors

  • Diet - High intake of red meat, organ meats, shellfish, sugary beverages
  • Alcohol - Beer and spirits more than wine
  • Obesity - Body produces more uric acid and kidneys struggle to eliminate it3
  • Medical conditions - Hypertension, diabetes, metabolic syndrome, kidney disease
  • Medications - Diuretics, low-dose aspirin, some immunosuppressants
  • Family history - Genetics affect uric acid handling
  • Age and sex - More common in men; women's risk increases after menopause
  • Recent surgery or trauma - Can trigger attacks

Diagnosis

  • Joint fluid analysis - Gold standard; examining fluid under microscope for uric acid crystals
  • Blood tests - Uric acid levels (though can be normal during attack)
  • X-rays - May show damage in chronic gout
  • Ultrasound or dual-energy CT - Can detect uric acid crystals

Treatment

Treating Acute Attacks

Goals are to reduce pain and inflammation quickly:4

  • NSAIDs - Indomethacin, naproxen, celecoxib at high doses
  • Colchicine - Most effective when started within 24 hours
  • Corticosteroids - Oral prednisone or injection for those who can't take NSAIDs
  • Ice - Apply for 20-30 minutes several times daily
  • Rest - Keep affected joint elevated

Preventing Future Attacks

Long-term urate-lowering therapy for recurrent gout:

  • Allopurinol - Reduces uric acid production; most common choice5
  • Febuxostat (Uloric) - Alternative for allopurinol intolerance
  • Probenecid - Increases uric acid excretion by kidneys
  • Pegloticase - For severe, treatment-resistant cases

Important: Starting Urate-Lowering Therapy

Starting allopurinol or similar medications can temporarily trigger gout attacks as stored crystals dissolve. Your doctor may prescribe low-dose colchicine or NSAIDs during the first 3-6 months of treatment to prevent this. Never stop urate-lowering therapy during an attack.

Dietary Management

Dietary changes can help reduce uric acid and prevent attacks:

Foods to Limit or Avoid

  • High-purine foods - Red meat, organ meats (liver, kidney), game meats
  • Seafood - Anchovies, sardines, mussels, scallops, trout
  • Alcohol - Especially beer and spirits
  • Sugar-sweetened beverages - Fructose increases uric acid
  • High-fructose foods

Foods That May Help

  • Cherries - Studies show they may reduce gout attacks6
  • Low-fat dairy - Associated with lower uric acid levels
  • Coffee - May lower uric acid
  • Vitamin C - May help lower uric acid (500-1000mg daily)
  • Plenty of water - Helps flush uric acid

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Maintain healthy weight - But avoid crash dieting, which can trigger attacks
  • Stay hydrated - 8 or more glasses of water daily
  • Exercise regularly - Helps with weight and overall health
  • Limit alcohol - Or avoid during attacks

Living with Gout

  • Take medications as prescribed - Even when feeling well
  • Know your triggers - Common ones include alcohol, certain foods, dehydration, stress
  • Keep emergency medication on hand - For prompt attack treatment
  • Monitor uric acid levels - Target is usually below 6 mg/dL
  • Protect affected joints - Wear comfortable shoes
  • Manage related conditions - Hypertension, diabetes, kidney disease
