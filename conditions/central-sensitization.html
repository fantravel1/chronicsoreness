Central Sensitization

Understanding how the nervous system contributes to chronic pain

Key Takeaways

  • Central sensitization is a real neurological change, not a psychological problem
  • It explains why chronic pain can persist without ongoing tissue damage
  • Understanding this mechanism is the first step toward effective treatment
  • The nervous system can be retrained with appropriate approaches

What Is Central Sensitization?

Central sensitization is a condition in which the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) becomes hypersensitive to pain signals.1 This amplification of pain processing means that stimuli that would not normally be painful become painful (allodynia), and painful stimuli produce exaggerated pain responses (hyperalgesia).

The Pain Alarm
becomes too sensitive and responds to non-threatening signals2

How Pain Normally Works

In acute pain, the process is straightforward:

  1. Tissue damage occurs (injury, inflammation)
  2. Nerves send danger signals to the spinal cord
  3. Signals are processed and sent to the brain
  4. Brain interprets signals and produces the experience of pain
  5. Tissue heals, signals stop, pain resolves

What Changes in Central Sensitization

With central sensitization:3

  • Neurons in the spinal cord become hyperexcitable
  • Pain signals are amplified before reaching the brain
  • Brain regions involved in pain become more active
  • Descending pain-inhibiting pathways become less effective
  • Pain persists even after tissues have healed

A Helpful Analogy

Think of your pain system like a smoke alarm. Normally, it only goes off when there is actual smoke (tissue damage). With central sensitization, the alarm becomes so sensitive that it responds to steam from the shower, burnt toast, or even humidity. The alarm is working, but the sensitivity setting has been turned up too high.

Conditions Involving Central Sensitization

Central sensitization plays a role in many chronic pain conditions:4

Primary Central Sensitization Syndromes

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Irritable bowel syndrome
  • Chronic tension-type headache
  • Temporomandibular disorders
  • Interstitial cystitis
  • Vulvodynia

Conditions Where It Can Develop

  • Chronic low back pain
  • Chronic neck pain
  • Osteoarthritis (some cases)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Post-surgical pain
  • Whiplash-associated disorders
  • Complex regional pain syndrome

Signs and Features

Features that suggest central sensitization:5

Pain Characteristics

  • Widespread pain: Affecting multiple body areas
  • Allodynia: Pain from normally non-painful touch (light pressure, clothing)
  • Hyperalgesia: Exaggerated response to painful stimuli
  • Pain that persists: Beyond expected tissue healing time
  • Disproportionate pain: Intensity does not match visible pathology

Sensory Changes

  • Sensitivity to light, sound, or smells
  • Temperature sensitivity
  • Skin sensitivity

Associated Features

  • Fatigue
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Cognitive difficulties (brain fog)
  • Mood changes (anxiety, depression)
  • Multiple overlapping pain conditions

Contributing Factors

Multiple factors can contribute to developing central sensitization:6

Physical Factors

  • Prolonged or intense pain from injury or illness
  • Repeated injuries
  • Inflammation
  • Nerve damage

Psychological Factors

  • Chronic stress
  • Anxiety and fear of pain
  • Depression
  • Trauma history
  • Catastrophizing (worst-case thinking about pain)

Lifestyle Factors

  • Poor sleep
  • Sedentary lifestyle
  • Social isolation

Genetic Factors

  • Some people may be genetically predisposed
  • Variations in pain-processing genes

Treatment Approaches

Effective treatment typically requires a multimodal approach:7

Education

Understanding pain neuroscience is therapeutic:

  • Learning how pain works reduces fear and threat
  • Understanding sensitization empowers self-management
  • Pain education has been shown to reduce pain intensity

Graded Exercise and Movement

  • Gentle, gradual increase in activity
  • Avoid boom-bust cycles (overdoing then crashing)
  • Exercise helps normalize nervous system sensitivity
  • Focus on consistency over intensity

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Helps address thoughts and behaviors that maintain pain:8

  • Reducing catastrophizing
  • Addressing fear-avoidance behaviors
  • Developing coping strategies
  • Improving function despite pain

Mindfulness and Relaxation

  • Mindfulness meditation
  • Deep breathing exercises
  • Progressive muscle relaxation
  • Help calm an overactive nervous system

Sleep Optimization

  • Poor sleep increases pain sensitivity
  • Address sleep disorders
  • Practice good sleep hygiene

Medications

Medications that work on the central nervous system may help:

  • Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline)
  • SNRIs (duloxetine, milnacipran)
  • Anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin)
  • Low-dose naltrexone (emerging evidence)

Physical Therapy

  • Gentle manual therapy
  • Graded exposure to movement
  • Desensitization techniques
  • Pacing strategies

The Role of the Brain

Modern pain science recognizes that pain is produced by the brain as a protective response:9

Key Concepts

  • Pain is an output of the brain, not just an input from tissues
  • The brain evaluates all available information to decide if pain is needed
  • Context, beliefs, emotions, and past experiences all influence pain
  • Pain can be influenced by changing how the brain interprets signals

Neuroplasticity

The good news: the nervous system can change:10

  • Just as the nervous system became sensitized, it can become desensitized
  • New neural pathways can be formed
  • With consistent practice, the pain system can recalibrate
  • Recovery is possible, though it takes time and effort

Self-Management Strategies

Pacing

  • Balance activity with rest
  • Avoid the boom-bust cycle
  • Gradually increase activity over time

Stress Management

  • Identify and address stressors
  • Practice relaxation techniques daily
  • Build in enjoyable activities

Sleep

  • Maintain consistent sleep schedule
  • Create restful sleep environment
  • Address sleep disorders

Movement

  • Gentle, regular movement
  • Activities you enjoy
  • Gradual progression

Social Connection

  • Maintain relationships
  • Seek support from others who understand
  • Avoid isolation
