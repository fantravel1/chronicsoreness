Central Sensitization

Understanding how the nervous system can amplify pain signals and what can be done about it

What Is Central Sensitization?

Central sensitization is a condition where the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) becomes hypersensitive and amplifies pain signals. Instead of accurately reporting tissue damage, the nervous system "turns up the volume" on pain, causing pain that is disproportionate to any physical findings.1

This concept has transformed our understanding of chronic pain. It explains why pain can persist long after tissues have healed and why some people experience widespread pain without identifiable tissue damage.

How It Differs from Normal Pain

  • Normal pain - Nervous system accurately reports tissue damage; pain matches injury
  • Central sensitization - Nervous system amplifies signals; pain exceeds any tissue damage
  • The problem shifts from the tissues to the nervous system itself
  • Pain becomes "nociplastic" - generated by altered nervous system function2

The Science Behind It

Several mechanisms contribute to central sensitization:

Neuroplastic Changes

  • Wind-up - Repeated stimulation causes progressively stronger pain responses
  • Long-term potentiation - Pain pathways become more efficient at transmitting signals
  • Expanded receptive fields - Pain-sensing neurons respond to larger body areas
  • Reduced inhibition - The body's natural pain-dampening systems weaken3

Brain Changes

  • Altered activity in pain-processing regions
  • Changes in brain structure visible on imaging
  • Emotional centers become more involved in pain processing
  • Disrupted descending pain modulation

Signs and Symptoms

Central sensitization produces characteristic patterns:

Heightened Pain Sensitivity

  • Hyperalgesia - Increased pain from normally painful stimuli
  • Allodynia - Pain from normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, temperature changes)
  • Widespread pain - Pain spreading beyond original injury site
  • Prolonged pain - Pain lasting after stimulus is removed

Associated Symptoms

  • Heightened sensitivity to light, sound, smell
  • Cognitive difficulties ("brain fog")
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Fatigue
  • Mood changes (anxiety, depression)
  • Autonomic symptoms (temperature regulation, sweating)

Conditions Associated with Central Sensitization

Central sensitization is recognized in many chronic pain conditions:4

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Irritable bowel syndrome
  • Chronic headaches and migraines
  • Temporomandibular disorders
  • Chronic low back pain
  • Chronic whiplash
  • Complex regional pain syndrome
  • Interstitial cystitis

The Pain Is Real

Central sensitization does not mean pain is "imaginary" or "all in your head." The nervous system changes are real and measurable. The pain experience is genuineâ€”it's just that the primary problem has shifted from the tissues to the nervous system's processing of signals.

Diagnosis

There's no single test for central sensitization. Diagnosis involves:

  • Clinical history - Pattern of symptoms, onset, triggers
  • Physical examination - Testing for allodynia, hyperalgesia, widespread tenderness
  • Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI) - Validated questionnaire5
  • Quantitative sensory testing - Measures pain thresholds (research settings)
  • Ruling out other causes - Ensuring no other treatable condition

Treatment Approaches

Treatment aims to "retrain" the nervous system and reduce hypersensitivity:

Pain Neuroscience Education

Understanding how pain works can itself reduce pain:6

  • Learning that pain doesn't always equal tissue damage
  • Understanding the nervous system's role
  • Reducing fear and catastrophizing about pain
  • Empowering self-management

Graded Exercise and Movement

  • Start below the level that triggers flares
  • Progress very gradually
  • Focus on consistency over intensity
  • Movement helps "recalibrate" the nervous system

Psychological Approaches

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) - Addresses thoughts and behaviors
  • Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) - Promotes psychological flexibility
  • Mindfulness-based interventions - Change relationship with pain
  • Graded Motor Imagery - Uses visualization to reduce pain

Medications

Medications that affect nervous system sensitization:

  • Antidepressants - SNRIs (duloxetine), tricyclics; modulate pain pathways
  • Anticonvulsants - Gabapentin, pregabalin; calm nerve signaling
  • Low-dose naltrexone - May reduce neuroinflammation
  • Note - Traditional painkillers often less effective for central sensitization

Other Approaches

  • Sleep improvement (critical for nervous system recovery)
  • Stress management
  • Pacing activities
  • Manual therapy (gentle approaches)
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)

Self-Management Strategies

Calm the Nervous System

  • Regular relaxation practice
  • Deep breathing exercises
  • Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
  • Reduce stress where possible

Gradual Exposure

  • Slowly reintroduce avoided activities
  • Start well below your threshold
  • Progress based on time, not pain
  • Expect some discomfortâ€”it doesn't mean damage

Shift Your Focus

  • Engage in meaningful activities
  • Practice mindfulness
  • Challenge catastrophic thoughts
  • Focus on what you can do, not what you can't

Recovery Is Possible

While central sensitization can be challenging, the nervous system's plasticity that created the problem can also reverse it. Many people experience significant improvement with appropriate treatment. The key is patience, consistency, and a multimodal approach addressing physical, psychological, and lifestyle factors.
