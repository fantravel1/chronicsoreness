Bursitis

Understanding inflammation of the bursae

Key Takeaways

  • Bursitis is inflammation of fluid-filled sacs that cushion joints
  • Most common in shoulders, hips, elbows, and knees
  • Usually caused by repetitive motion or prolonged pressure
  • Most cases resolve with rest and conservative treatment

What Bursae Do

  • Reduce friction between tissues
  • Cushion bones, tendons, and muscles
  • Allow smooth movement at joints
  • Contain lubricating fluid

Common Types

Trochanteric Bursitis (Hip)

  • Pain on outer hip
  • Worse lying on affected side
  • Pain with climbing stairs, getting up
  • Common in runners and older adults

Subacromial Bursitis (Shoulder)

  • Pain with overhead movements
  • Often part of shoulder impingement
  • May hurt reaching behind back
  • Night pain common

Prepatellar Bursitis (Knee)

  • "Housemaid's knee" or "carpet layer's knee"
  • Swelling in front of kneecap
  • From prolonged kneeling
  • Visible swelling common

Olecranon Bursitis (Elbow)

  • "Student's elbow" or "miner's elbow"
  • Swelling at tip of elbow
  • From leaning on elbows
  • Often visibly swollen

Pes Anserine Bursitis (Inner Knee)

  • Pain on inner knee below joint
  • Worse with stairs or getting up
  • Common with arthritis

Symptoms

Common Signs

  • Pain at the affected joint
  • Tenderness when pressing on the area
  • Stiffness
  • Swelling (may or may not be visible)
  • Pain with movement
  • Pain when lying on the area (especially hip)

When to Seek Care Urgently

  • Fever with joint pain
  • Rapid onset severe swelling
  • Redness and warmth over joint
  • Inability to move the joint
  • May indicate septic (infected) bursitis

Causes and Risk Factors

Common Causes

  • Repetitive motion: Same movements over and over
  • Prolonged pressure: Kneeling, leaning on elbows
  • Injury: Direct blow or fall
  • Overuse: Sudden increase in activity
  • Infection: Usually from skin break

Risk Factors

  • Age (more common with aging)
  • Occupations with repetitive motions or kneeling
  • Sports with repetitive movements
  • Rheumatoid arthritis, gout
  • Diabetes
  • Obesity

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History and physical examination
  • Location and nature of pain
  • Tenderness over bursa
  • Range of motion testing

Imaging

  • Often not needed for diagnosis
  • X-ray to rule out other causes
  • Ultrasound can show fluid in bursa
  • MRI for complex cases

If Infection Suspected

  • Aspiration of bursa fluid
  • Analysis for infection
  • Important to identify septic bursitis

Treatment

Most bursitis responds to conservative treatment:2,3

Initial Treatment

  • Rest: Avoid aggravating activities
  • Ice: 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Compression: If swelling present
  • Elevation: When possible

Medications

  • NSAIDs: Reduce pain and inflammation
  • Topical anti-inflammatories
  • Acetaminophen for pain

Physical Therapy

  • Stretching tight structures
  • Strengthening supporting muscles
  • Correcting movement patterns
  • Modalities (ultrasound, etc.)

Injections

  • Corticosteroid injection into bursa
  • Can provide significant relief
  • Usually limited to a few injections

For Septic Bursitis

  • Antibiotics (essential)
  • Aspiration of infected fluid
  • Sometimes surgical drainage

Self-Care

During Acute Phase

  • Rest from aggravating activities
  • Ice regularly
  • OTC anti-inflammatories if appropriate
  • Protect area from pressure

Prevention

  • Use knee pads if kneeling
  • Take breaks from repetitive tasks
  • Strengthen muscles around joints
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Warm up before exercise
  • Use proper technique in sports

Prognosis

  • Most cases resolve within weeks
  • May recur if underlying cause not addressed
  • Some cases become chronic
  • Chronic cases may need ongoing management
