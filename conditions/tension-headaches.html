Tension Headaches

Understanding and managing the most common type of headache

Key Takeaways

  • Tension-type headaches are the most common primary headache disorder
  • Women are affected more often than men
  • Lifestyle modifications are essential for prevention
  • Medication overuse can worsen chronic headaches

Understanding Tension Headaches

Tension-type headaches (TTH) are the most common form of primary headache, characterized by bilateral, pressing or tightening pain of mild to moderate intensity.1 Unlike migraines, they typically do not cause nausea or sensitivity to light and sound.

40-80%
of adults experience tension headaches at some point2

Types of Tension Headaches

  • Episodic infrequent: Less than 1 day per month
  • Episodic frequent: 1-14 days per month for at least 3 months
  • Chronic: 15 or more days per month for at least 3 months

Why Women Are More Affected

Women experience tension headaches at higher rates than men, possibly due to:3

  • Hormonal fluctuations
  • Higher rates of stress and anxiety
  • Differences in pain processing
  • Muscular differences in neck and shoulders

Symptoms

Characteristic features of tension headaches:1,4

Pain Characteristics

  • Location: Both sides of head (bilateral)
  • Quality: Pressing, tightening, or band-like (non-pulsating)
  • Intensity: Mild to moderate
  • Duration: 30 minutes to 7 days
  • Activity: Not worsened by routine physical activity

What Tension Headaches Do NOT Typically Cause

  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Significant sensitivity to light and sound
  • Throbbing or pulsating pain
  • One-sided pain only
  • Visual disturbances

Associated Features

  • Tenderness in scalp, neck, or shoulder muscles
  • Feeling of tightness or pressure around head
  • Mild sensitivity to light OR sound (not both)

Tension Headache vs. Migraine

While tension headaches are pressing and bilateral, migraines are typically pulsating, one-sided, and associated with nausea and sensitivity to light/sound. However, some people experience both types, and features can overlap.

Causes and Triggers

The exact cause of tension headaches is not fully understood, but several factors contribute:5

Contributing Factors

  • Muscle tension: Sustained contraction in head, neck, and shoulder muscles
  • Central sensitization: Increased pain sensitivity in the nervous system
  • Stress: Both physical and emotional
  • Poor posture: Especially forward head posture

Common Triggers

  • Stress and anxiety
  • Poor or insufficient sleep
  • Skipped meals
  • Dehydration
  • Eye strain
  • Poor posture
  • Caffeine withdrawal
  • Jaw clenching or teeth grinding
  • Bright lights or loud noise
  • Weather changes

Muscle Involvement

Pericranial tenderness (tenderness in muscles around the skull) is common:

  • Temporalis muscles (temples)
  • Frontalis (forehead)
  • Suboccipital muscles (base of skull)
  • Trapezius (upper back/neck)
  • Sternocleidomastoid (side of neck)

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical, based on headache characteristics:6

Medical History

  • Headache frequency, duration, and characteristics
  • Associated symptoms
  • Triggers and relieving factors
  • Medication use (including over-the-counter)
  • Impact on daily life

Physical Examination

  • Neurological examination
  • Palpation of head and neck muscles
  • Assessment of neck range of motion
  • Blood pressure check

When Imaging May Be Needed

Imaging is not routinely needed but may be ordered for:

  • Sudden severe headache
  • Neurological abnormalities on exam
  • Significant change in headache pattern
  • New headache after age 50
  • Headache with fever, weight loss, or other concerning features

When to Seek Immediate Care

  • Sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache
  • Headache with fever, stiff neck, confusion
  • Headache after head injury
  • Headache with weakness, vision changes, or speech difficulty
  • Worst headache of your life

Treatment

Acute Treatment

For occasional tension headaches:7

  • Simple analgesics: Acetaminophen, ibuprofen, aspirin, or naproxen
  • Rest: In a quiet, dark room if helpful
  • Heat or cold: Applied to neck and shoulders
  • Relaxation: Deep breathing, muscle relaxation

Medication Overuse Headache

Using acute pain medications more than 10-15 days per month can lead to medication overuse headache (MOH), making headaches more frequent and harder to treat. Limit acute medication use and discuss prevention strategies with your healthcare provider if headaches are frequent.8

Preventive Treatment

For chronic or frequent tension headaches, prevention is key:9

Non-medication approaches:

  • Stress management and relaxation training
  • Physical therapy for neck and shoulder tension
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Biofeedback
  • Regular exercise
  • Sleep optimization
  • Trigger identification and avoidance

Preventive medications (when needed):

  • Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline)
  • SNRIs (venlafaxine)
  • Muscle relaxants (tizanidine)

Self-Care Strategies

Immediate Relief

  • Apply heat to neck and shoulders
  • Take a warm shower
  • Gentle neck stretches
  • Massage temples and base of skull
  • Practice deep breathing
  • Rest in a quiet environment

Stress Management

  • Regular relaxation practice
  • Mindfulness meditation
  • Progressive muscle relaxation
  • Regular exercise
  • Adequate sleep
  • Time management and boundary setting

Posture and Ergonomics

  • Position computer screen at eye level
  • Take frequent breaks from screens
  • Avoid forward head posture
  • Use supportive chair and pillow

Lifestyle Factors

  • Maintain regular sleep schedule
  • Eat regular meals
  • Stay hydrated
  • Limit caffeine and alcohol
  • Regular physical activity

Exercises for Prevention

Neck Stretches

  1. Slowly tilt ear toward shoulder, hold 15-30 seconds
  2. Repeat on other side
  3. Turn head to look over each shoulder, hold 15-30 seconds
  4. Gently tilt chin toward chest

Chin Tucks

  1. Sit or stand with good posture
  2. Gently draw chin back, creating a double chin
  3. Hold 5 seconds
  4. Repeat 10-15 times, several times daily

Shoulder Rolls

  1. Roll shoulders up, back, and down
  2. Repeat 10 times
  3. Reverse direction

Suboccipital Release

  1. Place fingers at base of skull
  2. Apply gentle pressure
  3. Slowly nod head yes and no while maintaining pressure
  4. Continue for 1-2 minutes

Keeping a Headache Diary

Tracking headaches helps identify patterns and triggers:

  • Date and time of headache
  • Duration and intensity (1-10 scale)
  • Location and quality of pain
  • Potential triggers (stress, sleep, food, weather)
  • Medications taken and their effectiveness
  • Menstrual cycle (if applicable)
  • Impact on activities
