Hip Osteoarthritis

Managing degenerative hip joint disease

What Is Hip Osteoarthritis?

Hip osteoarthritis is a degenerative joint disease where the cartilage covering the hip joint gradually wears away, leading to bone-on-bone contact, pain, and stiffness. It's a common cause of disability in older adults. While it can't be reversed, many treatments can help manage symptoms.1

Symptoms2

  • Pain in groin, thigh, or buttock
  • Stiffness, especially in morning
  • Reduced range of motion
  • Difficulty with shoes and socks
  • Pain with walking or stairs
  • Limping
  • Clicking or grinding sensation

Risk Factors

  • Age (over 45)
  • Obesity
  • Previous hip injury
  • Hip abnormalities (dysplasia, FAI)
  • Genetics
  • Heavy physical occupations

Diagnosis3

  • Symptoms and physical examination
  • X-rays (show joint space, bone changes)
  • MRI rarely needed
  • Distinguish from other hip conditions

Treatment

First-Line

  • Exercise and physical therapy
  • Weight management
  • Education

Medications

  • Acetaminophen
  • Oral NSAIDs
  • Topical treatments (less effective for hip)

Injections

  • Corticosteroid (temporary relief)
  • Hyaluronic acid (mixed evidence)

Surgery

  • Total hip replacement (very effective)
  • When conservative measures fail
  • High patient satisfaction

Exercise for Hip OA

  • Strengthening (hip, core, leg muscles)
  • Flexibility and stretching
  • Low-impact cardio (swimming, cycling, walking)
  • Water exercises
  • Tai chi

Self-Management

  • Stay active within limits
  • Use assistive devices if helpful
  • Avoid prolonged standing
  • Use chairs with armrests
  • Heat for stiffness
