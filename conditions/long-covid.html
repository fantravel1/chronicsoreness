Long COVID & Chronic Pain

Understanding post-viral syndrome, why women are more affected, and evidence-based approaches to management

Why Women Are More Susceptible

Women appear significantly more susceptible to long COVID, with 4.4% affected versus 2.3% of men. Female sex is an independent risk factor with approximately 3 times higher adjusted odds compared to males.

The Immune System Factor

This gender disparity relates to immune system differences:

  • Stronger immune responses: Women have higher immunoglobulin levels and more robust antibody production
  • Better viral clearance: The stronger immune response clears the initial infection more effectively
  • Higher autoimmune risk: The same robust immune system creates greater risk of chronic immune activation
  • TLR7 gene: Located on the X chromosome, causes greater interferon signaling, predisposing to prolonged inflammation

Musculoskeletal Symptoms

Joint and muscle pain are common in long COVID:

  • 21% of patients report musculoskeletal symptoms
  • 61% prevalence at 429 days post-infection in some studies
  • Symptoms include joint pain, muscle aches, and weakness
  • Can affect previously healthy individuals with mild initial infections

ME/CFS Connection

25 of 29 known ME/CFS symptoms are reported in long COVID studies. Approximately half of long COVID patients meet ME/CFS diagnostic criteria at 6 months.

The ME/CFS patient population is expected to grow from 3.3 million to 5-9 million Americans due to the pandemic. If you have long COVID symptoms, learning about ME/CFS management is essential.

Common Long COVID Symptoms

Fatigue & PEM

  • Profound, debilitating fatigue
  • Post-exertional malaise (PEM)
  • Crashes after physical/mental activity
  • Unrefreshing sleep

Pain Symptoms

  • Muscle aches
  • Joint pain
  • Headaches
  • Chest pain

Cognitive

  • Brain fog
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Memory problems
  • Word-finding difficulties

Autonomic

  • Heart racing (POTS)
  • Dizziness on standing
  • Temperature dysregulation
  • Exercise intolerance

Critical Management Principles

Warning: Exercise Can Be Harmful

If you experience post-exertional malaise (PEM)—symptom worsening 12-48 hours after activity—standard exercise recommendations can make you worse.

Do NOT "push through." Graded exercise therapy (GET) is no longer recommended for ME/CFS-like presentations. Prioritize pacing and rest.

Pacing Strategies

  • Heart rate monitoring: Stay below resting heart rate + 15 beats
  • Activity logging: Track activities and symptoms to identify triggers
  • Energy budgeting: Allocate limited energy to priorities
  • Rest before exhaustion: Stop activities before you feel you need to
  • Avoid boom-bust cycles: Don't overdo on good days

When PEM Isn't Present

If you don't experience post-exertional malaise, gentle, gradual activity may be appropriate. Work with healthcare providers experienced in post-viral syndromes to develop an individualized approach.

Treatment Approaches

Symptom Management

  • Pain: OTC pain relievers, neuromodulators if widespread pain develops
  • Sleep: Sleep hygiene, low-dose amitriptyline if needed
  • Cognitive symptoms: Cognitive strategies, adequate rest, treating underlying causes
  • Dysautonomia/POTS: Increased salt and fluids, compression stockings, slow position changes

Emerging Treatments Under Investigation

  • Low-dose naltrexone (LDN)
  • Antihistamines for mast cell activation
  • Antivirals
  • Immunomodulators
  • Hyperbaric oxygen therapy

When to Seek Care

See a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Symptoms persisting beyond 4 weeks post-infection
  • Significant functional impairment
  • New symptoms developing
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath
  • Severe headaches
  • Symptoms worsening over time

Finding Specialized Care

  • Long COVID clinics: Many major health systems have established specialized programs
  • ME/CFS specialists: May understand post-exertional malaise better than general providers
  • Post-COVID programs: Rehabilitation programs designed for long COVID

Supporting Recovery

Evidence-Based Support

  • Rest: Prioritize sleep and rest periods
  • Anti-inflammatory nutrition: Mediterranean diet pattern
  • Stress management: Gentle mind-body practices as tolerated
  • Social support: Connect with others who understand
  • Pacing: The most important strategy for PEM prevention
  • Accommodations: Work and school modifications as needed

Advocacy and Documentation

Long COVID can be challenging to get diagnosed and treated. Important steps:

  • Document everything: Symptoms, triggers, functional limitations
  • Track your COVID infection date: Important for diagnosis and potential disability claims
  • Request testing: Rule out other conditions even if providers are dismissive
  • Seek second opinions: If not being taken seriously
  • Know your rights: Long COVID may qualify as a disability under ADA
