Hypermobility & Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome

Understanding joint hypermobility and connective tissue disorders

Key Takeaways

  • Hypermobility exists on a spectrum from benign flexibility to symptomatic disorders
  • Women are significantly more affected than men
  • Strengthening and stability exercises are cornerstone treatments
  • Many associated conditions benefit from targeted management

Understanding Hypermobility

Joint hypermobility means joints that move beyond the normal range of motion. While many people are hypermobile without problems, others experience pain, instability, and various systemic symptoms. The 2017 classification distinguishes between:1

  • Asymptomatic hypermobility: Flexible joints without symptoms
  • Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder (HSD): Symptomatic hypermobility not meeting EDS criteria
  • Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS): Specific diagnostic criteria with systemic features

The Beighton Score

A simple screening tool for generalized joint hypermobility (score 0-9):3

  • Pinky finger bends back beyond 90 degrees (1 point each hand)
  • Thumb touches forearm when bent toward wrist (1 point each hand)
  • Elbow hyperextends beyond 10 degrees (1 point each arm)
  • Knee hyperextends beyond 10 degrees (1 point each leg)
  • Palms flat on floor with knees straight (1 point)

A score of 5 or more (adults) suggests generalized hypermobility, but symptoms matter more than score.

Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS)

hEDS is the most common type of EDS and involves specific diagnostic criteria:1

Diagnostic Criteria Summary

  1. Generalized joint hypermobility (Beighton score thresholds vary by age)
  2. Two or more of the following:
    • Systemic features (soft/velvety skin, mild skin hyperextensibility, unexplained striae, etc.)
    • Positive family history
    • Musculoskeletal complications (chronic pain, recurrent dislocations, etc.)
  3. Exclusion of other conditions

HSD vs. hEDS

HSD and hEDS exist on a spectrum. Many people with HSD have similar symptoms and challenges as those with hEDS. Both conditions deserve proper recognition and management. The distinction is primarily for research and classification—treatment approaches are similar.

Symptoms

Symptoms can affect multiple body systems:4

Musculoskeletal

  • Chronic widespread pain
  • Frequent joint subluxations or dislocations
  • Joint instability
  • Early osteoarthritis
  • Muscle fatigue and weakness
  • TMJ dysfunction

Skin and Tissue

  • Soft, velvety skin
  • Easy bruising
  • Stretch marks (striae)
  • Slow wound healing
  • Atrophic scarring

Autonomic Dysfunction

  • POTS (postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome)
  • Dizziness upon standing
  • Fatigue
  • Temperature regulation issues
  • Gastrointestinal problems

Other Associated Features

  • Chronic fatigue
  • Anxiety
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Headaches and migraines
  • Bladder dysfunction
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction
  • Mast cell activation syndrome

Why Women Are More Affected

Several factors may explain the higher prevalence in women:5

  • Hormonal effects on connective tissue (estrogen, relaxin)
  • Symptoms often worsen with hormonal changes (menstruation, pregnancy)
  • Possible X-linked genetic factors
  • Women may naturally have more joint laxity

Pregnancy Considerations

  • Increased joint laxity during pregnancy
  • Higher risk of pelvic girdle pain
  • May have faster labors
  • Postpartum recovery may be challenging
  • Pelvic floor issues more common

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical, based on criteria and examination:1

Who Can Diagnose

  • Geneticists (preferred for EDS evaluation)
  • Rheumatologists familiar with hypermobility
  • Some physical medicine specialists
  • Knowledgeable primary care providers

What to Expect

  • Detailed medical and family history
  • Physical examination including Beighton score
  • Assessment of skin and other features
  • Ruling out other connective tissue disorders
  • Possible genetic testing (for other EDS types)

Other EDS Types

There are 13 types of EDS. While hEDS is most common, some rare types affect blood vessels and organs. If you have unusual bruising, organ problems, or family history of vascular issues, seek evaluation by a geneticist to rule out vascular EDS (vEDS).

Treatment and Management

Treatment focuses on symptom management and preventing complications:6

Physical Therapy

The cornerstone of hypermobility management:

  • Joint stabilization exercises
  • Strengthening (especially isometric and closed-chain)
  • Proprioception training
  • Core stability
  • Posture optimization
  • Pacing strategies

Exercise Approach for Hypermobility

Traditional stretching is usually not beneficial and may be harmful. Focus should be on strengthening within protected ranges. Low-impact activities like swimming, Pilates (modified), and gentle resistance training are often best. Avoid high-impact activities and end-range stretching.

Bracing and Support

  • Joint supports for unstable joints
  • Compression garments
  • Orthotics for foot problems
  • Ring splints for finger hypermobility

Pain Management

  • NSAIDs (use cautiously long-term)
  • Topical treatments
  • Low-dose medications for chronic pain
  • Avoiding opioids when possible
  • Heat for muscle pain
  • TENS for some patients

Managing Associated Conditions

  • POTS: Increased fluids, salt, compression, medications
  • GI issues: Dietary modifications, motility agents
  • Fatigue: Pacing, sleep optimization
  • Anxiety: Therapy, medications if needed

Self-Management Strategies

Joint Protection

  • Avoid locking joints in hyperextension
  • Use good body mechanics
  • Take frequent breaks from sustained positions
  • Avoid carrying heavy bags on one shoulder

Pacing

  • Balance activity with rest
  • Avoid boom-bust cycles
  • Build in recovery time
  • Listen to your body

Sleep

  • Supportive mattress and pillows
  • Avoid positions that stress joints
  • Address sleep disorders

Mental Health

  • Acknowledge the challenges of invisible illness
  • Connect with others who understand
  • Consider counseling for coping strategies
  • Address anxiety, which is common

Finding Knowledgeable Care

Finding healthcare providers who understand hypermobility can be challenging:

  • EDS Society maintains a provider directory
  • Ask for referrals in hypermobility support groups
  • Look for physical therapists with hypermobility experience
  • Consider multidisciplinary clinics when available
