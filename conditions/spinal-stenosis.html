Spinal Stenosis

Understanding narrowing of the spinal canal

Key Takeaways

  • Spinal stenosis is narrowing of the spinal canal that can compress nerves
  • Most common in adults over 50, usually due to degenerative changes
  • Many people with stenosis on imaging have no symptoms
  • Conservative treatment is effective for most; surgery reserved for severe cases

What Is Spinal Stenosis?

Spinal stenosis is a narrowing of the spaces within the spine, which can put pressure on the spinal cord and nerves. It most commonly occurs in the lower back (lumbar) and neck (cervical).1

Types

  • Lumbar stenosis: Most common, affects lower back
  • Cervical stenosis: Affects neck, can be more serious
  • Central stenosis: Narrowing of main spinal canal
  • Foraminal stenosis: Narrowing where nerves exit spine

Causes

Most stenosis results from age-related changes:3

Degenerative Changes

  • Disc degeneration: Discs lose height and may bulge
  • Bone spurs: Overgrowth of bone (osteophytes)
  • Ligament thickening: Ligamentum flavum hypertrophy
  • Facet joint arthritis: Joint enlargement

Other Causes

  • Congenital (born with narrow canal)
  • Spinal injuries
  • Tumors
  • Paget's disease
  • Previous spine surgery

Symptoms

Lumbar Stenosis

  • Neurogenic claudication: Leg pain/cramping with walking or standing
  • Pain relieved by sitting or bending forward
  • Can walk farther when leaning on shopping cart
  • Numbness or tingling in legs/feet
  • Weakness in legs
  • Lower back pain (may be mild)

Cervical Stenosis

  • Neck pain
  • Numbness or tingling in arms/hands
  • Weakness in arms or hands
  • Balance problems
  • Difficulty with fine motor tasks
  • In severe cases: bowel/bladder dysfunction (emergency)

Emergency Symptoms

Seek immediate care for: loss of bladder/bowel control, severe or progressive weakness, numbness in groin area, or rapidly worsening symptoms.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • Detailed symptom history
  • Neurological examination
  • Gait assessment
  • Provocative testing

Imaging

  • MRI: Best for visualizing soft tissues and nerve compression
  • CT scan: Better for bone detail
  • X-rays: Show bone structure, alignment

Imaging Findings vs. Symptoms

Many people have stenosis on imaging without symptoms. Imaging findings must be correlated with clinical symptoms. Treatment is based on symptoms, not just imaging.

Treatment

Most cases respond to conservative treatment:4

Activity Modification

  • Avoid prolonged standing and walking
  • Use shopping cart or walker for support
  • Flexion-based exercises often better tolerated
  • Cycling may be easier than walking

Physical Therapy

  • Flexion-based exercises
  • Core strengthening
  • Stationary cycling
  • Aquatic therapy
  • Manual therapy

Medications

  • NSAIDs
  • Acetaminophen
  • Neuropathic pain medications (gabapentin, pregabalin)
  • Muscle relaxants (short-term)

Injections

  • Epidural steroid injections
  • May provide temporary relief
  • Useful for flares or to enable PT
  • Limited number recommended

Surgery

Considered when conservative treatment fails:

  • Decompression: Removes tissue compressing nerves
  • Laminectomy: Removes part of vertebra
  • Fusion: Sometimes added for stability
  • Generally effective for leg symptoms5
  • Back pain may be less responsive

Living with Spinal Stenosis

Positioning

  • Flexion (bending forward) opens the spinal canal
  • Sleeping with knees bent may help
  • Reclining chairs often comfortable

Exercise

  • Stationary cycling often well-tolerated
  • Swimming and water exercise
  • Walking with support (cart, walker)
  • Core strengthening

Daily Activities

  • Plan activities to include rest breaks
  • Use assistive devices as needed
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Stop smoking
