Spinal Stenosis

Understanding narrowing of the spinal canal and its effects

What Is Spinal Stenosis?

Spinal stenosis is a narrowing of the spaces within the spine, which can put pressure on the nerves traveling through it. Most commonly affecting the lower back (lumbar stenosis) or neck (cervical stenosis), it typically develops gradually due to age-related changes.1

While some people have spinal stenosis on imaging without symptoms, others experience significant pain, numbness, or weaknessâ€”particularly with walking or standing.

Types of Spinal Stenosis

  • Lumbar stenosis - Lower back; most common type
  • Cervical stenosis - Neck; can affect arms and legs
  • Thoracic stenosis - Mid-back; rare
  • Central stenosis - Narrowing of main spinal canal
  • Foraminal stenosis - Narrowing where nerves exit spine

Causes

Primary Causes

  • Degenerative changes - Most common; age-related wear2
  • Osteoarthritis - Bone spurs encroach on space
  • Disc degeneration - Bulging discs narrow canal
  • Ligament thickening - Ligamentum flavum hypertrophy
  • Facet joint hypertrophy - Enlarged joints

Less Common Causes

  • Congenital (born with narrow canal)
  • Spondylolisthesis (vertebra slips forward)
  • Spinal tumors
  • Spinal injury
  • Paget's disease

Symptoms

Lumbar Stenosis Symptoms

  • Neurogenic claudication - Pain/cramping in legs with walking3
  • Relief with flexion - Better when bending forward, sitting, or lying
  • Limited walking distance - May need to stop and rest frequently
  • Shopping cart sign - Leaning on cart relieves symptoms
  • Lower back pain
  • Numbness or tingling in legs/feet
  • Weakness in legs
  • Difficulty with balance

Cervical Stenosis Symptoms

  • Neck pain
  • Numbness or tingling in arms, hands, legs
  • Weakness in arms or legs
  • Balance and coordination problems
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction (severe cases)

When to Seek Urgent Care

Seek immediate medical attention if you develop: sudden severe weakness in legs, loss of bladder or bowel control, significant numbness in the groin area (saddle anesthesia), or rapidly progressive symptoms. These may indicate cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of symptoms and aggravating factors
  • Physical examination
  • Assessment of walking ability
  • Neurological testing

Imaging Studies

  • MRI - Gold standard; shows soft tissues, nerves, discs4
  • CT scan - Better for bone detail
  • CT myelogram - When MRI not possible
  • X-rays - Show bone spurs, alignment; not stenosis directly

Imaging vs. Symptoms

Many people over 60 have some degree of spinal stenosis on imaging without symptoms. Treatment decisions should be based on symptoms and functional impact, not just imaging findings. Correlation between severity on MRI and symptom severity is imperfect.

Treatment

Conservative Treatment

First-line approach for most people:5

  • Physical therapy - Core strengthening, flexibility, posture
  • Exercises that flex the spine - Stationary biking, swimming
  • Activity modification - Avoid prolonged standing/walking
  • Walking aids - Rollator allows forward lean
  • NSAIDs - For pain and inflammation
  • Acetaminophen - For pain
  • Epidural steroid injections - Can provide temporary relief

Medications

  • NSAIDs - Ibuprofen, naproxen
  • Neuropathic pain medications - For nerve-related symptoms
  • Muscle relaxants - For muscle spasm
  • Opioids - Generally avoided long-term

Injections

  • Epidural steroid injections - Most common
  • Can provide weeks to months of relief
  • May allow participation in physical therapy
  • Limited to a few times per year

Surgical Treatment6

Considered when:

  • Conservative treatment fails after adequate trial
  • Significant neurological deficits
  • Progressive weakness
  • Severe functional limitation

Common Surgical Procedures

  • Laminectomy - Removes lamina to create more space
  • Laminotomy - Removes portion of lamina
  • Foraminotomy - Opens nerve exit tunnels
  • Spinal fusion - May be added for instability
  • Interspinous spacers - Less invasive option for some

Self-Management

  • Stay active - Avoid bed rest
  • Flexion-based exercises - Knee-to-chest stretches
  • Stationary bike - Allows forward lean
  • Pool exercises - Support from water
  • Core strengthening - Supports spine
  • Maintain healthy weight - Reduces spinal load
  • Use assistive devices - If helpful for walking

Prognosis

  • Often stable or slowly progressive
  • Many manage well with conservative treatment
  • Surgery has good success rates for appropriately selected patients
  • Symptoms may return over time even after treatment
