Scoliosis

Understanding spinal curvature and associated pain

Key Takeaways

  • Scoliosis is a sideways curve of the spine of 10 degrees or more
  • Many people with scoliosis experience no pain, especially with mild curves
  • Adult scoliosis and degenerative scoliosis can cause chronic pain
  • Exercise and physical therapy are cornerstones of management

What Is Scoliosis?

Scoliosis is an abnormal lateral (sideways) curvature of the spine. Rather than running straight, the spine curves to the side, often with rotation of the vertebrae. A curve of 10 degrees or more on X-ray is considered scoliosis.1

Types of Curves

  • C-curve: Single curve to one side
  • S-curve: Two curves in opposite directions
  • Curves can occur in thoracic (mid-back), lumbar (lower back), or thoracolumbar regions

Types of Scoliosis

Idiopathic Scoliosis (Most Common)

  • Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis: Develops during puberty, no known cause
  • More common and more likely to progress in females
  • Often discovered during screening
  • May or may not cause symptoms

Degenerative Scoliosis (Adult-Onset)

  • Develops in adulthood from disc degeneration, arthritis
  • More common after age 50
  • Often associated with back pain
  • Can progress over time

Adult Idiopathic Scoliosis

  • Adolescent scoliosis that persists into adulthood
  • May have had treatment or been untreated
  • Can progress with aging

Other Types

  • Congenital: Present at birth due to vertebral abnormalities
  • Neuromuscular: Due to conditions like cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy
  • Syndromic: Associated with syndromes like Marfan syndrome

Symptoms

Symptoms vary greatly depending on curve severity and type:3

Mild Scoliosis (10-25 degrees)

  • Often no symptoms
  • Uneven shoulders or waist
  • One shoulder blade more prominent
  • May not cause pain

Moderate to Severe Scoliosis (25+ degrees)

  • More noticeable asymmetry
  • Back pain (more common in adults)
  • Muscle fatigue
  • Rib prominence
  • In severe cases: breathing difficulties

Pain Patterns

  • Pain at the curve or adjacent areas
  • Muscle pain from asymmetric loading
  • Nerve pain if nerves are compressed
  • Pain often worse with activity, prolonged standing
  • May worsen as curve progresses

Scoliosis and Pain

Many people with scoliosis have little or no pain. Pain is more common in adult and degenerative scoliosis, severe curves, and when there is associated stenosis or arthritis. The severity of the curve doesn't always correlate with pain level.

Diagnosis

Physical Examination

  • Visual inspection of spine alignment
  • Adam's forward bend test
  • Assessment of shoulder, hip, waist symmetry
  • Neurological examination if indicated

Imaging

  • X-rays: Measure curve angle (Cobb angle)
  • MRI: If neurological symptoms or before surgery
  • CT scan: Detailed bone structure
  • Serial X-rays to monitor progression

Curve Classification

  • Mild: 10-25 degrees
  • Moderate: 25-40 degrees
  • Severe: 40-50+ degrees

Treatment

Treatment depends on age, curve severity, and symptoms:4,5

Observation

  • For mild curves, especially in adults
  • Regular monitoring for progression
  • Focus on symptom management

Physical Therapy

  • Core and back strengthening
  • Scoliosis-specific exercises (Schroth method)
  • Postural training
  • Flexibility work
  • Pain management techniques

Exercise

  • Swimming (excellent for scoliosis)
  • Core strengthening
  • Yoga (modified as needed)
  • Low-impact aerobic exercise
  • Generally, most activities are safe

Bracing

  • Primarily for growing adolescents
  • Can prevent curve progression
  • Not typically used in adults except for pain
  • Soft braces may help with pain

Pain Management

  • NSAIDs for inflammation and pain
  • Physical therapy modalities
  • Injections for associated conditions (stenosis, arthritis)
  • Nerve blocks if nerve compression present

Surgery

  • Reserved for severe or progressive curves
  • Spinal fusion most common procedure
  • For adults: usually for severe pain or neurological symptoms
  • Complex decision with significant recovery

Living with Scoliosis

Daily Life

  • Most people live normal, active lives
  • Regular exercise is important
  • Ergonomic considerations at work
  • Good posture habits help
  • Weight management reduces spine stress

Activity Considerations

  • Most activities are safe with scoliosis
  • High-impact or contact sports: discuss with provider
  • Avoid prolonged positions that increase pain
  • Strengthen core to support spine

Pregnancy

  • Most women with scoliosis have normal pregnancies
  • Epidural usually possible (discuss with provider)
  • Previous spinal fusion may require planning

When Pain Needs Attention

See a Provider If

  • New or worsening pain
  • Numbness, tingling, or weakness in legs
  • Changes in bladder or bowel function
  • Pain that doesn't respond to conservative treatment
  • Visible curve progression
