Failed Back Surgery Syndrome

Understanding pain after spine surgery

What Is Failed Back Surgery Syndrome?

Failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS), also called post-laminectomy syndrome, refers to chronic pain that persists or develops after spine surgery. The term is misleadingâ€”the surgery itself may have been technically successful, but the patient continues to experience significant pain. This occurs in 10-40% of patients after spine surgery.1

Important to Understand

  • "Failed" doesn't mean the surgery was done wrong
  • Surgery may have achieved its goal
  • Pain can have multiple sources
  • Often complex, multifactorial condition

Causes2

Pre-Surgical Factors

  • Wrong diagnosis initially
  • Multiple pain generators
  • Surgery not indicated
  • Pre-existing conditions

Surgical Factors

  • Incomplete decompression
  • Wrong level operated
  • Technical complications
  • Hardware issues

Post-Surgical Factors

  • Epidural fibrosis (scar tissue)
  • Recurrent disc herniation
  • Adjacent segment disease
  • Spinal instability
  • Muscle deconditioning

Symptoms

  • Persistent back and/or leg pain
  • Pain similar to before surgery
  • New or different pain patterns
  • Numbness or weakness
  • Difficulty with activities

Evaluation3

  • Detailed pain history
  • Review of surgical records
  • Physical examination
  • Updated imaging (MRI, CT)
  • Diagnostic injections
  • Psychological assessment

Treatment Options

Conservative

  • Physical therapy
  • Pain medications
  • Epidural injections
  • Spinal cord stimulation
  • Pain psychology

Interventional

  • Epidural adhesiolysis
  • Spinal cord stimulation (often very effective)
  • Intrathecal pain pumps

Revision Surgery

  • Only when clear indication exists
  • Lower success rate than initial surgery
  • Careful patient selection crucial

Prevention

  • Careful patient selection for surgery
  • Realistic expectations
  • Psychological screening
  • Conservative treatment first
