Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

Understanding compression of nerves and blood vessels at the thoracic outlet

What Is Thoracic Outlet Syndrome?

Thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS) is a group of disorders caused by compression of nerves, arteries, or veins in the thoracic outletâ€”the space between the collarbone and first rib. This compression can cause pain, numbness, and weakness in the shoulder, arm, and hand.1

Types of TOS

  • Neurogenic TOS - Most common (95%); affects brachial plexus nerves
  • Venous TOS - Compression of subclavian vein; causes swelling
  • Arterial TOS - Rarest; affects subclavian artery

Symptoms

Neurogenic TOS Symptoms

  • Pain in neck, shoulder, arm, or hand
  • Numbness and tingling in fingers (often ring and pinky)
  • Weakness in hand grip
  • Arm fatigue with overhead activities
  • Wasting of hand muscles (severe cases)

Venous TOS Symptoms

  • Arm swelling
  • Blue discoloration
  • Visible veins on chest/shoulder
  • Arm heaviness and fatigue

Causes

  • Anatomical defects - Cervical rib, abnormal muscle
  • Poor posture - Drooping shoulders
  • Trauma - Whiplash, repetitive injury
  • Repetitive activities - Overhead work, swimming
  • Pregnancy - Joint loosening

Diagnosis

TOS can be difficult to diagnose:2

  • Clinical examination and provocative tests
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • Imaging (X-ray, MRI, CT angiography)
  • Ultrasound with arm positioning

Treatment

Conservative Treatment

  • Physical therapy - Posture correction, stretching, strengthening3
  • Activity modification - Avoid aggravating positions
  • Pain medications - NSAIDs, muscle relaxants
  • Nerve gliding exercises

Surgical Treatment

When conservative treatment fails:

  • First rib resection
  • Scalenectomy (muscle removal)
  • Cervical rib removal if present
