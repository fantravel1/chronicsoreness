Tendinitis

Understanding tendon inflammation, its causes, and effective treatment approaches for lasting relief

What Is Tendinitis?

Tendinitis (also spelled tendonitis) is inflammation or irritation of a tendonâ€”the thick fibrous cords that attach muscle to bone. This condition causes pain and tenderness just outside a joint and can occur in any tendon, though it's most common around shoulders, elbows, wrists, knees, and heels.1

Modern research often uses the term "tendinopathy" as an umbrella term that includes both inflammatory (tendinitis) and degenerative (tendinosis) conditions affecting tendons. Studies show that chronic tendon problems often involve more degeneration than inflammation, which has important implications for treatment approaches.2

Common Types of Tendinitis

Tendinitis is often named based on the affected area or the sport/activity that causes it:

  • Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) - Affects the outside of the elbow
  • Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) - Affects the inside of the elbow
  • Achilles tendinitis - Affects the tendon connecting calf muscles to the heel
  • Patellar tendinitis (jumper's knee) - Affects the tendon below the kneecap
  • Rotator cuff tendinitis - Affects shoulder tendons
  • De Quervain's tenosynovitis - Affects thumb tendons

Causes and Risk Factors

Tendinitis typically results from repetitive movements or sudden injury. Understanding the underlying causes helps guide both treatment and prevention:

Primary Causes

  • Repetitive motions - Work or sports activities involving repeated movements
  • Sudden increase in activity - Ramping up exercise intensity too quickly
  • Poor technique - Improper form during sports or work tasks
  • Direct trauma - Acute injury to the tendon
  • Age-related changes - Tendons become less flexible with age

Risk Factors

  • Age - More common after age 40 as tendons lose elasticity3
  • Certain occupations - Jobs requiring repetitive motions, awkward positions, or vibration
  • Sports participation - Tennis, golf, swimming, running, and throwing sports
  • Certain medications - Fluoroquinolone antibiotics and statins may increase risk
  • Medical conditions - Diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, and thyroid disorders

Symptoms of Tendinitis

Tendinitis symptoms typically develop gradually and include:

  • Pain at the site of the tendon, often described as dull ache
  • Tenderness when pressing on the affected area
  • Mild swelling
  • Pain that worsens with movement or activity
  • Morning stiffness that improves with gentle movement
  • A grating or crackling sensation when moving the joint

When to Seek Medical Attention

Contact a healthcare provider if you experience severe pain, complete inability to move the joint, sudden onset of symptoms, or if self-care doesn't improve symptoms within 2-3 weeks. Seek immediate care if you hear a snapping or popping sound followed by severe pain, as this may indicate a tendon rupture.

Treatment Options

Treatment for tendinitis aims to reduce pain and inflammation while promoting healing. The approach depends on the severity and duration of symptoms.

Conservative Treatment

Most cases of tendinitis respond well to conservative measures:

  • Rest - Avoiding activities that aggravate the condition
  • Ice - Applying ice for 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Compression - Using bandages to reduce swelling
  • Elevation - Raising the affected area to decrease swelling
  • Over-the-counter pain relievers - NSAIDs like ibuprofen can reduce pain and inflammation

Physical Therapy

Physical therapy is crucial for tendinitis recovery. Research shows that eccentric exercise programsâ€”where muscles lengthen under loadâ€”are particularly effective for chronic tendinopathies.4

  • Eccentric exercises - Strengthening while the muscle lengthens
  • Stretching programs - Improving flexibility of the affected area
  • Manual therapy - Hands-on techniques to reduce pain and improve mobility
  • Ultrasound therapy - Using sound waves to promote healing
  • Activity modification - Learning proper techniques to prevent recurrence

Medical Interventions

When conservative measures aren't sufficient, additional treatments may be considered:

  • Corticosteroid injections - Can provide short-term relief but may weaken tendons with repeated use5
  • Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) therapy - Injection of concentrated growth factors from your own blood
  • Extracorporeal shock wave therapy - Sound waves that stimulate healing
  • Dry needling - Stimulating healing response through needle insertion
  • Surgery - Reserved for severe cases that don't respond to other treatments

Prevention Strategies

Preventing tendinitis involves making changes to how you perform activities and taking care of your tendons:

  • Warm up properly before physical activity
  • Progress gradually when increasing activity intensity
  • Use proper technique in sports and work tasks
  • Take breaks during repetitive activities
  • Maintain good posture and ergonomics
  • Strengthen supporting muscles to reduce stress on tendons
  • Stay flexible through regular stretching
  • Use appropriate equipment that fits properly

Living with Chronic Tendinitis

When tendinitis becomes chronic, a comprehensive management approach is needed:

  • Work with a physical therapist to develop a long-term exercise program
  • Identify and modify aggravating activities
  • Consider workplace ergonomic assessments
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce stress on tendons
  • Stay patientâ€”chronic tendon problems can take months to improve
  • Focus on overall fitness and strength, not just the affected area

Recovery Timeline

Acute tendinitis may improve in 2-3 weeks with proper care. Chronic tendinopathy often requires 3-6 months of consistent rehabilitation. Complete recovery depends on the severity, location, and how long the condition has been present. Patience and adherence to treatment are essential for optimal outcomes.
