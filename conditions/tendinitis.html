Tendinitis/Tendinopathy

When tendons become painful and dysfunctional

Understanding Tendon Problems

Tendons connect muscles to bones. When they become painful, we often use the term tendinitis (implying inflammation) or tendinopathy (which may involve degeneration rather than inflammation). These conditions are common causes of chronic pain.1

Common Types

  • Achilles tendinopathy - Heel/calf
  • Patellar tendinopathy - Below kneecap ("jumper's knee")
  • Rotator cuff tendinopathy - Shoulder
  • Lateral epicondylitis - "Tennis elbow"
  • De Quervain's - Thumb/wrist
  • Gluteal tendinopathy - Outer hip

Causes2

  • Repetitive strain/overuse
  • Sudden increase in activity
  • Poor biomechanics
  • Age-related degeneration
  • Inadequate recovery time
  • Training errors

Symptoms

  • Pain with activity, improving with rest
  • Stiffness, especially morning
  • Tenderness along tendon
  • Sometimes swelling or thickening
  • Pain may worsen over time

Treatment3

Load Management

  • Relative rest (not complete immobilization)
  • Modify aggravating activities
  • Gradual return to activity

Exercise Therapy (Key Component)

  • Eccentric exercise - Controlled lengthening
  • Progressive loading - Gradually increase stress
  • Isometric exercise - May help with pain

Other Treatments

  • Ice
  • NSAIDs (short-term)
  • Physical therapy
  • Shockwave therapy
  • PRP (platelet-rich plasma)
  • Corticosteroids (controversial - may delay healing)

Recovery Timeline

Tendinopathy often takes 3-6 months to fully resolve. Patience and consistent rehabilitation are essential. Quick fixes rarely work long-term.
