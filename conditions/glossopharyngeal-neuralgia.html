Glossopharyngeal Neuralgia

Severe pain in throat and ear

What Is Glossopharyngeal Neuralgia?

Glossopharyngeal neuralgia (GPN) is a rare condition causing episodes of severe, shock-like pain in the throat, tonsil area, back of tongue, and ear. It's caused by irritation of the glossopharyngeal nerve (cranial nerve IX). Though similar to trigeminal neuralgia, it affects a different area and is much less common.1

Symptoms2

  • Sudden, severe, stabbing pain
  • Pain in throat, tonsil, tongue base
  • Pain radiating to ear
  • Episodes last seconds to minutes
  • Usually one-sided
  • Attacks can occur in clusters
  • Pain-free periods between episodes

Triggers

  • Swallowing
  • Chewing
  • Talking
  • Coughing
  • Yawning
  • Certain foods (sour, spicy)
  • Cold drinks

Serious Complication

  • Vagal involvement can occur
  • May cause fainting (syncope)
  • Heart rhythm changes
  • This form requires urgent treatment

Causes3

  • Blood vessel compression (most common)
  • Tumors (rare)
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • Throat/neck surgery
  • Infections
  • Often no identifiable cause

Diagnosis

  • Clinical history is key
  • Response to numbing back of throat
  • MRI to look for causes
  • Rule out other conditions
  • Often misdiagnosed initially

Treatment

Medications

  • Carbamazepine (first-line)
  • Oxcarbazepine
  • Gabapentin
  • Pregabalin
  • Similar drugs to trigeminal neuralgia

Procedures

  • Nerve block with local anesthetic

Surgery

  • Microvascular decompression
  • Nerve sectioning (last resort)
  • For medication-resistant cases

Living with GPN

  • Identify and avoid triggers
  • Modify eating if swallowing triggers
  • Take medications consistently
  • Carry quick-dissolve meds
  • Alert medical team to syncope risk
