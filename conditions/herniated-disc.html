Herniated Disc

When disc material presses on nerves

What Is a Herniated Disc?

A herniated disc occurs when the soft inner portion of a spinal disc pushes through a crack in the tougher exterior. This can irritate nearby nerves, causing pain, numbness, or weakness. The terms "slipped disc" or "ruptured disc" describe the same condition.1

Disc Terminology

  • Bulging disc: Disc extends beyond normal space, no tear
  • Herniated disc: Inner material pushes through outer layer
  • Sequestered disc: Fragment breaks off completely

Symptoms2

Lumbar (Lower Back) Herniation

  • Low back pain
  • Sciatica (pain radiating down leg)
  • Numbness or tingling in leg/foot
  • Weakness in leg
  • Pain worse with sitting, coughing, sneezing

Cervical (Neck) Herniation

  • Neck pain
  • Pain radiating to arm/shoulder
  • Numbness in arm or hand
  • Weakness in arm

When to Seek Urgent Care

  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Progressive weakness in legs
  • Numbness in groin/inner thighs
  • These may indicate cauda equina syndrome (emergency)

Diagnosis3

  • Physical examination
  • MRI (best for visualizing disc)
  • CT scan
  • EMG (nerve function testing)

Treatment

Conservative (Most Improve)

  • Time (many heal within 6-12 weeks)
  • Activity modification
  • Physical therapy
  • Pain medications
  • Epidural injections

Surgery (If Needed)

  • Microdiscectomy (most common)
  • Usually for persistent leg pain, not back pain
  • Good success rates
  • Rarely needed urgently

Good News

  • Most herniations improve without surgery
  • Many herniations shrink over time
  • Large herniations often reabsorb better than small
  • Having a herniation doesn't mean lifelong pain

Prevention

  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Good lifting mechanics
  • Core strengthening
  • Stay active
  • Avoid prolonged sitting
