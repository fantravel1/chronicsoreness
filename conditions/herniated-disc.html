Herniated Disc

Understanding disc problems

What Is a Herniated Disc?

A herniated disc occurs when the soft inner portion of a spinal disc (nucleus pulposus) pushes through a tear in the tougher outer layer (annulus fibrosus). This can irritate nearby nerves, causing pain, numbness, or weakness. Also called a slipped, ruptured, or prolapsed disc, most improve without surgery.1

Disc Anatomy2

  • Discs act as shock absorbers
  • Tough outer layer (annulus fibrosus)
  • Soft gel-like center (nucleus pulposus)
  • Located between vertebrae

Types of Disc Problems

  • Bulging disc: disc extends beyond normal
  • Protrusion: nucleus pushes into annulus
  • Extrusion: nucleus breaks through annulus
  • Sequestration: fragment separates

Causes and Risk Factors

  • Age-related wear (most common)
  • Improper lifting
  • Sudden strain
  • Repetitive activities
  • Smoking
  • Obesity
  • Genetics

Symptoms3

Lumbar (Low Back)

  • Back pain
  • Leg pain (sciatica)
  • Numbness/tingling in leg or foot
  • Weakness in leg

Cervical (Neck)

  • Neck pain
  • Arm pain
  • Numbness/tingling in arm or hand
  • Weakness in arm

When to Seek Urgent Care

  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction
  • Progressive weakness
  • Saddle area numbness
  • Severe, worsening symptoms

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination
  • Neurological testing
  • MRI (best for soft tissue)
  • CT scan
  • EMG if needed

Treatment

Conservative (Most Effective)

  • Activity modification (not bed rest)
  • NSAIDs
  • Physical therapy
  • Muscle relaxants
  • Oral steroids (short course)
  • Epidural injections

Surgery

  • Microdiscectomy
  • For persistent severe symptoms
  • Progressive neurological deficit
  • Cauda equina (emergency)

Prognosis

  • Most improve within 6-12 weeks
  • Disc material often reabsorbs
  • Surgery successful when appropriate
  • Some become chronic
