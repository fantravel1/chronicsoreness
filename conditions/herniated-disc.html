Herniated Disc

Understanding disc bulges and herniations

Key Takeaways

  • Most herniated discs heal on their own within 6-12 weeks
  • Many people with herniations on MRI have no symptoms
  • Conservative treatment is successful for 80-90% of people
  • Surgery is rarely needed and reserved for specific situations

What Is a Herniated Disc?

A herniated disc occurs when the soft, gel-like center (nucleus pulposus) of a spinal disc pushes through a tear in the tougher outer layer (annulus fibrosus). This can irritate nearby nerves and cause pain, numbness, or weakness.1

Terminology

  • Bulging disc: Disc extends beyond its normal boundary but nucleus intact
  • Herniated/prolapsed disc: Nucleus pushes through annulus
  • Extruded disc: Nucleus pushes out but remains connected
  • Sequestered disc: Fragment breaks off completely
  • "Slipped disc" is a misnomer—discs don't actually slip
80-90%
of people with herniated discs improve without surgery2

Symptoms

Symptoms depend on location and whether nerves are affected:3

Lumbar (Lower Back) Herniation

  • Low back pain
  • Sciatica: Pain radiating into buttock and leg
  • Numbness or tingling in leg or foot
  • Weakness in leg or foot
  • Symptoms often worse with sitting, bending, coughing

Cervical (Neck) Herniation

  • Neck pain
  • Pain radiating into shoulder and arm
  • Numbness or tingling in arm or hand
  • Weakness in arm or hand

No Symptoms

Many herniations cause no symptoms at all and are found incidentally on imaging.

Emergency Symptoms (Cauda Equina)

Seek immediate care for: loss of bladder/bowel control, numbness in groin/inner thighs ("saddle anesthesia"), severe or progressive leg weakness, or sexual dysfunction. These may indicate cauda equina syndrome—a surgical emergency.

Causes and Risk Factors

How Herniations Occur

  • Often combination of disc degeneration and strain
  • May occur with lifting, twisting, or bending
  • Sometimes no clear trigger
  • Can develop gradually or suddenly

Risk Factors

  • Age: Most common ages 30-50
  • Occupation: Heavy lifting, repetitive bending
  • Genetics: Family history increases risk
  • Smoking: Reduces disc nutrition
  • Weight: Excess weight increases disc load
  • Sedentary lifestyle: Weak supporting muscles

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of symptoms and onset
  • Neurological examination
  • Straight leg raise test (for lumbar)
  • Spurling's test (for cervical)

Imaging

  • MRI: Best for visualizing disc and nerves
  • CT scan: Shows bone detail
  • X-rays: Don't show discs well but rule out other issues
  • Imaging often not needed initially unless red flags present

Imaging Perspective

Studies show disc herniations are common in people without pain. An MRI finding of a herniation does not prove it's causing your symptoms. Clinical correlation is essential.

Treatment

Most herniations improve with conservative treatment:4

Initial Management

  • Brief rest if needed (1-2 days maximum)
  • Stay active as tolerated
  • Pain medications (NSAIDs, acetaminophen)
  • Ice or heat
  • Avoid aggravating activities temporarily

Physical Therapy

  • Specific exercises (McKenzie method often helpful)
  • Core strengthening
  • Flexibility work
  • Education and body mechanics
  • Manual therapy

Medications

  • NSAIDs: Reduce inflammation and pain
  • Oral steroids: Short course for severe symptoms
  • Muscle relaxants: For muscle spasm
  • Neuropathic medications: Gabapentin if nerve pain

Injections

  • Epidural steroid injections
  • May provide temporary relief
  • Allow participation in PT
  • Not disease-modifying

Surgery

Consider if:

  • Conservative treatment fails after 6-12 weeks
  • Significant progressive weakness
  • Cauda equina syndrome (emergency)
  • Options: microdiscectomy, discectomy
  • Generally good outcomes for leg pain

Natural History

Good news about disc herniations:5

  • Most improve significantly within 6-12 weeks
  • Disc material can actually shrink over time (resorption)
  • Larger herniations may actually resorb more
  • Body mounts inflammatory response that breaks down disc material
  • Many people become pain-free even with persistent herniation on imaging

Prevention

  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly—especially core strengthening
  • Use proper lifting technique
  • Don't smoke
  • Good posture and ergonomics
  • Take breaks from prolonged sitting
