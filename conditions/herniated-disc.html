Herniated Disc

When disc material pushes outward

What Is a Herniated Disc?

A herniated disc occurs when the soft, gel-like center (nucleus pulposus) of a spinal disc pushes through a tear in the tough outer layer (annulus fibrosus). This can irritate or compress nearby nerves, causing pain, numbness, or weakness.1

Terminology

  • Bulging disc - Disc extends beyond normal boundaries but nucleus intact
  • Herniated/ruptured disc - Nucleus pushes through annulus
  • Sequestered disc - Fragment separates completely

Symptoms2

Lumbar (Low Back)

  • Low back pain
  • Sciatica (leg pain following nerve path)
  • Numbness or tingling in leg/foot
  • Weakness in leg

Cervical (Neck)

  • Neck pain
  • Arm pain
  • Numbness in arm/hand
  • Weakness in arm

Risk Factors

  • Age (most common 30-50)
  • Heavy lifting
  • Twisting while lifting
  • Smoking
  • Obesity
  • Sedentary lifestyle

Treatment3

Conservative (First Line)

  • Activity modification (not bed rest)
  • Physical therapy
  • Pain medications
  • Epidural steroid injections

Surgery

  • Considered if conservative treatment fails
  • Or if significant weakness/bowel/bladder issues
  • Discectomy (removing herniated portion)

Prognosis

Most herniated discs improve within 6-12 weeks with conservative care. The body often reabsorbs the herniated material over time.
