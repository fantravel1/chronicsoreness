Herniated Disc

When disc material pushes beyond its normal boundary

What Is a Herniated Disc?

A herniated disc occurs when the soft inner material (nucleus pulposus) of a spinal disc pushes through a tear in the outer ring (annulus fibrosus). This can irritate nearby nerves, causing pain, numbness, or weakness.1

Types

  • Bulging disc - Disc extends beyond normal space but inner material contained
  • Protrusion - Nucleus pushes into annulus but remains attached
  • Extrusion - Nucleus breaks through annulus
  • Sequestration - Fragment separates from main disc

Symptoms2

Lumbar (Low Back)

  • Sciatica - pain radiating down leg
  • Numbness/tingling in leg or foot
  • Leg weakness
  • Pain worse with sitting, bending

Cervical (Neck)

  • Pain radiating to shoulder/arm
  • Numbness in arm or hand
  • Arm weakness
  • Neck pain

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination (straight leg raise, reflexes)
  • MRI - gold standard for visualization
  • CT myelogram if MRI not possible
  • EMG for nerve function

Treatment3

Conservative (Most Improve)

  • Activity modification (not bed rest)
  • NSAIDs, acetaminophen
  • Short-term muscle relaxants
  • Physical therapy
  • Epidural steroid injections

Surgery (If Conservative Fails)

  • Microdiscectomy
  • Indicated for: progressive weakness, cauda equina syndrome, persistent severe symptoms

Prognosis

Most herniated discs improve within 6-12 weeks with conservative treatment. Many herniations shrink over time (resorption).
