Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome

Nerve compression at the ankle

What Is Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome?

Tarsal tunnel syndrome occurs when the tibial nerve is compressed as it travels through the tarsal tunnel on the inside of the ankle. It's similar to carpal tunnel syndrome in the wrist. The compression causes pain, numbness, and tingling in the foot.1

Anatomy2

  • Tarsal tunnel: passageway inside ankle
  • Contains tibial nerve, blood vessels, tendons
  • Covered by flexor retinaculum
  • Nerve branches to sole of foot

Causes

  • Flat feet (overpronation)
  • Ankle sprains or injuries
  • Cysts or masses in tunnel
  • Varicose veins
  • Diabetes
  • Arthritis
  • Swelling from systemic diseases
  • Sometimes unknown cause

Symptoms3

  • Pain along inner ankle and foot sole
  • Burning or tingling sensation
  • Numbness in sole of foot
  • Electric shock-like sensations
  • Symptoms worsen with standing/walking
  • May be worse at night
  • Relief with rest

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination
  • Tinel's sign (tapping nerve causes symptoms)
  • Nerve conduction studies/EMG
  • MRI to find cause
  • Ultrasound

Treatment

Conservative

  • Rest and activity modification
  • Orthotics (especially for flat feet)
  • Anti-inflammatory medications
  • Physical therapy
  • Bracing or immobilization
  • Corticosteroid injections

Surgical

  • Tarsal tunnel release
  • When conservative treatment fails
  • Releases flexor retinaculum
  • Addresses any masses or cysts

Self-Care

  • Supportive footwear
  • Avoid prolonged standing
  • Ice for acute symptoms
  • Elevate feet when resting
  • Weight management

Prognosis

  • Many improve with conservative care
  • Surgery success varies
  • Early treatment usually better outcomes
  • Address underlying causes
