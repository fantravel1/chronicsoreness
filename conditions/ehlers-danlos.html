Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome

Connective tissue disorders

What Is Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome?

Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS) is a group of inherited connective tissue disorders affecting collagen. Collagen provides structure throughout the body, so EDS can affect joints, skin, blood vessels, and other organs. Joint hypermobility and chronic pain are common features.1

Types of EDS2

Hypermobile EDS (hEDS)

  • Most common type
  • Joint hypermobility with chronic pain
  • No genetic test available yet
  • Diagnosed by clinical criteria

Classical EDS

  • Highly stretchy, fragile skin
  • Wide, atrophic scars
  • Joint hypermobility

Vascular EDS

  • Most serious type
  • Fragile blood vessels and organs
  • Requires genetic testing
  • Life-threatening complications possible

Common Symptoms

Joint Symptoms

  • Hypermobile ("double-jointed") joints
  • Frequent joint dislocations or subluxations
  • Chronic joint pain
  • Early osteoarthritis

Other Symptoms

  • Soft, velvety, stretchy skin
  • Easy bruising
  • Chronic fatigue
  • Digestive problems
  • Dysautonomia (POTS common)
  • Anxiety

Diagnosis3

  • Clinical evaluation by knowledgeable provider
  • Beighton score for hypermobility
  • Family history
  • Genetic testing (for most types except hEDS)
  • Exclusion of other conditions

Managing EDS

Physical Approaches

  • Physical therapy (low-impact, stabilization focus)
  • Strengthening muscles to support joints
  • Bracing for unstable joints
  • Avoiding hyperextension
  • Pacing activities

Pain Management

  • Multimodal approach often needed
  • Low-impact exercise
  • Pain psychology
  • Careful medication use
  • Addressing sleep and fatigue

What to Avoid

  • High-impact activities
  • Stretching already hypermobile joints
  • Heavy lifting without proper form
  • Contact sports (depends on type)

Living with EDS

  • Find knowledgeable healthcare providers
  • Connect with EDS community for support
  • Learn your limits and pace yourself
  • Advocate for your needs
  • Focus on quality of life
