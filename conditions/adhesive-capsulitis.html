Frozen Shoulder

Adhesive capsulitis: when your shoulder locks up

What Is Frozen Shoulder?

Frozen shoulder, medically known as adhesive capsulitis, is a condition where the shoulder joint becomes extremely stiff and painful. The capsule surrounding the shoulder joint thickens and tightens, restricting movement. It typically develops gradually, worsens over time, and eventually resolvesâ€”though this process can take 1-3 years.1

Who Gets It

  • Most common in people 40-60 years old
  • Women more often than men
  • People with diabetes (2-4x higher risk)
  • After shoulder injury or surgery
  • After prolonged immobilization
  • Associated with thyroid disorders

The Three Stages2

Stage 1: Freezing (2-9 months)

  • Pain gradually increases
  • Movement becomes more limited
  • Pain often worse at night
  • Most painful stage

Stage 2: Frozen (4-12 months)

  • Pain may decrease somewhat
  • Stiffness remains or worsens
  • Daily activities very difficult
  • Range of motion severely limited

Stage 3: Thawing (5-24 months)

  • Movement gradually returns
  • Pain continues to decrease
  • Recovery varies in completeness

Symptoms

  • Dull or aching shoulder pain
  • Pain over outer shoulder and sometimes upper arm
  • Difficulty reaching behind back
  • Can't raise arm overhead
  • Sleep disruption from pain
  • Both active and passive motion limited

Treatment Options3

Conservative Treatment

  • Physical therapy (cornerstone of treatment)
  • Gentle stretching exercises
  • Heat before exercises
  • NSAIDs for pain and inflammation
  • Corticosteroid injections

Procedures

  • Hydrodilatation (injection to stretch capsule)
  • Manipulation under anesthesia
  • Arthroscopic capsular release

Physical Therapy Exercises

  • Pendulum exercises
  • Cross-body stretch
  • Towel stretch
  • Finger wall walks
  • External rotation stretches

What to Know

  • Most cases resolve but it takes time
  • Aggressive stretching can make it worse
  • Stay within pain tolerance
  • Consistency more important than intensity
  • May affect the other shoulder later (10-20% chance)
