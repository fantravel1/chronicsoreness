Adhesive Capsulitis (Frozen Shoulder)

Understanding this frustrating condition that causes progressive shoulder stiffness and pain

What Is Adhesive Capsulitis?

Adhesive capsulitis, commonly known as frozen shoulder, is a condition characterized by stiffness, pain, and limited range of motion in the shoulder joint. It occurs when the connective tissue surrounding the shoulder joint (the capsule) becomes inflamed and thickened, eventually forming adhesions that restrict movement.1

The condition typically develops gradually, worsens over time, and then slowly resolvesâ€”but this natural course can take 1-3 years without treatment. It affects about 2-5% of the population, most commonly people between ages 40 and 60.2

Key Characteristics

  • Progressive loss of both active and passive shoulder motion
  • Pain, especially at night and at end ranges of motion
  • Characteristic pattern: external rotation affected most
  • Usually affects only one shoulder (can affect the other later)
  • Typically self-limiting but prolonged

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary (Idiopathic) Frozen Shoulder

Develops without a clear cause. More common in:

  • Women - 70% of cases
  • Age 40-60
  • People with diabetes - 10-20% of diabetics develop frozen shoulder3
  • Thyroid disorders
  • Parkinson's disease
  • Cardiac disease

Secondary Frozen Shoulder

Develops after:

  • Shoulder injury or surgery
  • Prolonged immobilization
  • Rotator cuff injury
  • Stroke
  • Breast cancer treatment

The Three Stages

Frozen shoulder progresses through characteristic stages:

Stage 1: Freezing (2-9 months)

  • Gradual onset of pain
  • Pain often worse at night
  • Progressively increasing stiffness
  • Pain may be severe
  • Range of motion starts to decline

Stage 2: Frozen (4-12 months)

  • Pain may begin to decrease
  • Stiffness remains or worsens
  • Daily activities significantly affected
  • Maximum restriction of movement

Stage 3: Thawing (5-24 months)

  • Gradual improvement in range of motion
  • Pain continues to decrease
  • Slow but steady recovery
  • Some people don't regain full motion

Total Duration

Without treatment, frozen shoulder typically lasts 1-3 years. Treatment may shorten this timeline. However, some studies suggest 20-50% of people have some degree of persistent limitation, though usually minor and not functionally significant.4

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical:

  • History - Progressive pain and stiffness pattern
  • Physical exam - Reduced active AND passive motion (unlike rotator cuff problems)
  • Characteristic pattern - External rotation limited more than other movements
  • X-rays - Usually normal; rule out other conditions
  • MRI - May show capsule thickening; often not needed

Treatment Options

Conservative Management

First-line treatment for most cases:

  • Physical therapy - Gentle stretching within pain tolerance5
  • Heat before exercise - Improves tissue flexibility
  • NSAIDs - For pain and inflammation
  • Acetaminophen - For pain relief
  • Home exercises - Regular stretching program

Corticosteroid Injections

  • Injected into the joint or subacromial space
  • Most helpful in the painful freezing stage
  • Provide temporary relief (weeks to months)
  • May allow more effective physical therapy
  • Multiple injections may be needed

Hydrodilatation (Distension Arthrography)

  • Injection of sterile fluid to stretch the capsule
  • Often combined with corticosteroid
  • Performed under imaging guidance
  • May speed recovery

Manipulation Under Anesthesia

  • Shoulder is forcibly moved while patient is asleep
  • Breaks up adhesions
  • Reserved for cases not responding to conservative treatment
  • Small risk of fracture or other complications

Surgical Release

  • Arthroscopic capsular release
  • Cuts through tight capsule tissue
  • Reserved for refractory cases
  • Followed by intensive physical therapy

Exercises for Frozen Shoulder

Regular gentle stretching is essential:6

Pendulum Stretch

  • Lean forward, supporting yourself with unaffected arm
  • Let affected arm hang down
  • Swing in small circles (10 in each direction)
  • Progress to larger circles as tolerated

Towel Stretch

  • Hold towel behind back (one hand up, one down)
  • Use unaffected arm to gently pull affected arm up
  • Hold 15-30 seconds
  • Repeat several times daily

Cross-Body Reach

  • Use unaffected arm to lift affected arm at elbow
  • Bring across body toward opposite shoulder
  • Hold 15-30 seconds
  • Gentle stretch, not pain

Finger Walk

  • Face a wall at arm's length
  • "Walk" fingers up the wall
  • Go as high as comfortable
  • Lower and repeat

Exercise Caution

Aggressive stretching can worsen frozen shoulder, especially in the painful freezing stage. Work within your pain tolerance. "No pain, no gain" does NOT apply here. If exercises significantly increase pain, reduce intensity and consult a physical therapist.

Living with Frozen Shoulder

  • Be patient - Recovery takes time even with treatment
  • Modify activities - Find different ways to do tasks
  • Sleep positioning - Use pillows to support arm; sleep on unaffected side
  • Stay consistent - Regular gentle exercise, even when frustrating
  • Manage expectations - Progress is often slow and non-linear
  • Address underlying conditions - Especially diabetes control
