Chiari Malformation

When brain tissue extends into the spinal canal

What Is Chiari Malformation?

Chiari malformation occurs when part of the brain (cerebellar tonsils) extends through the opening at the base of the skull into the spinal canal. This can put pressure on the brain and spinal cord, affecting fluid flow. Many people have no symptoms, but others develop significant problems.1

Types2

Type I (Most Common)

  • Cerebellar tonsils extend 5mm+ below skull base
  • Often develops as skull grows
  • Usually diagnosed in adulthood
  • May be asymptomatic

Type II (Arnold-Chiari)

  • More brain tissue involved
  • Associated with spina bifida
  • Present from birth

Symptoms

Classic Symptoms

  • Headache at back of head (worse with coughing, straining)
  • Neck pain
  • Balance problems
  • Numbness or tingling in hands
  • Dizziness

Other Possible Symptoms

  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Speech problems
  • Sleep apnea
  • Ringing in ears
  • Weakness
  • Scoliosis

Diagnosis3

  • MRI of brain and cervical spine
  • May be found incidentally on imaging
  • CSF flow studies
  • Additional testing as needed

Associated Conditions

  • Syringomyelia (fluid-filled cavity in spinal cord)
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (higher prevalence)
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Tethered cord

Treatment

Monitoring

  • If asymptomatic, may just need monitoring
  • Many people never need surgery

Surgery

  • Decompression surgery if symptomatic
  • Creates more space at skull base
  • May help symptoms and prevent progression
  • Not always fully curative

Symptom Management

  • Pain medications
  • Avoiding activities that worsen symptoms
  • Physical therapy

Living with Chiari

  • Avoid activities that increase pressure (heavy lifting, straining)
  • Find knowledgeable specialists
  • Some activities may need modification
  • Connect with support groups
  • Regular monitoring if watching and waiting
