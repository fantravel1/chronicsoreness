Diabetic Neuropathy

Understanding nerve damage caused by diabetes and strategies for prevention and management

What Is Diabetic Neuropathy?

Diabetic neuropathy is nerve damage that occurs as a complication of diabetes. High blood sugar (glucose) levels can injure nerve fibers throughout the body, but diabetic neuropathy most often affects nerves in the legs and feet.1

Approximately 50% of people with diabetes eventually develop some form of neuropathy. It's one of the most common complications of diabetes and a leading cause of disability.2

How High Blood Sugar Damages Nerves

Prolonged high blood glucose levels damage nerves through several mechanisms:

  • Direct damage to nerve cells from glucose toxicity
  • Damage to small blood vessels that supply nerves
  • Inflammation of nerve tissue
  • Accumulation of harmful metabolic byproducts
  • Oxidative stress from free radicals

Types of Diabetic Neuropathy

There are four main types of diabetic neuropathy, each affecting different nerve groups:

Peripheral Neuropathy

The most common type, affecting feet and legs first, then hands and arms:

  • Numbness or reduced ability to feel pain or temperature changes
  • Tingling or burning sensations
  • Sharp, jabbing pains that may worsen at night
  • Extreme sensitivity to light touch
  • Muscle weakness
  • Loss of balance and coordination
  • Serious foot problems including ulcers and infections

Autonomic Neuropathy

Affects the autonomic nervous system, which controls internal organs:3

  • Digestive problems - Gastroparesis, constipation, diarrhea
  • Bladder dysfunction - Urinary retention or incontinence
  • Sexual dysfunction - Erectile dysfunction, vaginal dryness
  • Heart rate changes - Inability to adjust heart rate with activity
  • Blood pressure problems - Orthostatic hypotension (dizziness when standing)
  • Sweating abnormalities - Excessive or reduced sweating
  • Hypoglycemia unawareness - Inability to sense low blood sugar

Proximal Neuropathy (Diabetic Amyotrophy)

Affects nerves in the thighs, hips, buttocks, or legs:

  • Severe pain in hip, thigh, or buttock
  • Weakness in thigh muscles
  • Difficulty rising from sitting position
  • Usually affects one side of the body
  • May improve over months to years

Focal Neuropathy (Mononeuropathy)

Affects specific individual nerves:

  • Carpal tunnel syndrome (most common focal neuropathy in diabetes)
  • Difficulty focusing eyes or double vision
  • Paralysis on one side of the face (Bell's palsy)
  • Pain in specific areas like shin, foot, or thigh

When to Seek Medical Care

Contact your healthcare provider if you experience: numbness or tingling in feet or hands; burning pain; wounds or sores that don't heal; dizziness when standing; digestive problems; or changes in urination or sexual function. Early treatment can prevent progression.

Risk Factors

Several factors increase the likelihood of developing diabetic neuropathy:

  • Poor blood sugar control - The most significant risk factor
  • Duration of diabetes - Risk increases the longer you have diabetes
  • Kidney disease - Damages nerves and releases toxins
  • Obesity - BMI over 24 increases risk
  • Smoking - Damages blood vessels and nerves
  • High blood pressure - Contributes to vessel damage
  • High cholesterol - Accelerates nerve damage

Prevention

The best way to prevent diabetic neuropathy is to maintain good blood sugar control:4

  • Monitor blood glucose regularly and maintain target levels
  • Take diabetes medications as prescribed
  • Follow a healthy eating plan low in processed foods
  • Exercise regularly - At least 150 minutes of moderate activity weekly
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Don't smoke - Quit if you currently smoke
  • Control blood pressure and cholesterol
  • Limit alcohol - Excessive alcohol worsens neuropathy

Treatment Options

While nerve damage can't be reversed, treatment focuses on slowing progression, managing pain, and treating complications.

Blood Sugar Management

Maintaining target blood sugar levels is the foundation of treatment:

  • Work with your healthcare team to optimize diabetes management
  • Consider intensive insulin therapy if appropriate
  • Regular HbA1c testing (goal usually below 7%)

Pain Management

Several medications help manage neuropathic pain:5

  • Anticonvulsants - Pregabalin (Lyrica), gabapentin (Neurontin)
  • Antidepressants - Duloxetine (Cymbalta), amitriptyline, venlafaxine
  • Topical treatments - Capsaicin cream, lidocaine patches
  • Opioids - Reserved for severe cases; tapentadol and tramadol may be options

Treatment for Specific Complications

  • Gastroparesis - Dietary modifications, medications like metoclopramide
  • Urinary problems - Timed voiding, medications, catheterization if needed
  • Orthostatic hypotension - Compression stockings, increased salt intake, medications
  • Sexual dysfunction - Medications, devices, or counseling

Complementary Approaches

  • Physical therapy - Exercises to maintain strength and balance
  • Acupuncture - May provide pain relief for some
  • Alpha-lipoic acid - Antioxidant supplement showing promise in studies6
  • TENS therapy - Electrical nerve stimulation for pain relief

Foot Care

Proper foot care is critical for people with diabetic neuropathy:

  • Inspect feet daily for cuts, blisters, redness, or swelling
  • Wash feet daily in lukewarm water and dry thoroughly
  • Moisturize feet but not between toes
  • Trim nails carefully, straight across
  • Wear comfortable, well-fitting shoes and clean, dry socks
  • Never walk barefoot, even indoors
  • Have feet examined by a healthcare provider at least annually

Living with Diabetic Neuropathy

Managing diabetic neuropathy is an ongoing process. Work closely with your healthcare team, including your primary care provider, endocrinologist, neurologist, and podiatrist. With proper care, many people with diabetic neuropathy maintain good quality of life and prevent serious complications like foot ulcers and amputations.
