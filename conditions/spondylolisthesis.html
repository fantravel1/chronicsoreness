Spondylolisthesis

When vertebrae slip out of place

What Is Spondylolisthesis?

Spondylolisthesis occurs when one vertebra slips forward over the bone below it. It most commonly affects the lower back. Many people with spondylolisthesis have no symptoms, while others develop back pain and leg symptoms. The severity depends on how much slippage has occurred.1

Types2

Isthmic

  • Caused by stress fracture in pars interarticularis
  • Common in young athletes
  • Often develops in adolescence

Degenerative

  • Age-related wear and tear
  • More common in adults over 50
  • Often at L4-L5 level

Other Types

  • Congenital (born with it)
  • Traumatic (from injury)
  • Pathological (from disease)

Grades of Slippage

  • Grade I: Less than 25% slippage
  • Grade II: 25-50% slippage
  • Grade III: 50-75% slippage
  • Grade IV: 75-100% slippage
  • Grade V: Complete slip-off (spondyloptosis)

Symptoms

  • Low back pain
  • Muscle tightness (hamstrings)
  • Pain with activity, especially extension
  • Leg pain or weakness (if nerves affected)
  • Some people have no symptoms

Diagnosis3

  • X-rays (including flexion/extension views)
  • CT scan for bone detail
  • MRI if nerve symptoms present

Treatment

Conservative

  • Physical therapy
  • Core strengthening (especially flexion-based)
  • Activity modification
  • Bracing (sometimes)
  • Pain management

Surgery

  • Usually for high-grade slips or failed conservative treatment
  • Spinal fusion most common
  • Decompression if nerves compressed

Exercise Considerations

  • Avoid hyperextension exercises
  • Focus on core stability
  • Flexion-based exercises often better tolerated
  • Swimming often well-tolerated

Prognosis

  • Most low-grade slips don't progress
  • Many manage well without surgery
  • Children may need monitoring during growth
