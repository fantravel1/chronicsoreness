Failed Back Surgery Syndrome

Persistent pain after spine surgery

What Is Failed Back Surgery Syndrome?

Failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS), also called post-laminectomy syndrome, refers to persistent or recurrent pain after spinal surgery. Despite the name, it doesn't necessarily mean the surgery "failed" - it's a complex condition with multiple possible causes. Up to 40% of spinal surgery patients may develop FBSS.1

Symptoms

  • Persistent back pain after surgery
  • Leg pain (radiculopathy)
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Weakness
  • Pain may differ from pre-surgery pain
  • Limited mobility

Possible Causes2

  • Scar tissue (epidural fibrosis)
  • Recurrent disc herniation
  • Spinal stenosis progression
  • Adjacent segment disease
  • Hardware issues
  • Nerve damage
  • Incorrect level operated
  • Infection (rare)
  • Central sensitization

Evaluation

  • Detailed history of symptoms and surgery
  • Physical examination
  • Updated imaging (MRI with contrast)
  • Diagnostic injections
  • Psychological evaluation

Treatment Options3

Conservative

  • Physical therapy
  • Pain medications
  • Injections (epidural, facet, nerve blocks)
  • Pain psychology

Neuromodulation

  • Spinal cord stimulation (strong evidence)
  • Dorsal root ganglion stimulation
  • Intrathecal drug delivery

Additional Surgery

  • May be appropriate if clear surgical cause found
  • Success rates lower with repeat surgery
  • Careful patient selection important

Living with FBSS

  • Multidisciplinary approach works best
  • Focus on function, not just pain relief
  • Physical and psychological strategies combined
  • Support groups can help
