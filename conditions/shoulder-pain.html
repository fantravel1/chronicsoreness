Chronic Shoulder Pain

Understanding common causes and evidence-based treatment approaches

Key Takeaways

  • Shoulder pain is the third most common musculoskeletal complaint
  • Most shoulder conditions respond well to conservative treatment
  • Physical therapy is first-line treatment for most shoulder problems
  • Understanding your specific condition guides effective treatment

Understanding Shoulder Pain

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body, which also makes it vulnerable to injury and pain. Shoulder problems can significantly impact daily activities like dressing, reaching, and sleeping.1

18-26%
of adults experience shoulder pain at any given time2

Shoulder Anatomy

Key structures involved in shoulder pain:

  • Rotator cuff: Four muscles that stabilize and move the shoulder
  • Bursa: Fluid-filled sacs that reduce friction
  • Labrum: Cartilage ring that deepens the shoulder socket
  • Joint capsule: Connective tissue surrounding the joint
  • Tendons: Connect muscles to bones

Common Shoulder Conditions

Rotator Cuff Problems

The most common cause of shoulder pain, especially in adults over 40:3

  • Tendinitis: Inflammation of rotator cuff tendons
  • Tendinopathy: Degenerative changes in tendons
  • Partial tears: Incomplete tendon tears
  • Full-thickness tears: Complete tendon tears

Symptoms:

  • Pain with overhead activities
  • Night pain, especially lying on affected side
  • Weakness with lifting or rotating arm
  • Pain reaching behind back

Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis)

A condition where the joint capsule becomes thickened and tight:4

  • More common in women, ages 40-60
  • Higher risk with diabetes, thyroid disorders
  • Can follow injury or surgery
  • Sometimes occurs without clear cause

Three stages:

  1. Freezing (2-9 months): Increasing pain and stiffness
  2. Frozen (4-12 months): Less pain but significant stiffness
  3. Thawing (5-24 months): Gradual return of movement

Shoulder Impingement Syndrome

Compression of rotator cuff tendons and bursa:5

  • Pain with overhead movements
  • Painful arc of movement (60-120 degrees)
  • Often related to posture and muscle imbalances

Shoulder Arthritis

Degenerative changes in the shoulder joint:

  • Osteoarthritis (wear and tear)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (inflammatory)
  • Post-traumatic arthritis (following injury)

Referred Pain

Pain felt in the shoulder but originating elsewhere:

  • Neck problems (cervical radiculopathy)
  • Heart conditions (especially left shoulder)
  • Gallbladder (right shoulder)
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome

When to Seek Immediate Care

  • Shoulder pain with chest pain or shortness of breath
  • Sudden severe pain after trauma
  • Visible deformity
  • Inability to move the arm at all
  • Signs of infection (fever, redness, warmth)

Diagnosis

Shoulder problems are typically diagnosed through:6

Physical Examination

  • Range of motion testing
  • Strength testing
  • Special tests for specific conditions
  • Neck examination to rule out referred pain

Imaging

  • X-rays: Show bone problems, arthritis, calcification
  • Ultrasound: Good for rotator cuff, dynamic assessment
  • MRI: Detailed soft tissue evaluation

Imaging Findings and Symptoms

Rotator cuff tears and other abnormalities are common on imaging even in people without pain. Studies show many asymptomatic adults have rotator cuff tears.7 Imaging findings should be interpreted alongside symptoms and examination.

Conservative Treatment

Most shoulder conditions respond well to conservative care:8

Physical Therapy

The cornerstone of shoulder treatment:

  • Rotator cuff strengthening
  • Scapular stabilization exercises
  • Stretching and range of motion
  • Posture correction
  • Manual therapy

Activity Modification

  • Avoid aggravating activities temporarily
  • Modify overhead movements
  • Use proper lifting techniques
  • Gradual return to activities

Pain Management

  • Ice for acute inflammation
  • Heat for chronic stiffness
  • NSAIDs for pain and inflammation
  • Topical treatments

Corticosteroid Injections

  • Can provide significant short-term relief
  • Useful as bridge to allow therapy to work
  • Limited number recommended
  • May weaken tendons with repeated use

Exercises for Shoulder Health

Always consult a healthcare provider before starting exercises. General shoulder-friendly movements include:

Pendulum Exercise

  1. Lean forward, supporting yourself with unaffected arm
  2. Let affected arm hang down
  3. Gently swing arm in small circles
  4. Perform 1-2 minutes, several times daily

Wall Slides

  1. Stand with back against wall
  2. Arms at sides, elbows bent 90 degrees
  3. Slide arms up the wall as far as comfortable
  4. Slowly lower and repeat 10-15 times

External Rotation with Band

  1. Hold resistance band with elbows at sides, bent 90 degrees
  2. Rotate forearms outward, keeping elbows in
  3. Slowly return to start
  4. Perform 2-3 sets of 10-15 repetitions

Scapular Squeezes

  1. Sit or stand with good posture
  2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
  3. Hold 5 seconds
  4. Repeat 10-15 times

Surgical Treatment

Surgery may be considered when conservative treatment fails:9

Common Procedures

  • Arthroscopic surgery: Minimally invasive for many conditions
  • Rotator cuff repair: Reattaching torn tendons
  • Subacromial decompression: Creating more space for tendons
  • Shoulder replacement: For severe arthritis
  • Manipulation under anesthesia: For frozen shoulder

Surgery Considerations

  • Many shoulder conditions improve without surgery
  • Age and activity level influence decisions
  • Rehabilitation is essential after surgery
  • Full recovery may take 6-12 months

Prevention and Self-Care

  • Maintain good posture
  • Strengthen rotator cuff and scapular muscles
  • Warm up before activities
  • Avoid repetitive overhead movements
  • Take breaks during repetitive tasks
  • Sleep in positions that do not stress shoulder
  • Address neck and upper back stiffness
