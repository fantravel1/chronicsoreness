Cervical Radiculopathy

When a neck nerve is compressed

What Is Cervical Radiculopathy?

Cervical radiculopathy occurs when a nerve root in the neck is compressed or irritated. This causes pain, numbness, tingling, or weakness that radiates from the neck into the shoulder, arm, or hand following the path of the affected nerve.1

Causes

  • Herniated disc (most common in younger people)
  • Degenerative changes (most common in older adults)
  • Bone spurs (osteophytes)
  • Spinal stenosis
  • Trauma or injury

Symptoms by Nerve Level2

  • C5 - Shoulder pain, deltoid weakness
  • C6 - Thumb/index finger numbness, bicep weakness
  • C7 - Middle finger numbness, tricep weakness (most common)
  • C8 - Ring/little finger numbness, grip weakness

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination
  • Spurling's test
  • MRI (best for soft tissue)
  • EMG/nerve conduction (if needed)

Treatment3

Conservative (First Line)

  • Physical therapy
  • Activity modification
  • NSAIDs or oral steroids
  • Cervical traction
  • Epidural steroid injections

Surgery

  • When conservative fails or severe weakness
  • Anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF)
  • Artificial disc replacement

Prognosis

Most cases improve with conservative treatment within 4-6 weeks. About 80-90% recover without surgery.
