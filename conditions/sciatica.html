Sciatica

Understanding sciatic nerve pain and evidence-based treatment approaches

Key Takeaways

  • Sciatica refers to pain radiating along the sciatic nerve, typically caused by nerve compression
  • Most cases improve within 4-6 weeks with conservative treatment
  • Staying active (within comfort) promotes faster recovery than bed rest
  • Surgery is rarely needed but highly effective when appropriate

Understanding Sciatica

Sciatica describes pain that radiates along the path of the sciatic nerve, which branches from your lower back through your hips and buttocks and down each leg.1 The term "sciatica" is actually a symptom rather than a diagnosis—it indicates nerve root irritation, most commonly in the lower lumbar spine.

lifetime probability of experiencing sciatica2

The Sciatic Nerve

The sciatic nerve is the largest and longest nerve in the body:

  • Forms from nerve roots L4, L5, S1, S2, and S3
  • About the thickness of a finger at its widest point
  • Runs from the lower back, through the buttock, down the back of the thigh
  • Branches into smaller nerves that continue into the lower leg and foot
  • Controls sensation and muscle function in the leg

Common Causes

Several conditions can compress or irritate the sciatic nerve:3

Herniated or Bulging Disc

The most common cause of sciatica (90% of cases):

  • Disc material pushes against nerve roots
  • Most often affects L4-L5 or L5-S1 levels
  • Chemical irritation from disc material also contributes to pain

Spinal Stenosis

Narrowing of the spinal canal, common in older adults:

  • Bone spurs and thickened ligaments reduce space for nerves
  • Symptoms often worse with walking, relieved by sitting or bending forward
  • Usually develops gradually over time

Degenerative Disc Disease

Age-related wear and tear on spinal discs:

  • Disc height decreases, potentially narrowing nerve pathways
  • May lead to instability and bone spur formation

Spondylolisthesis

Slippage of one vertebra over another:

  • Can compress nerve roots exiting the spine
  • May be developmental or degenerative

Piriformis Syndrome

Compression by the piriformis muscle in the buttock:

  • The sciatic nerve runs under (or through) this muscle
  • Muscle spasm or tightness can irritate the nerve
  • May be more common in women
  • Often related to sitting, running, or hip problems

Pregnancy and Sciatica

Sciatica is common during pregnancy due to weight gain, postural changes, and the baby's position potentially pressing on the sciatic nerve. The hormone relaxin also loosens ligaments, which can contribute to spine instability. Most pregnancy-related sciatica resolves after delivery.4

Symptoms

Classic sciatica symptoms include:1,5

Pain Characteristics

  • Pain radiating from lower back through buttock and down the leg
  • Usually affects one side only
  • May be sharp, burning, or electric shock-like
  • Often worse when sitting, coughing, or sneezing
  • May be accompanied by low back pain (but leg pain is usually worse)

Neurological Symptoms

  • Numbness: Decreased sensation in leg or foot
  • Tingling: "Pins and needles" sensations
  • Weakness: Difficulty lifting foot (foot drop) or rising on toes

Pain Location by Nerve Root

  • L4: Pain to inner lower leg and foot, difficulty with leg extension
  • L5: Pain to top of foot and big toe, difficulty lifting foot
  • S1: Pain to outer foot and small toes, difficulty with toe push-off

Seek Immediate Medical Care If You Experience:

  • Sudden severe weakness in the leg
  • Numbness in the groin or genital area (saddle anesthesia)
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Symptoms following significant trauma

These may indicate cauda equina syndrome, a medical emergency requiring immediate surgery.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on history and physical examination:6

Physical Examination

  • Straight leg raise test: Raising the leg while lying down—positive if it reproduces leg pain
  • Neurological exam: Testing reflexes, sensation, and muscle strength
  • Gait assessment: Observing walking patterns
  • Range of motion: Evaluating spine flexibility

Imaging Studies

Not usually needed initially, but may include:

  • MRI: Best for visualizing soft tissue, disc herniations, and nerve compression
  • CT scan: Detailed view of bone structures
  • X-rays: Can show bone abnormalities, alignment, instability

Imaging is typically reserved for:

  • Symptoms lasting more than 6 weeks
  • Progressive neurological deficits
  • Red flags suggesting serious pathology
  • Consideration of interventional procedures or surgery

Conservative Treatment

Most sciatica improves within 4-6 weeks with conservative care. Guidelines recommend starting with non-invasive treatments.7

Stay Active

Perhaps counterintuitively, staying moderately active promotes faster recovery:8

  • Bed rest is not recommended and may slow recovery
  • Continue normal activities as much as pain allows
  • Gentle walking is often helpful
  • Avoid activities that significantly worsen symptoms

Heat and Cold

  • Ice may help in the first 48-72 hours (reduce inflammation)
  • Heat often feels better after the acute phase
  • Use whichever provides more relief
  • Apply for 15-20 minutes at a time

Medications

Over-the-counter options:

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) for pain and inflammation
  • Acetaminophen for pain without anti-inflammatory effect

Prescription options (when needed):

  • Muscle relaxants for associated muscle spasm
  • Gabapentinoids for nerve pain
  • Short-term oral steroids for severe acute cases
  • Opioids rarely recommended (short-term only if needed)

Physical Therapy

Physical therapy is highly effective for sciatica:9

  • Exercises to improve flexibility and strength
  • McKenzie method (extension exercises)
  • Core stabilization training
  • Neural mobilization techniques
  • Manual therapy
  • Education on body mechanics

Exercises for Sciatica

Specific exercises can help relieve sciatic pain. Always check with a healthcare provider before starting an exercise program.

Knee-to-Chest Stretch

  1. Lie on your back with knees bent
  2. Bring one knee toward your chest, holding behind the thigh
  3. Keep the other foot flat on the floor
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 2-3 times each side

Piriformis Stretch

  1. Lie on your back, cross affected leg over opposite knee
  2. Pull the uncrossed leg toward your chest
  3. Feel the stretch in the buttock of the crossed leg
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 2-3 times

Sciatic Nerve Glide

  1. Sit on a chair, straighten one leg
  2. Flex the foot (toes toward you) while extending the leg
  3. Point the foot while bending the knee slightly
  4. Repeat 10-15 times as a gentle pumping motion

Press-Up (McKenzie Extension)

  1. Lie face down, hands under shoulders
  2. Press up, straightening arms while keeping hips on floor
  3. Hold briefly, then lower back down
  4. Repeat 10 times, several times daily

Exercise Tip

If an exercise significantly increases your leg pain, stop and try a different approach. Some pain relief should occur with proper exercises. If symptoms travel further down the leg ("peripheralization"), the exercise may not be appropriate.

Interventional Treatments

When conservative treatment isn't sufficient, interventional options include:10

Epidural Steroid Injections

  • Corticosteroid injected near the affected nerve root
  • May provide significant relief for weeks to months
  • Often used as a bridge to allow physical therapy to work
  • Limited number of injections recommended per year
  • Evidence supports short-term benefit; long-term efficacy less clear

Selective Nerve Root Blocks

  • More targeted injection at a specific nerve root
  • Can be diagnostic (confirming which nerve is affected) and therapeutic

Surgical Treatment

Surgery is considered when:11

  • Severe or progressive neurological deficits
  • Cauda equina syndrome (emergency)
  • Persistent severe pain despite 6-12 weeks of conservative treatment
  • Imaging findings correlate with symptoms

Common Surgical Procedures

Microdiscectomy:

  • Most common surgery for disc-related sciatica
  • Removes portion of disc pressing on the nerve
  • Minimally invasive with small incision
  • Success rate approximately 85-90%
  • Most patients return to normal activities within 4-6 weeks

Laminectomy:

  • Removes part of the vertebral bone (lamina)
  • Creates more space for nerves
  • Often used for spinal stenosis

Surgery Outcomes

Research shows that patients with severe sciatica who have surgery often experience faster relief than those who continue conservative treatment, but at 1-2 years outcomes are often similar.11

Prevention and Long-Term Management

  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce spine stress
  • Use proper lifting techniques (bend at knees, not waist)
  • Stay physically active with regular exercise
  • Strengthen core muscles to support the spine
  • Practice good posture, especially when sitting for long periods
  • Take breaks from prolonged sitting
  • Use ergonomic furniture and workstations
  • Quit smoking (smoking impairs disc health)
