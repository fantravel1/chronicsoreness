Sciatica

Understanding sciatic nerve pain

Key Takeaways

  • Sciatica is pain along the sciatic nerve, typically radiating down the leg
  • Most cases improve within weeks to months with conservative treatment
  • Staying active is usually better than bed rest
  • Surgery is rarely needed and reserved for specific situations

What Is Sciatica?

Sciatica refers to pain that radiates along the path of the sciatic nerve—from the lower back through the hip and buttock and down each leg. It typically affects only one side of the body. Sciatica is a symptom of an underlying condition, most commonly a herniated disc or bone spur pressing on the nerve.1

80-90%
of sciatica cases improve without surgery2

The Sciatic Nerve

  • Largest nerve in the body
  • Formed from nerve roots in lower spine (L4-S3)
  • Runs from lower back through buttock and down each leg
  • Controls muscles and provides sensation in legs and feet

Symptoms

Classic sciatica symptoms:3

Pain Pattern

  • Pain radiating from lower back through buttock and down leg
  • Usually affects one side
  • May be sharp, burning, or shooting
  • Can range from mild ache to severe, debilitating pain
  • May worsen with sitting, coughing, or sneezing

Other Symptoms

  • Numbness or tingling in leg or foot
  • Weakness in affected leg
  • Pins and needles sensation
  • Difficulty moving leg or foot

Where Pain May Travel

  • Buttock
  • Back of thigh
  • Calf
  • Foot (depending on which nerve root affected)

Seek Immediate Care If

Sudden severe weakness in leg, loss of bladder or bowel control, or numbness in groin/saddle area (cauda equina syndrome)—this is a medical emergency requiring immediate evaluation.

Causes

Several conditions can cause sciatica:

Most Common Causes

  • Herniated disc: Most common cause—disc material presses on nerve
  • Spinal stenosis: Narrowing of spinal canal
  • Degenerative disc disease: Age-related disc changes
  • Spondylolisthesis: Vertebra slips forward

Less Common Causes

  • Piriformis syndrome (muscle in buttock irritates nerve)
  • Spinal tumors (rare)
  • Infection
  • Trauma

Risk Factors

  • Age: Most common 30-50
  • Occupation: Jobs involving heavy lifting, twisting, or prolonged sitting
  • Prolonged sitting: Sedentary lifestyle
  • Obesity: Increased stress on spine
  • Diabetes: Increases nerve damage risk

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • History of symptoms and pain pattern
  • Physical examination
  • Straight leg raise test
  • Neurological exam (strength, reflexes, sensation)

Imaging

  • Often not needed initially if symptoms are typical
  • MRI: Best for seeing disc and nerve
  • CT scan: Shows bone detail
  • X-ray: Shows bone, not soft tissue
  • Imaging if symptoms persist or red flags present

Imaging Findings

Many people have disc abnormalities on MRI without symptoms. Finding a herniated disc doesn't mean it's causing your pain. Clinical correlation is essential.

Treatment

Most sciatica improves with conservative treatment:4,5

Initial Management

  • Stay active: Avoid prolonged bed rest
  • Pain medications: NSAIDs, acetaminophen
  • Ice or heat: Whichever provides relief
  • Gentle movement: Walking, gentle stretches

Physical Therapy

  • Exercises to reduce nerve compression
  • Core strengthening
  • Stretching
  • Posture and body mechanics training
  • McKenzie method or directional preference exercises

Medications

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen
  • Muscle relaxants: Short-term for spasm
  • Oral steroids: Short course for severe inflammation
  • Neuropathic pain medications: Gabapentin, pregabalin for persistent nerve pain

Injections

  • Epidural steroid injection: Delivers anti-inflammatory around nerve
  • May provide temporary relief
  • Can allow participation in physical therapy
  • Usually limited to 3 per year

Surgery

Consider when:

  • Conservative treatment fails after 6-12 weeks
  • Progressive neurological deficit
  • Cauda equina syndrome (emergency)
  • Severe, intractable pain
  • Microdiscectomy most common procedure

Self-Care

Do's

  • Keep moving—gentle activity helps
  • Use positions that reduce symptoms
  • Apply ice or heat
  • Take short walks
  • Gentle stretching

Don'ts

  • Prolonged bed rest (makes it worse)
  • Heavy lifting
  • Twisting movements
  • Prolonged sitting

Position Tips

  • Lying on back with knees bent may help
  • Pillow under knees when on back
  • Pillow between knees when side-lying
  • Find positions that centralize or reduce pain

Prognosis

  • Most episodes improve within 4-6 weeks
  • 80-90% improve without surgery
  • Some have recurrent episodes
  • Prevention through exercise and proper body mechanics important
