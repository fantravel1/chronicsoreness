Interstitial Cystitis

Understanding painful bladder syndrome and evidence-based management strategies

Key Takeaways

  • IC/PBS affects 3-8 million women in the United States
  • Dietary modification is often helpful and should be tried early
  • Multiple treatments exist; finding the right combination takes time
  • Pelvic floor physical therapy is beneficial for many patients

Understanding Interstitial Cystitis

Interstitial cystitis (IC), also called painful bladder syndrome (PBS) or bladder pain syndrome (BPS), is a chronic condition causing bladder pressure, bladder pain, and sometimes pelvic pain.1 The pain ranges from mild discomfort to severe pain that significantly impacts quality of life.

90%
of IC patients are women2

The Bladder Wall and IC

In a healthy bladder, the bladder wall has a protective lining (glycosaminoglycan layer) that prevents urine from irritating the underlying tissue. In IC, this protective layer may be deficient, allowing urine to irritate the bladder wall and trigger pain signals.3

Symptoms

IC symptoms can vary in intensity and may fluctuate with flare-ups and remissions:1,4

Bladder Symptoms

  • Pain, pressure, or discomfort: Related to bladder filling, often relieved by urination
  • Urinary urgency: Compelling need to urinate
  • Urinary frequency: Needing to urinate often (up to 60 times per day in severe cases)
  • Nocturia: Waking multiple times at night to urinate

Pelvic Symptoms

  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Pain during or after sexual intercourse
  • Pain that worsens with bladder filling and improves after voiding

Symptom Triggers

  • Certain foods and beverages
  • Stress
  • Menstrual cycle changes
  • Sexual activity
  • Prolonged sitting
  • Physical activity

IC vs. UTI

IC symptoms can mimic urinary tract infections, but urine cultures in IC are typically negative for bacteria. Many women with IC are repeatedly treated for UTIs before receiving a correct diagnosis. If you have recurrent UTI-like symptoms with negative cultures, ask about IC.

Causes and Associated Conditions

The exact cause of IC is unknown, but several factors may contribute:5

Possible Contributing Factors

  • Deficiency in the bladder lining (GAG layer)
  • Mast cell activation in the bladder wall
  • Autoimmune factors
  • Nerve dysfunction
  • Pelvic floor muscle dysfunction
  • Central sensitization

Associated Conditions

IC commonly co-occurs with:6

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Vulvodynia
  • Endometriosis
  • Migraines
  • Allergies

Diagnosis

IC is a diagnosis of exclusion, meaning other conditions must be ruled out first:7

Diagnostic Process

  • Medical history: Detailed symptom assessment
  • Physical examination: Including pelvic exam
  • Urinalysis and urine culture: To rule out infection
  • Bladder diary: Recording fluid intake, voiding frequency, and symptoms
  • Post-void residual: Ensuring bladder empties properly

Additional Tests (If Needed)

  • Cystoscopy: Visual examination of bladder interior
  • Hydrodistention: Filling bladder during cystoscopy to assess capacity and look for Hunner lesions
  • Potassium sensitivity test: Less commonly used now
  • Urodynamic studies: To evaluate bladder function

Dietary Management

Diet modification is often effective and should be tried early in treatment:8

Common Bladder Irritants

Foods and beverages that commonly trigger symptoms:

  • Acidic foods: Citrus fruits, tomatoes, vinegar
  • Caffeinated beverages: Coffee, tea, cola
  • Alcohol
  • Carbonated drinks
  • Artificial sweeteners
  • Spicy foods
  • Chocolate
  • Aged cheeses

The Elimination Diet Approach

  1. Eliminate all potential irritants for 1-2 weeks
  2. Reintroduce foods one at a time, every 3-5 days
  3. Monitor symptoms to identify personal triggers
  4. Not everyone reacts to the same foods

Bladder-Friendly Foods

  • Water (plain)
  • Low-acid fruits (blueberries, pears)
  • Most vegetables (except tomatoes)
  • Rice, oats, potatoes
  • Chicken, fish, eggs
  • Mild cheeses

Hydration Is Still Important

Do not restrict fluids to reduce urination. This concentrates urine and can worsen symptoms. Aim to drink enough water to keep urine light yellow. Sip throughout the day rather than drinking large amounts at once.

Treatment Options

Treatment is typically multimodal, combining several approaches:9

First-Line Treatments

  • Patient education: Understanding the condition
  • Dietary modification: Identifying and avoiding triggers
  • Stress management: Stress can trigger flares
  • Bladder training: Gradually increasing time between voids

Physical Therapy

Pelvic floor physical therapy is beneficial for many IC patients:10

  • Manual therapy to release tight muscles
  • Myofascial release
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Stretching exercises
  • Should NOT include Kegel exercises (these can worsen symptoms)

Oral Medications

  • Pentosan polysulfate (Elmiron): FDA-approved for IC; helps repair bladder lining
  • Amitriptyline: Low-dose tricyclic antidepressant for pain
  • Hydroxyzine: Antihistamine that may help
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin: For neuropathic pain
  • Cimetidine: H2 blocker sometimes helpful

Bladder Instillations

Medications delivered directly into the bladder via catheter:

  • DMSO (dimethyl sulfoxide)
  • Heparin
  • Lidocaine
  • "Rescue instillations" for flares

Procedures

  • Hydrodistention: Stretching the bladder may provide temporary relief
  • Fulguration/laser treatment: For Hunner lesions
  • Botox injections: Into bladder muscle
  • Nerve stimulation: Sacral neuromodulation

Managing Flares

Flare-ups are common. Having a management plan helps:

During a Flare

  • Apply heat to the lower abdomen or back
  • Take a warm bath
  • Use OTC pain relievers (as appropriate)
  • Drink extra water to dilute urine
  • Avoid known trigger foods
  • Rest and practice relaxation techniques
  • Contact your healthcare provider if severe

Preventing Flares

  • Maintain bladder-friendly diet
  • Manage stress
  • Get adequate sleep
  • Stay consistent with treatments
  • Keep a symptom diary to identify patterns

Living with IC

Work and Daily Life

  • Know bathroom locations wherever you go
  • Consider workplace accommodations if needed
  • Carry bladder-friendly snacks and drinks
  • Plan travel with frequent stops

Relationships and Intimacy

  • Communicate openly with your partner
  • Time intimacy when symptoms are better
  • Empty bladder before and after intercourse
  • Use lubricants
  • Consider working with a sex therapist if needed

Emotional Support

  • Join a support group (in-person or online)
  • Consider counseling for coping with chronic illness
  • Practice self-compassion
  • Educate family and friends about IC

Outlook

IC is a chronic condition, but many people find significant relief with treatment:11

  • Most patients improve with multimodal treatment
  • Some experience complete remission
  • Others have intermittent flares that can be managed
  • Finding the right treatment combination takes time and patience
