Interstitial Cystitis

Understanding bladder pain syndrome

Key Takeaways

  • IC/BPS causes chronic bladder pain and urinary frequency
  • Women are affected much more often than men
  • It's not an infection—antibiotics won't help
  • Multiple treatment options are available

What Is Interstitial Cystitis?

Interstitial cystitis/bladder pain syndrome (IC/BPS) is a chronic condition causing bladder pressure, bladder pain, and sometimes pelvic pain. The severity ranges from mild discomfort to severe pain. It's part of a spectrum of diseases known as painful bladder syndrome.1

3-8 Million
women in the US may have IC/BPS2

Who Gets IC/BPS?

  • Women much more often than men (5-10:1)
  • Can occur at any age
  • Often associated with other chronic pain conditions

Symptoms

Symptoms vary and may fluctuate:3

Bladder Symptoms

  • Pain, pressure, or discomfort in bladder area
  • Pain that worsens as bladder fills
  • Relief after urinating (temporary)
  • Urgent need to urinate (urgency)
  • Frequent urination (often 8+ times per day)
  • Nighttime urination (nocturia)

Pain Locations

  • Lower abdomen/suprapubic area
  • Pelvic area
  • Perineum
  • Urethra
  • Vaginal area (women)
  • Lower back

Triggers

  • Certain foods and drinks
  • Stress
  • Menstrual cycle
  • Sexual activity
  • Prolonged sitting

Flares

  • Symptoms often wax and wane
  • Flares may last days to weeks
  • May have periods of remission

Causes

The exact cause is unknown:4

Proposed Mechanisms

  • Bladder lining defect: Compromised protective layer
  • Mast cell activation: Inflammatory cells in bladder wall
  • Nerve dysfunction: Hypersensitive nerves
  • Autoimmune factors: Possible immune involvement
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction: Often coexists

Associated Conditions

  • Irritable bowel syndrome
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Vulvodynia
  • Endometriosis
  • Migraines

Diagnosis

IC/BPS is diagnosed by symptoms after ruling out other conditions:5

Evaluation

  • Symptom history and questionnaires
  • Pelvic examination
  • Urinalysis to rule out infection
  • Urine culture
  • Post-void residual (bladder emptying check)

Additional Tests (If Needed)

  • Cystoscopy (looking inside bladder)
  • Bladder biopsy
  • Urodynamic studies
  • Potassium sensitivity test (less commonly used now)

Not a UTI

IC/BPS symptoms can feel like a urinary tract infection, but cultures show no bacteria. Repeated negative cultures with persistent symptoms suggest IC/BPS rather than infection.

Treatment

Treatment is stepped and individualized:6

First-Line: Self-Care and Behavioral

  • Diet modification: Identify and avoid trigger foods
  • Stress management: Stress worsens symptoms
  • Bladder training: Gradually increasing intervals
  • Pelvic floor physical therapy: Very helpful for many

Dietary Triggers (Common)

  • Coffee and caffeine
  • Alcohol
  • Acidic foods (citrus, tomatoes)
  • Artificial sweeteners
  • Spicy foods
  • Carbonated beverages

Oral Medications

  • Pentosan polysulfate (Elmiron): Only FDA-approved oral medication for IC
  • Amitriptyline: Helps pain and frequency
  • Hydroxyzine: Antihistamine that may help
  • Pain medications: As needed

Bladder Instillations

  • Medications placed directly in bladder
  • DMSO, heparin, lidocaine, others
  • "Rescue" instillations for flares

Other Options

  • Nerve stimulation (sacral neuromodulation)
  • Botox injections to bladder
  • Bladder hydrodistension
  • Surgery (rare, last resort)

Living with IC/BPS

Daily Management

  • Track symptoms and triggers with diary
  • Follow IC-friendly diet
  • Manage stress
  • Stay hydrated (but not excessively)
  • Wear loose, comfortable clothing

During Flares

  • Rest when needed
  • Apply heat to pelvic area
  • Take prescribed rescue medications
  • Avoid known triggers strictly
  • Practice relaxation techniques

Support

  • Interstitial Cystitis Association (ichelp.org)
  • Support groups (online and in-person)
  • Mental health support—chronic pain is hard
