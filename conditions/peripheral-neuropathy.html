Peripheral Neuropathy

Understanding nerve damage and neuropathic pain

Key Takeaways

  • Peripheral neuropathy results from damage to nerves outside the brain and spinal cord
  • Diabetes is the most common cause, but many other causes exist
  • Symptoms include numbness, tingling, burning pain, and weakness
  • Treatment focuses on underlying cause and symptom management

What Is Peripheral Neuropathy?

Peripheral neuropathy refers to damage or dysfunction of the peripheral nerves—the nerves that transmit information between the brain and spinal cord and the rest of the body. This can cause pain, numbness, weakness, and other symptoms.1

Types of Nerves Affected

  • Sensory nerves: Carry sensation (touch, pain, temperature)
  • Motor nerves: Control muscle movement
  • Autonomic nerves: Control involuntary functions (heart rate, digestion, blood pressure)

Symptoms

Symptoms depend on which nerves are affected:3

Sensory Symptoms

  • Numbness: Reduced or absent sensation
  • Tingling: "Pins and needles" sensation
  • Burning pain: Often worse at night
  • Shooting or electric pain: Sharp, sudden pain
  • Hypersensitivity: Pain from normally non-painful stimuli
  • Loss of position sense: Difficulty knowing where limbs are in space

Motor Symptoms

  • Muscle weakness
  • Muscle cramps or twitching
  • Difficulty with coordination
  • Foot drop (difficulty lifting front of foot)
  • Muscle wasting over time

Autonomic Symptoms

  • Dizziness upon standing
  • Digestive problems
  • Bladder dysfunction
  • Excessive or reduced sweating
  • Heart rate abnormalities

Pattern of Symptoms

Most commonly affects feet and legs first, then hands—a "stocking-glove" distribution.

Causes

Many conditions can cause peripheral neuropathy:4

Common Causes

  • Diabetes: Most common cause (diabetic neuropathy)
  • Alcohol: Alcoholic neuropathy
  • Vitamin deficiencies: B12, B1, B6, E
  • Chemotherapy: Many chemotherapy drugs cause neuropathy
  • Autoimmune conditions: Guillain-Barré, CIDP, lupus, RA
  • Infections: Shingles, HIV, Lyme disease

Other Causes

  • Kidney disease
  • Thyroid disorders
  • Hereditary conditions (Charcot-Marie-Tooth)
  • Toxin exposure
  • Nerve compression
  • Medications (beyond chemotherapy)

Idiopathic Neuropathy

In about 30% of cases, no cause is found despite thorough evaluation. This is called idiopathic neuropathy.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis involves identifying both the neuropathy and its cause:5

Clinical Evaluation

  • Detailed medical history
  • Neurological examination
  • Assessment of sensation, strength, reflexes

Diagnostic Tests

  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS): Measure nerve signal speed
  • Electromyography (EMG): Assess muscle electrical activity
  • Blood tests: Glucose, B12, thyroid, kidney function, etc.
  • Skin biopsy: Can detect small fiber neuropathy
  • Lumbar puncture: If inflammatory cause suspected

Treatment

Treatment addresses the underlying cause and manages symptoms:6

Treating the Cause

  • Diabetes: Optimize blood sugar control
  • Vitamin deficiency: Supplement replacement
  • Alcohol: Cessation and nutritional support
  • Autoimmune: Immunomodulating therapies

Medications for Neuropathic Pain

  • Anticonvulsants: Gabapentin, pregabalin (first-line)
  • Antidepressants: Duloxetine, amitriptyline, nortriptyline
  • Topical treatments: Lidocaine patches, capsaicin
  • Opioids: Reserved for severe cases, limited benefit

Other Treatments

  • Physical therapy for strength and balance
  • Occupational therapy for daily activities
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)
  • Acupuncture (some evidence)
  • Spinal cord stimulation for severe cases

Self-Care and Safety

Foot Care (Critical)

  • Inspect feet daily for injuries
  • Wear well-fitting shoes
  • Never go barefoot
  • Keep feet clean and moisturized
  • Regular podiatry care

Fall Prevention

  • Remove tripping hazards at home
  • Good lighting, especially at night
  • Handrails on stairs
  • Assistive devices if needed
  • Balance exercises

Lifestyle

  • Regular exercise (as tolerated)
  • Healthy diet
  • No smoking
  • Limit alcohol
  • Manage blood sugar if diabetic
