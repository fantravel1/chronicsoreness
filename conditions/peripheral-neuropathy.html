Peripheral Neuropathy

Understanding nerve damage and its effects

What Is Peripheral Neuropathy?

Peripheral neuropathy occurs when nerves outside the brain and spinal cord are damaged, causing weakness, numbness, and painâ€”usually in the hands and feet. It affects an estimated 20 million Americans. Diabetes is the most common cause, but many conditions can damage peripheral nerves.1

Common Causes2

  • Diabetes (most common)
  • Chemotherapy
  • Alcoholism
  • Vitamin deficiencies (B12, B1, B6)
  • Autoimmune diseases
  • Infections (HIV, Lyme, shingles)
  • Hereditary conditions
  • Kidney or liver disease
  • Toxin exposure
  • Idiopathic (unknown cause)

Symptoms

Sensory

  • Numbness and tingling
  • Burning or shooting pain
  • Sensitivity to touch
  • "Pins and needles" feeling
  • Loss of sensation

Motor

  • Muscle weakness
  • Cramping
  • Difficulty walking
  • Balance problems
  • Coordination difficulties

Autonomic

  • Blood pressure changes
  • Digestive problems
  • Sweating abnormalities
  • Bladder dysfunction

Diagnosis3

  • Physical and neurological examination
  • Blood tests (glucose, B12, thyroid)
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • Electromyography (EMG)
  • Skin biopsy (small fiber neuropathy)
  • Sometimes nerve biopsy

Treatment Approaches

Treating the Cause

  • Blood sugar control for diabetes
  • Vitamin supplementation
  • Treating underlying conditions
  • Stopping offending medications/toxins

Pain Management

  • Anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin)
  • Antidepressants (duloxetine, amitriptyline)
  • Topical treatments (capsaicin, lidocaine)
  • Pain specialists if severe

Other Treatments

  • Physical therapy
  • TENS therapy
  • Balance training
  • Foot care and protection

Living with Neuropathy

  • Check feet daily for injuries
  • Wear protective footwear
  • Use night lights (balance)
  • Remove tripping hazards
  • Manage underlying conditions
