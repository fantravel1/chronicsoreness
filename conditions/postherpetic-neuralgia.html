Postherpetic Neuralgia

Understanding persistent nerve pain after shingles and effective approaches to management and relief

What Is Postherpetic Neuralgia?

Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) is the most common complication of shingles (herpes zoster), occurring when nerve fibers are damaged during the shingles infection. This damage causes the nerves to send exaggerated pain signals to the brain, resulting in chronic pain that persists long after the shingles rash has healed.1

PHN is defined as pain lasting more than 90 days after the onset of the shingles rash. It affects approximately 10-20% of people who develop shingles, with the risk increasing significantly with age.2

Understanding Shingles

Shingles is caused by the varicella-zoster virusâ€”the same virus that causes chickenpox. After a chickenpox infection, the virus remains dormant in nerve tissue near the spinal cord and brain. Years later, it may reactivate as shingles, causing a painful rash that typically affects one side of the body.

Risk Factors for PHN

Several factors increase the likelihood of developing postherpetic neuralgia:

  • Age - Risk increases dramatically after age 60; people over 80 have a 30% risk3
  • Severity of initial shingles - More severe rash and pain increase PHN risk
  • Early symptoms - Pain before the rash appears suggests higher risk
  • Location of shingles - Facial shingles carries higher PHN risk
  • Delayed treatment - Starting antiviral medication more than 72 hours after rash onset
  • Immune system weakness - Due to disease or medications
  • Diabetes - Associated with increased PHN risk

Symptoms of Postherpetic Neuralgia

PHN symptoms are confined to the area where the shingles rash occurred and may include:

Types of Pain

  • Burning pain - Constant, deep burning sensation
  • Stabbing or shooting pain - Intermittent sharp, electric-like pains
  • Aching pain - Deep, throbbing discomfort

Sensory Changes

  • Allodynia - Extreme sensitivity where light touch causes pain (even clothing brushing skin)
  • Hyperalgesia - Exaggerated response to painful stimuli
  • Numbness - Loss of sensation in the affected area
  • Itching - Severe, often deep itching sensation

Impact on Daily Life

PHN can significantly affect quality of life, leading to:

  • Sleep disturbances and insomnia
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Difficulty with daily activities
  • Social withdrawal
  • Reduced physical activity
  • Fatigue

Important Note

If you're experiencing severe pain from shingles, seek medical attention promptly. Early treatment with antiviral medications within 72 hours of rash onset can reduce the risk and severity of PHN.

Treatment Options

Treatment for PHN aims to relieve pain and improve quality of life. A multimodal approach combining several therapies often works best.4

First-Line Medications

  • Gabapentin (Neurontin) - An anticonvulsant that reduces nerve pain; typically started at low doses and gradually increased
  • Pregabalin (Lyrica) - Similar to gabapentin, may work faster for some patients
  • Tricyclic antidepressants - Such as amitriptyline or nortriptyline; effective for nerve pain at doses lower than those used for depression
  • Lidocaine patches (Lidoderm) - Topical numbing medication applied directly to the painful area

Second-Line Treatments

  • Opioid medications - May be considered for severe cases when other treatments fail
  • Capsaicin cream - Made from chili peppers; depletes substance P from nerve endings5
  • High-concentration capsaicin patches (Qutenza) - Applied by healthcare provider; can provide months of relief
  • Tramadol - A mild opioid that may be helpful for moderate pain

Interventional Treatments

  • Nerve blocks - Injection of anesthetic near affected nerves
  • Epidural steroid injections - May help when spinal nerves are involved
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation) - Electrical impulses that may reduce pain
  • Spinal cord stimulation - For severe, treatment-resistant cases

Complementary Approaches

  • Acupuncture - May provide additional pain relief for some patients
  • Physical therapy - Desensitization techniques and gentle exercises
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy - Helps with coping strategies and associated depression
  • Relaxation techniques - Meditation, deep breathing, and stress management

Prevention

The most effective prevention strategy is avoiding shingles in the first place:

Shingles Vaccination

The recombinant zoster vaccine (Shingrix) is recommended for adults 50 years and older and is more than 90% effective in preventing shingles and PHN. Even if you've had shingles before, vaccination can help prevent future occurrences.6

Early Shingles Treatment

If shingles does develop, prompt treatment can reduce PHN risk:

  • Start antiviral medication (acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir) within 72 hours
  • Manage acute pain aggressively
  • Consider early use of nerve pain medications in high-risk patients

Living with PHN

Managing PHN often requires patience and a comprehensive approach:

  • Work with your healthcare team to find the right combination of treatments
  • Protect the affected area from clothing friction with soft, loose garments
  • Stay active within your comfort level
  • Address sleep problems as they can worsen pain perception
  • Seek support through pain support groups or mental health counseling
  • Be patient - PHN often improves over time, though improvement may take months to years

Prognosis

While PHN can be challenging to treat, many people see improvement over time. Some studies show that pain resolves completely in about half of patients within one year, though others may have symptoms for years. Early, aggressive treatment of both shingles and PHN offers the best chance for pain relief.
