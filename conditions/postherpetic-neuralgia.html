Postherpetic Neuralgia

Chronic nerve pain following shingles

What Is Postherpetic Neuralgia?

Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) is a chronic pain condition that occurs as a complication of shingles (herpes zoster). It develops when nerve fibers damaged during shingles continue to send pain signals after the rash heals. It's the most common complication of shingles.1

Definition

  • Pain persisting 90+ days after shingles rash onset
  • Pain in area affected by shingles
  • Affects 10-18% of people who have shingles

Symptoms2

  • Burning, stabbing, or shooting pain
  • Extreme sensitivity to touch (allodynia)
  • Numbness and tingling
  • Itching
  • Pain confined to shingles-affected area
  • Can significantly impair quality of life

Risk Factors for Developing PHN

  • Older age (highest risk >60)
  • Severe acute shingles pain
  • Severe rash
  • Face/eye involvement
  • Delayed antiviral treatment
  • Immunosuppression

Treatment3

First-Line Medications

  • Gabapentin (Neurontin)
  • Pregabalin (Lyrica)
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
  • Lidocaine patches

Other Options

  • Capsaicin cream/patch
  • Opioids (for severe, refractory cases)
  • Nerve blocks
  • Spinal cord stimulation

Prevention

  • Shingles vaccine (Shingrix) - Most important
  • Recommended for adults 50+
  • Early antiviral treatment during shingles

Prognosis

  • Many improve over time
  • Some have persistent long-term pain
  • Early treatment may improve outcomes
