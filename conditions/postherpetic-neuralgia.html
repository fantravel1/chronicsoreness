Postherpetic Neuralgia

Chronic pain after shingles

What Is Postherpetic Neuralgia?

Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN) is chronic nerve pain that persists after a shingles (herpes zoster) outbreak. Shingles is caused by reactivation of the chickenpox virus. PHN occurs when nerve fibers are damaged during the outbreak, causing ongoing pain that can last months or years.1

Risk Factors2

  • Age over 50 (risk increases with age)
  • Severe shingles rash
  • Severe pain during shingles
  • Weakened immune system
  • Delayed antiviral treatment
  • Facial shingles involvement

Symptoms

  • Burning, stabbing, or shooting pain
  • Pain in area where rash occurred
  • Extreme sensitivity to touch (allodynia)
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Itching
  • Pain can be constant or intermittent

Prevention

Shingles Vaccine

  • Shingrix vaccine recommended for adults 50+
  • Reduces shingles risk by over 90%
  • Significantly reduces PHN risk
  • Two doses, 2-6 months apart

Early Treatment of Shingles

  • Antiviral medications within 72 hours
  • May reduce PHN risk and severity
  • Seek care immediately if shingles suspected

Treatment3

First-Line Medications

  • Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
  • Gabapentin or pregabalin
  • Lidocaine patches (5%)

Other Options

  • Capsaicin cream or patches
  • Opioids (for severe cases)
  • Nerve blocks
  • Spinal cord stimulation (refractory cases)

Non-Medication Approaches

  • TENS unit
  • Cold compresses
  • Loose clothing over affected area
  • Stress management
  • Sleep optimization

Prognosis

  • Many improve over time
  • Some have long-lasting pain
  • Early aggressive treatment may improve outcomes
  • Combination treatments often needed
