Lupus (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus)

Understanding this complex autoimmune condition

Key Takeaways

  • Lupus is a chronic autoimmune disease that can affect multiple organ systems
  • Symptoms vary widely—lupus is often called "the great imitator"
  • 9 out of 10 people with lupus are women
  • With proper treatment, most people with lupus can live full, active lives

What Is Lupus?

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), commonly called lupus, is a chronic autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks healthy tissues throughout the body. This can cause inflammation and damage to joints, skin, kidneys, heart, lungs, brain, and blood cells.1

1.5 Million
Americans have lupus; 90% are women2

Key Facts

  • Most commonly affects women of childbearing age (15-44)
  • More common in African American, Hispanic, Asian, and Native American women
  • Symptoms range from mild to life-threatening
  • Characterized by flares and remissions
  • No cure, but treatments can control symptoms

Symptoms

Lupus symptoms vary widely and can mimic other conditions:3

Common Symptoms

  • Fatigue: Often severe and disabling
  • Joint pain and swelling: Often symmetric
  • Skin rashes: Including butterfly rash across cheeks
  • Fever: Low-grade or higher during flares
  • Photosensitivity: Sun exposure triggers symptoms
  • Hair loss: May be patchy or diffuse
  • Mouth sores: Usually painless ulcers

Organ Involvement

  • Kidneys (lupus nephritis): Can lead to kidney damage
  • Heart: Pericarditis, increased cardiovascular risk
  • Lungs: Pleuritis, pneumonitis
  • Brain: Cognitive issues, headaches, seizures (rare)
  • Blood: Anemia, low platelets, clotting issues

The Butterfly Rash

A distinctive rash across the cheeks and bridge of the nose (malar rash) occurs in about 50% of people with lupus. It often appears or worsens after sun exposure.

When to Seek Immediate Care

Seek urgent care for: chest pain, difficulty breathing, severe headache, confusion, blood in urine, high fever, or severe flare symptoms.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing lupus can be challenging due to its variable presentation:4

Diagnostic Criteria

Doctors use classification criteria that include clinical and laboratory findings:

  • Clinical features (rash, arthritis, kidney involvement, etc.)
  • Immunological markers (ANA, anti-dsDNA, etc.)

Blood Tests

  • ANA (antinuclear antibody): Positive in 97%+ of lupus patients
  • Anti-dsDNA: More specific for lupus
  • Anti-Smith antibodies: Highly specific
  • Complement levels (C3, C4): Often low during active disease
  • CBC: May show anemia, low white cells, low platelets
  • Kidney function tests: If kidney involvement suspected

Other Tests

  • Urinalysis (for kidney involvement)
  • Kidney biopsy (if nephritis suspected)
  • Imaging as needed

Treatment

Treatment aims to control symptoms, prevent flares, and minimize organ damage:5

Medications

  • Hydroxychloroquine: Cornerstone treatment; reduces flares, protects organs
  • NSAIDs: For joint pain and inflammation
  • Corticosteroids: For flares; minimize long-term use
  • Immunosuppressants: Azathioprine, mycophenolate, methotrexate
  • Biologics: Belimumab (Benlysta), anifrolumab (Saphnelo)
  • Rituximab: For refractory cases

Treatment Goals

  • Achieve remission or low disease activity
  • Prevent organ damage
  • Minimize medication side effects
  • Maintain quality of life

Hydroxychloroquine

Nearly all lupus patients should take hydroxychloroquine unless contraindicated. It reduces flares, protects organs, improves survival, and helps prevent blood clots. Regular eye exams are needed to monitor for rare retinal toxicity.

Living with Lupus

Sun Protection

UV exposure can trigger flares:

  • Use broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen daily
  • Wear protective clothing and hats
  • Avoid peak sun hours
  • Be aware of UV exposure through windows

Managing Fatigue

  • Prioritize and pace activities
  • Get adequate sleep
  • Gentle exercise can help
  • Address contributing factors (anemia, thyroid, depression)

Lifestyle Factors

  • No smoking: Worsens lupus and reduces medication effectiveness
  • Exercise: Regular, gentle exercise is beneficial
  • Stress management: Stress can trigger flares
  • Vaccinations: Stay up-to-date (avoid live vaccines on immunosuppressants)

Pregnancy

  • Possible for most women with lupus
  • Plan pregnancy during stable disease
  • High-risk pregnancy—needs specialized care
  • Some medications must be stopped before conception

Monitoring and Follow-Up

  • Regular rheumatology visits
  • Periodic blood and urine tests
  • Annual eye exams if on hydroxychloroquine
  • Cardiovascular risk management
  • Bone health monitoring (especially if on steroids)
