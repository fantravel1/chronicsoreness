Brachial Plexus Injury

Nerve damage affecting the arm and hand

What Is the Brachial Plexus?

The brachial plexus is a network of nerves that originate from the spinal cord in the neck and travel down through the shoulder to the arm and hand. These nerves control movement and sensation in the shoulder, arm, and hand. Injury to these nerves can cause pain, weakness, and numbness.1

Types of Injury2

Stretch Injuries

  • Nerves stretched but not torn
  • Most common type
  • Often recover on their own

Rupture

  • Nerve torn but not from spine
  • May require surgery

Avulsion

  • Nerve root torn from spinal cord
  • Most severe type
  • Cannot be directly repaired

Causes

  • Motor vehicle accidents
  • Motorcycle crashes
  • Sports injuries (contact sports)
  • Birth injuries
  • Tumors or radiation
  • Inflammatory conditions

Symptoms3

  • Pain (can be severe)
  • Weakness in arm or hand
  • Numbness or loss of sensation
  • Inability to move arm, hand, or fingers
  • Burning or electric shock sensations
  • Muscle wasting over time

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination
  • Electromyography (EMG)
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • MRI
  • CT myelography

Treatment

Conservative

  • Physical therapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Pain management
  • Observation for milder injuries

Surgery

  • Nerve grafts
  • Nerve transfers
  • Muscle transfers
  • Timing important (usually within 6 months)

Pain Management

  • Nerve pain medications
  • Antidepressants
  • Physical therapy
  • TENS
  • Pain can persist even after nerve recovery

Prognosis

  • Depends on severity
  • Mild injuries often recover
  • Severe injuries may have permanent deficits
  • Early intervention improves outcomes
