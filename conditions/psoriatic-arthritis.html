Psoriatic Arthritis

When psoriasis affects the joints

What Is Psoriatic Arthritis?

Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) is an inflammatory arthritis associated with the skin condition psoriasis. About 30% of people with psoriasis develop PsA. It can affect the joints, spine, and sites where tendons attach to bone (entheses).1

Types of PsA2

  • Asymmetric oligoarthritis - Few joints, not matching sides
  • Symmetric polyarthritis - Similar to rheumatoid arthritis
  • Distal arthritis - Fingers and toes near nails
  • Spondylitis - Spine involvement
  • Arthritis mutilans - Severe, destructive (rare)

Symptoms

  • Joint pain and swelling
  • Morning stiffness
  • Sausage-like swelling of fingers/toes (dactylitis)
  • Heel or foot pain (enthesitis)
  • Nail changes (pitting, separation)
  • Back pain and stiffness
  • Fatigue

Diagnosis

  • Presence of psoriasis (current or past/family)
  • Characteristic pattern of arthritis
  • Imaging (X-rays, MRI)
  • Blood tests (usually RF negative)

Treatment3

Medications

  • NSAIDs (mild disease)
  • DMARDs (methotrexate, sulfasalazine)
  • Biologics (TNF inhibitors, IL-17/IL-23 inhibitors)
  • JAK inhibitors

Other Treatments

  • Physical therapy
  • Exercise
  • Joint protection
  • Weight management

Early Treatment Important

Early diagnosis and treatment help prevent joint damage. Without treatment, PsA can be destructive.
