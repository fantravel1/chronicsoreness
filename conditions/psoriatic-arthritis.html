Psoriatic Arthritis

Understanding this chronic inflammatory condition that affects both joints and skin

What Is Psoriatic Arthritis?

Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) is a chronic inflammatory disease that affects both the skin and joints. It typically occurs in people who have psoriasisâ€”a condition causing red, scaly skin patchesâ€”though joint symptoms can sometimes appear before skin symptoms.1

PsA affects approximately 30% of people with psoriasis and can range from mild to severe. Without treatment, it can cause permanent joint damage.2

Key Characteristics

  • Combination of skin and joint symptoms
  • Can affect any joint in the body
  • Often asymmetric (different joints on each side)
  • Commonly affects fingers and toes
  • Can involve the spine (spondylitis)
  • Enthesitisâ€”inflammation where tendons attach to bone

Types of Psoriatic Arthritis

PsA can take several forms:3

Symmetric Polyarthritis

  • Affects same joints on both sides of body
  • Similar to rheumatoid arthritis
  • Most common pattern (about 50% of cases)

Asymmetric Oligoarthritis

  • Affects a few joints (usually fewer than 5)
  • Different joints on each side
  • May involve large and small joints

Distal Interphalangeal Predominant

  • Affects joints closest to fingernails and toenails
  • Often associated with nail changes
  • More common in men

Spondylitis

  • Affects the spine and sacroiliac joints
  • Causes back pain and stiffness
  • May occur with or without peripheral joint involvement

Arthritis Mutilans

  • Most severe but rare form (less than 5%)
  • Causes severe destruction and deformity
  • "Pencil in cup" appearance on X-ray

Symptoms

Joint Symptoms

  • Pain and stiffness - Often worse in morning or after rest
  • Swelling - Joints may appear puffy
  • Dactylitis ("sausage digits") - Entire finger or toe swells
  • Enthesitis - Pain where tendons meet bone (Achilles, plantar fascia)
  • Reduced range of motion
  • Warmth - Affected joints may feel warm

Skin and Nail Symptoms

  • Psoriasis patchesâ€”red, scaly plaques on skin
  • Nail pittingâ€”small depressions in nails
  • Nail separation (onycholysis)
  • Nail thickening and discoloration
  • Scalp psoriasis

Other Symptoms

  • Fatigue
  • Eye inflammation (uveitis)
  • Inflammatory bowel symptoms in some

Early Treatment Is Important

PsA can cause permanent joint damage within the first two years of onset. Early diagnosis and aggressive treatment can prevent or slow this damage. If you have psoriasis and develop joint pain, see a rheumatologist promptly.

Diagnosis

No single test diagnoses PsA. Diagnosis involves:

  • Medical history - Symptoms, psoriasis history, family history
  • Physical examination - Joints, skin, nails
  • Blood tests - Inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP); usually RF-negative
  • Imaging - X-rays, MRI, or ultrasound to assess damage4
  • Rule out other conditions - RA, gout, osteoarthritis

Treatment

Treatment goals include reducing inflammation, relieving symptoms, preventing joint damage, and maintaining quality of life.

NSAIDs

  • First-line for mild disease
  • Help with pain and inflammation
  • Examples: naproxen, ibuprofen, celecoxib

DMARDs (Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs)

For moderate to severe disease:

  • Methotrexate - Most commonly used; helps skin and joints
  • Sulfasalazine
  • Leflunomide
  • Apremilast (Otezla) - PDE4 inhibitor; oral medication

Biologic Medications

Targeted therapies for moderate to severe PsA:5

  • TNF inhibitors - Etanercept, adalimumab, infliximab, golimumab, certolizumab
  • IL-17 inhibitors - Secukinumab, ixekizumab
  • IL-12/23 inhibitors - Ustekinumab
  • IL-23 inhibitors - Guselkumab, risankizumab

JAK Inhibitors

  • Tofacitinib (Xeljanz)
  • Upadacitinib (Rinvoq)
  • Oral medications; alternative to biologics

Other Treatments

  • Corticosteroid injections - For specific inflamed joints
  • Physical therapy - Maintain strength and flexibility
  • Occupational therapy - Protect joints during daily activities
  • Surgery - For severe joint damage (rare)

Living with Psoriatic Arthritis

Self-Care Strategies

  • Exercise regularly - Low-impact activities like swimming, walking, yoga
  • Protect your joints - Use assistive devices, practice good body mechanics
  • Maintain healthy weight - Reduces joint stress and inflammation6
  • Manage stress - Stress can trigger flares
  • Get adequate sleep - Rest helps manage fatigue
  • Don't smoke - Smoking worsens outcomes

Monitoring Your Health

  • Regular rheumatology visits
  • Cardiovascular screening (PsA increases heart disease risk)
  • Monitor for medication side effects
  • Eye exams for uveitis
  • Mental health support for depression/anxiety

Prognosis

With modern treatments, many people with PsA achieve good disease control and maintain active lives. Early, aggressive treatment provides the best outcomes. While there's no cure, the goal is remission or low disease activity with minimal joint damage.
