Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)

Understanding this challenging chronic pain condition and evidence-based management approaches

Key Takeaways

  • CRPS is a chronic pain condition usually affecting one limb after injury or surgery
  • Women are affected 3-4 times more often than men
  • Early diagnosis and treatment lead to better outcomes
  • Multidisciplinary treatment combining physical therapy, medications, and psychological support is most effective

Understanding CRPS

Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) is a chronic pain condition characterized by continuous, intense pain that is disproportionate to the original injury.1 Previously known as Reflex Sympathetic Dystrophy (RSD) or causalgia, CRPS typically affects one limb—usually an arm, leg, hand, or foot—and can spread to other areas.

3-4x
higher incidence in women compared to men2

Types of CRPS

  • CRPS Type I (90% of cases): Occurs after illness or injury that did not directly damage the nerves in the affected limb
  • CRPS Type II: Follows a distinct nerve injury

Causes and Triggers

CRPS commonly develops after:3

  • Fractures (most common trigger)
  • Sprains and strains
  • Surgery
  • Immobilization (casting)
  • Minor injuries
  • Rarely, without any identified trigger

Symptoms

CRPS symptoms can vary significantly and may change over time:1,4

Pain

  • Continuous burning or throbbing pain
  • Allodynia: Pain from normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, clothing)
  • Hyperalgesia: Increased sensitivity to painful stimuli
  • Pain often described as burning, stabbing, or electric
  • Pain disproportionate to the original injury

Sensory Changes

  • Extreme sensitivity to touch, temperature, or vibration
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Changes in how temperature is perceived

Skin and Temperature Changes

  • Skin color changes (red, blue, pale, or mottled)
  • Temperature differences between affected and unaffected limbs
  • Changes in skin texture (thin, shiny, or dry)
  • Abnormal sweating

Swelling and Motor Changes

  • Swelling of the affected limb
  • Joint stiffness
  • Decreased ability to move the affected body part
  • Muscle weakness, spasms, or tremors

Trophic Changes

  • Changes in hair growth (faster or slower)
  • Changes in nail growth (faster or slower, brittle)
  • Bone changes (osteoporosis in affected area)

Seek Early Treatment

Early diagnosis and treatment of CRPS significantly improves outcomes. If you develop unusual pain after an injury that seems out of proportion to what you would expect, especially with skin changes or swelling, seek medical attention promptly.

Diagnosis

CRPS is diagnosed clinically using the Budapest Criteria:5

Budapest Diagnostic Criteria

Diagnosis requires:

  1. Continuing pain disproportionate to any inciting event
  2. At least one symptom in three of these four categories:
    • Sensory: Hyperesthesia and/or allodynia
    • Vasomotor: Temperature asymmetry and/or skin color changes
    • Sudomotor/Edema: Edema and/or sweating changes
    • Motor/Trophic: Motor dysfunction and/or trophic changes
  3. At least one sign at examination in two or more categories
  4. No other diagnosis better explains the signs and symptoms

Diagnostic Tests

While diagnosis is primarily clinical, tests may include:

  • Bone scan: May show changes in blood flow and bone metabolism
  • X-rays: May reveal bone loss in affected area
  • MRI: Can show soft tissue changes
  • Thermography: Measures temperature differences
  • Sympathetic nerve blocks: Both diagnostic and therapeutic

Treatment Approaches

CRPS treatment is most effective when started early and involves a multidisciplinary approach:6

Physical and Occupational Therapy

The cornerstone of CRPS treatment:

  • Desensitization: Gradual exposure to different textures and sensations
  • Range of motion exercises: To prevent stiffness and muscle atrophy
  • Strengthening: As tolerated
  • Mirror therapy: Using visual feedback to retrain the brain
  • Graded motor imagery: Progressive approach to movement
  • Functional activities: Practicing daily tasks

Mirror Therapy

Mirror therapy involves placing a mirror between your limbs so that the reflection of the unaffected limb appears where the affected limb would be. Moving the healthy limb while watching its reflection can help retrain the brain and reduce pain. Research supports its effectiveness for CRPS.7

Medications

Pain medications:

  • NSAIDs for mild pain
  • Opioids (carefully considered, as they may be less effective for CRPS)
  • Topical analgesics (lidocaine patches, capsaicin)

Neuropathic pain medications:

  • Gabapentin (Neurontin)
  • Pregabalin (Lyrica)
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
  • SNRIs (duloxetine)

Other medications:

  • Bisphosphonates (may help reduce bone loss and pain)
  • Corticosteroids (short courses in early CRPS)
  • Muscle relaxants for spasms
  • Ketamine (in specialized settings)

Interventional Treatments

  • Sympathetic nerve blocks: Anesthetic injected near sympathetic nerves
  • Spinal cord stimulation: Implanted device that modifies pain signals
  • Intrathecal drug pumps: Deliver medication directly to spinal fluid
  • Peripheral nerve stimulation: Targets specific nerves

Psychological Support

Essential component of comprehensive care:

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy for pain management
  • Acceptance and commitment therapy
  • Stress management and relaxation techniques
  • Treatment of depression and anxiety
  • Support groups

Self-Management Strategies

Daily Strategies

  • Protect the affected limb from injury and extreme temperatures
  • Practice gentle movement and exercises as prescribed
  • Use desensitization techniques regularly
  • Pace activities to avoid overexertion
  • Maintain good posture and body mechanics

Stress Management

  • Practice relaxation techniques (deep breathing, meditation)
  • Engage in enjoyable activities
  • Maintain social connections
  • Get adequate sleep

Working with Your Healthcare Team

  • Keep detailed symptom records
  • Communicate openly about what is and is not working
  • Be an active participant in treatment decisions
  • Ask about new treatment options

Prognosis

CRPS outcomes vary significantly:8

  • With early treatment, many people improve substantially or fully recover
  • Some experience ongoing symptoms that can be managed
  • A minority develop chronic, severe symptoms
  • Children and adolescents often have better outcomes than adults

Factors Associated with Better Outcomes

  • Early diagnosis and treatment
  • Active participation in physical therapy
  • Multidisciplinary treatment approach
  • Good psychological coping skills
  • Strong support system
