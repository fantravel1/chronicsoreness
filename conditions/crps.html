Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)

Understanding this challenging condition characterized by severe, often burning pain

What Is CRPS?

Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) is a chronic pain condition that typically affects a limb after an injury, surgery, or other trauma. The pain is disproportionate to the initial injury and is often described as severe, burning, or "like fire." CRPS was formerly called Reflex Sympathetic Dystrophy (RSD).1

CRPS affects about 1 in 3,800 people annually, occurring more often in women and typically between ages 40-60. It's considered one of the most painful conditions known to medicine.

Two Types

  • CRPS Type I - No confirmed nerve injury (formerly RSD); about 90% of cases
  • CRPS Type II - With confirmed nerve injury (formerly causalgia)

Symptoms

CRPS typically affects one limb (arm, leg, hand, or foot) and can include:2

Pain

  • Continuous burning pain - Often severe
  • Allodynia - Pain from normally non-painful touch
  • Hyperalgesia - Increased sensitivity to painful stimuli
  • Pain spreads - May extend beyond original injury site

Sensory Changes

  • Extreme sensitivity to touch, cold, or movement
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Changes in how temperature is perceived

Skin Changes

  • Color changes - Blotchy, red, purple, or pale
  • Temperature changes - Warmer or colder than other limb
  • Texture changes - Thin, shiny skin
  • Sweating changes - Increased or decreased

Motor and Other Changes

  • Weakness and decreased range of motion
  • Tremor or spasms
  • Difficulty moving the affected limb
  • Nail and hair growth changes
  • Swelling

Early Treatment Is Crucial

CRPS responds best to early intervention. If you develop these symptoms after an injury, seek evaluation promptly. The longer CRPS goes untreated, the harder it becomes to manage. Early, aggressive treatment offers the best outcomes.3

Causes and Risk Factors

Common Triggers

  • Fractures - Most common trigger
  • Surgery
  • Sprains and strains
  • Soft tissue injuries
  • Immobilization - Casting, splinting
  • Rarely - No identifiable trigger

What Goes Wrong

CRPS involves dysfunction of both the nervous system and immune system:4

  • Abnormal inflammatory response
  • Sensitization of pain pathways
  • Autonomic nervous system dysfunction
  • Changes in the brain's pain processing
  • The pain response becomes self-perpetuating

Diagnosis

CRPS is diagnosed clinically based on the Budapest Criteria:

  • Continuing pain disproportionate to any inciting event
  • At least one symptom in 3 of 4 categories: sensory, vasomotor (blood flow), sudomotor (sweating)/edema, motor/trophic
  • At least one sign at examination in 2 of 4 categories
  • No other diagnosis better explains the findings

  • X-rays may show bone loss
  • Bone scan may show changes
  • Tests mainly rule out other conditions

Treatment

CRPS treatment is multidisciplinary and should begin early:5

Physical and Occupational Therapy

  • Essential component - Maintains function and prevents contractures
  • Graded motor imagery - Mental rehearsal before physical movement
  • Mirror therapy - Using reflection to "trick" the brain
  • Desensitization - Gradually increasing tolerance to touch
  • Range of motion exercises - Gentle, progressive

Medications

  • Anti-inflammatory medications - NSAIDs, steroids (especially early)
  • Neuropathic pain medications - Gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine
  • Bisphosphonates - May help some cases
  • Topical treatments - Lidocaine, capsaicin
  • Opioids - Generally avoided; limited benefit, risks
  • Low-dose naltrexone - Being studied

Interventional Treatments

  • Sympathetic nerve blocks - Can provide temporary relief
  • Spinal cord stimulation - For refractory cases6
  • Intrathecal drug delivery - Pumps delivering medication directly
  • Ketamine infusions - In specialized centers

Psychological Support

  • Essential part of comprehensive treatment
  • CBT for pain management
  • Coping skills training
  • Treatment of depression and anxiety

Prognosis

  • Varies widely from person to person
  • Some experience remission, especially with early treatment
  • Others have persistent symptoms
  • Can spread to other limbs in some cases
  • Early, aggressive treatment improves outcomes

Living with CRPS

  • Find a knowledgeable provider - CRPS specialists or pain medicine doctors
  • Stay active within limits - Avoid complete immobilization
  • Protect the limb - From bumps, temperature extremes
  • Seek support - Support groups understand your experience
  • Advocate for yourself - CRPS is often misunderstood
  • Address mental health - Living with severe pain is challenging
