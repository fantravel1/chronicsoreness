Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)

Understanding this challenging chronic pain condition

Key Takeaways

  • CRPS causes severe, often burning pain typically affecting a limb
  • Early diagnosis and treatment improve outcomes
  • Physical and occupational therapy are essential
  • Women are affected more often than men (3-4x)

What Is CRPS?

Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) is a chronic pain condition that usually affects one limb (arm, leg, hand, or foot), typically after an injury or trauma. The pain is out of proportion to the initial injury and is accompanied by changes in skin color, temperature, and swelling. CRPS was formerly known as Reflex Sympathetic Dystrophy (RSD).1

200,000
Americans are affected by CRPS, though it may be underdiagnosed2

Types of CRPS

  • CRPS Type I: No confirmed nerve injury (90% of cases); formerly called RSD
  • CRPS Type II: Follows confirmed nerve injury; formerly called causalgia
  • Both types have similar symptoms and treatment

Symptoms

CRPS symptoms are variable but typically include:3

Pain

  • Continuous burning or throbbing pain
  • Pain out of proportion to initial injury
  • Allodynia—pain from light touch, clothing
  • Hyperalgesia—increased sensitivity to painful stimuli
  • Pain may spread beyond the initial area

Sensory Changes

  • Increased sensitivity to touch
  • Temperature sensitivity
  • Pins and needles sensations
  • Numbness

Autonomic Symptoms

  • Skin color changes (red, blue, pale)
  • Temperature changes (hot or cold)
  • Sweating abnormalities
  • Swelling (edema)

Motor/Trophic Changes

  • Decreased range of motion
  • Weakness
  • Tremor or muscle spasms
  • Changes in nail and hair growth
  • Skin texture changes (shiny, thin)
  • Bone changes (osteoporosis)

Symptom Variability

CRPS symptoms can fluctuate throughout the day and over time. Not everyone has all symptoms. The pattern and severity vary significantly between individuals.

Causes and Triggers

Common Triggers

  • Fractures (most common trigger)
  • Sprains and strains
  • Surgery
  • Burns
  • Cuts or needle sticks
  • Immobilization (cast, splint)
  • Sometimes no clear trigger

Who Gets CRPS?

  • Women more often than men (3-4:1)
  • Peak age 40-60, but can occur at any age
  • Upper limbs more common than lower
  • Risk factors not fully understood

Proposed Mechanisms

  • Abnormal inflammatory response
  • Nervous system dysfunction (peripheral and central)
  • Autonomic nervous system changes
  • Brain plasticity changes
  • Immune system involvement

Diagnosis

CRPS is diagnosed clinically using the Budapest Criteria:4

Budapest Criteria

All of the following:

  1. Continuing pain disproportionate to inciting event
  2. Reports at least one symptom in 3 of 4 categories below
  3. Signs at time of evaluation in 2+ categories
  4. No other diagnosis explains the symptoms

Symptom Categories

  • Sensory: Hyperesthesia, allodynia
  • Vasomotor: Temperature or skin color changes
  • Sudomotor/edema: Sweating changes, edema
  • Motor/trophic: Decreased range of motion, weakness, tremor, hair/nail/skin changes

Testing

  • No definitive test for CRPS
  • Tests done to rule out other conditions
  • X-ray may show bone changes
  • Bone scan may show characteristic pattern
  • Thermography may show temperature differences

Treatment

Early, multimodal treatment improves outcomes:5,6

Physical and Occupational Therapy

The foundation of treatment:

  • Graded motor imagery
  • Mirror therapy
  • Desensitization techniques
  • Gentle range of motion
  • Progressive weight bearing
  • Functional rehabilitation

Medications

  • Neuropathic pain medications: Gabapentin, pregabalin
  • Antidepressants: SNRIs, tricyclics
  • NSAIDs: For inflammation
  • Corticosteroids: Short courses, especially early
  • Bisphosphonates: May help bone-related symptoms
  • Topical treatments: Lidocaine, capsaicin

Interventional Treatments

  • Sympathetic nerve blocks: May provide relief
  • Spinal cord stimulation: For refractory cases
  • Intrathecal drug delivery: Severe cases
  • Ketamine infusions: Research ongoing

Psychological Support

  • Pain psychology
  • CBT for pain coping
  • Treatment for depression/anxiety
  • Stress management

Early Treatment Is Crucial

CRPS responds best to early treatment. If you suspect CRPS, seek evaluation promptly. Delay can lead to more persistent symptoms. A multidisciplinary approach with rehabilitation at its core offers the best outcomes.

Living with CRPS

Daily Management

  • Protect the affected limb from injury
  • Keep moving within tolerance
  • Manage stress—it can worsen symptoms
  • Avoid temperature extremes
  • Pace activities

Coping Strategies

  • Connect with support groups
  • Educate family and friends
  • Work with pain psychologist
  • Focus on function, not just pain
  • Celebrate progress, however small

Prognosis

  • Varies widely between individuals
  • Many improve significantly, especially with early treatment
  • Some have persistent symptoms
  • Complete resolution possible, especially in children
  • Ongoing management often needed
