Trigeminal Neuralgia

Severe shock-like facial pain

What Is Trigeminal Neuralgia?

Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) is a chronic pain condition affecting the trigeminal nerve, which carries sensation from the face to the brain. It causes sudden, severe, shock-like facial pain that can be debilitating. It's often called "the suicide disease" due to its severity.1

Symptoms2

  • Sudden, severe, shock-like or stabbing pain
  • Attacks last seconds to minutes
  • Usually affects one side of face
  • Pain in cheek, jaw, teeth, gums, or lips
  • Triggered by everyday activities
  • Pain-free periods between attacks
  • May worsen over time

Common Triggers

  • Touching the face
  • Chewing
  • Speaking
  • Brushing teeth
  • Shaving
  • Wind or breeze on face
  • Smiling

Causes

  • Classic TN - Usually blood vessel compressing nerve
  • Secondary TN - Caused by MS, tumor, or other conditions
  • Nerve damage or dysfunction

Diagnosis

  • Clinical history is key
  • Neurological examination
  • MRI to rule out other causes
  • Response to carbamazepine supports diagnosis

Treatment3

Medications (First-Line)

  • Carbamazepine (most effective)
  • Oxcarbazepine
  • Baclofen
  • Lamotrigine
  • Other anticonvulsants

Surgical Options

  • Microvascular decompression - Moves vessel from nerve
  • Gamma knife - Focused radiation
  • Rhizotomy - Selectively damages nerve fibers
  • Balloon compression

Living with TN

  • Identify and avoid triggers when possible
  • Use straws for drinking
  • Eat soft foods during flares
  • Protect face from wind
  • Seek psychological support
