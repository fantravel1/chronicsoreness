Trigeminal Neuralgia

Understanding one of the most painful conditions known

What Is Trigeminal Neuralgia?

Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) is a chronic pain condition affecting the trigeminal nerve, which carries sensation from the face to the brain. It causes sudden, severe, shock-like facial pain that can be triggered by everyday activities like eating, talking, or touching the face.1

Often called the "suicide disease" due to its severity, TN is considered one of the most painful conditions in medicine.

Symptoms

  • Sudden, severe, stabbing or electric shock-like pain
  • Pain lasting seconds to minutes
  • Pain usually one-sided
  • Affects cheek, jaw, teeth, gums, lips, or eye/forehead
  • Triggered by light touch, eating, speaking, wind
  • Pain-free intervals between attacks
  • Episodes may increase in frequency over time

Types

  • Type 1 (Classic) - Sudden, intense, brief attacks
  • Type 2 (Atypical) - Constant aching, burning with occasional sharp attacks

Causes

  • Vascular compression - Blood vessel pressing on nerve (most common)2
  • Multiple sclerosis - Demyelination of nerve
  • Tumor - Compressing nerve
  • Stroke or trauma
  • Surgical injury

Diagnosis

  • Characteristic symptom description
  • Neurological examination
  • MRI to rule out tumors, MS, identify vascular compression

Treatment

Medications

  • Carbamazepine - First-line; effective in 70-80%3
  • Oxcarbazepine - Alternative with fewer side effects
  • Baclofen - May be added
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin
  • Lamotrigine

Surgical Options

When medications fail or become intolerable:

  • Microvascular decompression (MVD) - Most effective long-term
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife)
  • Percutaneous procedures - Balloon compression, glycerol injection, radiofrequency lesioning

Living with TN

  • Identify and avoid triggers when possible
  • Protect face from cold/wind
  • Eat soft foods during flares
  • Use a straw for cold drinks
  • Seek support - this is a challenging condition
