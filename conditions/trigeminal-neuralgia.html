Trigeminal Neuralgia

Severe facial pain from nerve dysfunction

What Is Trigeminal Neuralgia?

Trigeminal neuralgia (TN), sometimes called tic douloureux, is a chronic pain condition affecting the trigeminal nerve, which carries sensation from your face to your brain. It causes sudden, severe, electric shock-like or stabbing facial pain that can last from a few seconds to several minutes.1

Symptoms2

  • Sudden, severe, shock-like or stabbing pain
  • Usually on one side of face
  • Episodes last seconds to minutes
  • May have multiple attacks per day
  • Pain-free periods between attacks
  • Often described as worst pain ever experienced

Triggers

  • Touching the face
  • Chewing or eating
  • Talking
  • Brushing teeth
  • Shaving
  • Washing face
  • Cold wind on face

Types

Type 1 (Classic)

  • Sharp, shooting, shock-like pain
  • Distinct attacks with pain-free intervals
  • More common type

Type 2 (Atypical)

  • Constant aching, burning pain
  • May overlap with Type 1
  • Often harder to treat

Causes3

  • Blood vessel compressing nerve (most common)
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • Tumor (rare)
  • Injury to nerve
  • Sometimes cause unknown

Treatment

Medications (First-Line)

  • Carbamazepine (most effective)
  • Oxcarbazepine
  • Baclofen
  • Lamotrigine

Procedures and Surgery

  • Microvascular decompression surgery
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife)
  • Balloon compression
  • Glycerol injection
  • Radiofrequency thermal lesioning

Living with TN

  • Identify and avoid triggers when possible
  • Eat softer foods during flares
  • Protect face from cold wind
  • Use a soft toothbrush
  • Join support groups

When to Seek Help

  • Any sudden, severe facial pain
  • Pain not responding to treatment
  • Increasing frequency of attacks
  • Associated neurological symptoms
