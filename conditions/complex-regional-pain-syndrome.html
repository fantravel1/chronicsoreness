Complex Regional Pain Syndrome

Chronic pain with sensory and motor changes

What Is CRPS?

Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) is a chronic pain condition typically affecting a limb after injury, surgery, or other trauma. The pain is disproportionate to the original injury and is accompanied by sensory, motor, and autonomic changes. It was previously called reflex sympathetic dystrophy (RSD).1

Types

  • CRPS Type I - No confirmed nerve injury (most common)
  • CRPS Type II - Known nerve injury present

Symptoms2

Pain

  • Severe burning, throbbing, or aching pain
  • Pain out of proportion to injury
  • Extreme sensitivity to touch (allodynia)
  • Pain may spread beyond original area

Sensory Changes

  • Increased sensitivity to pain
  • Temperature sensitivity
  • Numbness or tingling

Autonomic Changes

  • Skin color changes (red, blue, pale)
  • Temperature changes in affected limb
  • Sweating abnormalities
  • Swelling

Motor Changes

  • Weakness
  • Tremor
  • Decreased range of motion
  • Muscle atrophy (later stages)

Causes

  • Often follows fractures
  • Sprains and strains
  • Surgery
  • Minor trauma
  • Sometimes no clear trigger

Treatment3

Early Treatment Is Key

Better outcomes with early, aggressive treatment.

Rehabilitation

  • Physical therapy (essential)
  • Occupational therapy
  • Graded motor imagery
  • Mirror therapy
  • Desensitization techniques

Medications

  • Anticonvulsants
  • Antidepressants
  • Bisphosphonates
  • NSAIDs
  • Topical treatments

Interventional

  • Sympathetic nerve blocks
  • Spinal cord stimulation
  • Intrathecal drug delivery

Psychological Support

CRPS can significantly impact mental health. Psychological support is an important part of treatment.
