Morton's Neuroma

Nerve thickening in the foot

What Is Morton's Neuroma?

Morton's neuroma is a thickening of the tissue around a nerve leading to the toes, usually between the third and fourth toes. It's not technically a tumor (despite the name) but rather fibrous tissue that forms around the nerve due to irritation or compression.1

Symptoms2

  • Pain in the ball of the foot
  • Burning sensation radiating to toes
  • Feeling of standing on a pebble or fold in sock
  • Numbness or tingling in toes
  • Pain worse with walking, relieved by rest
  • Symptoms often progress over time

Causes and Risk Factors

  • High heels and tight shoes
  • High-impact activities
  • Foot deformities (bunions, hammertoes, flat feet)
  • Repetitive trauma to the forefoot
  • More common in women

Diagnosis

  • Physical examination
  • Mulder's sign (clicking with compression)
  • Ultrasound (often helpful)
  • MRI if needed
  • X-rays (to rule out other conditions)

Treatment3

Conservative (First Line)

  • Wide, low-heeled shoes
  • Metatarsal pads
  • Custom orthotics
  • Ice and rest
  • NSAIDs for pain

Injections

  • Corticosteroid injections
  • May provide temporary relief
  • Can be repeated cautiously
  • Alcohol sclerosing injections (some centers)

Surgery

  • Neurectomy (nerve removal)
  • Decompression surgery
  • Usually for failed conservative treatment
  • Generally successful

Prevention

  • Wear properly fitting shoes
  • Avoid narrow toe boxes
  • Limit high heel use
  • Use arch supports if needed

Living with Morton's Neuroma

  • Shoe choice is critical
  • Remove shoes and massage foot when possible
  • Many improve with conservative measures
  • Surgery has good success rates when needed
