Cubital Tunnel Syndrome

Ulnar nerve compression at the elbow

What Is Cubital Tunnel Syndrome?

Cubital tunnel syndrome occurs when the ulnar nerveâ€”the "funny bone" nerveâ€”becomes compressed or irritated as it passes through the cubital tunnel at the inside of the elbow. It's the second most common nerve compression syndrome after carpal tunnel, causing numbness and weakness in the hand.1

Symptoms2

  • Numbness and tingling in ring and pinky fingers
  • Symptoms often worse with elbow bent
  • Aching pain inside of elbow
  • Weakness in hand grip
  • Difficulty with fine motor tasks
  • Waking with numb hand
  • Clumsiness in affected hand

Causes and Risk Factors

  • Prolonged elbow bending (sleeping, phone use)
  • Leaning on elbows
  • Repetitive elbow flexion
  • Previous elbow injury or fracture
  • Arthritis
  • Bone spurs
  • Anatomy variations

Diagnosis3

  • Physical examination
  • Tinel's sign at elbow
  • Elbow flexion test
  • Nerve conduction studies
  • X-rays (to rule out bone issues)
  • MRI (sometimes)

Treatment

Conservative

  • Avoid prolonged elbow bending
  • Elbow pad or splint
  • Night splint to keep elbow straight
  • Activity modification
  • NSAIDs for inflammation

Surgery

  • Cubital tunnel release
  • Ulnar nerve transposition
  • Medial epicondylectomy
  • Usually when conservative fails

Self-Care Tips

  • Don't sleep with elbows bent
  • Wrap towel around elbow at night
  • Avoid leaning on elbows
  • Take breaks from activities
  • Use hands-free devices for phone

Prognosis

  • Many improve with conservative care
  • Surgery often successful if needed
  • Early treatment improves outcomes
  • Severe cases may have permanent weakness
