Degenerative Disc Disease

Age-related changes in spinal discs

What Is Degenerative Disc Disease?

Degenerative disc disease (DDD) isn't really a "disease" but refers to normal age-related changes in spinal discs. The discs lose water content and become less flexible, which can cause pain. Nearly everyone has disc degeneration as they age, but not everyone develops symptoms.1

Understanding Spinal Discs

  • Discs are cushions between vertebrae
  • Outer ring (annulus) surrounds gel-like center (nucleus)
  • Act as shock absorbers
  • Allow spine to bend and twist

What Happens with Age2

  • Discs lose water content (dry out)
  • Become thinner and less flexible
  • Small tears can develop in outer ring
  • Disc height decreases
  • May bulge or herniate

Symptoms

  • Pain in neck or back (depending on location)
  • Pain worse with sitting, bending, lifting
  • Pain may radiate to arms or legs
  • Periods of severe pain (flare-ups)
  • Some people have no pain despite changes on imaging

Diagnosis3

  • Medical history and physical exam
  • X-rays (show disc space narrowing)
  • MRI (detailed view of discs)
  • Important: imaging findings don't always correlate with pain

Treatment

Conservative (First-Line)

  • Physical therapy
  • Exercise (core strengthening)
  • Over-the-counter pain relievers
  • Heat or ice
  • Activity modification

Medical Treatments

  • Prescription medications
  • Epidural steroid injections
  • Physical therapy programs

Surgery (Rarely Needed)

  • Spinal fusion
  • Artificial disc replacement
  • Usually only for failed conservative treatment

Important Perspective

  • Disc degeneration is normal aging
  • Many people with "bad" discs have no pain
  • Pain doesn't always mean damage is occurring
  • Most improve without surgery
  • Staying active is usually better than rest

Living with DDD

  • Stay active within comfort
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Practice good posture
  • Strengthen core muscles
  • Avoid prolonged sitting
