Degenerative Disc Disease

Understanding age-related changes in spinal discs

Key Takeaways

  • DDD is not actually a disease—it's normal age-related changes in spinal discs
  • Most people with disc degeneration on imaging have no pain
  • Conservative treatment is effective for most people
  • Exercise and staying active are among the best treatments

What Is Degenerative Disc Disease?

Degenerative disc disease (DDD) refers to changes in the spinal discs that occur with aging. Despite its name, it is not a disease but rather a normal part of aging. The term describes the gradual deterioration of the intervertebral discs that cushion the vertebrae.1

of people over 60 have disc degeneration on imaging—most without pain2

What Happens to Discs

  • Water loss: Discs lose hydration and become less flexible
  • Height loss: Discs become thinner
  • Tears: Small tears may develop in the outer layer (annulus)
  • Bulging: Disc material may bulge outward
  • Bone spurs: May develop at disc margins

Important Perspective

Disc degeneration is like gray hair for the spine—it happens to almost everyone with age. Finding it on an MRI does not mean it is causing your pain. Many people with significant degeneration have no symptoms, while some with minimal changes have pain. Correlation does not equal causation.

Symptoms

When DDD does cause symptoms, they may include:3

Common Symptoms

  • Low back or neck pain (depending on location)
  • Pain that worsens with sitting, bending, lifting, or twisting
  • Pain that improves with movement, walking, or lying down
  • Periods of severe pain that come and go
  • Stiffness

If Nerves Are Affected

  • Radiating pain into arms or legs
  • Numbness or tingling
  • Weakness

Symptom Patterns

  • Symptoms often fluctuate over time
  • Flares may last days to weeks
  • Many people improve over time even without treatment
  • Activity modification helps during flares

Risk Factors

  • Age: Primary factor—changes begin in 20s-30s
  • Genetics: Strong hereditary component
  • Smoking: Accelerates disc degeneration
  • Obesity: Increased load on discs
  • Occupation: Heavy lifting, vibration exposure
  • Previous injury: May accelerate changes

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

  • Medical history and symptom assessment
  • Physical examination
  • Neurological testing if needed

Imaging

  • X-rays: Show disc space narrowing, bone spurs
  • MRI: Shows disc hydration, bulges, nerve compression
  • Important: Imaging findings must correlate with symptoms

Imaging Caution

Studies show that many "abnormal" MRI findings are common in people without pain. Getting imaging too early can lead to unnecessary worry, treatments, and even surgery. Imaging is most useful when symptoms are severe, progressive, or don't respond to conservative care.2

Treatment

Most people improve with conservative treatment:4

Stay Active

  • Avoid prolonged bed rest
  • Gentle movement helps healing
  • Modify activities as needed, but stay moving

Exercise

  • Core strengthening: Supports the spine
  • Flexibility: Maintains mobility
  • Aerobic exercise: Walking, swimming, cycling
  • Yoga/Pilates: May help with flexibility and strength

Physical Therapy

  • Individualized exercise program
  • Manual therapy
  • Education about spine health
  • Body mechanics training

Medications

  • NSAIDs: For pain and inflammation
  • Acetaminophen: For pain
  • Muscle relaxants: Short-term for spasm
  • Avoid: Long-term opioids generally not recommended

Other Treatments

  • Heat or ice
  • Massage therapy
  • Chiropractic care
  • Acupuncture
  • Epidural injections (for severe flares)

Surgery

Rarely needed; considered only when:

  • Conservative treatment fails after 6+ months
  • Significant neurological deficits
  • Severe, disabling pain
  • Options include fusion or disc replacement

Living with DDD

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Maintain healthy weight: Reduces disc load
  • Don't smoke: Smoking worsens disc health
  • Good posture: Reduces strain
  • Ergonomics: Proper workstation setup
  • Lift properly: Bend at knees, keep back straight

Managing Flares

  • Modify activities but avoid complete rest
  • Use pain medications as needed
  • Apply heat or ice
  • Gentle stretching
  • Most flares resolve within days to weeks

Mindset

  • DDD is manageable—not a life sentence
  • Many people live active lives with disc changes
  • Focus on function, not imaging findings
  • Avoid catastrophizing—it worsens outcomes
