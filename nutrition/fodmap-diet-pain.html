Low FODMAP Diet and Pain

Understanding how reducing fermentable carbohydrates may help manage chronic pain and gut-related symptoms

13 min read

What Is the Low FODMAP Diet?

The Low FODMAP diet is a scientifically validated dietary approach developed by researchers at Monash University in Australia. FODMAP stands for Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, and Polyols—short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine and rapidly fermented by gut bacteria, producing gas and drawing water into the intestines.

Originally developed for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), the Low FODMAP diet has shown significant promise for reducing both gastrointestinal symptoms and systemic pain. A landmark study in Gastroenterology (2014) found that 76% of IBS patients experienced significant symptom improvement on a low FODMAP diet.1

Nutrition Facts: Low FODMAP at a Glance

  • Type: Three-phase elimination diet (restriction, reintroduction, personalization)
  • Elimination phase: 2–6 weeks
  • FODMAP groups: Fructans, galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS), lactose, excess fructose, polyols (sorbitol/mannitol)
  • Success rate: 50–80% symptom improvement in IBS patients
  • Evidence level: Strong (multiple RCTs, Monash University research program)
  • Best used with: Guidance from a FODMAP-trained dietitian

The Gut-Pain Connection

The relationship between gut health and systemic pain is mediated through several well-established pathways:

  • Visceral hypersensitivity: Up to 60% of IBS patients experience heightened pain perception in the gut, and this central sensitization often overlaps with conditions like fibromyalgia and chronic widespread pain2
  • Gut-brain axis: The bidirectional communication between the enteric nervous system and the central nervous system means that gut inflammation can amplify pain processing throughout the body
  • Systemic inflammation: Fermentation of FODMAPs can produce lipopolysaccharides and other pro-inflammatory compounds that enter the bloodstream through a permeable gut lining
  • Serotonin production: Approximately 95% of the body’s serotonin is produced in the gut; disrupted gut function can affect pain modulation and mood regulation
  • Microbiome imbalances: High FODMAP intake can feed certain bacterial populations that produce excessive gas, inflammatory metabolites, and compounds that activate pain receptors

Research in Pain (2018) demonstrated that fibromyalgia patients who followed a low FODMAP diet experienced significant reductions in widespread pain, somatic symptoms, and gastrointestinal distress compared to controls.3

Phase 1: The Elimination Phase

During the elimination phase (2–6 weeks), all high FODMAP foods are minimized:

High FODMAP Foods to Limit

  • Fructans: Wheat, rye, onions, garlic, artichokes, asparagus
  • GOS: Legumes (chickpeas, lentils, beans), cashews, pistachios
  • Lactose: Milk, soft cheeses, yogurt, ice cream
  • Excess fructose: Apples, pears, mangoes, honey, high-fructose corn syrup
  • Polyols: Stone fruits (peaches, plums, cherries), mushrooms, cauliflower, artificial sweeteners (sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol)

Low FODMAP Alternatives

  • Proteins: All unprocessed meats, fish, eggs, firm tofu
  • Grains: Rice, oats, quinoa, gluten-free bread (check ingredients)
  • Vegetables: Carrots, zucchini, bell peppers, spinach, tomatoes, potatoes, eggplant
  • Fruits: Strawberries, blueberries, oranges, grapes, kiwi, bananas (firm)
  • Dairy alternatives: Lactose-free milk, hard cheeses, almond milk
  • Flavor: Garlic-infused oil (FODMAP-safe), chives, ginger, lemongrass

Phase 2: Reintroduction

After symptom improvement, each FODMAP group is tested individually over 3 days to identify personal tolerance thresholds:

  • Day 1: Small portion of the challenge food
  • Day 2: Medium portion
  • Day 3: Large portion
  • Days 4–6: Washout period (return to low FODMAP baseline)

This process identifies not just which FODMAPs you react to, but at what dose. Many people discover they can tolerate small amounts of certain FODMAPs, allowing a much more varied long-term diet.

Phase 3: Personalization

The long-term personalized phase incorporates your reintroduction findings into a sustainable eating pattern. The goal is to eat the widest variety possible while keeping symptoms controlled. Key principles include:

  • Include tolerated FODMAPs at identified threshold levels
  • Avoid only the specific FODMAP groups that trigger symptoms
  • Periodically rechallenge previously reactive foods, as tolerance can change over time
  • Ensure adequate fiber intake through tolerated prebiotic sources to maintain gut microbiome diversity

Low FODMAP for Specific Pain Conditions

  • Fibromyalgia: A 2017 study showed significant improvement in pain scores, bloating, and quality of life in fibromyalgia patients following a low FODMAP diet3
  • Endometriosis: Research suggests that GI symptoms are common in endometriosis, and a low FODMAP diet may help manage the overlapping IBS-type symptoms
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: The gut-brain connection in CFS/ME suggests that reducing gut-derived inflammation may improve fatigue and pain
  • Interstitial cystitis: Many high FODMAP foods overlap with known bladder irritants, and gut inflammation may worsen pelvic pain conditions
  • Migraine: Some FODMAPs (particularly histamine-producing fermented foods) may trigger migraines in susceptible individuals

Practical Meal Planning Tips

  • Use the Monash University FODMAP app for accurate food data
  • Batch cook low FODMAP staples on low-pain days
  • Keep garlic-infused olive oil on hand for flavor without fructans
  • Use the green parts of spring onions/scallions instead of regular onions
  • Choose firm bananas over ripe ones (lower in FODMAPs)
  • Read labels carefully: wheat, onion powder, garlic powder, and inulin are hidden high FODMAP ingredients
  • At restaurants, request simple grilled proteins with safe vegetables and ask about hidden onion and garlic in sauces

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the low FODMAP diet gluten-free?

Not exactly. The low FODMAP diet limits wheat because of its fructan content, not its gluten. Small amounts of wheat-based soy sauce or sourdough bread (where fermentation reduces fructans) may be tolerated.

Can I follow low FODMAP long-term?

The strict elimination phase should not be followed long-term, as it can reduce beneficial gut bacteria diversity. The personalized phase is designed for long-term maintenance with the broadest tolerable diet.

How quickly will I see results for pain?

Gut symptoms often improve within 1–2 weeks. Systemic pain improvements typically take 4–6 weeks as gut inflammation and permeability gradually improve.

Can I combine low FODMAP with other dietary approaches?

Yes, but combining with other restrictive diets (e.g., AIP) can be very limiting. Work with a dietitian to ensure nutritional adequacy if combining approaches.

Does the low FODMAP diet help with bloating-related pain?

Yes, bloating and distension-related pain are among the most responsive symptoms to FODMAP restriction, with up to 82% of patients reporting improvement.
