Hydration and Pain

The importance of water for pain management

Why Hydration Matters

Adequate hydration is essential for nearly every body function, including those that affect pain. While drinking water won't cure chronic pain, dehydration can make pain worse. Many people with chronic pain don't drink enough, and improving hydration is a simple, low-risk way to potentially reduce pain intensity.1

Dehydration and Pain2

  • Dehydration can trigger headaches
  • Affects muscle function and cramping
  • Impacts disc hydration in spine
  • Affects joint lubrication
  • Can increase fatigue
  • May lower pain threshold

Signs of Dehydration

  • Dark yellow urine
  • Thirst (often late sign)
  • Dry mouth and lips
  • Headache
  • Fatigue
  • Dizziness
  • Muscle cramps

How Much to Drink3

  • General guideline: 8 cups (64 oz) daily
  • Individual needs vary
  • More if exercising, hot weather
  • Some conditions require different amounts
  • Urine color is good indicator
  • Pale yellow = well hydrated

Hydrating Beverages

Best Choices

  • Water
  • Herbal tea
  • Sparkling water
  • Water with fruit slices

In Moderation

  • Coffee and tea (count partially)
  • Milk
  • Juice (sugar content)

Limit

  • Sugary drinks
  • Alcohol (dehydrating)
  • Excessive caffeine

Practical Tips

  • Start day with a glass of water
  • Carry a water bottle
  • Set reminders to drink
  • Drink before meals
  • Eat water-rich foods (fruits, vegetables)
  • Track intake if helpful

Specific Conditions

Headaches/Migraines

  • Dehydration is common trigger
  • Increasing water may reduce frequency

Back Pain

  • Spinal discs need water for cushioning
  • Dehydration affects disc health

Joint Pain

  • Joint fluid depends on hydration
  • May help joint comfort
