Sugar and Inflammation

How sugar affects chronic pain

The Sugar-Inflammation Connection

Excess sugar consumption is linked to increased inflammation in the body, which may worsen chronic pain conditions. While sugar itself isn't the sole cause of inflammation, high intakeâ€”especially of added sugarsâ€”promotes inflammatory processes. Reducing sugar may help manage pain as part of an overall healthy lifestyle.1

How Sugar Promotes Inflammation2

  • Triggers release of inflammatory cytokines
  • Contributes to weight gain (fat promotes inflammation)
  • Causes blood sugar spikes and crashes
  • May affect gut bacteria (linked to inflammation)
  • Promotes formation of AGEs (advanced glycation end products)

Types of Sugar

Natural Sugars

  • Found in fruits, dairy
  • Come with fiber, nutrients
  • Generally less concerning

Added Sugars

  • Added during processing
  • No nutritional benefit
  • Main concern for health

Hidden Sugar Sources

  • Sauces and condiments
  • Bread and baked goods
  • Flavored yogurt
  • Breakfast cereals
  • Granola bars
  • "Healthy" drinks and smoothies
  • Processed foods

Names for Sugar on Labels3

  • High fructose corn syrup
  • Sucrose, glucose, fructose
  • Maltose, dextrose
  • Cane sugar, cane juice
  • Corn sweetener
  • Anything ending in "-ose"

Recommended Limits

  • AHA: 6 tsp (25g) women, 9 tsp (36g) men
  • Average American consumes 17+ tsp daily
  • Reading labels is essential

Reducing Sugar Intake

Strategies

  • Gradually reduce (sudden cuts can backfire)
  • Read nutrition labels
  • Choose whole foods
  • Drink water instead of sugary beverages
  • Reduce portion sizes of sweets
  • Find satisfying alternatives

Healthier Swaps

  • Fresh fruit for dessert
  • Plain yogurt with berries
  • Dark chocolate (small amounts)
  • Sparkling water for soda
  • Homemade treats (control sugar)

What to Expect

  • May have cravings initially
  • Taste buds adjust in 2-4 weeks
  • May notice improved energy
  • Part of overall dietary approach

Balance and Reality

  • Occasional treats are okay
  • Don't obsess over every gram
  • Focus on overall pattern
  • Sustainable changes matter most
