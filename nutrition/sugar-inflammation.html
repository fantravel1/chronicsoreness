Sugar and Inflammation

How added sugars may affect chronic pain

Sugar and Chronic Pain

Research suggests that high intake of added sugars may promote inflammation in the body. Since many chronic pain conditions involve inflammation, reducing sugar intake is often recommended as part of an anti-inflammatory dietary approach. The evidence is still evolving.1

How Sugar May Promote Inflammation2

  • Spikes blood sugar and insulin
  • Promotes release of inflammatory cytokines
  • May increase advanced glycation end products (AGEs)
  • Can contribute to obesity (which is inflammatory)
  • May alter gut bacteria

Types of Sugars

Added Sugars (Limit These)

  • Table sugar (sucrose)
  • High fructose corn syrup
  • Honey, agave, maple syrup
  • Sugar in processed foods
  • Sugar in beverages

Natural Sugars (Usually Fine)

  • Fruit (comes with fiber, vitamins)
  • Dairy (lactose)
  • Whole foods

Hidden Sources of Added Sugar3

  • Soda and sweetened beverages
  • Fruit juices
  • Breakfast cereals
  • Flavored yogurt
  • Condiments and sauces
  • Bread and baked goods
  • "Healthy" granola bars

How Much Is Too Much?

  • American Heart Association recommends:
  • Women: 25 grams (6 teaspoons) daily max
  • Men: 36 grams (9 teaspoons) daily max
  • Average American consumes 77 grams daily

Reading Labels

  • Check "Added Sugars" on nutrition label
  • Many names for sugar: dextrose, maltose, sucrose, etc.
  • Ingredients ending in "-ose" are usually sugars
  • Compare similar products

Reducing Sugar Gradually

  • Don't try to eliminate all at once
  • Start with sweetened beverages
  • Read labels and choose lower-sugar options
  • Reduce sugar in recipes by 1/3
  • Use fruit for natural sweetness
  • Let taste buds adjust (takes 2-4 weeks)

Sugar and Pain: The Evidence

  • Limited direct studies on sugar and pain
  • Better evidence for overall healthy diet patterns
  • Reducing sugar unlikely to hurt
  • May help through weight management
  • Part of overall anti-inflammatory approach

Realistic Approach

  • Aim for reduction, not perfection
  • Focus on major sources first
  • Allow occasional treats
  • Overall dietary pattern matters most
