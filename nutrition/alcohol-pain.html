Alcohol and Pain

Understanding the complex relationship

The Complicated Picture

Alcohol and chronic pain have a complex relationship. Some people use alcohol to cope with pain, and while it may provide temporary relief, regular use can worsen pain in the long run and create dangerous interactions with medications. Understanding these issues helps you make informed choices.1

Short-Term Effects2

  • May temporarily reduce pain perception
  • Muscle relaxation
  • Stress and anxiety reduction
  • These effects are temporary

Long-Term Problems

  • Can increase pain sensitivity over time
  • Disrupts sleep (worsens pain)
  • Increases inflammation
  • Can cause its own pain (alcoholic neuropathy)
  • Worsens depression and anxiety
  • Risk of dependence

Medication Interactions3

Dangerous Combinations

  • Opioids (respiratory depression, death)
  • Benzodiazepines (extreme sedation)
  • Muscle relaxants (increased sedation)
  • Sleep medications (dangerous sedation)

Other Risky Interactions

  • NSAIDs (increased GI bleeding risk)
  • Acetaminophen (liver damage)
  • Antidepressants (increased side effects)
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin (increased sedation)

Signs of Problematic Use

  • Using alcohol specifically for pain relief
  • Drinking more over time
  • Difficulty cutting back
  • Drinking despite negative consequences
  • Using alcohol with medications

If You Choose to Drink

  • Know your medication interactions
  • Stick to moderate amounts
  • Don't drink for pain relief
  • Separate alcohol and medication timing
  • Be honest with your doctor

Moderate Drinking Defined

  • Up to 1 drink/day for women
  • Up to 2 drinks/day for men
  • But less is better for chronic pain

Getting Help

  • Talk to your doctor honestly
  • Addiction specialists
  • Support groups (AA and others)
  • Treatment programs
  • Mental health support
