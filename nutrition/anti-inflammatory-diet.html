Anti-Inflammatory Diet

Eating to reduce inflammation and support pain management

Diet and Inflammation

Chronic inflammation is linked to many pain conditions. While diet alone won't cure chronic pain, eating patterns that reduce inflammation may help as part of a comprehensive approach. The Mediterranean diet is the most studied anti-inflammatory eating pattern.1

Foods to Emphasize

  • Fatty fish - Salmon, sardines, mackerel (omega-3s)
  • Vegetables - Especially leafy greens, colorful varieties
  • Fruits - Berries, cherries, citrus
  • Whole grains - Oats, quinoa, brown rice
  • Nuts and seeds - Walnuts, almonds, flaxseed
  • Olive oil - Extra virgin
  • Legumes - Beans, lentils
  • Herbs and spices - Turmeric, ginger, garlic

Foods to Limit2

  • Processed foods - High in additives, refined ingredients
  • Sugar and refined carbs - White bread, pastries, soda
  • Red and processed meat - Bacon, sausage, deli meats
  • Trans fats - Fried foods, some margarines
  • Excessive omega-6 - Vegetable oils (corn, soybean)
  • Excessive alcohol

Evidence3

  • Mediterranean diet associated with lower inflammatory markers
  • May help with rheumatoid arthritis symptoms
  • Some evidence for fibromyalgia
  • Benefits cardiovascular and metabolic health
  • No specific "pain diet" with strong evidence

Practical Tips

  • Make gradual changes, not drastic overhauls
  • Focus on adding good foods, not just restricting
  • Cook at home more often
  • Read labels - avoid added sugars, trans fats
  • Stay hydrated
  • Consider working with registered dietitian

Special Considerations

  • Elimination diets may help identify triggers
  • Some conditions may benefit from specific approaches (e.g., low FODMAP for IBS)
  • Weight management itself reduces inflammation
