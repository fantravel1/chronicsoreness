Anti-Inflammatory Diet

Eating to reduce inflammation and pain

The Concept

Chronic inflammation is linked to many pain conditions. While diet alone won't cure chronic pain, eating patterns that reduce inflammation may help manage it. The anti-inflammatory diet emphasizes whole foods, healthy fats, and plant-based foods while limiting processed foods, sugar, and unhealthy fats.1

How Food Affects Inflammation2

  • Some foods promote inflammation
  • Others have anti-inflammatory effects
  • Overall pattern matters more than single foods
  • Mediterranean diet is best studied
  • Effects take weeks to months

Foods to Emphasize

Vegetables

  • Leafy greens (spinach, kale, chard)
  • Colorful vegetables
  • Cruciferous (broccoli, cauliflower)
  • Tomatoes

Fruits

  • Berries (especially blueberries)
  • Cherries
  • Citrus fruits
  • Apples

Healthy Fats

  • Extra virgin olive oil
  • Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel)
  • Nuts (walnuts, almonds)
  • Avocados

Other

  • Whole grains
  • Legumes (beans, lentils)
  • Green tea
  • Herbs and spices (turmeric, ginger)

Foods to Limit3

  • Processed and ultra-processed foods
  • Refined sugars and carbohydrates
  • Red and processed meats
  • Fried foods
  • Trans fats and hydrogenated oils
  • Excessive alcohol
  • Sugary beverages

Mediterranean Diet Pattern

  • Most evidence-based approach
  • Plant foods as foundation
  • Olive oil as primary fat
  • Fish 2+ times per week
  • Limited red meat
  • Moderate wine (optional)

Getting Started

  • Make gradual changes
  • Add more vegetables first
  • Switch to olive oil
  • Reduce processed foods slowly
  • Don't aim for perfection
  • Consider consulting a dietitian

What to Expect

  • Not a quick fix
  • Changes may take 2-3 months
  • Part of overall management plan
  • May improve energy too
  • Benefits extend beyond pain

Cautions

  • Individual responses vary
  • Food sensitivities differ
  • Don't replace medical treatment
  • Watch for unrealistic claims
