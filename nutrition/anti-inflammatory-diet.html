Anti-Inflammatory Diet

Using food to help manage chronic inflammation and pain

The Inflammation Connection

Chronic low-grade inflammation contributes to many pain conditions including fibromyalgia, rheumatoid arthritis, and osteoarthritis. While diet alone won't cure chronic pain, anti-inflammatory eating patterns may help reduce symptoms and improve overall health.1,2

What is an Anti-Inflammatory Diet?

Rather than a specific diet, "anti-inflammatory" describes eating patterns that:

  • Emphasize whole, minimally processed foods
  • Include abundant fruits and vegetables
  • Favor healthy fats (omega-3s) over inflammatory fats
  • Limit added sugars and refined carbohydrates
  • Include anti-inflammatory compounds (polyphenols, antioxidants)

The Mediterranean Diet: Best Evidence

The Mediterranean diet has the strongest evidence for reducing inflammation and improving chronic pain outcomes.3,4

Mediterranean Diet Components

  • Daily: Vegetables, fruits, whole grains, olive oil, nuts, legumes
  • Weekly: Fish (2-3x), poultry, eggs
  • Moderate: Dairy (yogurt, cheese)
  • Limited: Red meat (few times/month), sweets
  • Primary fat: Extra virgin olive oil

Foods to Emphasize

Fatty Fish

Salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring

Why: Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA, DHA) reduce inflammatory cytokines7

Colorful Vegetables

Leafy greens, tomatoes, peppers, broccoli

Why: Antioxidants and polyphenols combat oxidative stress

Berries

Blueberries, strawberries, cherries

Why: Anthocyanins have anti-inflammatory properties; tart cherry may help pain8

Olive Oil

Extra virgin olive oil

Why: Oleocanthal has ibuprofen-like anti-inflammatory effects9

Nuts

Walnuts, almonds, pistachios

Why: Healthy fats, fiber, anti-inflammatory compounds

Spices

Turmeric, ginger, garlic

Why: Curcumin (turmeric) and gingerols have studied anti-inflammatory effects10

Foods to Limit

Added Sugars

Soda, candy, pastries, sweetened beverages. High sugar intake increases inflammatory markers.11

Refined Carbohydrates

White bread, white rice, pastries. Cause rapid blood sugar spikes and inflammation.

Processed Meats

Hot dogs, bacon, sausage. Associated with higher inflammation and chronic disease.

Trans Fats & Excessive Omega-6

Fried foods, many packaged snacks. Industrial trans fats increase inflammation.

Practical Implementation

Start Small

  • Don't overhaul everything at once
  • Add one anti-inflammatory food per week
  • Reduce one pro-inflammatory food per week
  • Sustainable changes beat perfect diets you can't maintain

Meal Ideas

Breakfast

  • Oatmeal with berries and walnuts
  • Greek yogurt with fruit and ground flaxseed
  • Eggs with vegetables cooked in olive oil

Lunch

  • Large salad with olive oil dressing, chickpeas, vegetables
  • Whole grain wrap with hummus and vegetables
  • Lentil soup with whole grain bread

Dinner

  • Baked salmon with roasted vegetables
  • Chicken stir-fry with turmeric and ginger
  • Bean and vegetable curry over brown rice

Special Considerations

Elimination Diets

Some people find specific foods trigger their symptoms. Common suspects:

  • Gluten (though only clearly helps if you have celiac disease)
  • Dairy
  • Nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, potatoes, eggplant)—limited evidence
  • Processed foods

Approach: Eliminate suspected food for 2-4 weeks, then reintroduce and monitor symptoms. Work with a dietitian if possible.

Weight Management

For conditions like osteoarthritis, weight loss provides direct benefit by reducing joint stress. A 10% weight loss can reduce knee pain by 50%.12

Realistic Expectations

Diet changes may take weeks to months to show effects on pain. Anti-inflammatory eating is one part of a comprehensive approach—it complements, not replaces, medical treatment. Evidence is strongest for overall dietary patterns, not individual "superfoods."

Working with Limited Energy

When chronic pain or fatigue makes cooking difficult:

  • Batch cook: Prepare large amounts on good days
  • Simple is fine: Rotisserie chicken + bagged salad = healthy meal
  • Frozen vegetables: Just as nutritious as fresh, easier to prepare
  • No-cook options: Canned beans, pre-washed greens, nuts, fruit
  • Accept help: Meal delivery services, family help
