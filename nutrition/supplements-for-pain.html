Supplements for Chronic Pain

Evidence-based guide to supplements that may support pain management

Key Takeaways

  • Some supplements have evidence supporting their use for specific pain conditions
  • Quality varies significantly—choose reputable brands
  • Supplements can interact with medications—consult your healthcare provider
  • Supplements work best as part of a comprehensive treatment approach

Understanding Supplement Evidence

The evidence for supplements in chronic pain management varies considerably. Some have strong research support, while others have limited or preliminary evidence. Always discuss supplements with your healthcare provider before starting them.1

Important Considerations

  • Supplements are not regulated like medications
  • Quality and purity vary between brands
  • Interactions with medications are possible
  • More is not always better—follow recommended doses
  • Results may take weeks to months

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Omega-3s have anti-inflammatory properties and are among the most studied supplements for pain.2

Evidence

  • Moderate evidence for rheumatoid arthritis pain and stiffness
  • May reduce need for NSAIDs in some patients
  • Possible benefits for other inflammatory conditions

Dosage

  • Typical dose: 1-3 grams EPA+DHA daily
  • Higher doses for inflammatory arthritis (under medical supervision)
  • Effects may take 2-3 months to notice

Considerations

  • Can increase bleeding risk—use caution with blood thinners
  • May cause fishy breath or GI upset
  • Choose products tested for mercury and contaminants
  • Prescription omega-3s available for higher doses

Vitamin D

Vitamin D deficiency is common and has been associated with chronic pain.3

Evidence

  • Deficiency associated with increased pain sensitivity
  • Correcting deficiency may reduce pain in some individuals
  • Important for bone health and immune function

Testing and Dosage

  • Get vitamin D levels tested before supplementing
  • Typical supplementation: 1,000-4,000 IU daily
  • Higher doses may be needed to correct deficiency
  • Take with fat for better absorption

Who Should Consider Testing

  • People with chronic widespread pain
  • Those with limited sun exposure
  • Darker skin tones (higher risk of deficiency)
  • Older adults
  • Those with malabsorption conditions

Turmeric/Curcumin

Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, has anti-inflammatory properties.4

Evidence

  • Promising evidence for osteoarthritis pain
  • May be comparable to NSAIDs for some conditions
  • Anti-inflammatory effects demonstrated in studies

Dosage and Absorption

  • Standard curcumin is poorly absorbed
  • Look for enhanced absorption formulas (with piperine, phospholipids, or nanoparticles)
  • Typical dose: 500-2,000 mg of enhanced formulas

Considerations

  • May interact with blood thinners
  • Can cause GI upset in some people
  • Avoid high doses before surgery
  • Quality varies significantly between products

Magnesium

Magnesium plays a role in muscle function and nervous system regulation.5

Evidence

  • May help with muscle cramps and tension
  • Some evidence for migraine prevention
  • Deficiency is common and linked to pain sensitivity

Forms and Dosage

  • Magnesium glycinate: Well-absorbed, gentle on stomach
  • Magnesium citrate: Good absorption, may have laxative effect
  • Magnesium oxide: Poorly absorbed, mainly laxative
  • Typical dose: 200-400 mg daily

Considerations

  • Can cause diarrhea at high doses
  • Use caution with kidney disease
  • May interact with certain medications

Glucosamine and Chondroitin

Popular supplements for joint health, particularly osteoarthritis.6

Evidence

  • Mixed evidence—some studies show benefit, others do not
  • May provide modest pain relief for knee osteoarthritis
  • Large trials have been inconsistent
  • Generally considered safe to try

Dosage

  • Glucosamine: 1,500 mg daily
  • Chondroitin: 800-1,200 mg daily
  • May take 2-4 months to see effects

Considerations

  • Glucosamine derived from shellfish—allergy concern
  • Vegetarian options available
  • May affect blood sugar in diabetics
  • Possible interaction with blood thinners

SAMe (S-adenosyl-L-methionine)

A compound naturally produced in the body, involved in many processes.7

Evidence

  • Some evidence for osteoarthritis pain
  • May also help with depression
  • Limited but promising research

Dosage

  • Typical dose: 600-1,200 mg daily
  • Start low and increase gradually
  • Take on empty stomach

Considerations

  • Expensive supplement
  • Can cause GI upset, anxiety, insomnia
  • Avoid with bipolar disorder
  • Interactions with antidepressants possible

B Vitamins

B vitamins play roles in nerve function and may help with neuropathic pain.8

Evidence

  • B12 deficiency can cause neuropathy
  • High-dose B vitamins may help diabetic neuropathy
  • B2 (riboflavin) has evidence for migraine prevention

Key B Vitamins for Pain

  • B12: Nerve health, especially important for vegans/vegetarians
  • B6: Nerve function, but toxic at high doses
  • B2 (Riboflavin): 400 mg daily for migraine prevention

Considerations

  • Get B12 tested, especially if vegan or over 50
  • B6 can cause nerve damage at high doses
  • B vitamins are water-soluble and generally safe

Other Supplements of Interest

Boswellia (Frankincense)

  • Anti-inflammatory properties
  • Some evidence for osteoarthritis
  • Generally well-tolerated

Ginger

  • Anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects
  • May help with muscle pain and osteoarthritis
  • Safe in moderate amounts

Alpha-Lipoic Acid

  • Antioxidant with evidence for diabetic neuropathy
  • Typical dose: 600 mg daily
  • Generally well-tolerated

Coenzyme Q10

  • Some evidence for migraine prevention
  • Important for mitochondrial function
  • May help those on statins

Choosing Quality Supplements

Since supplements are not strictly regulated, quality matters:9

What to Look For

  • Third-party testing: USP, NSF, ConsumerLab verification
  • Reputable brands: Established companies with quality control
  • Appropriate doses: Matching research-supported amounts
  • Minimal additives: Fewer unnecessary ingredients

Red Flags

  • Claims that sound too good to be true
  • "Proprietary blends" without specific amounts
  • Extremely low prices
  • No contact information for manufacturer

Working with Your Healthcare Team

  • Inform all providers about supplements you take
  • Bring supplement bottles to appointments
  • Ask about potential interactions
  • Report any side effects
  • Have realistic expectations about results
