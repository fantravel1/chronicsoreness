Yoga for Chronic Pain

Evidence-based guidance on the best yoga styles, modifications, and what to avoid

The Research

Cochrane review (21 trials, 2,223 participants) shows low-to-moderate quality evidence that yoga is slightly better than no exercise for back function and pain.

The American College of Physicians gives a strong recommendation for yoga for chronic low back pain.

Best Yoga Styles for Chronic Pain

Viniyoga

Best for: All chronic pain conditions

  • Gentle, breathing-focused
  • Highly adaptable to individual needs
  • Therapeutic approach
  • Emphasis on function over form

Restorative Yoga

Best for: Fibromyalgia, high pain days, fatigue

  • Fully supported poses with props
  • Very passive—body relaxes into poses
  • Activates parasympathetic system
  • Minimal physical exertion

Therapeutic Yoga

Best for: Specific conditions, recovery

  • Condition-specific sequences
  • Often taught by yoga therapists
  • Medical understanding integrated
  • Individualized approach

Chair Yoga

Best for: Mobility limitations, severe pain

  • All poses done seated or using chair for support
  • Accessible for all fitness levels
  • Can be done at work or home
  • Many free videos available

Styles to Approach with Caution

  • Bikram/Hot Yoga: Heat can worsen some conditions; dehydration risk
  • Ashtanga: Vigorous, set sequence—may be too demanding
  • Power Yoga: Athletic, high-intensity—often not appropriate
  • Vinyasa Flow: Can vary widely; some classes may be too fast-paced

Poses to Avoid or Modify

Use Caution With

  • Neck rolls: Can strain muscles—do half circles instead
  • Deep unsupported backbends: Risk of injury; use props
  • Prolonged overhead arm positions: Can aggravate shoulder issues
  • Any pose causing sharp pain: Pain is a signal to stop or modify
  • Headstands/shoulderstands: Risky for neck; avoid without extensive experience
  • Deep twists: May aggravate back conditions

General Guidelines

Before Class

  • Tell the instructor about your condition
  • Arrive early to set up near props
  • Choose a spot where you can see modifications demonstrated

During Class

  • Pain is not gain: Discomfort is okay; pain is not
  • Modify freely: Every body is different
  • Rest when needed: Child's pose is always an option
  • Skip poses: It's okay to sit out anything that doesn't work
  • Use props: Blocks, straps, bolsters, blankets are your friends

Props for Chronic Pain

  • Blocks: Bring the floor closer to you; reduce strain
  • Straps: Extend reach without straining
  • Bolsters: Support in restorative poses
  • Blankets: Cushioning for knees, support for seated poses
  • Wall: Balance support, gentle stretching

Yoga for Specific Conditions

Fibromyalgia

  • Gentle, restorative styles work best
  • Avoid hot yoga (may trigger flares)
  • Focus on relaxation response
  • Start with short sessions (15-20 minutes)

Low Back Pain

  • Strong recommendation from ACP guidelines
  • Focus on core stabilization poses
  • Avoid deep forward folds if disc issues
  • Cat-cow, bird-dog, bridge are often helpful

Arthritis

  • Gentle movement maintains joint mobility
  • Avoid poses that stress affected joints
  • Warm up thoroughly before practice
  • Chair yoga excellent option

For ME/CFS

If you have ME/CFS with post-exertional malaise, even gentle yoga may trigger crashes. Consider only restorative yoga with fully supported poses, very short sessions, and careful monitoring. See ME/CFS-specific guidance.

Finding the Right Class

  • In-person: Yoga studios, gyms, community centers, hospitals
  • Look for: "Gentle," "therapeutic," "restorative," "all levels"
  • Ask about: Instructor's experience with chronic pain
  • Trial classes: Most studios offer drop-ins or trial periods

Online Resources

  • Yoga with Adriene: Free YouTube; many gentle options
  • Yoga International: Therapeutic yoga courses
  • DoYogaWithMe: Free videos including chair yoga
  • Yoga for Chronic Pain (Karunā): Specifically designed for chronic pain

Building a Home Practice

  • Start small: 10-15 minutes is valuable
  • Consistency over duration: Daily short practice beats occasional long practice
  • Morning or evening: Find what works for your pain patterns
  • Create a space: Dedicated area increases likelihood of practice
