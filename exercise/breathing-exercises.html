Breathing Exercises

Using breath to manage pain and stress

Why Breathing Matters

Breathing is something we do automatically, but how we breathe can significantly affect pain and stress. Slow, deep breathing activates the body's relaxation response, reduces muscle tension, and can influence pain perception. Breathing exercises are free, portable, and can be done anywhere.1

How It Helps Pain2

  • Activates parasympathetic nervous system
  • Reduces stress hormones
  • Relaxes tense muscles
  • Shifts attention from pain
  • Reduces anxiety about pain
  • May reduce pain intensity

Diaphragmatic Breathing3

  1. Sit or lie comfortably
  2. Place one hand on chest, one on belly
  3. Breathe in slowly through nose
  4. Let belly rise (not chest)
  5. Exhale slowly through mouth
  6. Belly falls as you exhale
  7. Practice 5-10 minutes daily

4-7-8 Breathing

  1. Inhale through nose for 4 counts
  2. Hold breath for 7 counts
  3. Exhale through mouth for 8 counts
  4. Repeat 3-4 times
  5. Good for relaxation and sleep

Box Breathing

  1. Inhale for 4 counts
  2. Hold for 4 counts
  3. Exhale for 4 counts
  4. Hold for 4 counts
  5. Repeat several times

Paced Breathing

  • Slow breathing to 5-6 breaths per minute
  • Normal is 12-20 breaths per minute
  • Activates relaxation response
  • Use timer or counting

Tips for Practice

  • Start with 5 minutes daily
  • Practice when relatively calm first
  • Then use during pain or stress
  • Set reminders to practice
  • Apps can guide you
  • Combine with relaxation music

When to Use

  • Pain flares
  • Before stressful situations
  • During procedures
  • Before sleep
  • Anytime you feel tense
  • Regular daily practice

Common Mistakes

  • Breathing too fast
  • Shallow chest breathing
  • Tensing while breathing
  • Forcing or straining
  • Only using during crisis
