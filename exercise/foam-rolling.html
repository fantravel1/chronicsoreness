Foam Rolling Protocols

Self-myofascial release techniques to reduce muscle soreness and improve mobility

What Research Shows

Evidence shows foam rolling improves joint range of motion and reduces delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS). It works by releasing myofascial restrictions and increasing blood flow to tissues.

Basic Protocol

  • Duration: 30-120 seconds per muscle group
  • Technique: Roll slowly from distal (far from body) to proximal (close to body) attachment
  • Pressure: Enough to feel discomfort but not pain
  • Speed: Slow, controlled movements—about 1 inch per second
  • Breathing: Continue breathing; don't hold your breath

Trigger Point Technique

When you find a particularly tender area (trigger point):

  1. Stop rolling when you find the tender spot
  2. Hold pressure on that point for 20-30 seconds
  3. Breathe deeply and try to relax the muscle
  4. Wait until you feel a release or reduction in tenderness
  5. Continue rolling to find other trigger points

Common Areas to Foam Roll

Upper Back (Thoracic)

  • Lie on roller perpendicular to spine
  • Support head with hands
  • Roll from mid-back to shoulders
  • Avoid lower back/lumbar area

IT Band / Outer Thigh

  • Lie on side with roller under outer thigh
  • Roll from hip to just above knee
  • This area is often tender—start gently
  • Stack legs or place top foot on floor to adjust pressure

Quadriceps (Front Thigh)

  • Lie face down with roller under thighs
  • Roll from hip to just above knee
  • Rotate slightly to reach inner and outer quad
  • Support with forearms

Calves

  • Sit with roller under calf
  • Roll from ankle to below knee
  • Cross other leg on top for more pressure
  • Rotate foot in/out to target different areas

Glutes

  • Sit on roller, leaning to one side
  • Cross ankle over opposite knee
  • Roll around the glute muscle
  • Can use a tennis ball for more targeted release

Lats (Side of Back)

  • Lie on side with roller under armpit area
  • Extend arm overhead
  • Roll from armpit toward waist
  • Helpful for shoulder and upper back tension

Areas to Avoid

Don't Foam Roll

  • Lower back (lumbar spine): Can strain muscles; lacks rib cage protection
  • Directly on joints: Knees, elbows, ankles
  • Neck: Too delicate; risk of injury
  • Bony prominences: Hip bones, spine, shin bone
  • Areas with acute injury or inflammation
  • Over bruises, wounds, or skin irritation

Choosing a Foam Roller

Density

  • Soft (white): Best for beginners, sensitive areas, or chronic pain conditions
  • Medium (blue/green): General purpose, most common
  • Firm (black): More intense; for experienced users

Surface Texture

  • Smooth: Consistent pressure, good for beginners
  • Textured/Ridged: More targeted pressure, may be too intense initially

Size

  • Long (36"): Versatile, good for back and legs
  • Short (18"): Portable, good for targeted areas
  • Half roller: Stable base, good for balance exercises too

When to Foam Roll

  • Before exercise: Brief rolling (30-60 sec/area) to warm up
  • After exercise: Longer rolling to reduce DOMS
  • On rest days: Maintenance and recovery
  • Morning: Address overnight stiffness
  • Evening: Relaxation before bed

Alternative Tools

  • Tennis ball: Smaller, more targeted—good for feet, glutes, upper back
  • Lacrosse ball: Firmer than tennis ball for deeper pressure
  • Massage stick: Roll muscles without getting on the floor
  • Peanut ball: Two balls taped together—straddles spine for back work

For Fibromyalgia

Special Considerations

  • Start with the softest roller available
  • Begin with very light pressure
  • Short sessions (5-10 minutes total)
  • May need to start with a ball against a wall (less body weight)
  • Watch for delayed soreness—you may be more sensitive
  • Don't roll during flares
