Heat vs Cold Therapy

When to use heat, when to use cold—evidence-based guidelines for pain management

The Quick Guide

Use HEAT For:

  • Chronic pain (>4 weeks)
  • Muscle tension and stiffness
  • Morning stiffness
  • Before activity to warm muscles
  • Menstrual cramps
  • Arthritis stiffness

Use COLD For:

  • Acute injuries (<48-72 hours)
  • Swelling and inflammation
  • Areas that are hot, red, or swollen
  • Post-exercise soreness
  • Acute flares with visible inflammation
  • Migraines (some people)

Heat Therapy (Thermotherapy)

How It Works

  • Increases blood flow to the area
  • Relaxes muscles and reduces stiffness
  • Decreases muscle spasms
  • Increases tissue elasticity
  • Can trigger endorphin release

Methods

Dry Heat

  • Electric heating pads
  • Microwaveable heat wraps
  • Heat lamps
  • Sauna

Moist Heat

  • Warm bath or shower
  • Hot water bottle
  • Moist heating pads
  • Warm, damp towel

Guidelines

  • Duration: 15-30 minutes maximum
  • Temperature: Warm but not hot (104-113°F / 40-45°C)
  • Protection: Place cloth between heat source and skin
  • Frequency: Can use multiple times daily as needed

Cold Therapy (Cryotherapy)

How It Works

  • Constricts blood vessels, reducing swelling
  • Numbs nerve endings, reducing pain signals
  • Slows cellular metabolism (reduces secondary damage)
  • Decreases inflammation

Methods

Simple Cold

  • Ice pack wrapped in towel
  • Bag of frozen vegetables
  • Cold gel packs
  • Cold water immersion

Commercial Products

  • Reusable cold packs
  • Cold therapy wraps
  • Cooling sprays/gels
  • Ice massage tools

Guidelines

  • Duration: 10-20 minutes maximum
  • Protection: ALWAYS wrap ice in cloth—never apply directly to skin
  • Frequency: Every 2-3 hours for acute injuries
  • Watch for: Skin should be pink, not white or blue

Contrast Therapy

Alternating heat and cold can be helpful for some conditions:

  • Method: 3-4 minutes heat, 1 minute cold, repeat 3-4 cycles
  • End with: Cold for acute conditions, heat for chronic stiffness
  • Best for: Extremity pain, exercise recovery

When to Avoid

Avoid HEAT if:

  • Area is already hot, red, or swollen
  • You have an acute injury (<48 hours)
  • Open wounds or burns
  • Infection is present
  • Impaired sensation (risk of burns)
  • Over areas with decreased circulation

Avoid COLD if:

  • Poor circulation or vascular disease
  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Cold hypersensitivity
  • Over areas with impaired sensation
  • Directly over open wounds

For Specific Conditions

Fibromyalgia

  • Most patients prefer heat
  • Warm baths can help widespread pain
  • Avoid very cold applications (may trigger flares)

Arthritis

  • Morning stiffness: Heat to loosen joints
  • After activity: Cold if joints are swollen
  • Chronic pain: Usually heat

Menstrual Cramps

  • Heat is very effective—studies show it's as effective as NSAIDs
  • Use on lower abdomen or lower back
  • Heating pads or warm baths

Headaches/Migraines

  • Individual response varies
  • Some prefer cold on forehead/temples
  • Some prefer heat on neck and shoulders
  • Experiment to find what works for you

Aquatic Therapy

Warm water therapy combines heat with gentle exercise:

  • Ideal temperature: 82-93°F (28-34°C)
  • Benefits: Buoyancy reduces joint stress while heat relaxes muscles
  • Evidence: Strong support for fibromyalgia, arthritis, chronic back pain
