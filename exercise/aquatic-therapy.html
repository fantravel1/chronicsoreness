Aquatic Therapy

Water-based exercise that's gentle on joints while providing resistance and warmth

Strong Evidence Base

Multiple Cochrane reviews support aquatic therapy for fibromyalgia, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, and chronic low back pain. Water's unique properties make exercise possible for people who can't tolerate land-based activity.

Why Water Works

Buoyancy

Water supports 90% of body weight when submerged to neck. Reduces joint stress dramatically.

Warmth

Warm water relaxes muscles, reduces pain, and improves blood flow. Therapeutic pools are 82-93°F.

Resistance

Water provides gentle, multi-directional resistance for strengthening without weights.

Hydrostatic Pressure

Water pressure reduces swelling and supports circulation. Can decrease edema.

Pool Temperature Guidelines

  • Therapeutic warm water: 92-96°F (33-36°C) — Best for fibromyalgia, arthritis stiffness
  • Warm water: 86-92°F (30-33°C) — Good balance for most chronic pain
  • Standard pool: 78-84°F (26-29°C) — May be too cool for some conditions
  • Hot tub: 100-104°F (38-40°C) — Short duration only; avoid with certain conditions

Types of Aquatic Exercise

Water Walking

  • Walk forward, backward, and sideways
  • Chest-depth water for maximum benefit
  • Start with 5-10 minutes, gradually increase
  • Use pool noodle for flotation if needed

Water Aerobics

  • Low-impact cardiovascular exercise
  • Typically in waist to chest-deep water
  • Group classes provide social support
  • Look for "gentle" or "arthritis-friendly" classes

Ai Chi / Water Tai Chi

  • Slow, flowing movements in warm water
  • Combines breathing with movement
  • Excellent for balance and relaxation
  • Particularly helpful for fibromyalgia

Swimming

  • Full-body, zero-impact workout
  • Backstroke often easiest on neck/back
  • Start with short distances, rest between laps
  • Consider using fins or paddles for variety

For Specific Conditions

Fibromyalgia

  • Warm water (90°F+) is essential—cold water may trigger flares
  • Start with 15-20 minutes maximum
  • Ai Chi and gentle water walking work well
  • Cochrane review: Significant improvement in pain and function

Arthritis

  • Arthritis Foundation offers AFAP (Aquatic Program)
  • Excellent for maintaining joint mobility
  • Warm water helps morning stiffness
  • Avoid if joints are hot/inflamed (acute flare)

Chronic Back Pain

  • Water reduces spinal load significantly
  • Walking and gentle stretching in water
  • Core strengthening exercises in water
  • Strong evidence for pain reduction

ME/CFS Caution

If you have ME/CFS with post-exertional malaise, even gentle aquatic therapy may trigger crashes. Pool temperature, travel to the pool, and getting ready all expend energy. Start with very short sessions (5-10 minutes) and monitor carefully. See pacing guidance.

Finding Aquatic Therapy

Therapy Pools (Warmest)

  • Hospitals and rehabilitation centers: Often have warm therapy pools
  • Physical therapy clinics: Some have on-site pools
  • Arthritis Foundation programs: Partner with YMCAs and other facilities

Community Options

  • YMCA/YWCA: Many offer warm water classes and open swim
  • Community centers: Check pool temperatures before joining
  • Hotels: Some allow day passes; pools may be warmer
  • Fitness centers: Look for warm water options

Questions to Ask

  • What is the pool temperature?
  • Do you offer arthritis or gentle aquatic classes?
  • Is there pool access during quiet times?
  • Are there handrails and accessible entry?
  • Can I try a session before committing?

Getting Started Safely

  1. Get clearance: Check with your provider, especially for heart conditions
  2. Start slow: 10-15 minutes initially, increase gradually
  3. Stay hydrated: You still sweat in water
  4. Listen to your body: Stop if pain increases
  5. Rest after: Account for energy spent getting to/from pool
  6. Go with someone: Safety and support, especially initially

Home Alternatives

If pool access is limited:

  • Warm bath exercises: Simple stretches in the tub
  • Epsom salt soaks: Warmth plus potential muscle relaxation
  • Hot tub: Short sessions for relaxation (not exercise)

Track Your Progress

Document pool sessions and how your body responds to find your optimal routine.
