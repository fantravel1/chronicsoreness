Swimming and Aquatic Exercise

Harnessing the healing power of water for chronic pain relief and improved mobility

Why Water Exercise Works for Chronic Pain

Aquatic exercise offers unique benefits for people with chronic pain conditions. The buoyancy, warmth, and resistance of water create an ideal environment for movement that might be difficult or painful on land.1

Key Benefits of Water Exercise

  • Reduced weight-bearing - Water buoyancy reduces body weight by up to 90%, decreasing stress on joints
  • Natural resistance - Water provides gentle, even resistance for strength building
  • Hydrostatic pressure - Water pressure helps reduce swelling and improve circulation
  • Warmth - Warm water (83-88Â°F) relaxes muscles and reduces pain2
  • Sensory input - Water stimulation can reduce pain perception
  • Fall prevention - Water supports balance, reducing fear of falling

Conditions That Benefit from Aquatic Exercise

Research supports aquatic therapy for numerous chronic pain conditions:

  • Osteoarthritis - Particularly hip and knee arthritis3
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Gentle movement without joint stress
  • Fibromyalgia - Warm water exercise shows significant benefits4
  • Chronic low back pain - Core strengthening with spinal support
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Maintains spinal mobility
  • Post-surgical rehabilitation - Hip, knee, and spine surgeries
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - Low-impact exercise option
  • Obesity-related pain - Exercise without joint stress

Types of Aquatic Exercise

Swimming

Traditional swimming provides excellent cardiovascular and full-body workout:

  • Freestyle/front crawl - Good for overall fitness; rotate stroke to avoid shoulder strain
  • Backstroke - Excellent for back pain; keeps spine neutral
  • Breaststroke - Use with caution if you have neck or lower back issues
  • Sidestroke - Gentle option for beginners

Water Aerobics

Structured classes provide guidance and social support:

  • Shallow water classes (waist to chest deep)
  • Deep water classes with flotation belts
  • Aqua Zumba and other dance-based programs
  • Arthritis-specific aquatic programs (like Arthritis Foundation classes)

Water Walking

Simple and effective, even for beginners:

  • Walk forward, backward, and sideways
  • Vary depth for different challenges
  • Add arm movements for upper body work
  • Progress to jogging as fitness improves

Therapeutic Aquatic Exercise

Specific therapeutic approaches include:

  • Ai Chi - Water-based tai chi movements
  • Watsu - Water-based shiatsu therapy
  • Bad Ragaz Ring Method - Physical therapy technique using buoyancy
  • Halliwick Method - Focuses on balance and independence

Finding the Right Program

Many community pools, YMCAs, and fitness centers offer aquatic programs for people with chronic conditions. Ask about classes specifically designed for arthritis, back pain, or general rehabilitation. Some physical therapy clinics have therapy pools for individualized treatment.

Getting Started Safely

Before You Begin

  • Consult your healthcare provider, especially if you have heart or lung conditions
  • Start slowlyâ€”even water exercise can cause soreness if overdone
  • Choose appropriate water temperature (83-88Â°F is often ideal for chronic pain)
  • Consider working with a physical therapist or certified aquatic instructor initially

Essential Equipment

  • Swimsuit - Comfortable, appropriate for movement
  • Water shoes - Provide traction and protect feet
  • Flotation devices - Noodles, belts, or kickboards as needed
  • Goggles - If swimming laps
  • Resistance equipment - Water dumbbells, paddles (optional)

Sample Beginner Routine

A gentle 30-minute water workout:

  1. Warm-up (5 minutes) - Slow walking in shoulder-deep water
  2. Leg exercises (5 minutes) - Knee lifts, leg swings, calf raises
  3. Arm exercises (5 minutes) - Arm circles, push-pull movements
  4. Walking or swimming (10 minutes) - At comfortable pace
  5. Cool-down (5 minutes) - Slow movements and gentle stretching

Tips for Success

  • Stay hydrated - You sweat in water even though you can't feel it
  • Listen to your body - Stop if pain increases significantly
  • Progress gradually - Add 5-10% more time or intensity weekly
  • Be consistent - 2-3 sessions per week provides best results
  • Protect your skin - Rinse after swimming; moisturize if needed
  • Rest when needed - Take breaks during exercise if fatigued

Precautions and Contraindications

While aquatic exercise is generally safe, some situations require caution:

Avoid Water Exercise If You Have:

  • Open wounds or skin infections
  • Fever or acute illness
  • Uncontrolled seizures
  • Severe cardiac conditions without clearance
  • Urinary or bowel incontinence (for public pools)
  • Active infections

Use Caution With:

  • Very low or high blood pressure
  • Respiratory conditions
  • Fear of water (consider therapy pool with instructor)
  • Balance problems (stay in shallow water initially)

Making It Enjoyable

The best exercise is the one you'll actually do. Swimming and water exercise can be social activitiesâ€”join a class or bring a friend. Many people find water inherently relaxing, which adds mental health benefits to the physical ones. Music, varied routines, and setting achievable goals can help maintain motivation.5
