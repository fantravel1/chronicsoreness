Walking for Pain

The simplest and most accessible exercise for chronic pain

Key Takeaways

  • Walking is one of the best exercises for chronic pain—low impact and accessible
  • Benefits multiple pain conditions including back pain, arthritis, and fibromyalgia
  • Start slowly and gradually increase—any amount helps
  • Consistency matters more than intensity

Why Walking Works

Walking is nature's medicine for chronic pain. It's free, accessible, requires no special equipment, and has substantial research supporting its benefits for many pain conditions. Regular walking can reduce pain, improve function, and enhance quality of life.1

Recommended
Walking is recommended by clinical guidelines for chronic low back pain, osteoarthritis, and fibromyalgia2

Benefits for Pain

  • Reduces pain intensity
  • Improves physical function
  • Lubricates joints
  • Strengthens muscles that support joints and spine
  • Releases endorphins (natural pain relievers)
  • Improves sleep
  • Reduces stress and anxiety
  • Helps maintain healthy weight

Conditions That Benefit from Walking

Chronic Low Back Pain

  • Strong evidence for walking
  • As effective as other exercise types
  • Helps maintain spine mobility

Osteoarthritis

  • Reduces pain and stiffness
  • Strengthens muscles around joints
  • Movement helps nourish cartilage

Fibromyalgia

  • Moderate evidence for walking
  • Start very slowly
  • Improves function and quality of life

Other Conditions

  • Chronic neck pain
  • Hip pain
  • General chronic pain
  • Neuropathy (with appropriate footwear)

Getting Started

Start Where You Are

  • Begin with what you can do—even 5 minutes counts
  • Walk at a comfortable pace
  • Some discomfort is okay, significant increase in pain is not
  • Walking shouldn't feel like punishment

Beginner Plan

  • Week 1-2: 5-10 minutes, 3 times per week
  • Week 3-4: 10-15 minutes, 3-4 times per week
  • Week 5-6: 15-20 minutes, 4-5 times per week
  • Week 7-8: 20-30 minutes, most days
  • Adjust based on how you feel

The 10% Rule

  • Increase distance or time by no more than 10% per week
  • Allows body to adapt
  • Reduces risk of flares

Walking with Chronic Pain

Pacing Yourself

  • Start with less than you think you can do
  • Avoid "boom and bust" (overdoing on good days)
  • Consistency over intensity
  • It's okay to walk slower than others

Managing Flares

  • Don't stop completely—reduce rather than rest
  • Shorter, gentler walks during flares
  • Even a brief walk maintains habit
  • Gradually return to baseline as flare resolves

When Pain Increases

  • Some increase in symptoms is normal when starting
  • Should settle within 24-48 hours
  • If pain significantly worse, reduce distance
  • Distinguish muscle soreness from pain flare

Movement Is Medicine

It may seem counterintuitive to walk when you have pain, but rest often makes chronic pain worse. The key is finding the right amount—enough to get benefits without triggering significant flares.

Making Walking Work for You

Equipment

  • Shoes: Supportive, comfortable, appropriate for your feet
  • Clothing: Comfortable, weather-appropriate
  • Optional: Trekking poles (reduce joint stress), fitness tracker

Where to Walk

  • Flat surfaces easiest to start
  • Indoor options: malls, tracks, treadmill
  • Outdoor options: parks, neighborhoods, trails
  • Nature walking has additional mental health benefits

Building Habit

  • Same time each day if possible
  • Link to existing routine
  • Use reminders
  • Track your walks
  • Find a walking partner
  • Make it enjoyable (music, podcasts, nature)

Special Considerations

Back Pain

  • Usually better with walking
  • May need to start very gradually
  • Good posture matters
  • Avoid carrying heavy items

Knee or Hip Arthritis

  • Walking generally helps
  • Flat surfaces easier
  • Supportive shoes important
  • Consider walking poles to reduce joint stress

Fibromyalgia

  • Start very slowly—even 3-5 minutes
  • May take longer to build up
  • Pay attention to post-exertional response
  • Consistency over distance

Tips for Success

  • Something is better than nothing: Any walk counts
  • Be patient: Benefits build over weeks
  • Don't compare: Your pace is right for you
  • Celebrate progress: Notice improvements in function
  • Adapt don't abandon: Modify rather than skip
  • Warm up and cool down: Start and end slowly
