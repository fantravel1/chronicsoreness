Walking for Chronic Pain

The most accessible exercise for managing pain and improving quality of life

Why Walking Helps Chronic Pain

Walking is one of the most studied and recommended exercises for people with chronic pain. It's accessible, free, requires no equipment, and can be adapted to almost any fitness level. Research consistently shows that regular walking can reduce pain and improve function.1

Despite what fear and pain might tell you, movement is medicine for chronic pain. The key is starting slowly and building up gradually.

Benefits of Walking for Pain

  • Reduces pain intensity - Regular walking can lower pain levels over time2
  • Releases endorphins - Natural pain-relieving chemicals
  • Reduces inflammation - Regular moderate exercise has anti-inflammatory effects
  • Improves sleep - Better sleep helps manage pain
  • Boosts mood - Reduces depression and anxiety
  • Maintains mobility - Prevents deconditioning
  • Improves circulation - Better blood flow to tissues
  • Builds confidence - Demonstrates what you can do despite pain

Getting Started Safely

The Golden Rule: Start Low, Go Slow

The biggest mistake is doing too much too soon. This leads to flare-ups and discouragement. Instead:3

  • Start with what you can do - Even 5 minutes counts
  • Increase very gradually - Add 1-2 minutes per week at most
  • Consistency over intensity - Daily short walks beat occasional long ones
  • Listen to your body - But don't let fear stop you entirely

Sample Beginning Program

  • Week 1-2: 5-10 minutes, once daily
  • Week 3-4: 10-15 minutes, once daily
  • Week 5-6: 15-20 minutes, once daily
  • Week 7-8: 20-30 minutes, 5-7 days per week
  • Adjust as needed - This is a guideline, not a mandate

Pacing Strategies

  • Time-based, not distance-based - Focus on duration, not miles
  • Baseline approach - Find what you can do without significant flare-up
  • Split it up - Two 10-minute walks may be better than one 20-minute walk
  • Walk before you hurt - Stop before pain forces you to
  • Steady progress - Avoid boom-bust cycle

Finding Your Baseline

Walk until you notice your pain increasing, then note the time. Your baseline is about 80% of that time. Start there and build up. This prevents the pattern of overdoing it on good days and being unable to move on bad days.

Walking Technique for Pain

Posture Matters

  • Head up - Eyes forward, not looking at feet
  • Shoulders relaxed - Not hunched or tensed
  • Arms swinging naturally - Opposite arm to leg
  • Core gently engaged - Support your spine
  • Heel-to-toe gait - Land heel first, roll through foot

Pace and Intensity

  • Conversation pace - Should be able to talk while walking
  • Not too slow - Should feel like exercise
  • Not too fast - Should not be gasping
  • Steady rhythm - Find a comfortable cadence

Footwear

  • Supportive, comfortable walking shoes
  • Proper fit with room for toes
  • Replace worn shoes (generally every 300-500 miles)
  • Consider orthotics if recommended by provider

Managing Flare-Ups

Expect Some Variability

  • Pain fluctuatesâ€”this is normal
  • Some increase in pain after starting exercise is common
  • This doesn't mean damage is occurring4
  • Distinguish between "hurt" and "harm"

If Pain Increases

  • Don't stop completely - Reduce duration but keep walking
  • Return to previous level - Go back to what was tolerable
  • Progress more slowly - Smaller increases over longer time
  • Check your technique - Posture, pace, footwear
  • Consider timing - Morning vs. afternoon, before vs. after medications

Red Flags (Seek Medical Attention)

  • Sudden severe pain
  • Significant swelling
  • Inability to bear weight
  • Numbness or weakness
  • Pain that's completely different from usual

Making Walking a Habit

Strategies for Consistency

  • Same time daily - Build it into routine
  • Pair with existing habit - After coffee, after lunch
  • Prepare the night before - Shoes and clothes ready
  • Start immediately - Before you can talk yourself out of it
  • Track progress - App, journal, or calendar

Making It Enjoyable

  • Music or podcasts - Entertainment while walking
  • Walking buddy - Social accountability
  • Change scenery - Different routes prevent boredom
  • Nature walks - Parks and trails when possible
  • Walking meetings - Combine with work or social time

When Walking Is Difficult

  • Indoor options - Mall walking, treadmill, indoor track
  • Assistive devices - Walking poles, cane if needed
  • Water walking - Pool walking reduces joint stress
  • Shorter sessions - Multiple brief walks
  • Seated alternative - March in place while sitting if needed

Research Support

Studies show that walking programs improve pain and function in conditions including chronic low back pain, fibromyalgia, osteoarthritis, and many others. The benefits typically appear after several weeks of consistent practice.5

Practical Tips

  • Check the weather - Dress appropriately; extreme temperatures affect pain
  • Stay hydrated - Drink water before and after
  • Warm up - Start slowly for first few minutes
  • Cool down - Slow pace at end; gentle stretches
  • Be flexible - Adapt to how you feel each day
  • Celebrate progress - Acknowledge improvements, however small
