Strength Training

Building muscle to support joints and reduce chronic pain

Why Strength Training Matters

Muscles support and protect joints. When muscles weaken (from pain-related inactivity), joints become less stable and pain often worsens. Strength training breaks this cycle. Cochrane reviews support resistance training for fibromyalgia, osteoarthritis, and chronic low back pain.1,2,3

Benefits for Chronic Pain

  • Joint protection: Stronger muscles reduce load on joints4
  • Improved function: Daily activities become easier
  • Pain reduction: Meta-analyses show decreased pain intensity5
  • Better balance: Reduces fall risk
  • Bone health: Resistance training maintains bone density
  • Metabolic benefits: Improved insulin sensitivity, body composition
  • Mental health: Reduces depression and anxiety symptoms

ME/CFS Caution

If you have ME/CFS with post-exertional malaise, strength training may trigger crashes. Work with a specialist who understands ME/CFS. Very gentle, very brief resistance work may be possible for some, but must be approached with extreme caution. See pacing strategies.

Getting Started Safely

Before You Begin

  • Medical clearance: Check with your provider, especially for heart conditions, uncontrolled hypertension, or recent injuries
  • Start where you are: Not where you think you "should" be
  • Consider PT guidance: A physical therapist can design a safe program
  • Expect some discomfort: Muscle soreness is normal; sharp pain is not

The Progressive Overload Principle

Muscles strengthen when gradually challenged beyond their current capacity. For chronic pain, "gradual" is the key word.

  • Start lighter than you think necessary
  • Increase by small increments (5-10% per week maximum)
  • Master form before adding resistance
  • Some weeks, just maintaining is progress

Beginner Program

Week 1-2: Foundation

  • Frequency: 2 days per week (non-consecutive)
  • Exercises: 4-5 basic movements
  • Resistance: Body weight or very light weights
  • Reps: 8-10 per exercise
  • Sets: 1 set per exercise
  • Rest: 60-90 seconds between exercises

Week 3-4: Building

  • Frequency: 2-3 days per week
  • Sets: Increase to 2 sets per exercise
  • Reps: 10-12
  • Resistance: Add light weights if body weight became easy

Week 5+: Progressing

  • Frequency: 2-3 days per week
  • Sets: 2-3 sets per exercise
  • Reps: 10-15
  • Gradually increase resistance when current weight feels manageable

Beginner Exercises

Lower Body

Wall Sit / Chair Squat

Stand with back against wall, slide down to seated position. Or: squat to chair and stand. Targets: quadriceps, glutes.

Step-Ups

Step up onto low step or stair, alternating legs. Use railing for balance. Targets: legs, balance.

Calf Raises

Rise onto toes, hold, lower slowly. Hold wall for balance. Targets: calves.

Upper Body

Wall Push-Ups

Push-up position against wall (easier) or countertop (harder). Targets: chest, shoulders, arms.

Seated Rows

With resistance band or light weights, pull toward body. Targets: back muscles.

Bicep Curls

Light weights or resistance band. Targets: biceps.

Core

Dead Bug

Lying on back, extend opposite arm/leg while keeping back flat. Gentle on spine. Targets: deep core.

Bird Dog

On hands and knees, extend opposite arm/leg. Hold briefly. Targets: core, back.

Bridges

Lying on back, lift hips toward ceiling. Targets: glutes, core, lower back.

Equipment Options

  • Body weight: Free, always available, can be modified
  • Resistance bands: Inexpensive, portable, joint-friendly
  • Dumbbells: Start light (2-5 lbs); adjustable sets save space
  • Machines: Gyms have machines that guide movement (safer for beginners)
  • Household items: Water bottles, cans, or filled bags can work

Condition-Specific Modifications

Fibromyalgia

  • Start very light—muscle soreness may last longer6
  • Lower rep ranges (8-10) may be better tolerated
  • Allow extra recovery time between sessions
  • Track symptoms 24-48 hours post-exercise

Osteoarthritis

  • Strengthen muscles around affected joints7
  • Avoid end-range loading of arthritic joints
  • Warm up thoroughly before resistance work
  • Consider aquatic resistance training

Rheumatoid Arthritis

  • Avoid training during active flares (rest inflamed joints)
  • When stable: resistance training is safe and beneficial8
  • Focus on muscles supporting affected joints

Chronic Low Back Pain

  • Core strengthening is particularly important
  • Avoid exercises that worsen pain
  • Motor control exercises (Pilates-style) may help9
  • Progress slowly with any spinal loading

Managing Pain During Exercise

  • Mild discomfort during exercise: Often okay—this is working muscles
  • Sharp pain: Stop that exercise immediately
  • Pain increasing as you continue: Stop or reduce intensity
  • Delayed muscle soreness (24-72 hours): Normal with new exercises
  • Pain lasting >48 hours: You did too much—reduce next time

Practical Tips

  • Warm up: 5-10 minutes of light movement before strength work
  • Breathe: Exhale on effort, don't hold your breath
  • Control the movement: Slow and controlled is safer than fast
  • Full range of motion: Within your pain-free range
  • Rest days: Muscles strengthen during rest, not during exercise
  • Stay consistent: Regular, moderate effort beats sporadic intense sessions

Track Your Progress

Document exercises, weights, reps, and how you feel to optimize your program.
