Strength Training for Chronic Pain

Building muscle safely to support joints, reduce pain, and improve daily function

Why Strength Training Helps Chronic Pain

While it might seem counterintuitive, properly designed strength training is one of the most effective treatments for many chronic pain conditions. Research consistently shows that resistance exercise can reduce pain, improve function, and enhance quality of life.1

Benefits of Strength Training

  • Joint support - Stronger muscles better protect and stabilize joints
  • Pain reduction - Exercise triggers endorphin release and reduces pain sensitivity
  • Improved function - Greater strength makes daily activities easier
  • Better bone health - Resistance exercise increases bone density
  • Reduced inflammation - Regular exercise has anti-inflammatory effects2
  • Mental health benefits - Reduces depression and anxiety
  • Weight management - Muscle increases metabolism
  • Independence - Maintains ability to perform daily tasks

Conditions That Benefit

Research supports strength training for:

  • Osteoarthritis - One of the most effective treatments3
  • Chronic low back pain - Core and back strengthening
  • Fibromyalgia - Gradual progressive training
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - When disease is stable
  • Neck pain - Cervical and shoulder strengthening
  • Osteoporosis - Essential for bone health
  • Tendinopathies - Eccentric exercise particularly effective

Getting Started Safely

Before You Begin

  • Consult your healthcare provider, especially if you have cardiovascular issues
  • Consider working with a physical therapist or certified trainer experienced in chronic pain
  • Start during a period of relatively stable symptoms
  • Set realistic expectationsâ€”improvements take time

Key Principles

1. Start Low, Progress Slow

  • Begin with very light weights or bodyweight only
  • Increase by no more than 10% per week
  • Some initial discomfort is normal; significant pain increase is not

2. Focus on Form

  • Proper technique prevents injury and maximizes benefits
  • Controlled movementsâ€”avoid jerking or momentum
  • Full range of motion (as tolerated)
  • Consider mirrors or video to check form

3. Consistency Over Intensity

  • Regular, moderate exercise beats occasional intense sessions
  • 2-3 sessions per week is usually optimal
  • Allow recovery days between sessions

Understanding "Good" vs. "Bad" Pain

Muscle fatigue and mild discomfort during exercise are normal. Sharp, shooting, or significantly worsening pain suggests you should stop and modify. Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) 24-48 hours after exercise is common when starting out but should decrease as you adapt.4

Types of Strength Training

Bodyweight Exercises

Excellent starting point:

  • Wall push-ups (progress to regular push-ups)
  • Squats (use chair for support if needed)
  • Step-ups
  • Bridges
  • Bird-dogs and dead bugs (core)

Resistance Bands

Advantages for chronic pain:

  • Variable resistance levels
  • Portable and inexpensive
  • Easier on joints than heavy weights
  • Can exercise in multiple planes of motion

Free Weights

  • Dumbbells offer more control than barbells
  • Start with very light weights (1-5 pounds)
  • Progress gradually based on tolerance

Weight Machines

  • Provide guided movement and stability
  • Good for isolating specific muscles
  • May feel safer for beginners

Sample Beginner Program

A gentle full-body routine (2-3 times per week):

Lower Body

  • Sit-to-stand - From chair, 10 reps
  • Wall squats - Hold 20-30 seconds
  • Standing heel raises - 10-15 reps
  • Side-lying leg lifts - 10 reps each side

Upper Body

  • Wall push-ups - 10-15 reps
  • Seated rows (band) - 10-12 reps
  • Shoulder press (light weights) - 8-10 reps
  • Bicep curls - 10-12 reps

Core

  • Bridges - 10-15 reps
  • Bird-dogs - 8 reps each side
  • Dead bugs - 8 reps each side
  • Plank (modified) - Hold 10-30 seconds

Managing Flares

When symptoms increase:

  • Don't stop completely - Maintain activity at reduced level
  • Reduce intensity - Less weight, fewer reps
  • Focus on gentle movement - Walking, stretching
  • Avoid complete rest - Can worsen deconditioning
  • Return to normal gradually - As symptoms improve

Special Considerations by Condition

Osteoarthritis

  • Focus on muscles around affected joints
  • Exercise when symptoms are least severe
  • Consider partial range of motion if full range is painful5

Fibromyalgia

  • Start even more gently than usual
  • Progress very slowly
  • May need longer recovery between sessions

Back Pain

  • Core stability exercises are key
  • Avoid exercises that increase spine loading during flares
  • McGill's "Big Three" exercises are evidence-based

Tips for Success

  • Track your progress - Note weights, reps, and how you feel
  • Warm up - 5-10 minutes of light cardio before lifting
  • Stay hydrated
  • Breathe properly - Exhale on exertion, don't hold breath
  • Be patient - Strength gains take 4-6 weeks to notice
  • Celebrate progress - Every improvement matters

Working with Professionals

Consider working with a physical therapist or certified strength and conditioning specialist (CSCS) who has experience with chronic pain. They can design a program tailored to your specific needs, teach proper form, and help you progress safely.
