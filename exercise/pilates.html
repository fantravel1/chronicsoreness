Pilates for Pain

Core strength and controlled movement for pain management

Key Takeaways

  • Pilates builds core strength, flexibility, and body awareness
  • Research supports Pilates for chronic low back pain
  • Low-impact and adaptable for various fitness levels
  • Emphasis on quality movement over quantity

What Is Pilates?

Pilates is a mind-body exercise system developed in the early 20th century by Joseph Pilates. It emphasizes controlled movement, core strength, proper alignment, and breathing. Originally used for rehabilitation, Pilates has become popular for fitness and pain management.1

Evidence-Based
Clinical guidelines support Pilates for chronic low back pain2

Core Principles

  • Centering: Focus on core ("powerhouse")
  • Concentration: Mindful attention to movement
  • Control: Quality over quantity
  • Precision: Exact, deliberate movements
  • Breath: Coordinated with movement
  • Flow: Smooth, graceful transitions

Benefits for Chronic Pain

Physical Benefits

  • Strengthens core muscles
  • Improves posture and alignment
  • Increases flexibility
  • Enhances body awareness
  • Builds balanced muscle strength
  • Improves joint stability

Pain-Specific Benefits

  • Reduces chronic low back pain
  • May help neck and shoulder pain
  • Supports spine health
  • Teaches movement patterns that protect joints

Research Evidence3,4

  • Low back pain: Multiple studies show significant benefits
  • Improvements in pain and function
  • May be as effective as other exercise approaches
  • Benefits seen with regular practice

Types of Pilates

Mat Pilates

  • Exercises performed on floor with mat
  • Uses body weight for resistance
  • May use props (balls, bands, rings)
  • Accessible and affordable
  • Can be done at home

Equipment-Based Pilates

  • Reformer: Most common; sliding carriage with springs
  • Cadillac/Trapeze Table: Variety of exercises
  • Chair: For standing and seated work
  • Springs provide adjustable resistance
  • Helpful for rehabilitation

Mat vs. Equipment

Both are beneficial. Equipment-based Pilates allows for more support and modifications, which can be helpful with pain. Mat Pilates is more accessible and can be practiced anywhere. Many people do both.

Pilates for Specific Conditions

Low Back Pain

  • Core strengthening supports spine
  • Improves neutral spine awareness
  • Addresses muscle imbalances
  • Strong evidence base

Neck and Shoulder Pain

  • Addresses posture issues
  • Strengthens upper back
  • Releases neck and shoulder tension

Joint Hypermobility

  • Builds muscle control for joint stability
  • Can be modified to avoid hyperextension
  • Informed instructor important

Arthritis

  • Low-impact movement
  • Maintains flexibility and strength
  • Modifications as needed

Getting Started

Finding a Class or Instructor

  • Look for certified instructors
  • Certifications: NCPT, Balanced Body, Stott, Polestar
  • Ask about experience with chronic pain
  • Small classes allow for individual attention
  • Private sessions helpful initially

Where to Practice

  • Dedicated Pilates studios
  • Fitness centers and gyms
  • Physical therapy clinics
  • Online classes (for experienced practitioners)
  • Home practice (after learning basics)

What to Expect

  • Wear comfortable, fitted clothing
  • Sessions typically 45-60 minutes
  • Usually done barefoot or with grip socks
  • Instructor will explain and demonstrate
  • Focus on form, not speed or repetitions

Practicing with Chronic Pain

Important Principles

  • Start slowly and progress gradually
  • Communicate with instructor about pain
  • Modify exercises as needed
  • Quality over quantity
  • Some muscle fatigue is okay; sharp pain is not
  • Listen to your body

Modifications

  • Reduce range of motion
  • Use props for support
  • Perform exercises in different positions
  • Skip exercises that aggravate pain
  • Equipment offers more modification options

Building a Practice

  • Aim for 2-3 sessions per week
  • Consistency more important than intensity
  • Benefits develop over weeks to months
  • Home practice reinforces studio work

Basic Exercises

Breathing

  • Foundation of Pilates
  • Lateral thoracic breathing
  • Breathe wide into ribcage
  • Maintain engaged core while breathing

Pelvic Tilt

  • Lying on back, knees bent
  • Gently flatten low back to floor
  • Release to neutral
  • Builds awareness of pelvic position

Core Engagement

  • Gentle drawing in of lower abdominals
  • Should be able to breathe while engaged
  • Not holding breath or bracing hard
