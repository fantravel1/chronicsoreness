Pilates

Core-focused movement for pain management

What Is Pilates?

Pilates is a low-impact exercise method that focuses on core strength, flexibility, posture, and controlled movement. Developed by Joseph Pilates in the early 20th century, it emphasizes quality of movement over quantity. Many physical therapists incorporate Pilates principles into rehabilitation programs for chronic pain.1

Key Principles

  • Core engagement (powerhouse)
  • Concentration and mind-body connection
  • Control and precision
  • Breathing coordination
  • Flow of movement
  • Centering and alignment

Benefits for Chronic Pain2

  • Strengthens core muscles
  • Improves posture and alignment
  • Increases flexibility
  • Low impact on joints
  • Body awareness and movement patterns
  • Reduces muscle imbalances
  • May reduce pain intensity

Research Evidence3

  • Good evidence for chronic low back pain
  • May help neck pain
  • Some evidence for fibromyalgia
  • Improves function and quality of life
  • Comparable to other exercise approaches

Types of Pilates

Mat Pilates

  • Exercises done on floor mat
  • Uses body weight for resistance
  • May use props (bands, balls)
  • More accessible, less expensive

Equipment (Reformer) Pilates

  • Uses specialized equipment
  • Adjustable resistance
  • More support options
  • Typically in studios

Adaptations for Pain

  • Modify range of motion
  • Use props for support
  • Skip or adapt challenging exercises
  • Focus on form over intensity
  • Work within pain-free range
  • Private sessions initially helpful

Getting Started

  • Look for qualified instructor
  • Mention your pain condition
  • Consider private sessions first
  • Start with beginner or rehab-focused classes
  • Online videos for home practice (after learning basics)

Finding the Right Instructor

  • Ask about certification and experience
  • Experience with chronic pain preferred
  • Physical therapists who use Pilates
  • Willing to modify exercises

Cautions

  • Some exercises may not suit all conditions
  • Flexion-based moves may aggravate some back problems
  • Inform instructor of all limitations
  • Progress gradually
  • Stop if sharp pain occurs
