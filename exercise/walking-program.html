Walking for Chronic Pain

The most accessible, evidence-based exercise for pain management

Strong Recommendation

The American College of Physicians, NICE, and WHO all strongly recommend walking/aerobic exercise as first-line treatment for chronic pain1,2,3

Why Walking Works for Chronic Pain

Walking provides multiple benefits for chronic pain management:

  • Releases endorphins: Natural pain-relieving chemicals
  • Reduces inflammation: Regular movement decreases systemic inflammation4
  • Improves sleep: Better sleep reduces pain sensitivity
  • Maintains muscle: Prevents deconditioning that worsens pain
  • Reduces stiffness: Keeps joints mobile
  • Improves mood: Reduces depression and anxiety common with chronic pain
  • Low impact: Gentler on joints than running or high-intensity exercise

The Research Evidence

What Studies Show

  • Fibromyalgia: Cochrane review found aerobic exercise (including walking) improves pain, physical function, and quality of life5
  • Chronic low back pain: Walking programs show equivalent outcomes to supervised physical therapy in RCTs6
  • Osteoarthritis: Regular walking reduces pain and improves function without accelerating joint damage7
  • General chronic pain: Meta-analyses show aerobic exercise reduces pain intensity by 10-20% on average8

ME/CFS Exception

If you have ME/CFS with post-exertional malaise (PEM), walking programs may trigger crashes. The evidence for graded exercise in ME/CFS is now considered harmful by NICE guidelines9. Work with a specialist who understands ME/CFS. See pacing strategies instead.

Starting Your Walking Program

Week 1-2: Baseline

  • Duration: 5-10 minutes
  • Frequency: 3-4 days per week
  • Intensity: Easy pace—you should be able to hold a conversation
  • Goal: Establish routine, assess tolerance

Week 3-4: Gradual Increase

  • Duration: 10-15 minutes
  • Frequency: 4-5 days per week
  • Intensity: Still conversational
  • Increase by: No more than 10% per week

Week 5-8: Building

  • Duration: 15-20 minutes
  • Frequency: 5 days per week
  • Intensity: Moderate—slightly breathless but can still talk
  • Note: Increase duration OR intensity, not both simultaneously

Long-term Goal

  • Duration: 20-30 minutes
  • Frequency: 5+ days per week (or 150 min/week total)
  • Note: Some people may reach 30+ minutes; others plateau at 15-20. Both are valid.

Key Principles

The 10% Rule

Never increase time or intensity by more than 10% per week. For chronic pain, even slower progression (5%) may be appropriate.

Consistency Over Intensity

Five 10-minute walks are better than one 50-minute walk that triggers a flare. Sustainable, regular movement beats sporadic intense efforts.

Listen to Your Body

  • Good pain: Mild muscle tiredness that resolves within 24 hours
  • Bad pain: Sharp pain, significant increase in symptoms, lasting more than 48 hours
  • If you flare, rest and resume at a lower level

Practical Tips

Timing

  • Walk when pain is typically lowest in your day
  • Allow time for medication to take effect if needed
  • Morning walks may help with stiffness; evening walks may aid sleep

Environment

  • Indoor options: Mall walking, treadmill, indoor track
  • Flat surfaces: Easier on joints than hills initially
  • Climate control: Extreme temperatures can worsen symptoms
  • Benches nearby: For rest breaks if needed

Equipment

  • Supportive shoes: Proper fit, adequate cushioning
  • Walking poles: Can improve stability and reduce joint load
  • Comfortable clothing: Layers for temperature regulation
  • Phone: For safety and tracking

Tracking Progress

Keep a simple log of:

  • Date and time
  • Duration (minutes)
  • Pain before and after (0-10)
  • How you felt the next day
  • Any notes (weather, terrain, etc.)

Managing Setbacks

  • Flare during program: Rest, then resume at 50-75% of previous level
  • Missed days: Resume where you left off (don't try to "catch up")
  • Plateau: It's okay to maintain a level that works for you
  • Bad weather: Have an indoor backup plan

Variations

Interval Walking

Alternate periods of walking with rest:

  • Walk 2-3 minutes, rest 1 minute, repeat
  • Good for those who can't sustain continuous walking
  • Gradually reduce rest periods over time

Nordic Walking

Using poles engages upper body and improves posture. Studies show benefits for fibromyalgia and chronic pain10.

Treadmill Walking

Advantages: controlled environment, handrails for support, consistent surface. Start at 0% incline.

Track Your Walking

Document your walks and how your body responds to find your optimal routine.
