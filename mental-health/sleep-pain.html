Sleep and Pain

The bidirectional relationship between sleep and chronic pain

The Sleep-Pain Cycle

Sleep and pain have a bidirectional relationship - pain disrupts sleep, and poor sleep worsens pain. Breaking this cycle is an important part of chronic pain management. Up to 88% of chronic pain patients report sleep problems.1

How Pain Affects Sleep

  • Difficulty falling asleep
  • Frequent awakenings
  • Less deep sleep
  • Non-restorative sleep
  • Pain may worsen at night

How Poor Sleep Affects Pain2

  • Increases pain sensitivity
  • Reduces pain tolerance
  • Impairs pain processing
  • Increases inflammation
  • Worsens mood (affects pain)
  • Decreases coping ability

Sleep Hygiene Strategies

Environment

  • Cool, dark, quiet room
  • Comfortable mattress and pillows
  • Reserve bed for sleep (and intimacy)

Schedule

  • Consistent bedtime and wake time
  • Avoid long daytime naps
  • Get natural light during day

Before Bed

  • Wind-down routine
  • Avoid screens 1 hour before
  • Limit caffeine (especially afternoon)
  • Avoid heavy meals late
  • Relaxation techniques

CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I)3

  • Most effective treatment for chronic insomnia
  • Works well for pain-related sleep problems
  • Techniques include sleep restriction, stimulus control
  • Can be done individually or in groups
  • Online programs available

Positioning for Sleep

  • Back pain: Pillow under knees or between knees
  • Hip pain: Pillow between knees, side-lying
  • Shoulder pain: Avoid sleeping on affected side
  • Experiment to find comfortable positions

When to Seek Help

  • Sleep problems despite good hygiene
  • Signs of sleep apnea (snoring, gasping)
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness
  • Restless legs symptoms
