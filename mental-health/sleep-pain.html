Sleep and Chronic Pain

Breaking the pain-sleep cycle

The Sleep-Pain Connection

Sleep and pain have a bidirectional relationshipâ€”pain disrupts sleep, and poor sleep increases pain sensitivity. Up to 88% of people with chronic pain report sleep problems. Addressing sleep is a critical part of comprehensive pain management.1

How Pain Disrupts Sleep2

  • Difficulty falling asleep due to pain
  • Pain causes frequent awakenings
  • Difficulty finding comfortable position
  • Medications may affect sleep
  • Worry about pain keeps mind active

How Poor Sleep Worsens Pain

  • Increases pain sensitivity
  • Reduces pain tolerance
  • Impairs natural pain modulation
  • Increases inflammation
  • Worsens mood (which affects pain)
  • Reduces pain medication effectiveness

Sleep Hygiene Basics3

  • Keep consistent sleep/wake times
  • Create dark, quiet, cool environment
  • Use bed only for sleep (and intimacy)
  • Avoid screens before bed
  • Limit caffeine, especially afternoon
  • Avoid alcohol near bedtime
  • Regular exercise (but not too close to bedtime)

Pain-Specific Sleep Strategies

Physical Comfort

  • Supportive mattress and pillows
  • Body pillow for positioning
  • Temperature regulation
  • Reduce pressure on painful areas

Pre-Sleep Routine

  • Gentle stretching
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Warm bath or shower
  • Time medication appropriately

CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I)

  • Most effective treatment for chronic insomnia
  • Addresses thoughts and behaviors around sleep
  • Sleep restriction therapy
  • Stimulus control
  • Works well even with chronic pain

Medication Considerations

  • Some pain medications cause drowsinessâ€”time appropriately
  • Some cause insomniaâ€”avoid taking late
  • Sleep medications have pros and cons
  • Tricyclic antidepressants may help both
  • Discuss options with your doctor

Sleep Disorders to Consider

  • Insomnia
  • Sleep apnea (common in chronic pain)
  • Restless legs syndrome
  • Periodic limb movements
  • May need sleep study evaluation

When to Get Help

  • Sleep problems persist despite self-help
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness
  • Snoring or breathing pauses
  • Taking sleep medications regularly
