Chronic Pain and Depression

Understanding the connection

The Pain-Depression Link

Chronic pain and depression frequently occur together. About 30-50% of people with chronic pain also have depression, and people with depression are more likely to develop chronic pain. They share common biological pathways and can worsen each other.1

Why They're Connected2

  • Shared neurotransmitters (serotonin, norepinephrine)
  • Pain causes life changes that lead to depression
  • Depression lowers pain threshold
  • Both involve inflammation
  • Sleep disruption affects both
  • Reduced activity impacts mood

Signs of Depression

  • Persistent sad or empty mood
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Sleep changes (too much or too little)
  • Appetite changes
  • Fatigue
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Feelings of worthlessness
  • Thoughts of death or suicide

Treatment Approaches3

Both Conditions Together

  • Treating one often helps the other
  • Integrated treatment most effective
  • Multidisciplinary approach recommended

Medications

  • Some antidepressants help both (SNRIs, TCAs)
  • Treat depression to improve pain coping

Therapy

  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)
  • Behavioral activation
  • Mindfulness-based approaches

Lifestyle

  • Exercise (even gentle movement)
  • Sleep improvement
  • Social connection
  • Meaningful activities

Getting Help

  • Tell your doctor about mood changes
  • Depression is treatable
  • It's not weakness or character flaw
  • Seek help if thoughts of self-harm
