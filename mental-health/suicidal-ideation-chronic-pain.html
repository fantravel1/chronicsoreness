Suicidal Thoughts and Chronic Pain

You are not alone. Understanding this painful intersection and finding help.

Key Facts

  • Chronic pain doubles the risk of suicide compared to the general population
  • Suicidal ideation is reported by 20–30% of chronic pain patients in clinical studies
  • Having suicidal thoughts does not mean you will act on them—but they always deserve attention
  • Suicidal thoughts in chronic pain are often about wanting pain to stop, not wanting life to end
  • Effective pain treatment and mental health support can significantly reduce suicidal ideation
  • You are not weak, selfish, or broken for having these thoughts

Why Chronic Pain Increases Suicide Risk

The relationship between chronic pain and suicidal ideation is well-documented in clinical research. A large meta-analysis published in Archives of Suicide Research found that chronic pain approximately doubles the risk of suicidal ideation, plans, and attempts. Several factors contribute to this elevated risk:1

  • Unrelenting suffering: Pain that does not stop erodes hope over time. The perception that pain will never improve is a stronger predictor of suicidal ideation than pain intensity itself
  • Hopelessness: Failed treatments, dismissed concerns, and uncertain prognoses create a sense that things cannot get better
  • Loss of identity and purpose: Inability to work, engage in hobbies, or fulfill social roles strips away meaning and self-worth
  • Social isolation: Pain limits social participation, and people often withdraw from relationships, losing a critical protective factor
  • Sleep deprivation: Chronic insomnia is an independent risk factor for suicidal ideation, and pain-related sleep disruption is nearly universal
  • Perceived burdensomeness: Feeling like a burden on family members is one of the strongest predictors of suicidal desire
  • Access to means: Chronic pain patients often have access to large quantities of medications

Understanding Suicidal Thoughts in Chronic Pain

Suicidal thoughts in the context of chronic pain exist on a spectrum:2

  • Passive ideation: Wishing you would not wake up, thinking life is not worth living, fantasizing about an escape from pain. These are more common and often reflect exhaustion and desperation rather than a desire to die
  • Active ideation without plan: Thinking about ending your life but without a specific plan or intent
  • Active ideation with plan: Having specific thoughts about how, when, or where—this represents an immediate crisis requiring professional help

Many chronic pain patients describe wanting the pain to stop, not wanting to die. This distinction matters because it means that if pain can be better managed, if hopelessness can be addressed, if isolation can be reduced, the desire to die often resolves.

Warning Signs to Watch For

In yourself or someone you care about:

  • Talking about wanting to die, being a burden, or having no reason to live
  • Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities more than usual
  • Giving away possessions or putting affairs in order
  • Sudden calm after a period of deep depression (this can indicate a decision has been made)
  • Increased alcohol or substance use
  • Stockpiling medications
  • Researching methods of suicide
  • Saying goodbye to people in a way that feels final

What Helps: Evidence-Based Approaches

Immediate Steps

  • Tell someone: A therapist, doctor, trusted friend, family member, or crisis line counselor. Speaking the thoughts aloud reduces their power
  • Safety planning: Work with a professional to create a written plan listing warning signs, coping strategies, people to contact, and crisis resources
  • Means restriction: Have a trusted person hold your medications and reduce access to lethal means. This single step saves lives

Therapeutic Approaches

  • Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS): A therapeutic framework specifically designed to address suicidal ideation
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): Originally developed for suicidal patients, DBT teaches distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness3
  • Problem-solving therapy: Addresses the hopelessness and perceived unsolvability that drive suicidal ideation
  • Pain psychology: Integrated treatment addressing both pain management and mental health

Addressing Root Causes

  • Aggressive pain management: pursue all available treatments, seek second opinions, consider multidisciplinary programs
  • Sleep treatment: treating insomnia can independently reduce suicidal ideation
  • Social reconnection: even small steps toward reducing isolation provide protection
  • Purpose restoration: finding new roles, volunteer work, or creative expression that provides meaning

For Loved Ones

  • Ask directly: “Are you thinking about suicide?” Asking does not plant the idea; it shows you care and opens the door to help
  • Listen without judgment. Do not minimize (“It could be worse”) or problem-solve immediately
  • Help with practical steps: driving to appointments, researching resources, holding medications
  • Take all expressions of suicidal thoughts seriously, even if they seem casual
  • Take care of yourself too. Supporting someone in crisis is emotionally demanding

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it normal to have suicidal thoughts with chronic pain?

While it is common (affecting 20–30% of chronic pain patients), it is not something you should endure alone. These thoughts are a signal that you need more support—better pain management, mental health care, or both. Please reach out to a professional.

Will my doctor take me seriously or just label me as mentally ill?

A good clinician will take suicidal ideation seriously and understand its connection to inadequately treated pain. If your provider dismisses your concerns, seek a different provider. Pain psychologists and psychiatrists familiar with chronic pain are particularly well-equipped to help.

I do not want to be hospitalized. Will telling someone lead to that?

Hospitalization is typically reserved for situations of imminent danger. Most suicidal ideation can be safely managed with outpatient care, safety planning, and increased support. Be honest with your provider about your concerns regarding hospitalization.

What if I have tried everything for my pain and nothing works?

Pain medicine is evolving rapidly. New treatments, approaches, and understandings are emerging. A multidisciplinary pain rehabilitation program may offer options you have not yet tried. Even when pain cannot be eliminated, its impact on your life can almost always be reduced.
