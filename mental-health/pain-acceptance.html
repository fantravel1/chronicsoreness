Pain Acceptance

A different approach to living with chronic pain

What Is Pain Acceptance?

Pain acceptance doesn't mean giving up or liking pain. It means acknowledging pain without constantly struggling against it, allowing you to engage in meaningful activities despite pain. Research shows acceptance is associated with better outcomes in chronic pain.1

What Acceptance Is NOT

  • Giving up on treatment
  • Pretending pain doesn't exist
  • Liking or wanting pain
  • Passive resignation
  • Believing nothing can help

What Acceptance IS

  • Acknowledging reality as it is
  • Reducing struggle against pain
  • Focusing on what you can control
  • Living according to values despite pain
  • Being willing to experience discomfort

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)2

ACT is a therapy approach that emphasizes:

  • Acceptance - Of difficult experiences
  • Cognitive defusion - Changing relationship to thoughts
  • Present moment awareness - Mindfulness
  • Self as context - Observing self
  • Values - Clarifying what matters
  • Committed action - Acting on values

Research Evidence3

  • Higher acceptance associated with less disability
  • Better emotional functioning
  • ACT effective for chronic pain
  • May reduce pain intensity over time

Practicing Acceptance

  • Notice pain without judgment
  • Observe thoughts about pain
  • Ask: "What can I do right now despite pain?"
  • Focus on values and meaning
  • Take small steps toward important activities
