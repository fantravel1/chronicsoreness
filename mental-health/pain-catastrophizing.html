Pain Catastrophizing

When thoughts amplify pain

What Is Pain Catastrophizing?

Pain catastrophizing is a pattern of negative thinking about pain that includes rumination (dwelling on pain), magnification (exaggerating the threat), and helplessness (feeling unable to cope). Research shows catastrophizing is one of the strongest predictors of poor outcomes in chronic painâ€”but it can be changed.1

This Is Not Blaming You

  • Catastrophizing is a common human response
  • It's a learned pattern, not a character flaw
  • Your pain is still real
  • Many people aren't aware they do it
  • It can be unlearned

Three Components2

Rumination

  • "I can't stop thinking about the pain"
  • "I keep thinking about how much it hurts"
  • Mind constantly focused on pain

Magnification

  • "Something terrible is going to happen"
  • "This pain must mean something is seriously wrong"
  • Expecting the worst

Helplessness

  • "There's nothing I can do"
  • "This will never get better"
  • "I can't stand this anymore"

Why It Matters3

  • Amplifies pain signals in the brain
  • Increases depression and anxiety
  • Reduces engagement in activities
  • Predicts disability level
  • Affects treatment outcomes
  • Can be changedâ€”improves outcomes

Recognizing Catastrophizing

  • Notice when you're fixating on pain
  • Listen for "always," "never," "can't"
  • Notice worst-case scenario thinking
  • Track thoughts when pain spikes

Strategies to Reduce Catastrophizing

Cognitive Strategies

  • Challenge catastrophic thoughts
  • Ask: Is this definitely true?
  • What's a more balanced view?
  • What would I tell a friend?
  • Develop coping self-statements

Behavioral Strategies

  • Engage in activities despite pain
  • Distraction techniques
  • Relaxation practices
  • Gradual exposure to feared activities

Mindfulness

  • Observe thoughts without engaging
  • Return focus to present moment
  • Accept pain without adding suffering

Professional Help

  • CBT highly effective for catastrophizing
  • Pain psychologists specialize in this
  • Group programs available
  • Online programs can help

Helpful Self-Statements

  • "This is uncomfortable but I can cope"
  • "Pain doesn't always mean damage"
  • "I've gotten through this before"
  • "This will pass"
  • "I can take it one moment at a time"
