Depression and Chronic Pain

Understanding the bidirectional relationship

The Pain-Depression Connection

Depression and chronic pain frequently occur together. Up to 85% of people with chronic pain experience depression, and depression increases the risk of developing chronic pain. The relationship is bidirectional - each condition can worsen the other.1

Why They Co-occur

  • Shared neurotransmitters (serotonin, norepinephrine)
  • Overlapping brain regions
  • Pain limits activities â†’ isolation â†’ depression
  • Depression amplifies pain perception
  • Sleep disruption affects both
  • Chronic stress and inflammation

Signs of Depression2

  • Persistent sadness or emptiness
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Sleep changes (too much or too little)
  • Fatigue, low energy
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
  • Appetite changes
  • Thoughts of death or suicide

Why Treating Both Matters

  • Untreated depression worsens pain outcomes
  • Pain can prevent depression recovery
  • Treating both improves quality of life
  • Some treatments help both conditions

Treatment Approaches3

Medications

  • SNRIs (duloxetine, venlafaxine) - treat both
  • TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline)
  • Other antidepressants as needed

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
  • Behavioral activation
  • Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)

Lifestyle

  • Regular physical activity
  • Social connection
  • Sleep improvement
  • Meaningful activities despite pain

When to Seek Help

If you're experiencing depression symptoms, especially thoughts of self-harm, please reach out to a healthcare provider or crisis line. Depression is treatable, and treatment can improve both mood and pain.
