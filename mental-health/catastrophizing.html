Pain Catastrophizing

When thoughts amplify pain

What Is Pain Catastrophizing?

Pain catastrophizing is a negative thought pattern characterized by magnifying the threat of pain, feeling helpless, and being unable to stop thinking about pain. It's one of the strongest psychological predictors of pain intensity and disability. Importantly, catastrophizing is not a character flawâ€”it's a learned pattern that can be changed.1

Three Components2

Rumination

  • "I can't stop thinking about how much it hurts"
  • Constant focus on pain
  • Difficulty shifting attention

Magnification

  • "Something terrible is going to happen"
  • Expecting the worst
  • Seeing pain as overwhelming

Helplessness

  • "There's nothing I can do"
  • Feeling unable to cope
  • Believing pain will never improve

How It Affects Pain3

  • Increases pain intensity
  • Worsens disability
  • Leads to depression and anxiety
  • Reduces treatment effectiveness
  • Creates avoidance behaviors
  • Amplifies nervous system sensitization

Why It Happens

  • Natural response to threat
  • Previous negative experiences
  • Lack of effective coping tools
  • Learned from others
  • Reinforced over time
  • Not intentional or chosen

Strategies to Reduce Catastrophizing

Awareness

  • Notice catastrophic thoughts
  • Recognize patterns
  • Label them as "just thoughts"

Cognitive Restructuring

  • Question catastrophic predictions
  • Look for evidence
  • Generate alternative thoughts
  • Focus on what you can control

Attention Shifting

  • Engage in activities
  • Practice mindfulness
  • Use distraction deliberately

Build Self-Efficacy

  • Recall times you coped
  • Take small manageable steps
  • Celebrate small successes

Helpful Questions to Ask

  • "Is this definitely true or am I assuming?"
  • "What would I tell a friend thinking this?"
  • "Have I coped with this before?"
  • "What's the most likely outcome?"
  • "What can I do right now?"

Professional Help

  • CBT specifically targets catastrophizing
  • Pain psychology programs
  • Acceptance and Commitment Therapy
  • Mindfulness-based approaches
