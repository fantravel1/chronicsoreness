Self-Compassion

Treating yourself with kindness

What Is Self-Compassion?

Self-compassion means treating yourself with the same kindness, understanding, and care you would offer a good friend who is suffering. For people with chronic pain, self-compassion can reduce distress, improve coping, and even influence pain perception.1

Three Components2

Self-Kindness (vs. Self-Judgment)

  • Treating yourself gently when things are hard
  • Avoiding harsh self-criticism
  • Speaking to yourself as you would a friend

Common Humanity (vs. Isolation)

  • Recognizing suffering is part of human experience
  • You're not alone in your struggles
  • Many others understand what you're going through

Mindfulness (vs. Over-identification)

  • Acknowledging pain without drowning in it
  • Balanced awareness of difficulties
  • Neither ignoring nor amplifying suffering

Why It Helps with Pain3

  • Reduces catastrophizing
  • Lowers stress (which amplifies pain)
  • Improves emotional regulation
  • May reduce pain intensity
  • Increases willingness to engage in helpful activities

Common Self-Critical Thoughts

  • "I should be handling this better"
  • "I'm a burden to others"
  • "This is my fault"
  • "I'm weak for struggling"
  • "Others have it worse"

Self-Compassion Practices

Self-Compassion Break

  • Acknowledge: "This is a moment of suffering"
  • Connect: "Suffering is part of life"
  • Kindness: "May I be kind to myself"

Compassionate Self-Talk

  • What would you say to a friend?
  • Use a warm, understanding tone
  • Acknowledge the difficulty

Self-Compassion Letter

  • Write to yourself from perspective of compassionate friend
  • Acknowledge your suffering
  • Offer understanding and support

Barriers to Self-Compassion

  • Worry it means self-pity (it doesn't)
  • Fear of becoming lazy (research shows opposite)
  • Belief you don't deserve it
  • Feels unfamiliar or uncomfortable
