Pain and Depression

Understanding their connection

The Pain-Depression Connection

Chronic pain and depression frequently occur together. Up to 50-85% of people with chronic pain experience depression. This isn't coincidenceâ€”they share biological pathways and each condition makes the other worse. Understanding this connection is key to effective treatment.1

Why They're Connected2

  • Shared brain pathways and neurotransmitters
  • Pain causes loss, isolation, and hopelessness
  • Depression amplifies pain perception
  • Both affect sleep, which worsens both
  • Chronic stress affects both conditions
  • Reduced activity affects both

Signs of Depression

  • Persistent sad or empty mood
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Changes in sleep (too much or too little)
  • Fatigue and low energy
  • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Thoughts of death or suicide

Treatment Approaches3

Integrated Treatment

  • Addressing both conditions together works best
  • Multidisciplinary pain programs
  • Coordination between providers

Medications That Help Both

  • SNRIs (duloxetine, venlafaxine)
  • Tricyclic antidepressants
  • May treat both conditions simultaneously

Psychological Treatments

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Behavioral activation
  • Mindfulness-based approaches

Lifestyle Factors

  • Exercise (even gentle movement helps)
  • Sleep improvement
  • Social connection
  • Meaningful activities

Getting Help

  • Tell your pain provider about mood symptoms
  • Don't dismiss depression as "just" from pain
  • Treatment for depression often improves pain
  • Crisis resources if having thoughts of suicide
