Sleep & Chronic Pain

Breaking the vicious cycle—because poor sleep worsens pain and pain disrupts sleep

The Scope of the Problem

Nearly 89% of chronic pain patients report disrupted sleep. Poor sleep increases inflammatory markers and pain sensitivity, creating a vicious cycle that's critical to address.

The Bidirectional Relationship

Sleep and pain are intimately connected in both directions:

Pain → Sleep Problems

  • Pain makes it hard to fall asleep
  • Pain causes frequent awakenings
  • Pain prevents deep, restorative sleep
  • Fear of pain can create bedtime anxiety

Sleep Problems → More Pain

  • Poor sleep increases inflammatory markers
  • Sleep deprivation heightens pain sensitivity
  • Fatigue lowers pain tolerance
  • Mood effects (depression, anxiety) worsen pain perception

Sleep Hygiene for Chronic Pain

Foundation practices that set the stage for better sleep:

Environment

  • Temperature: Keep bedroom cool (65-68°F / 18-20°C)
  • Darkness: Use blackout curtains or eye mask
  • Quiet: White noise machine if needed
  • Bed purpose: Reserve bed for sleep and intimacy only—no TV, work, or phone scrolling

Timing

  • Consistent schedule: Same sleep/wake times daily, even weekends
  • Limit caffeine: None after noon (or earlier if sensitive)
  • Screen curfew: Stop screens 1-2 hours before bed
  • Meal timing: Avoid large meals close to bedtime

Pain Management at Night

  • Medication timing: Discuss optimal timing with your provider
  • Positioning: Use pillows strategically for support
  • Pre-bed routine: Gentle stretching, warm bath, relaxation
  • Heat therapy: Heating pad before bed (not while sleeping)

CBT-I: The Gold Standard

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is more effective than medication long-term for chronic pain patients with sleep problems.

Core Components

  • Sleep restriction: Temporarily limiting time in bed to increase sleep drive
  • Stimulus control: Strengthening the bed-sleep association
  • Cognitive restructuring: Addressing unhelpful thoughts about sleep
  • Relaxation training: Progressive muscle relaxation, breathing techniques
  • Sleep hygiene education: Optimizing sleep environment and habits

Finding CBT-I

  • In-person: Sleep psychologists, behavioral sleep medicine specialists
  • Digital programs: Sleepio, Somryst (FDA-cleared), CBT-i Coach app
  • Workbooks: "Quiet Your Mind and Get to Sleep" by Colleen Carney

Sleep Medications: Considerations

When behavioral approaches aren't enough:

Options Sometimes Used

  • Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants: Amitriptyline, nortriptyline—help pain and sleep
  • Trazodone: Often used off-label; sedating antidepressant
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin: May improve sleep alongside pain
  • Melatonin: May help with sleep timing; generally safe short-term

Use with Caution

  • Benzodiazepines: Dependency risk; not recommended long-term
  • "Z-drugs" (zolpidem, etc.): Short-term use only; dependency concerns
  • OTC sleep aids: Antihistamines—can cause next-day drowsiness, not for chronic use

Important Note

Sleep medications should be discussed with your healthcare provider in the context of your overall pain treatment plan. Many chronic pain patients take multiple medications, and interactions must be considered.

Special Considerations

For Fibromyalgia

  • Non-restorative sleep is a core symptom
  • Alpha-delta sleep intrusion (light sleep waves intruding on deep sleep)
  • May need medications targeting both pain and sleep

For ME/CFS

  • Sleep is often unrefreshing regardless of hours slept
  • Sleep inversion (sleeping days, awake nights) can develop
  • Prioritize sleep hygiene but don't expect it to resolve fatigue

For Menstrual-Related Pain

  • Sleep disruption often worsens premenstrually
  • Track sleep alongside menstrual cycle
  • May need adjusted strategies around period

Relaxation Techniques for Bedtime

Progressive Muscle Relaxation

  1. Starting with feet, tense muscles for 5 seconds
  2. Release and notice the contrast for 30 seconds
  3. Move up through legs, abdomen, chest, arms, face
  4. Takes about 15 minutes

4-7-8 Breathing

  1. Inhale through nose for 4 counts
  2. Hold breath for 7 counts
  3. Exhale through mouth for 8 counts
  4. Repeat 4 cycles

Body Scan Meditation

  • Lie comfortably in bed
  • Bring attention to each body part sequentially
  • Notice sensations without trying to change them
  • Many guided versions available (apps, YouTube)
