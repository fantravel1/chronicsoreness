Assistive Devices

Tools to reduce strain and improve function

Why Use Assistive Devices?

Assistive devices can reduce strain on painful joints and muscles, conserve energy, and help you maintain independence. Using the right tools isn't giving upâ€”it's working smarter to protect your body and do more of what matters to you.1

Mobility Aids2

Walking Aids

  • Canes (single point or quad)
  • Walkers (standard or rolling)
  • Rollators with seats
  • Walking poles

Wheelchairs and Scooters

  • Manual wheelchairs
  • Power wheelchairs
  • Mobility scooters
  • Part-time use for long distances

Kitchen Aids

  • Jar openers and grip aids
  • Ergonomic utensils (built-up handles)
  • Electric can openers
  • Lightweight pots and pans
  • Perching stools
  • Reacher/grabbers

Bathroom Aids3

  • Grab bars
  • Shower chairs or benches
  • Handheld showerheads
  • Raised toilet seats
  • Non-slip mats
  • Long-handled sponges

Dressing Aids

  • Long-handled shoe horns
  • Sock aids
  • Button hooks
  • Zipper pulls
  • Elastic shoelaces
  • Dressing sticks

Work and Computer Aids

  • Ergonomic keyboards and mice
  • Monitor stands
  • Document holders
  • Voice recognition software
  • Sit-stand desks
  • Ergonomic chairs

Sleep and Rest Aids

  • Supportive pillows (body, wedge, cervical)
  • Adjustable beds
  • Mattress toppers
  • Bed rails for getting up
  • Overbed tables

Where to Get Assistive Devices

  • Medical supply stores
  • Online retailers
  • Occupational therapy departments
  • Some may be covered by insurance
  • Consider used equipment to save money

Working with Professionals

  • Occupational therapists specialize in adaptive equipment
  • Physical therapists help with mobility devices
  • Proper fitting is important
  • Training ensures safe and effective use
