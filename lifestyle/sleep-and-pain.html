Sleep and Chronic Pain

Understanding and breaking the pain-sleep cycle

Key Takeaways

  • Sleep and pain have a bidirectional relationship—each affects the other
  • Poor sleep increases pain sensitivity the following day
  • Improving sleep can significantly reduce pain levels
  • CBT for insomnia is highly effective, even with chronic pain

The Pain-Sleep Connection

Sleep and chronic pain are deeply interconnected. Research shows this relationship is bidirectional—poor sleep worsens pain, and pain disrupts sleep, creating a vicious cycle.1

50-80%
of chronic pain patients report sleep problems2

How Poor Sleep Affects Pain

  • Increases pain sensitivity (hyperalgesia)
  • Reduces pain inhibition pathways
  • Increases inflammation markers
  • Impairs tissue healing
  • Worsens mood, which affects pain perception
  • Decreases pain tolerance

How Pain Affects Sleep

  • Difficulty falling asleep due to discomfort
  • Frequent awakenings during the night
  • Less time in deep, restorative sleep
  • Increased arousal and hypervigilance
  • Difficulty finding comfortable positions

Which Comes First?

Research suggests sleep problems may be more predictive of pain than pain is of sleep problems.3 This means improving sleep could be one of the most effective ways to reduce chronic pain—and it is a modifiable factor within your control.

Common Sleep Problems in Chronic Pain

Insomnia

The most common sleep complaint:4

  • Difficulty falling asleep
  • Difficulty staying asleep
  • Waking too early
  • Non-restorative sleep

Sleep Fragmentation

  • Multiple awakenings throughout the night
  • Reduced sleep continuity
  • Less time in deep sleep stages

Sleep Disorders to Consider

Some sleep disorders are more common with chronic pain:

  • Sleep apnea: Breathing interruptions during sleep
  • Restless legs syndrome: Urge to move legs, especially at night
  • Periodic limb movement disorder: Leg movements during sleep

If you snore loudly, stop breathing during sleep, or have excessive daytime sleepiness, discuss sleep testing with your healthcare provider.

Sleep Hygiene Basics

Good sleep habits form the foundation of better sleep:5

Environment

  • Keep bedroom cool (65-68°F / 18-20°C)
  • Make room as dark as possible
  • Reduce noise or use white noise
  • Reserve bed for sleep and intimacy only
  • Invest in comfortable mattress and pillows

Timing

  • Keep consistent sleep and wake times (even weekends)
  • Avoid long naps (limit to 20-30 minutes if needed)
  • Get morning light exposure
  • Dim lights in the evening

Before Bed

  • Avoid screens 1-2 hours before bed (or use blue light filters)
  • Limit caffeine after noon
  • Avoid alcohol close to bedtime
  • Avoid large meals late at night
  • Create a relaxing wind-down routine

During the Day

  • Get regular physical activity (but not too close to bedtime)
  • Expose yourself to natural daylight
  • Manage stress throughout the day
  • Limit caffeine intake overall

CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I)

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia is the first-line treatment for chronic insomnia and is highly effective even for people with chronic pain:6

Components of CBT-I

  • Sleep restriction: Limiting time in bed to match actual sleep time
  • Stimulus control: Strengthening bed-sleep association
  • Cognitive restructuring: Addressing unhelpful thoughts about sleep
  • Relaxation training: Techniques to calm mind and body
  • Sleep hygiene education: Optimizing sleep habits

Key CBT-I Strategies

Stimulus control rules:

  • Only go to bed when sleepy
  • If not asleep in 20 minutes, get up and do something relaxing
  • Return to bed only when sleepy again
  • Use bed only for sleep and intimacy
  • Get up at the same time every day

Finding CBT-I

CBT-I can be delivered by trained therapists, through structured programs, or via digital apps. It typically involves 4-8 sessions and has lasting benefits. Ask your healthcare provider for a referral to a CBT-I provider, or explore evidence-based digital programs.

Sleep Positioning for Pain

Finding comfortable sleep positions can reduce nighttime pain:7

Back Pain

  • Back sleeping: Place pillow under knees
  • Side sleeping: Pillow between knees, slight fetal position
  • Avoid stomach sleeping: Strains lower back and neck

Neck Pain

  • Use pillow that keeps neck aligned with spine
  • Avoid too-high or too-flat pillows
  • Consider cervical pillow
  • Avoid stomach sleeping

Hip Pain

  • Side sleeping with pillow between knees
  • Sleep on non-painful side if possible
  • Consider mattress topper for cushioning

Shoulder Pain

  • Avoid sleeping on painful shoulder
  • Use pillow to support arm when side sleeping
  • Back sleeping with arm supported

Relaxation Techniques for Sleep

Progressive Muscle Relaxation

  1. Lie in bed in comfortable position
  2. Starting with feet, tense muscles for 5 seconds
  3. Release and notice the relaxation
  4. Move up through body: calves, thighs, buttocks, stomach, chest, arms, hands, face
  5. Focus on the contrast between tension and relaxation

Diaphragmatic Breathing

  1. Place one hand on chest, one on belly
  2. Breathe in slowly so belly rises
  3. Exhale slowly, letting belly fall
  4. Keep chest relatively still
  5. Continue for 5-10 minutes

Body Scan

  1. Lie comfortably and close eyes
  2. Bring attention to feet, noticing sensations without judgment
  3. Slowly move attention up through body
  4. Notice areas of tension and breathe into them
  5. Complete scan from feet to head

Guided Imagery

  • Imagine a peaceful, calming place
  • Engage all senses in the visualization
  • Use guided meditation apps or recordings

Medications and Sleep

Sleep Medications

Use cautiously and under medical guidance:8

  • Over-the-counter sleep aids (antihistamines) can cause morning grogginess
  • Prescription sleep medications have risks with long-term use
  • Melatonin may help with sleep timing
  • Some pain medications cause drowsiness (can help or hurt sleep)

Pain Medications and Sleep

  • Opioids can disrupt sleep architecture
  • Some medications cause sleep apnea
  • Timing of medications may affect sleep
  • Discuss medication timing with your provider

Medications That May Help Both

  • Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline)
  • Trazodone
  • Gabapentinoids (can improve sleep in some)

When to Seek Help

Consider professional evaluation if:

  • Sleep problems persist despite good sleep hygiene
  • You snore loudly or stop breathing during sleep
  • You have restless legs or leg movements at night
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness interferes with life
  • Sleep problems significantly worsen pain

Specialists Who Can Help

  • Sleep medicine physicians
  • Psychologists trained in CBT-I
  • Pain medicine specialists
