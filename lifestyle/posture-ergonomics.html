Posture and Ergonomics

Optimizing body positioning for pain prevention and management

Key Takeaways

  • Prolonged static postures—not just "bad" posture—contribute to pain
  • Movement and position variety are more important than perfect posture
  • Ergonomic adjustments can significantly reduce strain
  • Regular breaks and movement are essential for musculoskeletal health

Understanding Posture

The relationship between posture and pain is more nuanced than traditionally thought. While severe postural abnormalities can contribute to pain, research suggests that prolonged static positioning—holding any posture for extended periods—may be more problematic than the specific posture itself.1

The Modern Posture Problem

Key postural challenges in modern life:

  • Prolonged sitting: Office work, driving, screen time
  • Forward head posture: From phone and computer use
  • Rounded shoulders: Slouching at desks
  • Static positions: Lack of movement variety
  • Repetitive motions: Same movements repeated daily

The Best Posture Is Your Next Posture

Rather than searching for the perfect posture, focus on position variety. Our bodies are designed for movement, and any position held too long creates strain. The goal is not to sit or stand perfectly, but to change positions frequently and move regularly throughout the day.

Workstation Ergonomics

Proper workstation setup can significantly reduce strain during desk work:3

Chair Setup

  • Height: Feet flat on floor (or on footrest), thighs parallel to floor
  • Depth: 2-3 finger widths between seat edge and back of knees
  • Back support: Lumbar support at the small of your back
  • Armrests: Shoulders relaxed, elbows at 90-100 degrees

Monitor Position

  • Distance: Arm's length away
  • Height: Top of screen at or slightly below eye level
  • Angle: Tilted slightly back to reduce glare
  • Position: Directly in front of you, not to the side

Keyboard and Mouse

  • Position: At elbow height with shoulders relaxed
  • Wrist position: Neutral (not bent up, down, or sideways)
  • Mouse: Close to keyboard, at same level
  • Consider: Split keyboards, vertical mice for comfort

Laptop Considerations

Laptops create ergonomic challenges because the screen and keyboard are attached:

  • Use an external keyboard and mouse when possible
  • Elevate laptop screen to proper height
  • Consider a laptop stand or riser
  • If using laptop keyboard, adjust chair height and use document holder

Standing Desk Considerations

Standing desks can be beneficial but are not a complete solution:4

Benefits

  • Reduces prolonged sitting
  • Encourages more movement
  • May reduce some types of back pain

Potential Issues

  • Standing all day can cause fatigue and foot/leg pain
  • May worsen some conditions
  • Not suitable for everyone

Best Practices

  • Alternate between sitting and standing
  • Start with short standing periods (20-30 minutes)
  • Use anti-fatigue mat
  • Wear supportive footwear
  • Shift weight and move while standing

Movement and Breaks

Regular movement is essential for preventing pain:2,5

Movement Strategies

  • Change position every 30 minutes
  • Take a brief movement break every hour
  • Vary tasks throughout the day
  • Walk during phone calls
  • Take stairs when possible

Desk Stretches

Neck stretches:

  • Slowly tilt ear toward shoulder, hold 15-30 seconds each side
  • Chin tucks: Pull chin back, creating a double chin, hold 5 seconds

Shoulder stretches:

  • Roll shoulders backward in circles
  • Squeeze shoulder blades together, hold 5 seconds
  • Clasp hands behind back and gently lift

Wrist stretches:

  • Extend arm, pull fingers back gently with other hand
  • Make fists and rotate wrists in circles

Back stretches:

  • Seated spinal twist: Turn torso to each side
  • Cat-cow: Arch and round your back while seated
  • Stand and reach overhead with gentle side bend

Phone and Tablet Use

Mobile device use contributes significantly to neck and upper back strain:6

"Text Neck" Prevention

  • Raise device to eye level when possible
  • Take frequent breaks from scrolling
  • Use voice-to-text features
  • Prop tablet on stand at eye level
  • Limit extended phone use sessions

Phone Ergonomics Tips

  • Hold phone at eye level instead of looking down
  • Use speaker or earbuds for calls
  • Avoid cradling phone between ear and shoulder
  • Take regular breaks from screen time

Sleeping Posture

Sleep position affects morning pain and stiffness:7

General Principles

  • Choose a mattress that supports your natural curves
  • Keep spine in neutral alignment
  • Pillow should support neck in line with spine

Position-Specific Tips

Back sleepers:

  • Use pillow that supports neck curve
  • Consider pillow under knees for low back support

Side sleepers:

  • Use pillow thick enough to keep head aligned with spine
  • Place pillow between knees for hip alignment
  • Avoid fetal position that rounds the back excessively

Stomach sleeping:

  • Generally not recommended as it stresses neck and back
  • If unavoidable, use thin or no pillow
  • Place pillow under pelvis to reduce low back strain

Lifting and Carrying

Proper body mechanics for daily activities:8

Lifting Technique

  • Stand close to the object
  • Bend at hips and knees, not at waist
  • Keep back straight, engage core
  • Hold object close to your body
  • Lift with legs, not back
  • Avoid twisting while lifting—turn with feet

Carrying

  • Distribute weight evenly when possible
  • Use both straps of a backpack
  • Alternate sides when carrying single-strap bags
  • Keep heavy items close to body

Purse and Bag Tips for Women

  • Lighten your bag—carry only essentials
  • Choose bags with wide, padded straps
  • Switch shoulders regularly
  • Consider crossbody bags to distribute weight
  • Use a backpack for heavy loads

Driving Ergonomics

Long drives can contribute to back and neck pain:9

Seat Adjustment

  • Adjust seat height so hips are level with knees
  • Position seat close enough to reach pedals comfortably
  • Use lumbar support (built-in or add pillow)
  • Tilt seat back slightly (100-110 degrees)

Steering and Mirrors

  • Position steering wheel so arms are slightly bent
  • Adjust mirrors to avoid reaching or twisting
  • Use hands-free devices for calls

On Long Trips

  • Stop every 1-2 hours to stretch and walk
  • Shift position frequently while driving
  • Use cruise control when appropriate to move legs

Making Changes Sustainable

Tips for implementing ergonomic improvements:

  • Make one change at a time
  • Set reminders to check posture and take breaks
  • Use apps that prompt movement breaks
  • Adjust environment to encourage good habits
  • Be patient—new habits take time to develop
