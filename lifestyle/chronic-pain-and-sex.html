Sexuality and Chronic Pain

Maintaining intimacy with pain

An Important Topic

Chronic pain often affects sexuality and intimacy, yet this is rarely discussed in medical appointments. Sexual activity can be painful, medications may affect desire or function, and fatigue and mood changes complicate things further. Despite these challenges, satisfying intimacy is possible with adaptation and communication.1

How Pain Affects Sexuality2

  • Pain during sexual activity
  • Reduced desire (libido)
  • Fatigue and energy limitations
  • Body image concerns
  • Depression and anxiety effects
  • Medication side effects
  • Fear of causing pain flare

Medication Effects

  • Opioids can reduce testosterone, libido
  • Antidepressants may affect arousal/orgasm
  • Anticonvulsants may cause issues
  • Discuss concerns with your doctor
  • Sometimes alternatives exist

Communication Is Key3

  • Talk openly with partner
  • Share what feels good and what doesn't
  • Discuss fears and concerns
  • Be honest about limitations
  • Plan together
  • Couples therapy can help

Practical Strategies

Timing

  • Choose times when pain is lower
  • After pain medication has taken effect
  • When you're most rested
  • Not during major flares

Preparation

  • Warm bath or shower beforehand
  • Gentle stretching
  • Use heating pad before
  • Take pain medication if appropriate

Positioning

  • Experiment with different positions
  • Use pillows for support
  • Side-lying often more comfortable
  • Positioning wedges available

Expanding Intimacy

  • Intimacy isn't just intercourse
  • Focus on pleasure, not performance
  • Massage and touch
  • Emotional connection matters

Condition-Specific Considerations

  • Back pain: side positions, avoid arching
  • Arthritis: warm-up, supportive surfaces
  • Pelvic pain: pelvic floor PT, lubricants
  • Headaches: avoid positions increasing head pressure

When to Seek Help

  • Pain specifically during intercourse (see gynecologist/urologist)
  • Significant relationship strain
  • Depression affecting sexuality
  • Medication concerns

Resources

  • Sex therapists
  • Pelvic floor physical therapists
  • Couples counselors
  • Pain specialists
