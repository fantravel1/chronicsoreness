Sleep and Pain

Breaking the pain-sleep cycle

The Pain-Sleep Connection

Sleep and chronic pain have a complex, bidirectional relationship. Pain disrupts sleep, and poor sleep makes pain worse. This creates a vicious cycle that can be difficult to break. Research shows that treating sleep problems can significantly improve pain outcomes, making sleep a crucial target in pain management.1

How Pain Affects Sleep2

  • Difficulty falling asleep
  • Frequent awakenings
  • Lighter sleep stages
  • Less restorative sleep
  • Early morning waking
  • Pain worse at night for some

How Poor Sleep Worsens Pain3

  • Increases pain sensitivity
  • Reduces pain inhibition
  • Amplifies inflammation
  • Worsens mood (affects pain)
  • Decreases coping ability
  • Affects healing processes

Sleep Hygiene Basics

Environment

  • Cool, dark, quiet room
  • Comfortable mattress and pillows
  • Bedroom for sleep only
  • Remove screens

Schedule

  • Consistent wake time
  • Avoid long naps
  • Regular bedtime

Habits

  • Limit caffeine (especially afternoon)
  • Avoid alcohol before bed
  • Wind-down routine
  • No screens 1 hour before bed

Pain-Specific Strategies

  • Time medications for night pain
  • Position with pillows strategically
  • Use heat or ice before bed
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Address anxiety about sleep

Positioning Tips

  • Back sleepers: pillow under knees
  • Side sleepers: pillow between knees
  • Body pillows for support
  • Experiment with positions
  • Mattress firmness matters

CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I)

  • Evidence-based treatment
  • Works well with chronic pain
  • Addresses thoughts about sleep
  • Sleep restriction component
  • More effective than sleeping pills long-term

Medications

  • Some pain meds help sleep
  • Sleep aids have limited role
  • Not long-term solutions
  • Discuss with provider

When to Seek Help

  • Sleep apnea symptoms
  • Severe persistent insomnia
  • Restless legs
  • Sleep study may be needed
