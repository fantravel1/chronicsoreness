Breathing Exercises for Pain

Using breath to calm the nervous system and reduce pain

Key Takeaways

  • Slow, deep breathing activates the body's relaxation response
  • Can reduce pain intensity, anxiety, and muscle tension
  • Free, portable, and can be done anywhere
  • Regular practice builds skill and increases benefits

How Breathing Helps Pain

Breathing is one of the few bodily functions we can consciously control, and it provides a direct pathway to influence the nervous system. Slow, deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system (the "rest and digest" response), which can reduce pain perception, lower stress hormones, and relax tense muscles.1

Always Available
Breathing exercises are free, portable, and can be used anytime pain flares

Mechanisms

  • Activates parasympathetic nervous system: Calms fight-or-flight response
  • Reduces muscle tension: Relaxes chronically tight muscles
  • Lowers stress hormones: Decreases cortisol
  • Changes pain perception: Shifts focus, alters processing
  • Improves oxygenation: Better oxygen delivery to tissues
  • Reduces anxiety: Calms mind, reduces pain catastrophizing

Diaphragmatic Breathing

Also called belly breathing or abdominal breathing—the foundation technique:2

How to Practice

  1. Sit comfortably or lie on your back
  2. Place one hand on your chest, one on your belly
  3. Breathe in slowly through your nose
  4. Let your belly rise (the hand on belly moves out)
  5. Chest should stay relatively still
  6. Exhale slowly through pursed lips or nose
  7. Feel belly fall as you exhale
  8. Aim for 6-10 breaths per minute

Tips

  • Exhale should be longer than inhale (e.g., 4 counts in, 6 counts out)
  • Don't force—keep it gentle and comfortable
  • Practice 5-10 minutes, 2-3 times daily
  • With practice, it becomes more natural

Why Belly Breathing?

Many people breathe shallowly into their chest, especially when stressed or in pain. This can perpetuate the stress response. Diaphragmatic breathing uses the diaphragm muscle as nature intended, promoting full oxygen exchange and triggering relaxation.

4-7-8 Breathing

A structured technique for relaxation:

Steps

  1. Exhale completely through your mouth
  2. Inhale quietly through nose for 4 counts
  3. Hold breath for 7 counts
  4. Exhale completely through mouth for 8 counts
  5. Repeat for 4 breath cycles

Uses

  • Particularly helpful for sleep
  • Acute stress or anxiety
  • Pain flares
  • Can adjust counts if 4-7-8 is too long

Box Breathing

Equal counts for structure and focus:

Steps

  1. Inhale for 4 counts
  2. Hold for 4 counts
  3. Exhale for 4 counts
  4. Hold for 4 counts
  5. Repeat

Benefits

  • Easy to remember
  • Good for beginners
  • Promotes focus and calm
  • Can visualize tracing a box

Paced Breathing

Simply slowing down your breath rate:

Target

  • Aim for 5-6 breaths per minute
  • Normal is 12-20 breaths per minute
  • Slower breathing = greater relaxation response

How to Practice

  • Use a timer or app with pacing cues
  • Count breaths
  • Gradually slow down from your normal rate

Breathing for Pain Flares

In the Moment

  • Pause and notice your breathing
  • Take a slow, deep breath
  • Let shoulders drop as you exhale
  • Continue slow breathing for several minutes
  • Focus on the breath, not the pain

Breathing Through Pain

  • Breathe into areas of tension
  • Imagine breath flowing to painful area
  • Exhale and release tension
  • Don't fight the pain—breathe with it

Building a Practice

Getting Started

  • Start with 5 minutes, once or twice daily
  • Choose a consistent time (morning, before bed)
  • Find a quiet, comfortable spot
  • Gradually increase duration

Making It Habit

  • Link to existing routine (morning coffee, before meals)
  • Set phone reminders
  • Use apps for guided practice
  • Practice during routine activities (commute, waiting)

Progression

  • Week 1-2: 5 minutes, once daily
  • Week 3-4: 5-10 minutes, twice daily
  • Ongoing: 10-20 minutes daily, plus as needed
  • Use brief breathing throughout day

Common Challenges

"I Feel Dizzy"

  • You may be breathing too fast or deeply
  • Slow down, breathe more gently
  • Don't force deep breaths

"I Can't Focus"

  • Mind wandering is normal
  • Gently return focus to breath
  • Counting can help maintain focus
  • Guided recordings can help

"It Doesn't Work"

  • Benefits build with regular practice
  • May take weeks to notice significant effects
  • Even if pain doesn't change, reduced stress helps
  • It's a skill—gets easier with practice

Resources

  • Apps: Breathe, Calm, Headspace, Insight Timer
  • Videos: Many free guided breathing videos online
  • Devices: Biofeedback devices can guide breathing
