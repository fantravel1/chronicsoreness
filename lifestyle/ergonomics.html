Ergonomics for Pain

Setting up your environment to reduce pain

Key Takeaways

  • Proper ergonomics can help prevent and manage chronic pain
  • Movement variety is as important as "perfect" posture
  • Small adjustments can make a big difference
  • Take frequent breaks—no posture is perfect if held too long

Understanding Ergonomics

Ergonomics is the science of fitting the environment to the person. Rather than forcing your body to adapt to your workspace, ergonomics adapts the workspace to support your body and reduce strain.1

Key Principle
The best posture is your next posture—movement and variety matter most

Core Principles

  • Neutral body positions reduce strain
  • Frequent position changes are essential
  • Tools should fit the task and the person
  • Reduce repetitive motions when possible
  • Minimize sustained static postures

Computer Workstation Setup

Monitor

  • Top of screen at or slightly below eye level
  • Arm's length distance away
  • Directly in front of you (not to the side)
  • Reduce glare (position away from windows, adjust brightness)
  • Consider larger screen or dual monitors for less squinting

Chair

  • Feet flat on floor (or footrest)
  • Knees at approximately 90 degrees
  • Lower back supported (lumbar support or pillow)
  • Seat depth allows 2-3 finger widths behind knees
  • Armrests support arms without hunching shoulders

Keyboard and Mouse

  • Elbows at approximately 90 degrees
  • Wrists in neutral position (not bent up or down)
  • Mouse close to keyboard
  • Shoulders relaxed, not hunched
  • Consider ergonomic keyboard or mouse if needed

Standing Desks

  • Alternate between sitting and standing
  • Don't stand all day—variety is key
  • Use anti-fatigue mat when standing
  • Same monitor and keyboard principles apply

Perfect Posture Myth

There's no single "perfect" posture. What matters most is variety—changing positions frequently. Even good posture becomes problematic if maintained too long. Set reminders to move.

Breaking Up Sitting

Movement Breaks

  • Every 20-30 minutes, change position or move
  • Stand up, stretch, walk briefly
  • Set timer reminders
  • Use apps that prompt breaks

Microbreaks

  • Brief pauses every few minutes
  • Look away from screen (20-20-20 rule for eyes)
  • Relax shoulders
  • Unclench jaw
  • Take a few deep breaths

Active Sitting Options

  • Stability balls (for periods, not all day)
  • Active sitting chairs
  • Perching stools
  • Kneeling chairs
  • Variety is key—switch between options

Laptop Ergonomics

The Problem

  • Screen and keyboard connected = compromise
  • Looking down strains neck
  • Reaching up strains shoulders

Solutions

  • Use external keyboard and mouse
  • Raise laptop on stand to eye level
  • Use external monitor when possible
  • Limit continuous laptop use without modifications

Phone and Tablet Use

Reducing "Tech Neck"

  • Bring device up rather than looking down
  • Use stands or holders
  • Take frequent breaks
  • Strengthen neck and upper back

Texting

  • Hold phone higher
  • Use voice-to-text when possible
  • Alternate hands
  • Take breaks from extended texting

Home Ergonomics

Kitchen

  • Store frequently used items at waist height
  • Use step stool for high items
  • Anti-fatigue mat at sink/counter
  • Sit while doing prep work when possible

Sleeping

  • Mattress that supports your spine
  • Pillow that keeps neck neutral
  • Side sleepers: pillow between knees
  • Back sleepers: pillow under knees

Lifting

  • Bend knees, not back
  • Keep load close to body
  • Don't twist while lifting
  • Ask for help with heavy items
  • Use assistive devices when available

Desk Stretches

Neck

  • Gentle chin tucks
  • Side neck stretches
  • Slow neck rolls (avoid full circles if painful)

Shoulders

  • Shoulder shrugs and rolls
  • Chest opener (arms behind, squeeze shoulder blades)
  • Arm circles

Wrists and Hands

  • Wrist circles
  • Finger stretches
  • Prayer stretch and reverse prayer

Back

  • Seated twist
  • Cat-cow in chair
  • Standing extension

Common Mistakes

  • Sitting in same position all day (even if "ergonomic")
  • Monitor too low or too high
  • Reaching for mouse
  • Cradling phone between ear and shoulder
  • Ignoring pain signals
  • Not taking breaks

When to Seek Help

  • Pain persists despite ergonomic changes
  • Numbness or tingling in hands or arms
  • Symptoms worsening
  • Consider ergonomic assessment from professional
  • Physical therapy for persistent issues
