Flare Management

Strategies for when pain intensifies

Understanding Flares

Pain flares are temporary increases in pain intensity above your usual baseline. They're a normal part of living with chronic pain. Having a plan helps you cope more effectively and recover faster.1

Common Flare Triggers

  • Overactivity (boom-bust pattern)
  • Weather changes
  • Poor sleep
  • Stress
  • Illness or infection
  • Hormonal changes
  • Travel or disrupted routine

Creating a Flare Plan2

Before a Flare (Preparation)

  • Identify your warning signs
  • Know your helpful strategies
  • Have supplies ready (medications, heat/ice packs)
  • Inform support people of your plan

During a Flare

  • Remind yourself it's temporary
  • Use your pre-planned coping strategies
  • Reduce activity but don't stop completely
  • Prioritize essential tasks only
  • Practice relaxation techniques
  • Accept help from others

Coping Strategies

Physical

  • Heat or cold therapy
  • Gentle movement (even if limited)
  • Comfortable positioning
  • Pain medications as prescribed
  • TENS unit

Mental

  • Mindfulness and breathing
  • Distraction (TV, audiobooks, music)
  • Self-compassion
  • Avoid catastrophizing

Recovery Phase3

  • Gradually return to normal activities
  • Don't rush to "catch up"
  • Reflect on what helped
  • Update your flare plan if needed

When to Seek Medical Help

  • New or different type of pain
  • Severe symptoms beyond your usual flares
  • Neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness)
  • Signs of infection (fever, swelling, redness)
  • Flare doesn't improve as expected
