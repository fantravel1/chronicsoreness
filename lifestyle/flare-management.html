Managing Pain Flares

Preparation and strategies for getting through the hard days

Flares Are Part of Chronic Pain

Even with good management, flares happen. Being prepared with tools and strategies can make them shorter and more bearable. This guide helps you build your flare survival toolkit.

Recognizing Early Warning Signs

Many people notice warning signs before a full flare. Learning yours helps you intervene early:

  • Increasing fatigue despite adequate rest
  • Sleep becoming more disrupted
  • Brain fog worsening
  • Mood changes (irritability, feeling "off")
  • Subtle increase in pain or stiffness
  • Increased sensitivity (to light, sound, touch)
  • Digestive changes

When You Notice Warning Signs

  • Cancel non-essential commitments immediately
  • Reduce activity level to bare minimum
  • Prioritize rest and sleep
  • Stay hydrated and nourished (easy meals)
  • Avoid known triggers

Building Your Flare Toolkit

Assemble supplies when you're feeling better so they're ready when you need them:

Physical Comfort

  • Heating pad / hot water bottle
  • Ice packs (wrapped)
  • Comfortable loose clothing
  • Extra pillows for positioning
  • Soft blanket
  • TENS unit (if you use one)

Medications & Supplements

  • Regular medications filled
  • PRN (as-needed) medications
  • Pain relievers (OTC)
  • Topical treatments
  • Magnesium (if you use)
  • Know what to take and when

Easy Nutrition

  • Frozen easy meals
  • Protein shakes/bars
  • Crackers, applesauce, easy snacks
  • Electrolyte drinks
  • Water bottle at bedside
  • Delivery app ready on phone

Mental/Emotional

  • Guided meditation app ready
  • Comfort TV/movies queued
  • Audiobooks/podcasts downloaded
  • Support person contact list
  • Affirmation/reminder notes to self

During a Flare: Hour by Hour

Immediate Actions

  1. Accept it: Fighting the flare mentally makes it worse
  2. Cancel commitments: Send the texts/emails now
  3. Take medications: Follow your flare plan
  4. Apply heat/cold: Whatever helps your pain
  5. Get comfortable: Position yourself for rest

Throughout the Flare

  • Rest aggressively: This is not the time to push through
  • Stay hydrated: Dehydration worsens everything
  • Eat something: Even if just crackers—empty stomach worsens medication side effects
  • Gentle movement: Only if it helps; stop if it doesn't
  • Distraction: TV, audiobooks, music—whatever takes your mind elsewhere
  • Breathing exercises: Short, simple ones if possible

Reminder During Flares

"This is temporary. You have survived every flare before this. You will survive this one too."

When to Seek Help

Most flares can be managed at home, but call your provider or seek care if:

  • Pain is significantly worse than your "normal" worst
  • New symptoms appear (weakness, numbness, fever)
  • You're having thoughts of self-harm
  • Medications aren't providing any relief
  • Flare lasts much longer than typical
  • You're unable to eat or drink

Crisis Resources

If you're having thoughts of suicide or self-harm, please reach out: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) or Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741). More crisis resources here.

Communicating About Flares

Pre-Written Messages (Save These)

For work: "I'm experiencing a flare-up of my chronic condition and need to [work from home / take a sick day / reduce my hours today]. I'll keep you updated on my status."

For friends/family: "Having a rough day with my health. I need to rest and may be slow to respond. I'm okay, just need to take care of myself."

To cancel plans: "I'm so sorry, but I'm having a health flare and need to cancel. I was really looking forward to this. Can we reschedule?"

After the Flare

Recovery Phase

  • Don't rush back: Gradually increase activity over days
  • Avoid the "catch-up" trap: This triggers another flare
  • Continue rest: Even when feeling better
  • Return to baseline activity: Not more

Learning From It

  • Document in your symptom diary
  • Note potential triggers (activity, food, stress, weather)
  • What helped? What didn't?
  • How long did it last?
  • Any patterns emerging?

For Caregivers/Support People

How you can help during flares:

  • Don't ask "what can I do"—offer specific help
  • Handle meals, household tasks
  • Manage communications (canceling plans)
  • Provide quiet company if wanted, space if not
  • Refill water, bring medications
  • Don't try to "fix" or give advice
  • Validate: "I'm sorry you're going through this"

Preventing Future Flares

While you can't prevent all flares, you can reduce frequency and severity:

  • Consistent pacing: See our pacing guide
  • Sleep protection: Prioritize sleep hygiene
  • Stress management: Regular relaxation practices
  • Trigger awareness: Learn and avoid your triggers
  • Medication adherence: Take preventive medications consistently
  • Regular gentle movement: When able
