Pacing Strategies

The primary management strategy for ME/CFS and chronic fatigue—preventing crashes through energy management

Why Pacing Is Critical for ME/CFS

74-81% of ME/CFS patients report worsening with traditional exercise programs. Graded Exercise Therapy (GET) is no longer recommended.

Pacing is the primary management strategy—learning to stay within your "energy envelope" to prevent post-exertional malaise (PEM).

Understanding Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM)

PEM is the hallmark symptom of ME/CFS—it's not normal tiredness:

  • Delayed onset: Typically appears 12-48 hours after exertion
  • Duration: Can last days to weeks
  • Trigger: Physical, mental, or emotional exertion
  • Effect: Worsening of all symptoms, not just fatigue
  • Not helped by rest: Once triggered, you must wait it out

Heart Rate Monitoring

One of the most effective pacing tools—using your heart rate to stay in safe zones.

The Formula

Maximum heart rate = Resting heart rate + 15 beats

Example: If your resting heart rate is 70 bpm, your threshold is 85 bpm. When your heart rate exceeds this during activity, stop and rest.

How to Use Heart Rate Monitoring

  1. Find your resting heart rate: Measure first thing in morning, lying still, for several days. Average the results.
  2. Calculate your threshold: Add 15 beats to your resting rate
  3. Wear a heart rate monitor: Fitness watch or chest strap
  4. Set alerts: Alarm when approaching threshold
  5. Stop and rest: When you hit your threshold, stop activity until heart rate drops

The Energy Envelope

Imagine you have a limited "energy budget" each day. Pacing means:

  • Stay within your envelope: Don't spend more energy than you have
  • Don't borrow from tomorrow: Overdoing today means paying for it later
  • Plan for activities: Budget energy for what matters most
  • Include rest as an activity: Scheduled rest prevents crashes

Breaking the Push-Crash Cycle

The Cycle (Avoid)

  1. Feel somewhat better
  2. Do too much ("catch up" on tasks)
  3. Crash (PEM hits 24-48 hours later)
  4. Rest for days/weeks
  5. Feel somewhat better... repeat

Pacing Approach (Goal)

  1. Feel somewhat better
  2. Do the same amount as yesterday
  3. Rest before you need to
  4. Maintain consistent baseline
  5. Gradually improve over time

Practical Pacing Strategies

Activity Management

  • Break tasks into chunks: 10-15 minute activities with rest between
  • Alternate activity types: Physical, mental, social—don't stack same type
  • Pre-rest: Rest before demanding activities, not just after
  • Say no: Protect your energy for what matters most
  • Plan ahead: Schedule rest around known demands

Rest Strategies

  • Scheduled rest: Planned rest periods throughout the day
  • Rest doesn't mean sleep: Lying down, eyes closed, minimal stimulation
  • Reduce stimulation: Quiet, dark room; no screens
  • Rest before you crash: Stop before you feel you need to

Warning Signs to Stop

  • Heart rate exceeding your threshold
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Breathlessness
  • Brain fog increasing
  • Nausea
  • Muscle weakness or trembling
  • "Hitting a wall" feeling

Good Days vs Bad Days

The Good Day Trap

On good days, the temptation is to do more—catch up on tasks, exercise more, see friends. This is the biggest trigger for crashes. On good days, do the same as your baseline. Use good days to build consistency, not to overdo.

Activity Logging

Keep a detailed log to understand your patterns:

  • Activities (type, duration, intensity)
  • Rest periods
  • Symptoms each day (0-10 scale)
  • Heart rate data
  • Sleep quality
  • Triggers you notice

Over time, you'll learn your personal limits and triggers.

For Caregivers/Family

  • Understand that pacing is medical treatment, not laziness
  • Don't encourage "pushing through"—it causes harm
  • Help protect the person's energy budget
  • Take over tasks during crashes
  • Learn the warning signs

Tools and Apps

  • Visible: App designed for ME/CFS pacing with HRV tracking
  • Fitbit/Apple Watch: Heart rate monitoring and activity tracking
  • Garmin: Body battery feature can help track energy
  • Bearable: Symptom tracking app with activity correlation
