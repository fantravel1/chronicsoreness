Pacing Strategies

Balancing activity and rest for better pain management

Key Takeaways

  • Pacing helps break the "boom and bust" cycle of overactivity and flares
  • Stop activities before pain forces you to stop
  • Consistent activity levels are better than erratic patterns
  • Pacing is a skill that improves with practice

What Is Pacing?

Pacing is an activity management strategy that involves balancing periods of activity with rest, and adjusting activity levels to prevent pain flares. Rather than doing as much as possible on good days and paying for it later, pacing aims for consistent, sustainable activity levels.1

The Problem: Boom and Bust

Many people with chronic pain fall into a boom and bust pattern:

  • Boom: On a good day, do as much as possible (catch up on tasks)
  • Bust: Overactivity triggers a flare; forced to rest for days
  • Cycle repeats, with activity levels becoming increasingly erratic
  • Overall function often declines over time

The Solution: Pacing

  • Maintain consistent activity levels day to day
  • Stop activities before reaching your limit
  • Take regular rest breaks
  • Gradually increase activity over time

Key Pacing Principles

1. Know Your Baseline

Identify how much activity you can do consistently without triggering a flare:

  • Track your activities and pain levels for a week
  • Note what triggers flares
  • Find your sustainable level (often less than you'd like)
  • Start from a realistic baseline

2. Stop Before You Have To

  • Set time limits for activities
  • Stop at 80% of your capacity, not 100%
  • Don't push through pain
  • "I could do more" means you're doing it right

3. Take Regular Breaks

  • Schedule rest breaks, don't wait until you need them
  • Try 20-30 minutes of activity, then 5-10 minutes rest
  • Adjust ratios based on your needs
  • Breaks can be position changes, not necessarily lying down

4. Be Consistent

  • Same approximate activity level each day
  • Resist the urge to "make up for lost time" on good days
  • Avoid complete inactivity on bad days (gentle movement helps)
  • Predictability helps your nervous system calm down

5. Gradually Progress

  • Once baseline is stable, slowly increase
  • Add small amounts (5-10%)
  • Wait for the new level to become comfortable
  • Setbacks are normal; return to last stable level

Practical Strategies

Breaking Down Tasks

  • Divide large tasks into smaller chunks
  • Spread tasks across multiple days
  • Alternate between different types of activities
  • Mix physical, mental, and social activities

Using Timers

  • Set a timer for activity periods
  • Stop when the timer goes off, not when pain makes you
  • Phone apps can remind you to take breaks

Planning Ahead

  • Plan your week, including rest periods
  • Don't schedule demanding activities back-to-back
  • Include buffer time for unexpected demands
  • Plan recovery time after events

Prioritizing

  • Identify what's essential vs. what can wait
  • It's okay to let some things go
  • Delegate when possible
  • Focus energy on what matters most

Common Challenges

"But I Feel Fine Today"

On good days, it's tempting to overdo it. Remember:

  • Today's overactivity often causes tomorrow's flare
  • Consistency matters more than peak performance
  • Save some capacity for unexpected demands
  • Long-term progress comes from sustainable effort

"I Have Too Much to Do"

  • Chronic pain requires adjusting expectations
  • Doing less now may mean doing more overall
  • Ask for help—it's not weakness
  • Some things may need to be let go

"People Don't Understand"

  • Explain pacing to family and friends
  • They may not see your pain or fatigue
  • Setting boundaries is necessary self-care
  • Practice saying "no" or "not right now"

"It Feels Wrong to Stop"

  • Pacing can feel unnatural at first
  • Stopping when you feel fine is counterintuitive
  • Trust the process—it gets easier
  • The payoff is fewer flares and more overall function

Pacing vs. Avoidance

Pacing is NOT the same as avoiding activity:2

Pacing

  • Maintains consistent activity
  • Gradually increases over time
  • Based on time/quotas, not pain level
  • Goal: sustainable function

Avoidance

  • Reduces or eliminates activity
  • Activity levels decrease over time
  • Based on fear of pain
  • Leads to deconditioning and more pain

The Goal

Pacing aims to do MORE over time by being strategic—not less. It's about working smarter, not harder, and building sustainable capacity rather than depleting yourself.

Getting Started

Week 1: Track

  • Log activities and pain levels
  • Note what triggers flares
  • Identify your boom and bust pattern

Week 2: Find Baseline

  • Determine sustainable activity levels
  • Set realistic time limits for activities
  • Plan rest breaks

Weeks 3-4: Practice

  • Follow your pacing plan
  • Stop before you have to
  • Take scheduled breaks
  • Resist overdoing on good days

Ongoing: Adjust and Progress

  • Review what's working
  • Gradually increase as tolerated
  • Expect some trial and error
  • Be patient with yourself
