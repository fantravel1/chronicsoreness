Sleep Hygiene

Evidence-based sleep practices adapted for chronic pain

50-90%

of people with chronic pain have sleep problems1

The Sleep-Pain Connection

Sleep and pain have a bidirectional relationship:2

  • Poor sleep increases pain sensitivity the next day
  • Pain disrupts sleep quality and duration
  • Sleep deprivation reduces pain tolerance
  • Improving sleep often improves pain

Research Finding

Studies show that one night of poor sleep can lower pain threshold by 15-25%. Sleep may be one of the most modifiable factors affecting chronic pain.3

Core Sleep Hygiene Practices

1. Consistent Sleep Schedule

  • Same wake time every day—including weekends
  • This is the single most important sleep hygiene practice4
  • Bedtime can be flexible; wake time should not be
  • Avoid "sleeping in" more than 1 hour on weekends

2. Optimize Your Sleep Environment

  • Dark: Blackout curtains or eye mask
  • Quiet: Earplugs, white noise machine, or fan
  • Cool: 60-67°F (15-19°C) is ideal for most5
  • Comfortable: Supportive mattress and pillows
  • Bed = sleep only: Don't work, watch TV, or scroll in bed

3. Wind-Down Routine

  • Begin 30-60 minutes before bed
  • Same routine each night (signals sleep to your brain)
  • Dim lights throughout the house
  • Relaxing activities: reading, gentle stretching, warm bath
  • Avoid stimulating activities, arguments, work emails

4. Light Exposure

  • Morning bright light: 15-30 minutes within an hour of waking6
  • Evening dim light: Dim lights 2 hours before bed
  • Blue light: Avoid screens 1-2 hours before bed, or use blue light filters
  • Light exposure regulates circadian rhythm and melatonin production

5. Watch What You Consume

  • Caffeine: None after noon (half-life is 5-6 hours)
  • Alcohol: Avoid 3+ hours before bed—disrupts sleep architecture
  • Large meals: Avoid eating heavily within 2-3 hours of bed
  • Fluids: Limit close to bedtime to reduce night waking

Chronic Pain-Specific Strategies

Pain Management at Night

  • Time medications: If you take pain medication, time it so you're covered through the night
  • Topical treatments: Apply before bed if helpful
  • Heat/cold: Use whatever helps (heating pad on timer, ice pack)
  • Positioning: Pillows between knees (side sleeper), under knees (back sleeper)

Managing Night Pain

  • If you wake in pain, use pre-planned strategies (medication, repositioning, relaxation)
  • Don't lie in bed awake suffering—if it's a bad night, get up briefly
  • Have rescue medications accessible if prescribed for breakthrough pain

Dealing with Daytime Fatigue

  • Naps: If needed, keep to 20-30 minutes, before 2-3 PM
  • Longer or later naps interfere with nighttime sleep
  • Don't compensate for bad nights with excessive daytime rest

CBT-I: The Gold Standard

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line treatment for chronic insomnia, including in chronic pain patients.7,8

Components of CBT-I

  • Sleep restriction: Limiting time in bed to match actual sleep (counterintuitive but effective)
  • Stimulus control: Bed only for sleep; get up if not sleeping
  • Cognitive restructuring: Addressing unhelpful thoughts about sleep
  • Sleep hygiene education
  • Relaxation training

Accessing CBT-I

  • Psychologists/therapists: Trained in CBT-I
  • Sleep medicine clinics: Often offer CBT-I
  • Digital CBT-I: Apps like Sleepio, CBT-I Coach (VA), Somryst
  • Books: "Say Good Night to Insomnia" by Gregg Jacobs

Sleep Position for Chronic Pain

Back Pain

  • Back sleeper: Pillow under knees to reduce lumbar curve
  • Side sleeper: Pillow between knees, supportive pillow for head
  • Avoid: Stomach sleeping (strains neck and back)

Neck Pain

  • Supportive pillow that maintains neutral spine
  • Avoid too-high or too-flat pillows
  • Cervical pillows may help some people

Hip/Knee Pain

  • Side sleeper: Pillow between knees essential
  • Back sleeper: Pillow under knees
  • Body pillow can help maintain position

Sleep Medications: Caution

Important Considerations

  • Sleep medications don't address underlying causes
  • Can lead to dependence and rebound insomnia
  • CBT-I is more effective long-term than sleeping pills9
  • Discuss with your provider; some may be appropriate short-term
  • Melatonin (0.5-3mg) may help some; start low

When to See a Sleep Specialist

  • Snoring, witnessed apneas, or gasping at night (possible sleep apnea)
  • Leg discomfort or movement at night (possible restless legs syndrome)
  • Severe insomnia not responding to sleep hygiene
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep time
  • Unusual behaviors during sleep

Tracking Your Sleep

Consider tracking:

  • Bedtime and wake time
  • Time to fall asleep (estimate)
  • Night wakings (number and duration)
  • Sleep quality (1-10)
  • Next-day pain and energy levels
  • Factors that helped or hurt

Document sleep patterns and their relationship to your pain.
