Pacing Activities

Managing energy and preventing flares

What Is Pacing?

Pacing is a strategy of breaking up activities into manageable chunks, alternating between activity and rest to prevent symptom flares. It helps avoid the "boom-bust" cycle where good days lead to overdoing it, followed by days of increased pain and inability to function.1

Benefits of Pacing2

  • More consistent daily function
  • Fewer severe flares
  • Better energy management
  • Gradual increase in tolerance
  • More reliable routine
  • Less frustration and disappointment

Key Principles

Know Your Baseline

  • Determine how much you can do consistently
  • Activity level that doesn't cause flare
  • Usually less than "good day" capacity
  • Track for several days to find it

Break Up Activities

  • Divide large tasks into smaller parts
  • Take scheduled rest breaks
  • Stop before symptoms increase
  • Use timers as reminders

Alternate Activities

  • Switch between activity types
  • Alternate sitting and standing
  • Mix physical and mental tasks
  • Balance demanding and easier activities

Practical Strategies3

  • Set time limits for activities
  • Use "activity quotas" (time-based not pain-based)
  • Plan ahead for demanding days
  • Rest before exhaustion
  • Say no when needed
  • Prioritize important activities

Common Pacing Mistakes

  • Resting only when exhausted
  • Overdoing on good days
  • Not scheduling enjoyable activities
  • Being too rigid
  • Comparing to pre-pain abilities

Pacing vs. Avoiding

  • Pacing maintains activity at sustainable level
  • Complete rest/avoidance causes deconditioning
  • Goal is steady activity, not no activity
  • Gradually increase baseline over time

Getting Started

  • Track activities and symptoms for a week
  • Identify your baseline capacity
  • Practice breaking up one activity
  • Add scheduled rest breaks
  • Adjust based on results
