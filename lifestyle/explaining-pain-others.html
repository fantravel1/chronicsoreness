Explaining Pain to Others

Helping others understand your experience

Why It's Difficult

Chronic pain is often invisible. You may look fine while struggling inside. Others may not understand why you can't do things you used to do, or why some days are better than others. Explaining chronic pain to others is challenging but can improve relationships and support.

Common Misunderstandings

  • "You don't look sick"
  • "Have you tried...?"
  • "It's all in your head"
  • "Just push through it"
  • "You were fine yesterday"
  • Assuming rest will fix it

Key Points to Communicate

Pain Is Real

  • Chronic pain is a medical condition
  • It's not weakness or imagination
  • It's different from acute pain that heals

It Fluctuates

  • Good days don't mean you're cured
  • Bad days don't mean you're getting worse
  • Activity today may cause pain tomorrow

It's Exhausting

  • Fighting pain takes energy
  • May need more rest than others
  • Fatigue is part of the condition

Analogies That Help

The Spoon Theory

  • Each day you have limited "spoons" (energy units)
  • Every activity costs spoons
  • When spoons run out, you can't do more
  • Search "spoon theory" for full explanation

The Alarm System

  • Pain is like a faulty alarm
  • Keeps going off even when there's no fire
  • Real alarm, false message

What You Might Say

  • "My condition means I have to pace my activities"
  • "Pain varies day to dayâ€”that's normal for chronic conditions"
  • "I'm working with doctors to manage this"
  • "What would help is..." (be specific)

What You Need From Others

  • Belief that your pain is real
  • Flexibility with plans
  • No judgment about limitations
  • Specific practical help (not just "let me know")
  • Continued invitations even if you sometimes decline

Setting Boundaries

  • You don't owe everyone detailed explanations
  • It's okay to say "I'm not up for that today"
  • You can decline unwanted advice
  • Protect your energy for what matters

Resources to Share

  • Educational videos about chronic pain
  • Articles from reputable sources
  • Letters explaining your condition
  • Books about chronic pain (for interested family)

Accepting Limitations

  • Some people won't understand despite efforts
  • You can't force understanding
  • Focus energy on supportive relationships
  • Find community with others who "get it"
