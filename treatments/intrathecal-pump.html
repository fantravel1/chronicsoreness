Intrathecal Pain Pump

Targeted drug delivery for severe pain

What Is an Intrathecal Pump?

An intrathecal pump (also called a pain pump) is an implanted device that delivers pain medication directly into the spinal fluid. This allows much smaller doses to be effective, reducing systemic side effects. It's reserved for severe chronic pain that hasn't responded to other treatments.1

How It Works

  • Pump implanted under skin (abdomen)
  • Catheter delivers medication to spinal fluid
  • Medication acts directly on spinal cord
  • Uses 1/300th of oral dose
  • Programmable delivery rates
  • Refilled every 1-6 months

Medications Used2

  • Morphine - Most common
  • Hydromorphone - Alternative opioid
  • Ziconotide - Non-opioid option
  • Baclofen - For spasticity
  • Combinations may be used

Who May Benefit

  • Cancer pain
  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • Chronic non-cancer pain unresponsive to other treatments
  • Patients with intolerable oral medication side effects
  • Life expectancy considerations for implant

The Process

Trial

  • Test injection or temporary catheter
  • Evaluates pain relief
  • Assesses side effects
  • Must show significant improvement

Implantation

  • Surgical procedure
  • General anesthesia
  • 1-3 day hospital stay
  • Programming and adjustment period

Benefits3

  • Effective pain relief with lower doses
  • Fewer systemic side effects
  • Steady medication delivery
  • Adjustable dosing
  • May reduce oral medications

Risks and Considerations

  • Surgical risks
  • Infection
  • Catheter complications
  • Medication side effects
  • Regular refill appointments required
  • Pump replacement every 5-7 years
