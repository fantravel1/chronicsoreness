Pain Neuroscience Education

Understanding pain science to improve outcomes

Key Takeaways

  • Pain is produced by the brain as a protective response—not simply a measure of tissue damage
  • Understanding how pain works can reduce fear, improve function, and decrease pain
  • PNE is most effective when combined with active treatments like exercise
  • Learning about pain changes how the brain processes it

What Is Pain Neuroscience Education?

Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE), also called Explain Pain or Therapeutic Neuroscience Education, is an educational approach that teaches people about the biology and physiology of pain. Understanding how pain really works can reduce fear, change beliefs, and improve outcomes.1

Key Concepts

  • Pain is an output of the brain, not an input from tissues
  • The brain decides whether to produce pain based on many factors
  • Pain does not always equal tissue damage
  • The nervous system can become sensitized
  • Pain can be influenced by thoughts, emotions, and context

This Does Not Mean Pain Is "In Your Head"

PNE does not suggest pain is imaginary or that it is your fault. Pain is always real. What PNE teaches is that the brain is involved in all pain experiences, and understanding this can help you influence pain through various strategies.

How Pain Really Works

The traditional view of pain as a direct signal from damaged tissue is outdated:2

The Old Model (Biomedical)

  • Tissue damage → pain signal → brain receives signal
  • Pain intensity = amount of tissue damage
  • Fix the tissue, fix the pain

The New Model (Biopsychosocial)

  • Sensors detect potential threats and send information to the brain
  • The brain evaluates ALL information (sensory, emotional, contextual)
  • The brain decides whether to produce pain as a protective response
  • Pain is meant to protect you—sometimes it becomes overprotective
Pain ≠ Damage
Pain intensity does not reliably indicate tissue damage3

Evidence That Pain ≠ Damage

  • Many people with severe injuries report no pain initially (soldiers, athletes)
  • Many people with abnormal imaging (herniated discs, arthritis) have no pain
  • Phantom limb pain occurs in a limb that no longer exists
  • Placebo treatments can significantly reduce pain
  • Expecting pain increases pain (nocebo effect)

Central Sensitization

In chronic pain, the nervous system can become more sensitive:4

What Happens

  • Nerves become more easily activated
  • Pain signals are amplified in the spinal cord and brain
  • Normal sensations may be interpreted as painful
  • Pain persists even after tissue has healed
  • The alarm system becomes too sensitive

Signs of Sensitization

  • Pain that spreads beyond the original area
  • Pain from normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, temperature)
  • Heightened responses to painful stimuli
  • Pain that seems disproportionate to findings

Good News: The Nervous System Can Change

  • Sensitization is reversible—this is neuroplasticity
  • The same plasticity that caused sensitization can reverse it
  • Understanding pain is part of reversing sensitization
  • Gradual exposure, exercise, and reducing threat help

Factors That Influence Pain

The brain considers many factors when deciding whether to produce pain:

Increases Pain

  • Fear and anxiety about pain
  • Belief that pain means damage
  • Poor sleep
  • Stress
  • Depression
  • Previous bad experiences
  • Focusing on pain
  • Social isolation
  • Uncertainty about diagnosis

Decreases Pain

  • Understanding that pain does not equal damage
  • Feeling safe and in control
  • Good sleep
  • Relaxation and stress reduction
  • Positive mood
  • Distraction and engagement
  • Social support
  • Gradual exposure to feared activities
  • Exercise and movement

How PNE Helps

Research shows PNE can:5,6

Benefits

  • Reduce pain intensity
  • Decrease fear of movement
  • Improve physical function
  • Reduce catastrophizing
  • Improve exercise tolerance
  • Reduce healthcare utilization

Why It Works

  • Reduces the threat value of pain
  • Changes unhelpful beliefs about pain
  • Decreases fear and avoidance
  • Empowers active participation in recovery
  • Prepares for active treatments like exercise

Who Benefits Most

  • People with chronic pain
  • Those with high fear of pain or movement
  • People who believe pain always means damage
  • Those avoiding activity due to pain
  • Patients preparing for exercise therapy

Key PNE Concepts

Pain Is Protection

Pain exists to protect you from perceived threats. In chronic pain, the protection system becomes overly sensitive—like an alarm that goes off too easily. The system can be retrained.

Hurt Does Not Equal Harm

Pain during movement usually does not mean you are damaging your body. With chronic pain, tissues have usually healed—the nervous system is just still on alert. Movement is generally safe and therapeutic.

Your Brain Has a Body Map

The brain maintains a map of your body. In chronic pain, this map can become "smudged" or distorted. Movements, visualization, and touch can help sharpen the map again.

Thoughts and Emotions Matter

Because pain is a brain output, anything that changes brain activity can change pain. Thoughts, emotions, beliefs, stress, and attention all influence pain—this is biology, not imagination.

Motion Is Lotion

Movement is one of the best things for chronic pain. It promotes healing, improves the body map, builds confidence, and helps desensitize the nervous system. Start gently and progress gradually.

Combining PNE with Other Treatments

PNE works best when combined with active treatments:6

Exercise and Movement

  • PNE reduces fear, making exercise possible
  • Understanding that "hurt doesn't equal harm" enables activity
  • Graded exercise then desensitizes the system

Graded Exposure

  • Gradually facing feared activities
  • PNE provides the rationale
  • Experience proves that activities are safe

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

  • PNE provides the framework
  • CBT provides tools for changing thoughts and behaviors
  • Complementary approaches

Mindfulness

  • Changes relationship to pain sensations
  • Reduces emotional reactivity
  • Aligns with PNE understanding

Finding PNE-Trained Providers

Look for physical therapists or healthcare providers who mention pain neuroscience education, explain pain, or therapeutic neuroscience education in their approach. Pain clinics and multidisciplinary programs often incorporate PNE.

Applying PNE to Your Life

Step 1: Learn

  • Read about pain science from reputable sources
  • Watch educational videos
  • Consider professional PNE sessions

Step 2: Reflect

  • Consider how your beliefs about pain might affect you
  • Notice what makes your pain better or worse
  • Identify unhelpful thoughts about pain

Step 3: Act

  • Gradually increase activity despite some pain
  • Test your beliefs (does activity actually cause harm?)
  • Address other factors (sleep, stress, mood)
  • Work with healthcare providers who understand pain science
