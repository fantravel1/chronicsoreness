TENS Therapy

Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation for pain relief

What is TENS?

TENS delivers small electrical pulses through electrodes placed on the skin. It's non-invasive, drug-free, and can be used at home. Many people find it helpful for managing chronic pain, though research evidence is mixed.

How TENS Works

Two main theories explain TENS pain relief:

Gate Control Theory

Electrical signals from TENS "close the gate" to pain signals traveling to the brain. The tingling sensation blocks or reduces pain perception.1

Endorphin Release

Certain TENS frequencies may stimulate the release of endorphins—the body's natural painkillers. This provides longer-lasting relief.2

Research Evidence

Mixed Evidence

Cochrane reviews note that evidence for TENS is inconclusive for many conditions due to methodological limitations in studies. However, many individual patients report significant benefit, and it's considered safe when used properly.3,4 NICE recommends considering TENS as part of a multimodal approach.5

Condition-Specific Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain: Low to moderate quality evidence of benefit6
  • Osteoarthritis: Some evidence for knee OA pain reduction7
  • Fibromyalgia: Limited evidence; some patients report benefit8
  • Neuropathic pain: May help some types; evidence limited9
  • Dysmenorrhea: Good evidence for menstrual pain relief10

Types of TENS

Conventional (High-Frequency) TENS

  • Frequency: 80-120 Hz
  • Intensity: Strong but comfortable tingling
  • Sensation: Buzzing, tingling
  • Pain relief: Quick onset, shorter duration
  • Best for: Acute pain, during activities

Acupuncture-Like (Low-Frequency) TENS

  • Frequency: 2-10 Hz
  • Intensity: Higher—may cause muscle twitch
  • Sensation: Muscle contraction, tapping
  • Pain relief: Slower onset, may last longer
  • Best for: Chronic pain, endorphin release

Burst TENS

Combines features of both: high-frequency pulses delivered in low-frequency bursts. Some find this more comfortable.

How to Use TENS

Basic Setup

  1. Clean and dry the skin where electrodes will be placed
  2. Apply electrode pads (ensure good contact)
  3. Connect leads to the TENS unit
  4. Start at lowest intensity
  5. Gradually increase until you feel tingling (not pain)
  6. Adjust frequency/mode as needed

Electrode Placement

  • General rule: Place electrodes around or near the painful area
  • Four-electrode units: Two channels allow targeting multiple areas
  • Minimum distance: Electrodes should be at least 1 inch apart
  • Experiment: Different placements may work better—try various positions

Common Placement Sites

  • Lower back: On either side of spine, above buttocks
  • Neck/shoulder: On trapezius muscles, avoiding front of neck
  • Knee: On either side of kneecap
  • Menstrual pain: Lower abdomen or lower back

Duration and Frequency

  • Session length: 20-60 minutes typically
  • Frequency: Can be used multiple times daily
  • Some people: Use for several hours with breaks
  • Skin checks: Remove pads periodically to check for irritation

Do NOT Use TENS If You Have:

  • Pacemaker or implanted defibrillator: Electrical interference risk
  • Pregnancy: Without medical guidance (avoid abdomen/lower back)
  • Epilepsy: Without consulting your doctor
  • Active cancer: Without oncologist approval
  • Open wounds or broken skin: At electrode sites

Avoid Placing Electrodes On:

  • Front or sides of neck (carotid sinus)
  • Over the heart or chest
  • Over the eyes
  • On the head
  • Broken or irritated skin
  • Areas with numbness (can't feel if too intense)

Choosing a TENS Unit

Features to Consider

  • Channels: 2-channel units offer more flexibility
  • Modes: Multiple preset programs for different pain types
  • Timer: Automatic shut-off for safety
  • Rechargeable: Saves money on batteries
  • Size: Compact units for portable use
  • FDA clearance: Look for OTC FDA-cleared devices

Cost

  • Basic units: $25-50
  • Mid-range: $50-100
  • Premium: $100-300
  • Insurance: May cover with prescription
  • Electrode pads: Replacement needed periodically ($10-20/pack)

Tips for Best Results

  • Experiment with settings: Different modes work for different people
  • Try different placements: Small changes can make big differences
  • Use consistently: Regular use may provide cumulative benefit
  • Combine with other treatments: Part of multimodal approach
  • Replace pads: When they lose stickiness, effectiveness decreases
  • Keep a log: Track what settings and placements work best
