TENS Therapy

Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation for pain relief

Key Takeaways

  • TENS uses mild electrical currents to help relieve pain
  • Generally safe and can be used at home
  • Evidence is mixed but many people find it helpful
  • Best used as part of a comprehensive pain management plan

What Is TENS?

Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) is a therapy that uses low-voltage electrical currents delivered through electrodes placed on the skin to help relieve pain. It is non-invasive, relatively inexpensive, and has few side effects.1

How TENS Works

TENS is thought to work through several mechanisms:

  • Gate control theory: Electrical stimulation activates non-painful nerve fibers that "close the gate" to pain signals at the spinal cord level
  • Endorphin release: Certain TENS settings may stimulate the release of natural pain-relieving endorphins
  • Disrupting pain signals: The electrical sensation provides a competing sensory input that may reduce pain perception

Types of TENS

There are two main TENS approaches:

  • Conventional (high-frequency) TENS: High frequency (50-150 Hz), low intensity—produces tingling sensation
  • Acupuncture-like (low-frequency) TENS: Low frequency (2-10 Hz), higher intensity—produces muscle twitching

Conditions TENS May Help

TENS is commonly used for many pain conditions:2,3

Commonly Treated Conditions

  • Low back pain
  • Neck pain
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Post-surgical pain
  • Labor pain
  • Menstrual pain
  • Neuropathic pain
  • Musculoskeletal pain

Evidence Quality

The research on TENS shows mixed results:4

  • Some studies show modest benefits
  • Others show no difference from sham treatment
  • Individual responses vary significantly
  • May work better for some conditions than others
  • Quality of studies has been criticized

Despite mixed research, many patients and clinicians report TENS to be helpful, and it remains widely used due to its safety and low cost.

Using a TENS Unit

Basic Setup

  1. Clean and dry the skin where electrodes will be placed
  2. Apply electrodes to the skin (usually 2 or 4 pads)
  3. Connect electrode wires to the TENS unit
  4. Turn on unit and gradually increase intensity
  5. Adjust settings for comfort

Electrode Placement

General principles for electrode placement:

  • Place electrodes around or near the painful area
  • Electrodes should not touch each other
  • Place on either side of the painful area
  • Can also place along nerve pathways
  • Experiment with placement for best results

Common Placements by Area

  • Low back: On either side of the spine at the painful level
  • Neck: On either side of the cervical spine
  • Knee: Around the knee joint
  • Shoulder: Above and below the shoulder joint

Settings to Try

  • Frequency: Start with 80-100 Hz for acute pain; try lower (2-10 Hz) for chronic pain
  • Intensity: Strong but comfortable tingling sensation
  • Pulse width: Typically 100-200 microseconds
  • Duration: 20-60 minutes per session

Finding Optimal Settings

There is no single "best" setting for everyone. Experiment with different frequencies, intensities, and electrode placements to find what works best for your pain. Keep a log to track what settings provide the most relief.

Safety Considerations

Contraindications

Do NOT use TENS if you have:5

  • Pacemaker or implanted defibrillator: TENS can interfere with these devices
  • Pregnancy: Avoid on abdomen, pelvis, or lower back (except during labor under supervision)
  • Epilepsy: Avoid use on head or neck
  • Deep vein thrombosis: Risk of dislodging clot
  • Active cancer: Without medical guidance

Areas to Avoid

  • Over the heart or across the chest
  • On the front or sides of the neck
  • Over the eyes
  • On numb areas (cannot gauge intensity)
  • On broken or irritated skin
  • Over metal implants (relative caution)

General Safety Tips

  • Start with lowest intensity and gradually increase
  • Do not use while driving or operating machinery
  • Do not use in water or shower
  • Check skin regularly for irritation
  • Replace electrodes when they lose stickiness
  • Do not sleep with TENS on unless specifically advised

Consult Healthcare Provider

Consult with your healthcare provider before using TENS, especially if you have any health conditions or are unsure if it is appropriate for you.

Getting the Most from TENS

Tips for Effectiveness

  • Use consistently—regular use may provide better results
  • Adjust intensity to feel strong tingling without discomfort
  • Vary electrode placement if current position is not working
  • Try different frequencies for different types of pain
  • Use during activities that typically increase pain
  • Combine with other treatments (stretching, heat, etc.)

When to Use

  • During pain flares
  • Before or during activities that trigger pain
  • To reduce reliance on pain medications
  • As part of a daily pain management routine

Limitations

  • Effects are often temporary
  • Does not address underlying cause of pain
  • May become less effective over time for some people
  • Not effective for everyone

Choosing a TENS Unit

Features to Consider

  • Number of channels: 2-channel units allow treatment of multiple areas
  • Adjustable settings: Frequency, intensity, pulse width controls
  • Pre-set programs: Convenient but less customizable
  • Battery type: Rechargeable vs. disposable
  • Portability: Size and weight for mobile use
  • Display: Easy-to-read screen

Where to Get TENS

  • Prescription from healthcare provider (may be covered by insurance)
  • Over-the-counter units at pharmacies and online
  • Physical therapy clinics (often provide training)

Electrode Care

  • Store with plastic backing on gel surface
  • Replace when they no longer stick well
  • Keep skin clean and dry before applying
  • Some electrodes can be moistened to extend life
  • Keep extra electrodes on hand

Related Electrical Therapies

Other forms of electrical stimulation for pain:6

Interferential Current (IFC)

  • Uses higher frequency currents that cross in tissue
  • Deeper penetration than TENS
  • Usually administered by physical therapists

Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation (NMES)

  • Causes muscle contractions
  • Used for muscle strengthening and rehabilitation
  • Different purpose than pure pain relief

Percutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (PENS)

  • Uses needle electrodes inserted into tissue
  • Combines acupuncture and electrical stimulation
  • Requires professional administration

Spinal Cord Stimulation

  • Implanted device for severe chronic pain
  • Reserved for cases not responding to other treatments
  • Requires surgery and careful patient selection

Research Findings

Current evidence on TENS effectiveness:4,7

Low Back Pain

  • Cochrane reviews find evidence inconclusive
  • Some studies show short-term benefits
  • May be helpful as part of multimodal treatment

Osteoarthritis

  • More positive evidence for knee osteoarthritis
  • May reduce pain and improve function short-term

Labor Pain

  • Popular use with some supportive evidence
  • May reduce need for other pain relief

Fibromyalgia

  • Limited evidence of benefit
  • Some patients report helpful
