Radiofrequency Ablation

Using heat to disrupt pain signals

What Is Radiofrequency Ablation?

Radiofrequency ablation (RFA) uses heat generated by radio waves to create a lesion on nerves that transmit pain signals. This disrupts the nerve's ability to send pain signals to the brain.1

How It Works

  • A needle with electrode tip is placed near target nerve
  • Radio waves create heat (80-90Â°C)
  • Heat creates lesion on nerve
  • Pain signals are interrupted
  • Guided by fluoroscopy

Conditions Treated

  • Facet joint pain (neck and back)
  • Sacroiliac joint pain
  • Knee osteoarthritis (genicular nerve)
  • Chronic headaches
  • Peripheral nerve pain
  • Trigeminal neuralgia

Prerequisites

  • Diagnostic blocks first (must show good response)
  • Usually requires 50-80% relief from blocks
  • Helps predict RFA success

The Procedure2

  • Outpatient procedure
  • Local anesthesia and sedation
  • Takes 30-60 minutes
  • Sensory and motor testing before lesioning

Expected Outcomes

  • Relief typically lasts 6-12 months
  • Some get longer relief
  • Nerves can regenerate - may need repeat
  • About 70% effective when properly selected

Side Effects and Risks

  • Post-procedure discomfort (days to weeks)
  • Numbness in treated area
  • Infection (rare)
  • Nerve damage (rare)
