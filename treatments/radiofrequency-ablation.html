Radiofrequency Ablation

Using heat to interrupt pain signals

What Is Radiofrequency Ablation?

Radiofrequency ablation (RFA), also called radiofrequency neurotomy, uses heat generated by radio waves to damage specific nerves and interrupt pain signals. It's commonly used for chronic back pain, neck pain, and certain types of arthritis. RFA can provide longer-lasting relief than nerve blocks alone.1

How It Works2

  • Needle placed near target nerve
  • Position confirmed with imaging
  • Radio waves generate heat
  • Heat disrupts nerve function
  • Pain signals no longer transmitted

Types of RFA

Conventional/Thermal RFA

  • Continuous heat application
  • Creates lesion on nerve
  • Most studied technique

Pulsed RFA

  • Intermittent heat application
  • Lower temperatures used
  • May preserve motor function

Cooled RFA

  • Water-cooled electrode
  • Creates larger lesion
  • May improve outcomes

Conditions Treated3

  • Facet joint pain (spine)
  • Sacroiliac joint pain
  • Chronic neck pain
  • Chronic low back pain
  • Knee osteoarthritis (genicular nerves)
  • Some headache conditions

Before the Procedure

  • Diagnostic nerve blocks required first
  • Must show positive response to blocks
  • Review medications with doctor
  • May need to stop blood thinners
  • Arrange for driver home

During the Procedure

  • Outpatient procedure
  • Local anesthesia used
  • Fluoroscopy (X-ray) guides placement
  • Sensory and motor testing performed
  • Takes 30-90 minutes
  • May feel heat or tingling

After the Procedure

  • Observation period
  • May have soreness for days/weeks
  • Full relief may take 2-4 weeks
  • Avoid strenuous activity briefly

Expected Results

  • Relief typically lasts 6-18 months
  • Some get years of relief
  • Nerves may regenerate (pain returns)
  • Procedure can be repeated
  • 70-80% success rate when well-selected

Risks

  • Temporary increased pain
  • Numbness (usually temporary)
  • Infection (rare)
  • Nerve damage (rare)
  • Allergic reaction (rare)
