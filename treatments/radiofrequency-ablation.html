Radiofrequency Ablation

Using heat to reduce nerve pain signals

What Is Radiofrequency Ablation?

Radiofrequency ablation (RFA), also called radiofrequency neurotomy, uses heat generated by radio waves to target specific nerves and interrupt pain signals. It's used when other treatments haven't provided adequate relief and diagnostic nerve blocks have confirmed the pain source.1

How It Works

  • Needle placed near target nerve using X-ray guidance
  • Radiofrequency current heats nerve tissue
  • Creates a lesion on the nerve
  • Interrupts pain signal transmission
  • Outpatient procedure

Common Uses2

Spine Pain

  • Facet joint pain (neck and back)
  • Sacroiliac joint pain
  • Post-laminectomy syndrome

Other Areas

  • Knee pain (genicular nerve ablation)
  • Hip pain
  • Some peripheral nerve conditions

Before the Procedure

  • Diagnostic nerve blocks typically done first
  • Must show good response to blocks
  • May need to stop blood thinners
  • Discuss medications with doctor
  • Arrange transportation home

What to Expect3

During Procedure

  • Done under local anesthesia
  • May have sedation
  • Needle placement guided by fluoroscopy
  • Stimulation testing to verify position
  • Takes about 30-90 minutes

After Procedure

  • May have increased pain for 1-2 weeks
  • Pain relief typically develops over weeks
  • Full effect may take 6-8 weeks
  • Relief can last 6 months to 2+ years
  • Can be repeated when effects wear off

Types of RFA

  • Conventional (thermal): Uses heat to create lesion
  • Pulsed: Uses intermittent current, less tissue damage
  • Cooled: Larger lesion size

Risks and Side Effects

  • Temporary increase in pain
  • Numbness (usually temporary)
  • Infection (rare)
  • Nerve injury (uncommon)
  • Motor weakness (rare)

Success Rates

  • About 50-80% of patients get significant relief
  • Better outcomes when diagnostic blocks were helpful
  • Effects may diminish over time as nerves regenerate
  • Repeat procedures often effective
