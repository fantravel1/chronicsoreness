Radiofrequency Ablation

Using heat to disrupt pain signals

What Is Radiofrequency Ablation?

Radiofrequency ablation (RFA) uses heat generated from radio waves to create a lesion on pain-transmitting nerves. This interrupts pain signals, providing longer-lasting relief than injections alone. It's commonly used for facet joint pain and sacroiliac joint pain.1

Types

  • Conventional (thermal) RFA - Continuous heat
  • Pulsed RFA - Intermittent heat, less tissue destruction
  • Cooled RFA - Larger lesions, useful for SI joint

Conditions Treated2

  • Facet joint pain (neck and back)
  • Sacroiliac joint pain
  • Some knee pain (genicular nerves)
  • Trigeminal neuralgia
  • Occipital neuralgia

The Procedure

Before RFA

  • Diagnostic nerve blocks confirm pain source
  • Usually requires 50-80% relief from blocks

During RFA

  • Outpatient procedure
  • Local anesthesia with sedation
  • Fluoroscopic (X-ray) guidance
  • Needle placement near target nerve
  • Heat applied for 60-90 seconds

Results3

  • Relief typically lasts 6-12 months
  • Some get 2+ years of relief
  • Nerves regenerate, so repeat may be needed
  • Success rates 50-80%

Recovery

  • May have increased pain for 1-2 weeks
  • Full effect in 2-4 weeks
  • Resume activities as tolerated
