Scrambler Therapy

Retraining pain perception

What Is Scrambler Therapy?

Scrambler therapy (also called Calmare or MC-5A) is a non-invasive treatment that uses surface electrodes to deliver electrical impulses that the brain may interpret as non-pain signals. The theory is that it can "retrain" the brain's perception of pain by replacing pain information with normal sensation signals.1

How It Works (Theory)2

  • Electrodes placed around painful area
  • Delivers specific electrical patterns
  • Aims to send "no pain" signals to brain
  • May modulate central sensitization
  • Different mechanism than TENS
  • Exact mechanisms still being studied

Conditions Studied

  • Chemotherapy-induced neuropathy
  • Postherpetic neuralgia
  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • CRPS
  • Diabetic neuropathy
  • Phantom limb pain
  • Chronic post-surgical pain

The Treatment3

  • Series of sessions (usually 10-12)
  • 45-60 minutes per session
  • Daily or near-daily initially
  • Surface electrodes (non-invasive)
  • Sensation should be comfortable
  • No pain during treatment

Research Evidence

  • Growing body of research
  • Some studies show promising results
  • Especially for chemotherapy neuropathy
  • Evidence quality still developing
  • More research needed
  • Not yet mainstream

Potential Benefits

  • Non-invasive
  • No medications
  • Generally well tolerated
  • Effects may last after treatment
  • Can repeat if needed

Limitations

  • Limited availability
  • Often not covered by insurance
  • Requires multiple sessions
  • Not effective for everyone
  • Evidence still emerging
  • Expensive out-of-pocket

Contraindications

  • Pacemakers or implanted devices
  • Pregnancy
  • Active skin conditions in treatment area
  • Certain heart conditions

Finding Treatment

  • Specialized pain centers
  • Some oncology centers
  • Limited number of providers
  • Ask about provider experience
