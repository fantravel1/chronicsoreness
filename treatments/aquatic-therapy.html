Aquatic Therapy

Water-based exercise for chronic pain relief

Key Takeaways

  • Water provides buoyancy that reduces stress on painful joints
  • Strong evidence supports aquatic therapy for fibromyalgia and osteoarthritis
  • Warm water can help relax muscles and reduce pain
  • Excellent option when land-based exercise is too painful

What Is Aquatic Therapy?

Aquatic therapy (also called hydrotherapy or pool therapy) involves exercises performed in water, typically in a heated pool. The unique properties of water make it an ideal environment for people with chronic pain to exercise with less discomfort.1

Properties of Water That Help

  • Buoyancy: Reduces body weight on joints by up to 90% when immersed to the neck
  • Hydrostatic pressure: Provides gentle compression that reduces swelling
  • Resistance: Water provides resistance in all directions for strengthening
  • Warmth: Heated pools (typically 92-96°F / 33-36°C) relax muscles and increase circulation
  • Sensory input: Water pressure provides proprioceptive feedback

Conditions That Benefit

Research supports aquatic therapy for many chronic pain conditions:2,3

Strong Evidence

  • Fibromyalgia: Multiple studies show improvements in pain, function, and quality of life4
  • Osteoarthritis: Effective for hip and knee arthritis
  • Low back pain: May be especially helpful for acute flares
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Gentle exercise during flares

Also Beneficial For

  • Ankylosing spondylitis
  • Post-surgical rehabilitation
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • Pregnancy-related pain
  • Obesity-related joint pain
  • Post-stroke rehabilitation

Ideal Candidates

Aquatic therapy may be particularly helpful if you:

  • Find land-based exercise too painful
  • Have weight-bearing limitations
  • Have multiple painful joints
  • Need a gentler starting point for exercise
  • Have balance concerns

Types of Aquatic Therapy

Individual Physical Therapy

  • One-on-one sessions with a physical therapist
  • Customized exercises for your condition
  • Hands-on assistance and manual therapy in water
  • May be covered by insurance with prescription

Group Classes

  • Water aerobics or aqua fitness
  • Arthritis-specific water classes (Arthritis Foundation programs)
  • Low-impact and accessible
  • Social support from group setting

Independent Pool Exercise

  • Self-directed exercise using learned techniques
  • Walking, swimming, or exercises in water
  • Maintenance after structured program

Specialized Approaches

  • Ai Chi: Water-based Tai Chi movements
  • Bad Ragaz Ring Method: Therapist-guided floating exercises
  • Watsu: Passive aquatic bodywork
  • Halliwick Method: Focus on balance and motor control

Benefits of Aquatic Exercise

Pain-Related Benefits

  • Reduced joint loading and impact
  • Muscle relaxation from warm water
  • Improved pain tolerance
  • Ability to exercise longer with less pain

Physical Benefits

  • Improved strength through water resistance
  • Better flexibility and range of motion
  • Enhanced balance and coordination
  • Cardiovascular fitness
  • Reduced swelling

Psychological Benefits

  • Relaxation and stress relief
  • Improved mood
  • Better sleep
  • Increased confidence in movement
  • Social connection in group settings

Common Exercises

Walking and Movement

  • Water walking: Forward, backward, sideways
  • Marching: High knee lifts in place
  • Leg swings: Forward/back and side to side
  • Gentle jogging: In shallow or deep water

Strengthening

  • Arm circles and sweeps: Using water resistance
  • Leg kicks: Various directions
  • Squats: With buoyancy support
  • Resistance equipment: Noodles, paddles, water weights

Stretching and Range of Motion

  • Arm stretches: Using pool edge or wall
  • Leg stretches: Supported by buoyancy
  • Trunk rotations: Gentle twisting movements
  • Floating stretches: Using noodles for support

Balance Exercises

  • Single leg stance: Easier with water support
  • Weight shifts: Side to side, front to back
  • Reaching activities: Challenging balance

Getting Started

Finding Programs

  • Ask your doctor or physical therapist for referrals
  • Local hospitals and rehabilitation centers
  • YMCA, community centers, and fitness facilities
  • Arthritis Foundation (arthritis.org) water exercise programs

What to Bring

  • Swimsuit (comfortable, secure fit)
  • Water shoes (for grip and foot protection)
  • Towel and change of clothes
  • Goggles if needed
  • Water bottle (you still sweat in water)

Starting Tips

  • Start slowly—water exercise can be more tiring than expected
  • Begin with shorter sessions (15-20 minutes)
  • Listen to your body and rest when needed
  • Gradually increase duration and intensity
  • Stay hydrated despite being in water

Water Temperature

For chronic pain, warmer water (92-96°F / 33-36°C) is typically best. Some facilities have therapy pools kept warmer than standard lap pools. Check water temperature before starting if you have temperature sensitivities.

Safety Considerations

When to Use Caution

  • Open wounds or skin infections
  • Uncontrolled seizures
  • Severe cardiac conditions
  • Uncontrolled blood pressure
  • Fear of water
  • Incontinence issues
  • Certain infections

Pool Safety

  • Never swim alone
  • Know the pool depth and layout
  • Use handrails when entering and exiting
  • Stay near the edge if not a confident swimmer
  • Use flotation devices if needed

After Swimming

  • Shower after to remove chlorine
  • Moisturize skin to prevent dryness
  • Allow time to rest if needed
  • Note any symptom changes

Research Evidence

Evidence supporting aquatic therapy:2,4,5

Fibromyalgia

  • Cochrane review found moderate-quality evidence for pain reduction
  • Improvements in physical function and stiffness
  • Benefits may persist after treatment ends

Osteoarthritis

  • Reduces pain and improves function
  • Effects comparable to land-based exercise
  • May be better tolerated for severe arthritis

Low Back Pain

  • Evidence shows short-term benefits
  • May help during acute flares when land exercise is difficult

Compared to Land Exercise

  • Generally similar outcomes for many conditions
  • Advantage is better tolerance for those who struggle with land exercise
  • May allow more exercise with less pain

Practical Considerations

Cost

  • Individual PT: Often covered by insurance with prescription
  • Group classes: Typically $5-20 per class
  • Pool memberships: Variable by facility
  • Some Medicare Advantage plans cover gym memberships

Access

  • Not all areas have accessible therapy pools
  • Public pools may be too cold for therapy
  • Some facilities have limited hours for therapy pools
  • Travel distance may be a barrier

Alternatives

If pool access is limited:

  • Warm baths with gentle stretching at home
  • Hot tub exercises (if available)
  • Look for warm-water community programs
  • Consider seasonal outdoor options in summer
