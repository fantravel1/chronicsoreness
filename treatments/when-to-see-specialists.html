When to See Specialists

Recognizing when you need expert help—and knowing which specialist to see

Red Flags Requiring URGENT Evaluation

Seek immediate medical care (ER or urgent care) if you experience:

  • Sudden loss of bowel or bladder control
  • Bilateral leg weakness or saddle anesthesia (numbness in groin area)
  • Progressive neurological deficits (worsening weakness or numbness)
  • Pain with fever and chills (possible infection)
  • Unexplained weight loss with pain
  • Pain after significant trauma
  • Worst headache of your life (sudden onset)

Which Specialist to See

Rheumatologist

Specializes in: Autoimmune conditions, inflammatory arthritis, fibromyalgia, systemic inflammatory diseases

See a rheumatologist if you have:

  • Morning stiffness lasting more than 1 hour
  • Joint swelling, redness, or warmth
  • Suspected autoimmune condition
  • Elevated inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP)
  • Positive ANA or other autoimmune antibodies
  • Symptoms suggesting lupus, RA, or Sjögren's
  • Fibromyalgia (some rheumatologists specialize in this)

Neurologist

Specializes in: Nervous system disorders, neuropathic pain, headaches, complex regional pain syndrome

See a neurologist if you have:

  • Peripheral neuropathy symptoms (burning, tingling, numbness in hands/feet)
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  • Trigeminal neuralgia
  • Chronic migraines or headache disorders
  • Radiculopathy (nerve root compression)
  • Unexplained weakness or sensory changes

Pain Specialist / Physiatrist

Specializes in: Comprehensive pain management, interventional procedures, rehabilitation medicine

See a pain specialist if you have:

  • Pain persisting 6-12 weeks despite conservative treatment
  • Complex multisite pain
  • Need for interventional procedures (injections, nerve blocks)
  • Medication management for chronic pain
  • Functional limitations requiring rehabilitation approach
  • Need for multidisciplinary coordination

Gynecologist / Endometriosis Specialist

Specializes in: Pelvic pain, endometriosis, adenomyosis, chronic pelvic pain conditions

See this specialist if you have:

  • Severe menstrual pain
  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Pain with intercourse
  • Suspected endometriosis
  • Bladder or bowel symptoms with menstrual cycle

Endocrinologist

Specializes in: Hormonal disorders, thyroid conditions, metabolic issues

See an endocrinologist if you have:

  • Thyroid disorder not responding to treatment
  • Complex hormonal symptoms
  • Metabolic bone disease
  • Adrenal or pituitary concerns

General Guidelines for Specialist Referral

Time-Based Criteria

  • Acute pain not improving after 2-4 weeks: Consider evaluation
  • Pain persisting 6-12 weeks despite conservative treatment: Strong indication for specialist
  • Pain lasting 3+ months: Chronic pain threshold—multimodal approach usually needed

Symptom-Based Criteria

  • Pain significantly impacting daily function or work
  • Pain not responding to appropriate first-line treatments
  • Diagnostic uncertainty after initial evaluation
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats)
  • Neurological symptoms
  • Signs of inflammation (swelling, redness, elevated markers)

Preparing for Your Specialist Appointment

Maximize your limited appointment time with preparation:

Bring Documentation

  • Symptom diary: Patterns, triggers, what helps/worsens
  • Treatment history: What you've tried and responses
  • Test results: Labs, imaging, other workup
  • Medication list: Current and past medications with doses
  • Functional impact: How pain affects your daily life

Prepare Questions

  • What conditions could be causing these symptoms?
  • What tests would help rule out or confirm a diagnosis?
  • What are ALL my treatment options, including doing nothing?
  • What does research say about success rates?
  • How will we know if treatment is working?
  • When should I follow up?

Bring Support

Consider bringing a trusted person to:

  • Take notes
  • Ask follow-up questions
  • Provide their observations of your symptoms
  • Help you remember information afterward

If You're Not Taken Seriously

Unfortunately, women's pain is often dismissed. If you feel your concerns aren't being addressed:

Advocacy Scripts

When told "It's stress/anxiety":

"I understand stress can affect health, but these symptoms are significantly impacting my daily life. I'd like to rule out physical causes first. What tests would help us do that?"

When told "Tests are normal":

"I appreciate the testing, but normal results don't match my experience. What other conditions should we consider, or what additional testing might be helpful?"

Critical script for documentation:

"If you're declining to order this test or prescribe this treatment, I'd like you to document that refusal and your reasoning in my medical record."

Seeking Second Opinions

It's appropriate to seek a second opinion when:

  • You've received a serious or life-altering diagnosis
  • Treatment isn't working despite adequate trial
  • Surgery is recommended
  • No clear diagnosis after multiple visits
  • You feel uncertain or unheard

How to request: "I value our relationship. Before moving forward with treatment, I'd like to get a second opinion to make sure I'm exploring all options."

Request your records: Get complete medical records including office notes, test results, and imaging files—not just reports.
