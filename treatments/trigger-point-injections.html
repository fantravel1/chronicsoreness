Trigger Point Injections

Targeted treatment for myofascial pain

What Are Trigger Point Injections?

Trigger point injections (TPIs) involve inserting a needle into trigger points - tight, painful areas in muscles that can cause local and referred pain. The injection may contain local anesthetic, saline, or be "dry needling" with no injectate.1

What Are Trigger Points?

  • Taut bands in muscle
  • Tender nodules
  • Can cause local and referred pain
  • May limit range of motion
  • Can produce twitch response when palpated

Conditions Treated2

  • Myofascial pain syndrome
  • Tension headaches
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic neck and back pain
  • Shoulder pain
  • TMJ-related muscle pain

The Procedure

  • Trigger point identified by palpation
  • Skin cleaned
  • Needle inserted into trigger point
  • May feel twitch response
  • Medication injected (or dry needling)
  • Multiple points may be treated
  • Takes only a few minutes

Injectates Used

  • Local anesthetic (lidocaine, bupivacaine)
  • Saline
  • Nothing (dry needling)
  • Sometimes corticosteroid or botox

Evidence3

  • Mixed evidence overall
  • Needle insertion itself may be therapeutic
  • No clear superiority of one injectate
  • Often combined with physical therapy

After the Procedure

  • Stretch treated muscles
  • May have soreness for 1-2 days
  • Ice if needed
  • Follow up with stretching/PT

Risks

  • Temporary soreness
  • Bruising
  • Infection (rare)
  • Pneumothorax (if chest muscles treated - rare)
