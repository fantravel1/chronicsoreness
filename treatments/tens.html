TENS Therapy

Understanding transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation for pain management

What Is TENS?

Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) is a non-invasive pain treatment that uses low-voltage electrical currents delivered through electrodes placed on the skin. It's one of the most widely used electrotherapy treatments for chronic pain.1

TENS units are available both by prescription and over the counter. They're portable, relatively inexpensive, and have an excellent safety profileâ€”making them accessible for home use as part of a pain management plan.

How TENS Works

TENS is believed to reduce pain through two main mechanisms:2

  • Gate control theory - Electrical stimulation activates large nerve fibers that "close the gate" to pain signals traveling to the brain
  • Endorphin release - Lower frequency stimulation may trigger the release of natural pain-relieving endorphins
  • Distraction - The tingling sensation may redirect attention from pain

Types of TENS Settings

Conventional (High-Frequency) TENS

  • Frequency - 80-120 Hz (pulses per second)
  • Intensity - Low, comfortable tingling
  • Sensation - Strong but comfortable
  • Mechanism - Gate control; works during stimulation
  • Duration - Can use for extended periods

Acupuncture-Like (Low-Frequency) TENS

  • Frequency - 2-10 Hz
  • Intensity - Higher, causing muscle twitching
  • Sensation - Rhythmic muscle contractions
  • Mechanism - Endorphin release; effects may last longer
  • Duration - Usually 20-30 minute sessions

Burst Mode

  • Bursts of high-frequency pulses at low-frequency intervals
  • Combines aspects of both modes
  • May be more comfortable than continuous low-frequency

Research on TENS Effectiveness

What the Evidence Shows

Research on TENS for chronic pain is mixed:3

  • Many studies show benefit, but quality varies
  • Placebo control is challenging (people feel the stimulation)
  • Effects may be modest on average
  • Individual responses vary significantly
  • Some people find it very helpful; others notice little effect

Conditions Where TENS May Help

  • Osteoarthritis - Some evidence for knee OA4
  • Chronic low back pain - Mixed results
  • Neck pain - Limited evidence
  • Fibromyalgia - May help some people
  • Neuropathic pain - Variable responses
  • Postoperative pain - Can reduce medication needs
  • Labor pain - Popular option

Try It and See

Given TENS's safety and low cost, many providers recommend trying it to see if it helps you. Research averages don't predict individual response. If it provides relief with no side effects, that's a win regardless of what studies show for populations.

How to Use TENS

Electrode Placement

  • Around painful area - Place electrodes on or near the painful region
  • Along nerve pathways - Target nerves supplying the painful area
  • Acupuncture points - Some protocols use traditional points
  • Avoid - Front of neck, over eyes, on wounds, over the heart
  • Experiment - Small changes in placement can affect results

Settings and Duration

  • Start low - Increase intensity gradually
  • Strong but comfortable - Should not be painful
  • Conventional TENS - Can use for hours if needed
  • Low-frequency TENS - Usually 20-30 minute sessions
  • Consistency - Regular use often more effective than occasional

Tips for Best Results

  • Clean, dry skin for good electrode contact
  • Replace electrodes when they lose stickiness
  • Try different settings and placements
  • Use during activities or when pain is worst
  • Keep a log to track what works

Safety and Precautions

TENS is Generally Very Safe

  • Non-invasive and drug-free
  • Side effects rare and mild
  • May cause skin irritation under electrodes
  • Can use alongside other treatments

Who Should NOT Use TENS

  • Pacemaker or implanted defibrillator - Can interfere with device5
  • Pregnancy - Avoid over abdomen/pelvis (labor use is different)
  • Epilepsy - Avoid head and neck placement
  • Over malignancy - Theoretical concerns
  • Undiagnosed pain - Get proper diagnosis first

Placement Precautions

  • Never place electrodes on front or sides of neck
  • Avoid eyes, mouth, and head
  • Don't use over broken skin or wounds
  • Avoid directly over the heart
  • Be cautious with decreased sensation areas

Important Safety Note

While TENS is generally safe, consult your healthcare provider before use if you have a cardiac pacemaker, implanted defibrillator, or other implanted electronic device. Also check with your provider if you're pregnant or have epilepsy or heart disease.

Choosing a TENS Unit

Features to Consider

  • Adjustable settings - Frequency, intensity, pulse width
  • Multiple channels - 2-4 channels for different areas
  • Preset programs - Convenient but manual control useful
  • Portability - Size and battery life
  • Timer - Automatic shut-off

Price Range

  • Basic units: $20-50
  • Mid-range: $50-100
  • Professional-grade: $100-300+
  • More expensive doesn't always mean more effective

Realistic Expectations

  • TENS is one tool among many for pain management
  • Not a cure, but may provide temporary relief
  • Effects usually last during and shortly after use
  • Some people find lasting benefit; others don't respond
  • Safe to try as part of comprehensive approach
  • Low risk makes it worth experimenting with
