PRP Therapy

Using your body's healing factors

What Is PRP?

Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) therapy involves injecting a concentration of a patient's own platelets to accelerate healing of injured tendons, ligaments, muscles, and joints. Platelets contain growth factors that may promote tissue repair. PRP has gained popularity as a regenerative treatment option.1

How It Works2

  • Blood drawn from patient
  • Centrifuged to concentrate platelets
  • PRP injected at injury site
  • Releases growth factors
  • May stimulate healing cascade

Conditions Treated

  • Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis)
  • Knee osteoarthritis
  • Patellar tendinopathy
  • Achilles tendinopathy
  • Plantar fasciitis
  • Rotator cuff injuries
  • Hip osteoarthritis

The Procedure3

  • Blood draw (15-60 mL)
  • Processing in centrifuge (15-30 min)
  • Injection at treatment site
  • Often ultrasound-guided
  • Outpatient procedure
  • Takes about 30-60 minutes total

After Treatment

  • May have soreness at injection site
  • Avoid NSAIDs (may interfere with healing)
  • Rest period varies by condition
  • May need multiple treatments
  • Full effects may take weeks to months

Evidence

  • Promising for some conditions
  • Best evidence for knee OA, tennis elbow
  • Research ongoing
  • Results variable
  • Not all studies show benefit

Advantages

  • Uses your own blood (autologous)
  • Low risk of allergic reaction
  • Minimally invasive
  • Generally safe
  • May delay or avoid surgery

Limitations

  • Not covered by most insurance
  • Can be expensive
  • Results not guaranteed
  • Preparation methods vary
  • Lack of standardization

Who Might Consider

  • Failed conservative treatment
  • Wanting to avoid surgery
  • Active individuals
  • Chronic tendon problems
  • Early to moderate osteoarthritis
