Pain Psychology

Psychological approaches to chronic pain management

What Is Pain Psychology?

Pain psychology is a specialty within psychology focused on understanding and treating chronic pain. Pain psychologists use evidence-based approaches to help people cope with pain, improve function, and enhance quality of life. This is not about "pain being in your head."1

Why Psychology for Pain?

  • Pain involves both physical and psychological processes
  • How we think about pain affects experience
  • Emotions influence pain perception
  • Behaviors can help or worsen pain
  • Psychological approaches have strong evidence

Approaches Used2

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

  • Identify and change unhelpful thoughts
  • Modify behaviors that worsen pain
  • Develop coping skills

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

  • Accept what can't be changed
  • Focus on valued activities
  • Mindfulness skills

Other Approaches

  • Mindfulness-based stress reduction
  • Biofeedback
  • Relaxation training
  • Pain neuroscience education

What Pain Psychologists Help With3

  • Catastrophizing and fear of pain
  • Depression and anxiety related to pain
  • Sleep problems
  • Stress management
  • Improving function despite pain
  • Communication about pain
  • Adjusting to living with chronic pain

What to Expect

  • Assessment of pain and its impact
  • Collaborative goal setting
  • Learning and practicing skills
  • Usually time-limited (8-12 sessions)
  • Homework between sessions

Finding a Pain Psychologist

  • Ask your pain doctor for referral
  • Multidisciplinary pain programs
  • Psychology directories (search "pain")
  • Some offer telehealth
