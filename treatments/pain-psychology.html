Pain Psychology

Mind-body approaches to chronic pain

What Is Pain Psychology?

Pain psychology is a specialized field that uses psychological techniques to help manage chronic pain. It's based on the understanding that pain is a complex experience involving physical, emotional, and cognitive factors. This is not about pain being "in your head"â€”it's about using proven techniques to change how your brain processes pain.1

Why Psychology for Pain?2

  • Pain is processed in the brain
  • Thoughts and emotions affect pain experience
  • Stress amplifies pain
  • Learning new skills can reduce suffering
  • Addresses the whole person

Evidence-Based Approaches3

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

  • Identifies and changes unhelpful thoughts
  • Develops coping strategies
  • Reduces catastrophizing
  • Most studied psychological treatment

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

  • Accepts pain without fighting it
  • Focuses on valued life activities
  • Builds psychological flexibility

Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)

  • Present-moment awareness
  • Meditation practices
  • Reduces stress and pain reactivity

Other Techniques

  • Relaxation training
  • Biofeedback
  • Hypnosis
  • Pain neuroscience education

What to Expect

  • Assessment of pain and its impact
  • Setting goals for treatment
  • Learning and practicing new skills
  • Typically 8-12 sessions
  • Homework between sessions

Common Misconceptions

  • "It means my pain isn't real" - FALSE
  • "I'll just be told to think positive" - FALSE
  • "It's a last resort" - FALSE (works best early)
  • "I don't have mental problems" - It's about skills, not diagnosis

Finding a Pain Psychologist

  • Ask your pain doctor for referral
  • Pain management programs
  • Psychologists with chronic pain specialty
  • Telehealth options available
