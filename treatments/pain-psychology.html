Pain Psychology

The mind-body approach to pain management

What Is Pain Psychology?

Pain psychology is a specialty that uses psychological principles to help people manage chronic pain. Pain psychologists understand that pain is a brain experience influenced by thoughts, emotions, behaviors, and social factors. This doesn't mean pain is "in your head"â€”it means the brain plays a key role in how pain is processed and experienced.1

Why Doesn't This Mean Pain Is Imaginary?

  • All pain is real and processed by the brain
  • Brain scans show chronic pain changes brain structure
  • Psychological factors can increase or decrease pain signals
  • Mind-body treatments affect the same pathways as medications

What Pain Psychologists Do2

  • Assess how pain affects your life
  • Teach coping skills
  • Address depression, anxiety, trauma
  • Help with sleep problems
  • Work on relationship impacts
  • Support behavior change

Treatment Approaches3

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

  • Identify unhelpful thought patterns
  • Challenge catastrophizing
  • Behavioral activation
  • Problem-solving skills
  • Most researched for pain

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

  • Accept what can't be controlled
  • Focus on values-based living
  • Psychological flexibility
  • Mindfulness components

Other Approaches

  • Mindfulness-based stress reduction
  • Biofeedback
  • Hypnosis
  • Relaxation training
  • Pain reprocessing therapy

What Treatment Addresses

  • Pain catastrophizing
  • Fear-avoidance behaviors
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Sleep disruption
  • Stress management
  • Activity pacing
  • Communication about pain

What to Expect

  • Initial assessment (1-2 sessions)
  • Goal setting together
  • Weekly sessions typically
  • Homework/practice between sessions
  • Treatment length varies (8-16 sessions common)

Finding a Pain Psychologist

  • Ask your pain doctor for referral
  • Pain clinics often have psychologists
  • Search "pain psychologist" in your area
  • Telehealth options available
  • Ask about chronic pain experience

Effectiveness

  • Strong research support
  • Can reduce pain intensity
  • Improves function and quality of life
  • Reduces depression and anxiety
  • Effects can be long-lasting
