Pain Psychology

Evidence-based psychological approaches to help you manage chronic pain and improve quality of life

What Is Pain Psychology?

Pain psychology is a specialized field that applies psychological principles and techniques to help people cope with chronic pain. It recognizes that pain is a complex experience influenced by thoughts, emotions, behaviors, and social factorsâ€”not just tissue damage.1

Psychological treatments don't aim to convince you that pain is "all in your head." Instead, they provide evidence-based tools to reduce pain intensity, improve function, and enhance quality of lifeâ€”even when some pain persists.

Why Psychology Matters for Pain

  • The brain processes and interprets all pain signals
  • Thoughts and emotions can amplify or dampen pain
  • Behaviors (avoidance, pacing) affect pain outcomes
  • Psychological approaches produce measurable brain changes
  • Research shows these treatments work for chronic pain2

Key Psychological Approaches

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

The most researched psychological treatment for chronic pain:

  • Core idea - Thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected
  • Cognitive techniques - Identify and challenge unhelpful thoughts about pain
  • Behavioral techniques - Activity pacing, graded exposure, relaxation
  • Evidence - Consistently shows modest but significant benefits for pain and function3
  • Format - Usually 8-12 sessions, individual or group

What CBT Addresses

  • Catastrophizing - "This pain will never end; I can't cope"
  • All-or-nothing thinking - "If I can't do everything, why do anything?"
  • Fear-avoidance - Avoiding activities due to fear of pain
  • Helplessness - "There's nothing I can do"

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

A newer approach focusing on acceptance and values-based living:4

  • Acceptance - Willingness to experience pain without excessive struggle
  • Defusion - Seeing thoughts as thoughts, not facts
  • Present moment - Focusing on now rather than past/future
  • Self-as-context - You are more than your pain
  • Values clarification - Identifying what matters most to you
  • Committed action - Taking steps toward valued goals despite pain

Mindfulness-Based Interventions

  • Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) - 8-week program with meditation and yoga
  • Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) - Combines mindfulness with CBT elements
  • Benefits - Reduces pain intensity, improves coping, decreases distress5
  • How it works - Changes how the brain processes pain; reduces reactivity

Pain Neuroscience Education

Teaching people how pain works:

  • Explains the biology of chronic pain
  • Helps understand pain doesn't always mean damage
  • Reduces fear and catastrophizing
  • Changes pain beliefs and behaviors
  • Often combined with other treatments

Other Approaches

  • Hypnosis - Can reduce pain perception; works for some people
  • Biofeedback - Learning to control physiological responses
  • Relaxation training - Progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery
  • Graded Motor Imagery - Uses visualization to reduce pain

Not "Just Talking"

Pain psychology is active and skill-based. You'll learn specific techniques to practice between sessions. These skills become tools you can use independently long after treatment ends. The goal is to become your own pain manager.

What to Expect from Treatment

Initial Assessment

  • Comprehensive pain history
  • Impact on daily life and function
  • Current coping strategies
  • Mood and sleep assessment
  • Goals for treatment

Typical Treatment Components

  • Education - Understanding pain and the treatment approach
  • Skills training - Learning and practicing techniques
  • Home practice - Applying skills in daily life
  • Problem-solving - Addressing barriers and challenges
  • Relapse prevention - Maintaining gains long-term

Treatment Formats

  • Individual therapy - One-on-one with a psychologist
  • Group programs - Learning with others who have chronic pain
  • Online/digital programs - Self-paced with or without therapist support
  • Interdisciplinary programs - Combined with physical therapy, medical care

Who Can Benefit?

Pain psychology can help people with:

  • Any chronic pain condition
  • Pain that hasn't responded fully to medical treatment
  • Difficulty coping with pain
  • Depression or anxiety related to pain
  • Avoidance of activities due to fear of pain
  • Desire for non-medication approaches
  • Interest in active self-management

Finding a Pain Psychologist

What to Look For

  • Specialization in pain - Not all psychologists have pain expertise
  • Licensed psychologist - PhD or PsyD with state licensure
  • Evidence-based approaches - CBT, ACT, mindfulness training
  • Experience with your condition

Where to Find Help

  • Pain management clinics often have psychologists
  • Hospital behavioral health departments
  • Ask your pain doctor for referrals
  • Professional directories (APA, ABPP)
  • Telehealth options expand access

Addressing Common Concerns

"Does this mean my pain isn't real?"

Absolutely not. Psychology helps because pain is processed in the brainâ€”all pain, including pain from clear physical causes. Using psychology doesn't mean your pain is imaginary.

"Will this cure my pain?"

The goal isn't necessarily eliminating pain. It's reducing suffering, improving function, and enhancing quality of life. Many people also experience reduced pain intensity.

"I've already tried therapy"

General therapy differs from specialized pain psychology. Pain-specific techniques may offer new tools. Different approaches (CBT vs. ACT) also work differently for different people.6

Realistic Expectations

Psychological treatments typically produce moderate improvements in pain and larger improvements in function and quality of life. They work best as part of comprehensive pain management, not as a sole treatment. Benefits require practice and application of skills over time.
