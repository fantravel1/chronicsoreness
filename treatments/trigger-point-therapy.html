Trigger Point Therapy

Understanding and treating myofascial pain through targeted trigger point interventions

Key Takeaways

  • Trigger points are hyperirritable spots in skeletal muscle that can cause local and referred pain
  • Multiple treatment approaches exist, from manual therapy to dry needling
  • Self-treatment techniques can provide effective relief for many people
  • Research supports trigger point therapy as part of comprehensive pain management

Understanding Trigger Points

Trigger points, often called "muscle knots," are hyperirritable spots located within taut bands of skeletal muscle or fascia.1 These sensitive areas can produce local pain when compressed and often refer pain to distant locations in characteristic patterns. Dr. Janet Travell pioneered the clinical understanding of trigger points in the mid-20th century, mapping referral patterns that remain clinically relevant today.2

Types of Trigger Points

Active trigger points cause spontaneous pain and tenderness, restrict range of motion, and may produce weakness in the affected muscle. They can refer pain in specific patterns even without direct compression.1

Latent trigger points don't cause spontaneous pain but are tender when palpated. They can restrict movement and cause muscle weakness, and may become active under certain conditions such as stress, overuse, or injury.

Common Locations in Women

  • Upper trapezius: Causes neck pain and headaches
  • Levator scapulae: Contributes to stiff neck and shoulder blade pain
  • Suboccipital muscles: Associated with tension headaches
  • Piriformis: Can cause buttock and sciatic-type pain
  • Quadratus lumborum: Common source of low back pain
  • Gluteal muscles: Contribute to hip and lower back discomfort

Professional Treatment Approaches

Manual Trigger Point Release

Manual therapy involves applying sustained pressure to trigger points to release muscle tension and reduce pain. Techniques include:4

  • Ischemic compression: Sustained pressure applied until tissue releases
  • Myofascial release: Gentle sustained pressure on fascial restrictions
  • Muscle energy techniques: Active patient participation in stretching
  • Positional release: Placing muscle in position of comfort

A systematic review found that manual trigger point therapy provides significant pain reduction compared to placebo, with effects lasting several weeks post-treatment.4

Dry Needling

Dry needling involves inserting thin filiform needles directly into trigger points without injecting any substance. This technique aims to elicit a local twitch response, which is associated with pain reduction and improved muscle function.5

Dry Needling vs. Acupuncture

While both use thin needles, dry needling targets specific anatomical trigger points based on Western medical understanding, while acupuncture follows traditional Chinese medicine meridian points. Some points may overlap, but the theoretical frameworks and treatment goals differ.6

Research evidence for dry needling shows:5,7

  • Significant reduction in pain intensity for neck and shoulder trigger points
  • Improved range of motion in affected muscles
  • Decreased muscle stiffness and tension
  • Effects may be enhanced when combined with stretching exercises

Trigger Point Injections

Injections into trigger points typically use local anesthetics (lidocaine), saline, or corticosteroids. Some practitioners use "wet needling" with various solutions.8

A Cochrane review examining injection therapies found that while injections can provide short-term relief, the evidence doesn't strongly support one injection solution over another, suggesting the mechanical effect of needling may be more important than the substance injected.8

Self-Treatment Techniques

Many people can effectively manage trigger points at home using various self-release tools and techniques. Research supports self-applied trigger point therapy as a complementary approach to professional treatment.9

Foam Rolling

Foam rollers apply broad pressure to muscle tissue and can help release superficial trigger points:

  • Roll slowly over tender areas, spending 30-60 seconds on each spot
  • Use body weight to control pressure intensity
  • Breathe deeply and try to relax into the pressure
  • Avoid rolling directly over bones or joints

Trigger Point Balls and Tools

Smaller tools provide more targeted pressure for deeper or more specific trigger points:

  • Tennis balls: Gentle, good for beginners
  • Lacrosse balls: Firmer pressure, more targeted
  • Specialized trigger point tools: Designed for specific body areas
  • Theracanes and similar devices: Allow self-treatment of hard-to-reach areas

Self-Treatment Precautions

  • Start with lighter pressure and gradually increase
  • Avoid areas with bruising, infection, or skin conditions
  • Stop if you experience sharp, shooting, or electrical pain
  • Don't treat the same area for more than a few minutes
  • Consult a healthcare provider if pain worsens or doesn't improve

Self-Release Technique

  1. Locate: Find the tender spot by palpating the muscle
  2. Press: Apply steady pressure using appropriate tool or fingers
  3. Hold: Maintain pressure for 30-90 seconds while breathing deeply
  4. Release: Slowly release pressure when tenderness decreases
  5. Stretch: Gently stretch the treated muscle afterward

Stretching for Trigger Points

Stretching is essential for maintaining improvements gained from trigger point therapy. Research indicates that combining trigger point release with stretching produces better outcomes than either approach alone.10

Key Stretches by Area

Upper Trapezius (Neck/Shoulder): Tilt head toward opposite shoulder while gently pulling down on the same-side arm. Hold 30 seconds.

Levator Scapulae (Neck): Look toward armpit of the side being stretched while gently pulling head down. Hold 30 seconds.

Piriformis (Hip/Buttock): Lying on back, cross ankle over opposite knee and pull underlying thigh toward chest. Hold 30-60 seconds.

Quadratus Lumborum (Low Back): Side-lying stretch or standing side bend with arm overhead. Hold 30 seconds each side.

Contributing Factors

Understanding what activates and perpetuates trigger points helps prevent recurrence:1,2

Mechanical Factors

  • Poor posture (especially forward head posture)
  • Repetitive motions or sustained positions
  • Muscle overload or direct trauma
  • Structural factors (leg length discrepancy, scoliosis)
  • Joint dysfunction

Systemic Factors

  • Nutritional deficiencies (vitamin D, B12, iron)
  • Hormonal imbalances (thyroid, estrogen)
  • Sleep disorders
  • Chronic stress and anxiety
  • Metabolic conditions

Ergonomic Considerations

Addressing workplace and home ergonomics is crucial for preventing trigger point formation:

  • Ensure computer monitor is at eye level
  • Keep keyboard and mouse at proper height
  • Use supportive seating with lumbar support
  • Take regular breaks from sustained positions
  • Vary activities throughout the day

When to Seek Professional Help

Consider consulting a healthcare provider trained in trigger point therapy if:

  • Self-treatment doesn't provide relief after 2-3 weeks
  • Pain is severe or significantly affecting daily activities
  • You experience numbness, tingling, or weakness
  • Trigger points keep recurring despite addressing contributing factors
  • You're unsure whether your pain is from trigger points or another condition

Finding a Practitioner

Practitioners who commonly treat trigger points include:

  • Physical therapists
  • Massage therapists (especially neuromuscular therapists)
  • Chiropractors
  • Physiatrists (PM&R physicians)
  • Some osteopathic physicians
  • Pain medicine specialists

Integration with Other Treatments

Trigger point therapy often works best as part of a comprehensive approach:

  • Exercise: Strengthening weak muscles and maintaining flexibility
  • Stress management: Reducing tension that contributes to trigger points
  • Sleep optimization: Allowing proper muscle recovery
  • Nutritional support: Addressing any deficiencies
  • Posture correction: Preventing mechanical contributors
