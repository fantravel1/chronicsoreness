Heat Therapy

Using warmth to ease pain and stiffness

How Heat Helps Pain

Heat therapy (thermotherapy) is one of the oldest and most accessible treatments for chronic pain. Heat increases blood flow to the area, relaxes muscles, reduces stiffness, and provides comfort. For many chronic pain conditions, it's a simple, safe, and effective part of self-management.1

How It Works2

  • Increases blood flow and circulation
  • Relaxes tense muscles
  • Reduces joint stiffness
  • Increases tissue extensibility
  • May reduce pain signals
  • Promotes relaxation

When to Use Heat

  • Chronic muscle pain and stiffness
  • Arthritis stiffness
  • Muscle spasms
  • Before stretching or exercise
  • Chronic back and neck pain
  • Fibromyalgia

When NOT to Use Heat

  • Acute injuries (first 48-72 hours)
  • Swelling or inflammation
  • Open wounds or infections
  • Areas with poor circulation
  • Over areas with decreased sensation
  • During a flare of inflammatory arthritis

Types of Heat Application3

Dry Heat

  • Electric heating pads
  • Microwaveable heat packs
  • Heat wraps
  • Infrared heat lamps

Moist Heat

  • Hot baths or showers
  • Moist heating pads
  • Hot towels
  • May penetrate deeper than dry heat

How to Apply

  • Use moderate heat (not hot)
  • Apply for 15-30 minutes
  • Use barrier (towel) between heat source and skin
  • Check skin periodically
  • Don't fall asleep on heating pad

Safety Tips

  • Never use high settings
  • Don't apply to broken skin
  • Check for burns
  • Don't use if you can't feel temperature
  • Keep heating pads in good condition
  • Follow product instructions

Heat vs. Cold

  • Heat: chronic pain, stiffness, muscle tension
  • Cold: acute injuries, swelling, inflammation
  • Some people alternate both
  • Personal preference matters

  • Elderly: skin may be more sensitive
  • Circulatory problems: consult doctor
  • Don't use over topical medications
