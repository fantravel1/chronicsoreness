Nerve Block Injections

Interventional procedures for pain relief

Key Takeaways

  • Nerve blocks can provide temporary pain relief and help with diagnosis
  • Usually part of a comprehensive treatment plan, not standalone treatment
  • Many types exist for different pain conditions
  • Relief duration varies—some get weeks to months of benefit

What Are Nerve Blocks?

A nerve block is an injection of medication (usually local anesthetic with or without corticosteroid) near specific nerves to interrupt pain signals. They can be used for both diagnosis and treatment of chronic pain conditions.1

Purposes

  • Diagnostic: Determine if a specific structure is causing pain
  • Therapeutic: Provide pain relief
  • Prognostic: Predict response to longer-term procedures

Medications Used

  • Local anesthetics: Lidocaine, bupivacaine—numb the area
  • Corticosteroids: Reduce inflammation
  • Sometimes combined

Types of Nerve Blocks

Epidural Steroid Injections

  • Medication injected into epidural space around spinal nerves
  • Used for herniated disc, spinal stenosis, radiculopathy
  • Can be done at cervical, thoracic, or lumbar levels
  • Types: interlaminar, transforaminal, caudal

Facet Joint Injections

  • Target the small joints between vertebrae
  • Used for facet-mediated back or neck pain
  • Can be diagnostic or therapeutic

Medial Branch Blocks

  • Target nerves supplying facet joints
  • Used to diagnose facet pain
  • If successful, may proceed to radiofrequency ablation

Sacroiliac Joint Injections

  • For SI joint pain
  • Diagnostic and therapeutic

Peripheral Nerve Blocks

  • Target specific peripheral nerves
  • Examples: occipital nerve block (headaches), intercostal nerve block

Trigger Point Injections

  • Into muscle trigger points (knots)
  • For myofascial pain
  • Local anesthetic, saline, or dry needling

Sympathetic Blocks

  • Target sympathetic nervous system
  • Used for CRPS, some vascular conditions
  • Examples: stellate ganglion block, lumbar sympathetic block

What to Expect

Before the Procedure

  • Review of medical history and imaging
  • Discussion of risks and benefits
  • Instructions about medications (may need to stop blood thinners)
  • Arrange for someone to drive you home

During the Procedure

  • Usually done in outpatient setting
  • Local numbing of skin first
  • Fluoroscopy (X-ray) or ultrasound guidance often used
  • Procedure typically takes 15-30 minutes
  • May feel pressure or brief discomfort

After the Procedure

  • Monitoring period (15-30 minutes)
  • May have temporary numbness or weakness
  • Don't drive day of procedure
  • Ice can help with soreness at injection site
  • Resume normal activities next day usually

Response Timeline

  • Local anesthetic: immediate relief (hours)
  • Steroid effects: may take 2-7 days to develop
  • Duration of relief: highly variable (days to months)

Evidence and Effectiveness

Evidence varies by type of injection:2,3

Epidural Steroid Injections

  • May provide short-term relief for radicular pain
  • Mixed evidence for long-term benefit
  • May allow participation in physical therapy
  • Less effective for chronic, non-radicular back pain

Facet Interventions

  • Medial branch blocks useful for diagnosis
  • Radiofrequency ablation can provide longer relief

Trigger Point Injections

  • Evidence is limited but some patients benefit
  • May work similarly to dry needling

Part of a Comprehensive Plan

Injections work best as part of a multimodal treatment plan that includes physical therapy, exercise, and other treatments. They should not be the only treatment for chronic pain.4

Risks and Side Effects

Common Side Effects

  • Soreness at injection site
  • Temporary increase in pain
  • Flushing (from steroids)
  • Temporary numbness or weakness

Less Common Risks

  • Infection (rare)
  • Bleeding
  • Nerve damage (rare)
  • Headache (with certain procedures)

Steroid-Related Concerns

  • Blood sugar elevation (diabetics especially)
  • Limits on number of injections per year
  • Potential effects on bone density with repeated use

Seek Care If

Contact your provider or seek care for: fever, severe headache, significant weakness, signs of infection at injection site, or loss of bladder/bowel control.

When Nerve Blocks May Help

  • Radicular pain (shooting pain down arm or leg)
  • Pain not responding to conservative treatment
  • To diagnose the source of pain
  • To enable participation in physical therapy
  • Managing flares of chronic conditions

When They May Not Help

  • Non-specific chronic pain without clear source
  • As sole treatment (need comprehensive approach)
  • When imaging doesn't correlate with symptoms

Questions to Ask

  • What type of injection do you recommend and why?
  • What are the risks specific to this procedure?
  • What results can I realistically expect?
  • How many injections might I need?
  • What should I do if it doesn't help?
  • Will this be combined with other treatments?
