Graded Motor Imagery

Brain-based rehabilitation for chronic pain conditions

Key Takeaways

  • GMI is a three-stage brain training program for chronic pain
  • Most evidence supports its use for CRPS and phantom limb pain
  • Works by gradually retraining how the brain processes movement and pain
  • Can be done at home with apps and online resources

What Is Graded Motor Imagery?

Graded Motor Imagery (GMI) is a rehabilitation approach that uses the brain's ability to change (neuroplasticity) to reduce pain and improve function. It was developed by researchers studying how the brain processes pain and movement.1

Evidence-Based
approach particularly for CRPS and phantom limb pain2

The Brain-Pain Connection

In chronic pain conditions, the brain's representation of the painful body part often becomes disrupted:

  • The body map in the brain becomes less precise
  • Movement planning areas become overactive or underactive
  • Even thinking about movement can trigger pain
  • Normal sensory input may be interpreted as threatening

GMI aims to gradually retrain these brain processes without triggering pain.

Who Developed GMI?

GMI was developed by researchers including Lorimer Moseley, David Butler, and colleagues based on extensive research into pain neuroscience and brain plasticity. It builds on discoveries about how the brain processes pain and movement.

Conditions GMI May Help

Research supports GMI for several conditions:2,3

Strong Evidence

  • Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS): One of the most studied applications
  • Phantom limb pain: Pain in an amputated limb

Emerging Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain
  • Chronic neck pain
  • Post-stroke shoulder pain
  • Chronic hand and arm pain
  • Other chronic pain conditions with central sensitization

Best Candidates

GMI may be particularly helpful if you:

  • Have difficulty identifying left vs. right body parts
  • Experience pain with movement or even thinking about movement
  • Have an altered sense of the painful body part
  • Have not responded well to traditional physical therapy

The Three Stages of GMI

GMI follows a specific sequence, progressing only when each stage can be done without increasing pain:4

Stage 1: Laterality Recognition (Left/Right Discrimination)

Training the brain to quickly and accurately identify left versus right body parts.

  • View images of body parts (hands, feet, backs, etc.)
  • Identify as quickly as possible if image shows left or right
  • Practice until accuracy and speed improve
  • Does not involve moving your own body

Why it works: People with chronic pain often have slower, less accurate laterality recognition on the painful side. Improving this activates motor planning areas without actual movement.

Stage 2: Explicit Motor Imagery (Imagined Movements)

Vividly imagining moving the painful body part without actually moving.

  • Imagine specific movements of the affected area
  • Make imagery as vivid as possible—feel the movement
  • Start with simple movements, progress to complex
  • Practice imagining from first-person perspective

Why it works: Mental rehearsal activates many of the same brain areas as actual movement but with less risk of triggering pain.

Stage 3: Mirror Therapy

Using a mirror to create the visual illusion of the painful limb moving.

  • Place mirror between limbs (or use virtual reality)
  • Move the non-painful limb while watching its reflection
  • Brain interprets reflection as the painful limb moving
  • Gradually progress to more complex movements

Why it works: Provides visual feedback of pain-free movement, helping to update the brain's model of the body and reduce perceived threat.5

How to Practice GMI

General Guidelines

  • Practice each stage until proficient before moving to next
  • Stop or step back if pain increases significantly
  • Practice frequently—multiple short sessions are better than one long session
  • Track your progress and pain levels
  • Be patient—brain changes take time

Laterality Training Tips

  • Use apps or online programs with timed image presentation
  • Aim for accuracy first, then speed
  • Target accuracy of >80% with response time under 2 seconds
  • Practice 3-4 times daily for 5-10 minutes
  • Progress when accuracy and speed plateau

Motor Imagery Tips

  • Find a quiet, comfortable environment
  • Close your eyes and relax
  • Imagine the movement from your own perspective (not watching yourself)
  • Include all senses: feel joint movements, muscle contractions
  • Start with 10-15 repetitions of imagined movements
  • Practice several times daily

Mirror Therapy Tips

  • Position mirror so it blocks view of affected limb
  • Focus on the reflection, not the moving limb
  • Start with simple movements (opening/closing hand, pointing)
  • Move slowly and smoothly
  • Sessions of 10-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily
  • Virtual reality programs can substitute for traditional mirrors

Progression Criteria

Move to the next stage only when:

  • Current stage does not increase pain
  • You've achieved proficiency (for laterality: fast and accurate)
  • You can perform the task comfortably

Resources for GMI

Apps and Online Programs

  • Recognise app: Official GMI laterality training app
  • NOI Group resources: From GMI developers
  • Various free online left/right discrimination tests

Equipment

  • Mirror box (commercial or DIY)
  • Smartphone/tablet for laterality apps
  • Virtual reality headset (optional advanced option)

Professional Guidance

Consider working with professionals trained in GMI:

  • Physical therapists with pain science training
  • Occupational therapists
  • Pain specialists familiar with GMI

Research Evidence

The evidence base for GMI continues to grow:6,7

CRPS

  • Multiple randomized controlled trials show benefits
  • Improvements in pain, function, and quality of life
  • Effects may persist after treatment ends

Phantom Limb Pain

  • Mirror therapy shows strong evidence5
  • Can significantly reduce phantom pain intensity
  • Widely used in rehabilitation settings

Other Conditions

  • Emerging positive evidence for chronic back and neck pain
  • More research needed for definitive conclusions
  • Generally safe with minimal adverse effects

Limitations

  • Not all studies show positive results
  • Individual responses vary
  • Requires consistent practice and patience
  • Best used as part of comprehensive treatment

Combining GMI with Other Treatments

GMI works well alongside other approaches:

  • Pain neuroscience education: Understanding pain enhances GMI effects
  • Physical therapy: GMI can prepare for and complement movement therapy
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy: Addresses thoughts and fears about pain
  • Graded exposure: Gradually returning to feared activities
  • Medications: May help reduce pain enough to engage with GMI

When to Start Physical Movement

After successfully completing mirror therapy without pain increase, you can begin gentle actual movements of the affected body part. This represents a natural progression from brain training to physical rehabilitation.

Potential Challenges

Pain During Practice

  • Some temporary discomfort may occur but should settle quickly
  • Significant pain increase suggests need to step back
  • May need to reduce intensity or duration
  • Consider returning to an earlier stage

Difficulty with Motor Imagery

  • Not everyone finds visualization easy
  • Practice improves imagery ability
  • Try different types of movements
  • Some benefit from guided imagery recordings

Lack of Progress

  • Brain changes take weeks to months
  • Ensure adequate practice frequency
  • Consider professional guidance
  • May need additional treatments alongside GMI
