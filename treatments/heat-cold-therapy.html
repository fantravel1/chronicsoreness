Heat and Cold Therapy

Understanding when and how to use temperature-based treatments for pain relief

Key Takeaways

  • Cold therapy is generally best for acute injuries and inflammation
  • Heat therapy is typically better for chronic muscle pain and stiffness
  • Both are safe, inexpensive, and can be used alongside other treatments
  • Personal preference matters—use whichever provides more relief

Understanding Temperature Therapy

Heat and cold are among the oldest and most accessible pain relief methods available. These simple, low-cost treatments can be used at home to complement other pain management strategies.1 While scientific evidence for their effectiveness is sometimes mixed, clinical experience and patient reports consistently support their value.

Low Risk
Heat and cold therapy are safe for most people when used properly2

How They Work

Cold therapy (cryotherapy) works by:3

  • Constricting blood vessels (vasoconstriction)
  • Reducing blood flow to the area
  • Decreasing metabolic activity and inflammation
  • Numbing nerve endings to reduce pain signals
  • Reducing muscle spasm

Heat therapy (thermotherapy) works by:4

  • Dilating blood vessels (vasodilation)
  • Increasing blood flow and oxygen delivery
  • Promoting tissue healing and flexibility
  • Relaxing muscles and reducing stiffness
  • Decreasing joint stiffness
  • Providing comfort and reducing pain perception

When to Use Cold Therapy

Cold is generally most effective for:3,5

Acute Injuries (First 48-72 Hours)

  • Sprains and strains
  • Bruises and contusions
  • Post-surgical swelling
  • Sports injuries
  • Falls or accidents

Inflammatory Conditions

  • Arthritis flare-ups (especially inflammatory types)
  • Tendinitis
  • Bursitis
  • Gout attacks

Other Applications

  • After exercise to reduce muscle soreness
  • Headaches and migraines
  • Dental pain
  • Insect bites and stings

Cold Therapy Methods

  • Ice packs: Commercial gel packs or homemade (ice in plastic bag)
  • Frozen vegetables: Peas or corn conform well to body contours
  • Ice massage: Frozen water in paper cup, rubbed on skin
  • Cold baths: For larger areas or whole-body treatment
  • Cooling sprays: Provide quick, superficial cooling

Application Guidelines

  • Apply for 15-20 minutes at a time
  • Always use a cloth barrier between ice and skin
  • Wait at least 1-2 hours before reapplying
  • Check skin regularly for signs of damage
  • Remove if skin becomes numb or white

Cold Therapy Precautions

Avoid cold therapy if you have:

  • Raynaud's disease or other circulatory problems
  • Cold allergy (cold urticaria)
  • Sensory nerve impairment
  • Open wounds in the treatment area

When to Use Heat Therapy

Heat is generally most effective for:4,6

Chronic Pain and Stiffness

  • Chronic low back pain
  • Chronic neck pain
  • Osteoarthritis joint stiffness
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Muscle spasms

Before Activity

  • Warming up stiff joints before exercise
  • Preparing muscles for stretching
  • Reducing morning stiffness

Other Applications

  • Menstrual cramps
  • Stress-related muscle tension
  • Sore, overworked muscles (after acute phase)
  • Trigger point release

Heat Therapy Methods

Dry heat:

  • Electric heating pads
  • Microwaveable heat packs
  • Infrared heat lamps
  • Chemical heat wraps (ThermaCare, etc.)

Moist heat:

  • Warm, damp towels
  • Moist heating pads
  • Hot baths or showers
  • Warm paraffin wax (for hands)
  • Hydrocollator packs

Moist vs. Dry Heat

Some research suggests moist heat penetrates tissues faster and more deeply than dry heat, though both can be effective.7 Moist heat may feel more comfortable and cause fewer skin reactions. Try both to see which works better for you.

Application Guidelines

  • Apply for 15-30 minutes at a time
  • Use low to medium heat setting—comfortable, not hot
  • Never lie on a heating pad or fall asleep with it on
  • Check skin periodically for burns or irritation
  • Avoid heat immediately after exercise (inflammation period)

Heat Therapy Precautions

Avoid heat therapy if you have:

  • Acute injuries with swelling (first 48-72 hours)
  • Open wounds or skin infections
  • Diabetes with decreased sensation
  • Peripheral vascular disease
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
  • Dermatitis or skin conditions
  • Impaired sensation in the area

Also avoid during pregnancy over the abdomen.

Contrast Therapy

Contrast therapy alternates between heat and cold, potentially providing benefits of both:8

How It Works

  • Creates a "pumping" effect in blood vessels
  • May reduce swelling while promoting healing
  • Often used for chronic conditions with recurring flare-ups

Application Method

  1. Start with heat for 3-4 minutes
  2. Switch to cold for 1 minute
  3. Repeat cycle 3-4 times
  4. End with whichever temperature provides more relief

Commonly used for:

  • Subacute injuries (after acute phase)
  • Repetitive strain injuries
  • Chronic conditions with periodic flare-ups

Condition-Specific Recommendations

Arthritis

  • Morning stiffness: Heat (warm shower, heating pad)
  • After activity: Cold if swollen, heat if stiff
  • Inflammatory flare: Cold therapy
  • Chronic OA pain: Heat often preferred

Back Pain

  • Acute episode: Cold first 48-72 hours, then heat
  • Chronic pain: Heat usually preferred
  • Muscle spasm: Heat to relax muscles
  • Before stretching: Heat to improve flexibility

Headaches

  • Tension headaches: Heat to neck and shoulders
  • Migraines: Cold to head and neck often preferred
  • Try both: Individual response varies

Fibromyalgia

  • Many prefer heat for widespread pain and stiffness
  • Warm baths can be particularly helpful
  • Some areas may respond better to cold

Evidence Summary

What does the research say?1,2,9

Heat Therapy

  • Moderate evidence for short-term relief in acute low back pain
  • Limited but supportive evidence for neck pain and osteoarthritis
  • Consistently reported patient satisfaction and preference

Cold Therapy

  • Well-established for acute injury management (RICE protocol)
  • Limited high-quality evidence for chronic pain conditions
  • Effective for reducing post-exercise muscle soreness

Practical Takeaway

While research evidence is sometimes limited, heat and cold therapy are:

  • Safe when used properly
  • Inexpensive and accessible
  • Free of drug interactions
  • Supported by centuries of clinical experience
  • Often helpful as part of a comprehensive pain management plan

Practical Tips

DIY Cold Packs

  • Gel pack: Mix 3 parts water with 1 part rubbing alcohol in a freezer bag
  • Rice sock: Fill a sock with rice, freeze for cold pack or microwave for heat
  • Frozen sponge: Wet a sponge, freeze in a bag—stays cold longer than ice

Making Heat Therapy More Effective

  • Use before stretching or exercise for better results
  • Combine with gentle movement once tissues are warm
  • Stay hydrated—heat can cause fluid loss
  • Don't use high settings; moderate heat works better long-term

When Neither Helps

If heat and cold don't provide relief, consider:

  • The underlying cause may need different treatment
  • Trying the other temperature (heat vs. cold)
  • Consulting a healthcare provider for persistent pain
  • Exploring other treatment options
