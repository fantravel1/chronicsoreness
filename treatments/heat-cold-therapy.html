Heat and Cold Therapy

Simple, accessible tools for managing chronic pain at home

Thermotherapy Basics

Heat and cold are among the oldest and most accessible pain treatments. They're safe, inexpensive, and can be used at home as part of a comprehensive pain management plan. While they won't cure chronic pain, they can provide meaningful temporary relief.1

The key is knowing when to use eachâ€”and recognizing that personal preference matters. Despite traditional "rules," many people find that the "wrong" choice feels better for them.

Heat Therapy (Thermotherapy)

How Heat Works

  • Increases blood flow - Brings oxygen and nutrients to tissues
  • Relaxes muscles - Reduces tension and spasm
  • Improves tissue elasticity - Makes stretching easier
  • Provides comfort - Warmth is inherently soothing
  • Reduces stiffness - Particularly helpful for morning stiffness2

When Heat Often Helps

  • Muscle stiffness and tension
  • Chronic back and neck pain
  • Arthritis stiffness (especially morning stiffness)
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Menstrual cramps
  • Before stretching or exercise
  • Stress-related muscle tension

Heat Application Methods

  • Heating pads - Electric or microwavable
  • Hot water bottles - Traditional option
  • Warm baths or showers - Full-body warmth
  • Heat wraps - Continuous low-level heat
  • Hot packs - Gel or rice-filled
  • Paraffin wax - For hands and feet

Heat Therapy Guidelines

  • Duration - 15-20 minutes typically; up to 2 hours for low-level heat wraps
  • Temperature - Warm, not hot; should feel comfortable
  • Barrier - Use towel between heat source and skin
  • Check skin - Watch for redness or burns
  • Stay awake - Don't fall asleep with heating pad on

When to Avoid Heat

Don't use heat over areas with acute inflammation or swelling, open wounds, decreased sensation (diabetes, neuropathy), poor circulation, or active skin conditions. Avoid heat if you have deep vein thrombosis or during the acute phase of injury (first 48-72 hours).

Cold Therapy (Cryotherapy)

How Cold Works

  • Reduces inflammation - Constricts blood vessels, limits swelling
  • Numbs pain - Slows nerve conduction
  • Decreases muscle spasm - Reduces nerve activity
  • Limits tissue damage - After acute injury3

When Cold Often Helps

  • Acute injuries (first 48-72 hours)
  • Inflammatory conditions (RA flares, gout)
  • Post-exercise soreness
  • Headaches and migraines
  • Swollen, hot joints
  • After activity-related flares
  • Some nerve pain

Cold Application Methods

  • Ice packs - Crushed ice in bag or commercial packs
  • Gel packs - Reusable, store in freezer
  • Frozen vegetables - Convenient, moldable
  • Ice massage - Frozen water in paper cup
  • Cold water immersion - Especially for hands/feet
  • Cooling sprays - For localized areas

Cold Therapy Guidelines

  • Duration - 10-20 minutes maximum
  • Barrier - Always use towel between ice and skin
  • Watch for warning signs - Excessive redness, numbness beyond surface
  • Intervals - Allow skin to return to normal between applications
  • Never sleep with ice - Risk of frostbite

When to Avoid Cold

Don't use cold over areas with poor circulation, Raynaud's phenomenon, cold hypersensitivity, or decreased sensation. Avoid on open wounds and in people with cold urticaria (hives from cold). Be cautious with large areas in elderly individuals.

Heat vs. Cold: Which to Choose?

Traditional Guidelines

  • Acute injury - Cold for first 48-72 hours, then heat
  • Inflammation present - Cold usually preferred
  • Muscle tension/stiffness - Heat usually preferred
  • Chronic pain - Either may help; try both

Personal Preference Matters

Research shows that for chronic pain, personal preference often predicts response better than traditional rules:4

  • Some people always prefer heat; others always prefer cold
  • If one doesn't help, try the other
  • Some find alternating (contrast therapy) helpful
  • Listen to your body's response

Contrast Therapy

  • Alternating heat and cold
  • May create "pumping" effect for circulation
  • Typical protocol: 3-4 minutes heat, 1 minute cold, repeat
  • End with whichever feels better
  • Limited research but some find it helpful

Research Evidence

What studies show:5

  • Both heat and cold can provide short-term pain relief
  • Effects are typically modest and temporary
  • Continuous low-level heat wraps show good evidence for back pain
  • Cold is well-established for acute injuries
  • For chronic pain, evidence is limited but supports use
  • Low risk makes them reasonable to try

Practical Tips

Making It Part of Your Routine

  • Morning stiffness - Warm shower or heating pad
  • Before exercise - Heat to prepare muscles
  • After activity - Cold if sore; heat if stiff
  • Before bed - Whichever helps you relax
  • Flare-ups - Have supplies ready

Keep Supplies Available

  • Heating pad with automatic shut-off
  • Multiple gel packs (some in freezer)
  • Heat wraps for use at work or out
  • Portable options for travel

Safety Summary

  • Always use barrier between skin and heat/cold source
  • Limit cold to 20 minutes maximum
  • Check skin regularly for adverse reactions
  • Be extra careful with reduced sensation
  • Never sleep with heating pad or ice
  • Consult provider if unsure about safety for your condition
