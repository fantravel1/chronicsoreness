Acupuncture for Chronic Pain

An ancient practice with modern evidence for pain relief

Guideline Recommendations

The American College of Physicians recommends considering acupuncture for chronic low back pain.1 NICE guidelines include acupuncture as a treatment option for chronic pain.2 It's now widely used as part of multimodal pain management.

What is Acupuncture?

Acupuncture involves inserting thin, sterile needles into specific points on the body. It originated in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) over 2,500 years ago and is now practiced worldwide, including in many Western medical settings.

How It May Work

Several mechanisms have been proposed:

  • Endorphin release: Stimulates release of natural pain-relieving chemicals3
  • Nervous system modulation: Affects pain signal transmission to the brain
  • Anti-inflammatory effects: May reduce local inflammation4
  • Connective tissue stimulation: Needle manipulation affects fascia
  • Blood flow changes: Increases local circulation
  • Placebo component: The therapeutic context and expectation also contribute

Research Evidence

Good Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain5
  • Chronic neck pain6
  • Osteoarthritis (especially knee)7
  • Chronic headache/migraine8

Moderate Evidence

  • Fibromyalgia9
  • Myofascial pain
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Dysmenorrhea (menstrual pain)10

Key Research Findings

  • Large meta-analyses show acupuncture is more effective than sham (fake) acupuncture for chronic pain11
  • Effects are modest but clinically meaningful for many patients
  • Benefits may persist for months after treatment ends
  • Best results when combined with other treatments (multimodal approach)

The Placebo Question

Some research suggests acupuncture's benefit may partly come from placebo effects. However, placebo effects are real neurobiological phenomena that reduce pain—the distinction between "real" and placebo effects in pain treatment is increasingly questioned.12

What to Expect at a Session

First Visit

  1. Intake: Detailed health history, current symptoms, lifestyle questions
  2. Assessment: Pulse and tongue diagnosis (TCM practitioners), examination of painful areas
  3. Treatment plan: Discussion of approach and expected number of sessions
  4. Treatment: Usually 30-60 minutes

The Needle Experience

  • Needle size: Very thin—much thinner than injection needles (0.12-0.35mm diameter)
  • Sensation: Brief pinch on insertion; may feel dull ache, tingling, or heaviness (called "de qi")
  • Number of needles: Typically 5-20 per session
  • Duration in place: Usually 15-30 minutes
  • Relaxation: Most people feel deeply relaxed during treatment

After Treatment

  • May feel relaxed, energized, or sleepy
  • Temporary symptom relief or temporary worsening both possible
  • Benefits often cumulative over multiple sessions
  • Minor bruising at needle sites occasionally

How Many Sessions?

  • Initial course: 6-12 sessions is typical for chronic pain
  • Frequency: Usually weekly or twice weekly initially
  • Response time: Some notice improvement in 3-4 sessions; others need longer
  • Maintenance: Monthly sessions may maintain benefits for some
  • If no response: If no improvement after 6-8 sessions, may not be effective for you

Types of Acupuncture

  • Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM): Follows meridian theory, holistic approach
  • Medical acupuncture: Western approach, focuses on neuroanatomy, often used by MDs/PTs
  • Trigger point acupuncture: Focuses on releasing myofascial trigger points
  • Electroacupuncture: Electrical stimulation added to needles
  • Auricular acupuncture: Points on the ear only
  • Dry needling: Similar technique, different training/philosophy (often by PTs)

Finding a Qualified Practitioner

Credentials to Look For

  • Licensed Acupuncturist (L.Ac.): Completed master's or doctoral training (3-4 years)
  • NCCAOM Certified: National Certification Commission for Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine
  • MD/DO with acupuncture training: Medical doctors with additional certification
  • Physical therapist: With dry needling certification (varies by state)

Questions to Ask

  • What is your training and licensure?
  • Do you have experience treating my condition?
  • What can I expect from treatment?
  • How many sessions do you recommend?
  • What are the costs? Do you accept insurance?
  • Do you use disposable needles?

Safety

Acupuncture is generally very safe when performed by qualified practitioners:13

  • Serious adverse events: Very rare when proper technique and sterile needles used
  • Common minor effects: Bruising, minor bleeding, temporary soreness
  • Sterile needles: All practitioners should use single-use, disposable needles

Contraindications/Precautions

  • Bleeding disorders or blood thinners: Increased bruising risk
  • Pacemaker: Avoid electroacupuncture
  • Pregnancy: Some points should be avoided—inform your practitioner
  • Skin infections/wounds: At needle sites
  • Immune compromise: May need extra precautions

Cost and Insurance

  • Session cost: Typically $75-150 per session
  • Insurance: Increasingly covered, especially for chronic pain
  • Medicare: Covers acupuncture for chronic low back pain
  • HSA/FSA: Often eligible expense
  • Community acupuncture: Lower-cost group sessions available in some areas

Maximizing Benefit

  • Commit to a full course of treatment (not just 1-2 sessions)
  • Communicate openly with your practitioner about your response
  • Combine with other evidence-based treatments
  • Don't expect immediate results—benefits often build over time
  • Keep notes on how you feel before and after sessions
