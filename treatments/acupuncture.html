Acupuncture

Ancient practice, modern evidence for pain relief

Key Takeaways

  • Acupuncture involves inserting thin needles into specific body points
  • Research supports its use for several chronic pain conditions
  • Generally safe when performed by trained practitioners
  • May work through multiple mechanisms including endorphin release

What Is Acupuncture?

Acupuncture is a practice that originated in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) over 2,500 years ago. It involves inserting very thin needles into specific points on the body to stimulate healing and relieve pain.1

Traditional View

In TCM, acupuncture is believed to:

  • Balance the flow of energy (qi) through pathways (meridians)
  • Restore harmony between opposing forces (yin and yang)
  • Stimulate the body's natural healing

Modern Scientific View

Research suggests acupuncture may work through:

  • Stimulating release of endorphins (natural painkillers)
  • Modulating pain signal transmission
  • Affecting neurotransmitters and hormones
  • Promoting blood flow and tissue healing
  • Activating the parasympathetic nervous system

Conditions Acupuncture May Help

Research evidence varies by condition:2,3

Good Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain: Recommended in clinical guidelines
  • Knee osteoarthritis: May reduce pain and improve function
  • Chronic headaches and migraines: Comparable to preventive medications
  • Neck pain: Short-term benefit shown

Moderate Evidence

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Shoulder pain
  • Myofascial pain
  • Postoperative pain
  • Dental pain

Emerging/Limited Evidence

  • Sciatica
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • TMJ disorders
  • Chronic pelvic pain

Understanding the Evidence

Acupuncture research faces challenges: it's difficult to create true "placebo" acupuncture, and expectations may influence outcomes. However, meta-analyses show acupuncture provides benefits beyond placebo for several conditions.4

What to Expect

Before Treatment

  • Health history and assessment
  • Discussion of symptoms and treatment goals
  • TCM practitioners may also examine tongue and pulse
  • Explanation of the procedure

During Treatment

  • Thin, sterile needles inserted at specific points
  • 5-20+ needles typically used
  • Needles left in place for 15-30 minutes
  • You may feel nothing, tingling, warmth, or mild aching
  • Most people find it relaxing

Sensations

  • De qi: Dull ache or tingling at needle siteâ€”often considered therapeutic
  • Minimal discomfort for most people
  • Needles much thinner than injection needles

After Treatment

  • May feel relaxed or energized
  • Mild soreness at needle sites is possible
  • Effects may be immediate or cumulative

Treatment Course

Number of Sessions

  • Typically 6-12 sessions for chronic conditions
  • Weekly initially, then less frequent
  • Some improvement often seen within 4-6 sessions
  • Maintenance sessions may be helpful long-term

Evaluating Response

  • Give adequate trial (6-8 sessions) before concluding
  • Track pain and function between sessions
  • Discuss progress with practitioner
  • If no benefit after 8-10 sessions, may not be effective for you

Safety

Acupuncture is generally safe when performed by trained practitioners:5

Common Side Effects

  • Minor bleeding or bruising at needle sites
  • Temporary soreness
  • Lightheadedness (rare)

Rare Complications

  • Infection (very rare with sterile technique)
  • Pneumothorax (extremely rare, with chest needling)
  • Nerve injury (very rare)

Precautions

  • Inform practitioner of bleeding disorders or blood thinners
  • Tell practitioner if pregnant (some points avoided)
  • Mention pacemaker if electrical stimulation used
  • Ensure sterile, single-use needles are used

Finding an Acupuncturist

Credentials to Look For

  • Licensed Acupuncturist (L.Ac.): State licensure
  • NCCAOM certification: National certification
  • Medical acupuncture: Some MDs and DOs trained in acupuncture
  • Physical therapists: Some trained in dry needling (related technique)

Questions to Ask

  • What training and credentials do you have?
  • Do you have experience with my condition?
  • How many sessions might I need?
  • Do you use sterile, disposable needles?

Cost and Insurance

  • Sessions typically $65-120+
  • Some insurance covers acupuncture
  • Medicare covers for chronic low back pain
  • Ask about package pricing

Related Techniques

Electroacupuncture

  • Mild electrical current passed through needles
  • May enhance pain relief
  • Used for various pain conditions

Auricular Acupuncture

  • Needles or seeds placed on ear points
  • Used for pain, addiction, anxiety
  • May be used between treatments

Dry Needling

  • Western technique targeting trigger points
  • Uses similar needles, different theory
  • Often performed by physical therapists
