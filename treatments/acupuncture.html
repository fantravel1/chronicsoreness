Acupuncture

Ancient practice with modern applications

What Is Acupuncture?

Acupuncture involves inserting thin needles into specific points on the body. Originating in traditional Chinese medicine, it's now widely used for pain management. Modern research suggests it may work partly through effects on the nervous system.1

How It May Work2

  • Stimulates nerve fibers
  • Releases endorphins (natural painkillers)
  • Affects inflammatory pathways
  • Changes brain pain processing
  • May have local tissue effects

Conditions with Evidence

  • Good evidence: Chronic low back pain, neck pain, osteoarthritis, headaches
  • Some evidence: Fibromyalgia, postoperative pain
  • Often part of multimodal treatment

What to Expect3

  • Initial consultation and history
  • Thin, sterile needles inserted
  • 5-20+ needles per session
  • Needles left in 15-30 minutes
  • May feel dull ache, tingling
  • Should not be very painful
  • Often 6-12 sessions recommended

Types

  • Traditional - Based on meridian theory
  • Medical/dry needling - Based on anatomy
  • Electroacupuncture - Mild electrical stimulation
  • Auricular - Ear points

Safety

  • Generally very safe with trained practitioner
  • Minor bruising or bleeding possible
  • Serious complications rare
  • Use licensed/certified practitioners
