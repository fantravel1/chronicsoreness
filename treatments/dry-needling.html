Dry Needling

Trigger point therapy for myofascial pain

Key Takeaways

  • Dry needling uses thin needles to treat trigger points and muscle pain
  • Different from acupuncture—based on Western medical concepts
  • Growing evidence supports its use for various musculoskeletal conditions
  • Should be performed by trained healthcare professionals

What Is Dry Needling?

Dry needling is a treatment technique in which thin filiform needles are inserted into the skin and muscle to treat myofascial pain and dysfunction. The term "dry" refers to the fact that no medication is injected—the needle itself is the treatment.1

How It Works

Proposed mechanisms include:

  • Local twitch response: Needle insertion into a trigger point causes a brief muscle contraction that may help release the tight band
  • Blood flow changes: Increases local blood flow to the area
  • Neurophysiological effects: May reduce muscle tension and pain through nervous system mechanisms
  • Chemical environment: May reduce inflammatory and pain-related chemicals at the trigger point

Dry Needling vs. Acupuncture

While both use thin needles, they are different approaches:

  • Dry needling: Based on Western anatomy and neurophysiology; targets trigger points and muscles
  • Acupuncture: Based on Traditional Chinese Medicine; targets meridians and energy flow

Some needle placements may overlap, but the underlying philosophy and treatment goals differ.

What Are Trigger Points?

Trigger points are hyperirritable spots within skeletal muscle that are associated with palpable nodules in taut bands of muscle fibers.2

Characteristics

  • Tender to pressure
  • Can cause local and referred pain patterns
  • May cause muscle weakness or restricted range of motion
  • Often associated with muscle tightness

Types of Trigger Points

  • Active trigger points: Currently causing pain, either at rest or with movement
  • Latent trigger points: Only painful when pressed; may still affect function

Common Trigger Point Locations

  • Upper trapezius (neck/shoulder pain)
  • Levator scapulae (neck pain)
  • Infraspinatus (shoulder pain)
  • Quadratus lumborum (low back pain)
  • Piriformis (hip/buttock pain)
  • Suboccipital muscles (headaches)

Conditions Treated

Dry needling is commonly used for:3,4

Musculoskeletal Conditions

  • Myofascial pain syndrome
  • Neck pain and cervicogenic headaches
  • Low back pain
  • Shoulder pain (rotator cuff issues, frozen shoulder)
  • Tennis elbow and golfer's elbow
  • Hip and gluteal pain
  • Knee pain
  • Plantar fasciitis

Other Conditions

  • Tension-type headaches
  • TMJ dysfunction
  • Post-surgical pain and stiffness
  • Chronic muscle tightness
Growing Evidence
supports dry needling for neck, low back, and shoulder pain3

What to Expect

Before Treatment

  • Practitioner assesses your pain and identifies trigger points
  • Review of medical history and contraindications
  • Explanation of the procedure and obtaining consent
  • Positioning for access to treatment area

During Treatment

  • Thin needle inserted through skin into trigger point
  • May feel a brief cramping or twitch sensation
  • Needle may be manipulated to elicit local twitch response
  • Multiple trigger points may be treated in one session
  • Treatment typically takes 15-30 minutes

Sensations

  • Local twitch response: Brief muscle contraction—often a good sign
  • Reproduction of pain: May briefly reproduce your familiar pain pattern
  • Aching: Mild aching during or after treatment is common
  • Relief: Some people experience immediate pain relief

After Treatment

  • Soreness similar to post-exercise muscle soreness is common
  • Soreness typically resolves within 24-48 hours
  • Light stretching and heat may help
  • Stay hydrated
  • Follow practitioner's instructions for activity

Number of Sessions

The number of sessions needed varies. Some people experience significant improvement in 1-3 sessions, while others may need more. Chronic conditions may require periodic maintenance. If no improvement after 4-6 sessions, re-evaluate the treatment approach.

Techniques

Different dry needling approaches exist:5

Superficial Dry Needling

  • Needle inserted only into superficial tissues (skin, subcutaneous tissue)
  • Less invasive, often less painful
  • May work through neurophysiological mechanisms

Deep Dry Needling

  • Needle inserted directly into the trigger point within the muscle
  • Aims to elicit local twitch response
  • More commonly used approach

Intramuscular Electrical Stimulation

  • Combines dry needling with electrical stimulation
  • Needles connected to electrical stimulation device
  • May enhance treatment effects

Safety and Risks

Common Side Effects

  • Soreness at needle site (very common)
  • Bruising
  • Minor bleeding
  • Temporary increase in pain
  • Fatigue

Rare but Serious Risks

  • Pneumothorax: Lung puncture when needling near the chest—rare with proper training
  • Nerve injury: Rare with knowledge of anatomy
  • Infection: Very rare with sterile technique

Contraindications

Dry needling may not be appropriate if you have:6

  • Bleeding disorders or taking blood thinners (relative caution)
  • Local infection at treatment site
  • Compromised immune system
  • Pregnancy (certain areas avoided)
  • Needle phobia (may cause vasovagal response)
  • Lymphedema in treatment area

Seek Qualified Practitioners

Always ensure dry needling is performed by a licensed healthcare professional with proper training in the technique. Requirements vary by location—check credentials and training hours.

Who Performs Dry Needling?

Practitioners who may be trained in dry needling:

  • Physical therapists (in states where permitted)
  • Chiropractors
  • Athletic trainers
  • Medical doctors and osteopaths
  • Naturopathic doctors

Questions to Ask

  • What training have you completed in dry needling?
  • How many hours of dry needling training do you have?
  • How long have you been practicing dry needling?
  • Is dry needling within your scope of practice in this state/province?

Research Evidence

Current evidence on dry needling effectiveness:3,4,7

Neck Pain

  • Moderate evidence supports dry needling for neck pain
  • May reduce pain and improve function short-term
  • Often combined with other treatments

Low Back Pain

  • Growing evidence for effectiveness
  • May help when trigger points contribute to pain
  • Best as part of comprehensive treatment

Shoulder Pain

  • Promising evidence for rotator cuff-related pain
  • May reduce pain and improve range of motion

Headaches

  • Some evidence for tension-type headaches
  • Targets cervical and suboccipital trigger points

Limitations of Research

  • Many studies are small or of moderate quality
  • Difficult to design placebo-controlled studies
  • Techniques vary between practitioners
  • More high-quality research needed

Combining with Other Treatments

Dry needling is often most effective as part of a comprehensive approach:

  • Stretching: Stretch treated muscles after dry needling
  • Exercise: Strengthen weak muscles contributing to trigger points
  • Manual therapy: Massage, joint mobilization
  • Postural correction: Address factors creating trigger points
  • Ergonomic modifications: Reduce repetitive strain
  • Heat therapy: Can help after treatment

Why Combination Works Best

Dry needling may release a trigger point, but if the underlying cause (poor posture, weakness, overuse) is not addressed, trigger points often return. A comprehensive approach addresses both the trigger point and its cause.
