Dry Needling

Targeting trigger points with thin needles

What Is Dry Needling?

Dry needling is a treatment where thin filament needles are inserted into myofascial trigger points, muscles, or connective tissue to relieve pain and improve movement. It's called "dry" because nothing is injectedâ€”the needle itself creates the therapeutic effect. It's commonly performed by physical therapists, chiropractors, and some physicians.1

How It Works2

  • Needle insertion causes local twitch response
  • Releases muscle tension
  • Increases blood flow to area
  • May reduce chemical irritants
  • Stimulates healing response
  • May affect pain signaling

Dry Needling vs. Acupuncture

Similarities

  • Both use thin needles
  • Both can reduce pain
  • Needles may be similar

Differences

  • Different theoretical foundations
  • Acupuncture based on traditional Chinese medicine
  • Dry needling based on Western anatomy
  • Different training requirements
  • Different needle placement rationale

Conditions Treated3

  • Myofascial pain syndrome
  • Neck pain
  • Back pain
  • Shoulder pain
  • Tension headaches
  • Tennis elbow
  • Hip and gluteal pain
  • Knee pain
  • Plantar fasciitis

What to Expect

During Treatment

  • Needle insertion may cause brief discomfort
  • Twitch response feels like muscle cramp
  • Session typically 15-30 minutes
  • Multiple sites may be treated

After Treatment

  • Muscle soreness for 24-48 hours is common
  • Bruising may occur
  • Increased range of motion
  • May need multiple sessions

Evidence

  • Growing body of research
  • Some evidence for short-term pain relief
  • Often combined with other treatments
  • Research quality varies

Risks and Precautions

  • Soreness (common)
  • Bruising (common)
  • Bleeding (minor)
  • Infection (rare with proper technique)
  • Pneumothorax (rare, with thoracic needling)

Who Should Avoid

  • Needle phobia
  • Bleeding disorders
  • Compromised immune system
  • Pregnancy (some areas)
  • Over areas of infection
