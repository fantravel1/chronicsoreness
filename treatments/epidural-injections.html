Epidural Injections

Steroid injections for spine-related pain

What Are Epidural Steroid Injections?

Epidural steroid injections (ESIs) deliver corticosteroid medication into the epidural space around the spinal nerves. They're used to reduce inflammation and pain from conditions affecting the spine. ESIs are one of the most common procedures performed for back and leg pain.1

How They Work

  • Corticosteroid reduces inflammation around nerves
  • Local anesthetic provides immediate pain relief
  • Medication delivered close to pain source
  • May reduce swelling compressing nerves

Types of Epidural Injections2

By Approach

  • Interlaminar: Between vertebrae, into epidural space
  • Transforaminal: Through nerve opening, more targeted
  • Caudal: Through tailbone area, into lower epidural space

By Location

  • Cervical (neck)
  • Thoracic (mid-back)
  • Lumbar (lower back)

Conditions Treated

  • Herniated disc with radiculopathy
  • Spinal stenosis
  • Degenerative disc disease
  • Sciatica
  • Failed back surgery syndrome

The Procedure3

  • Usually done with fluoroscopy (X-ray guidance)
  • Local anesthesia at injection site
  • Contrast dye confirms proper placement
  • Steroid and anesthetic injected
  • Takes about 15-30 minutes
  • Outpatient procedure

What to Expect After

  • May have temporary leg weakness or numbness
  • Rest for remainder of day
  • Pain may increase initially (steroid flare)
  • Full effect may take 2-7 days
  • Relief varies: weeks to months

Effectiveness

  • Best for radicular pain (shooting leg/arm pain)
  • About 50-75% get some benefit
  • Works better for recent-onset pain
  • May allow participation in physical therapy
  • Not a permanent fix

Limitations and Risks

  • Limited to 3-4 per year due to steroid effects
  • Infection (rare)
  • Headache (if dura punctured)
  • Temporary blood sugar elevation
  • Nerve injury (rare)
  • Allergic reaction

When They May Not Help

  • Primarily axial back pain without leg pain
  • Long-standing chronic pain
  • Severe spinal stenosis
  • When surgery is clearly indicated
