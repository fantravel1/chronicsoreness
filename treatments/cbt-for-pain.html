Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Pain

Evidence-based psychological approaches for chronic pain management

Key Takeaways

  • CBT is one of the most effective non-medication treatments for chronic pain
  • It helps change thoughts and behaviors that worsen pain
  • Benefits include reduced pain intensity, improved function, and better quality of life
  • CBT does not suggest pain is imaginaryâ€”it works by changing how the brain processes pain

Understanding CBT for Pain

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for chronic pain is an evidence-based treatment that helps people change the thoughts, emotions, and behaviors that can worsen pain and disability.1 It is recommended by major guidelines as a first-line treatment for chronic pain.

Strong Evidence
supports CBT for chronic pain management (Cochrane Review)2

How CBT Works for Pain

CBT helps by addressing:

  • Thoughts: Identifying and changing unhelpful thinking patterns about pain
  • Emotions: Managing anxiety, depression, and frustration related to pain
  • Behaviors: Changing activity patterns and pain-related behaviors
  • Nervous system: Reducing central sensitization through these changes

CBT Does Not Mean Pain Is "In Your Head"

Recommending CBT does not mean your pain is imaginary or psychological. Pain is always real, and CBT works by changing how the brain and nervous system process pain signals. Research shows CBT can actually change brain structure and function in pain-related regions.3

What CBT Addresses

Pain Catastrophizing

Catastrophizing involves:4

  • Rumination: Constantly thinking about pain
  • Magnification: Exaggerating pain's threat
  • Helplessness: Feeling nothing can be done

CBT helps recognize and challenge these thought patterns.

Fear-Avoidance

Fear of pain leading to:

  • Avoiding activities that might cause pain
  • Physical deconditioning from inactivity
  • Increased disability over time
  • Worse pain in the long run

CBT uses graded exposure to safely re-engage with feared activities.

Unhelpful Thinking Patterns

  • "The pain will never get better"
  • "I cannot do anything when I am in pain"
  • "Something must be seriously wrong"
  • "I have to rest until the pain is completely gone"

Mood and Pain

  • Depression and anxiety worsen pain perception
  • Pain increases risk of mood disorders
  • CBT breaks this cycle by addressing both

Core CBT Techniques

Cognitive Restructuring

Learning to identify and change unhelpful thoughts:

  1. Identify: Notice automatic thoughts about pain
  2. Evaluate: Examine evidence for and against the thought
  3. Reframe: Develop more balanced, realistic thoughts
  4. Practice: Reinforce new thinking patterns

Example transformation:

  • Unhelpful: "This pain means something is badly damaged."
  • Balanced: "Pain does not always mean damage. My doctor has found no serious problem, and pain can be influenced by many factors including stress and sleep."

Behavioral Activation

  • Gradually increasing meaningful activities
  • Scheduling pleasant activities
  • Breaking the inactivity-pain cycle
  • Building confidence through successful experiences

Pacing

  • Breaking tasks into manageable chunks
  • Balancing activity with rest
  • Avoiding boom-bust cycles
  • Gradually increasing activity over time

Relaxation Training

  • Progressive muscle relaxation
  • Diaphragmatic breathing
  • Guided imagery
  • Mindfulness techniques

Graded Exposure

For fear-avoidance:

  1. Identify feared activities
  2. Rank them from least to most feared
  3. Gradually approach each activity
  4. Build confidence through experience

What to Expect in CBT

Structure

  • Typically 8-12 weekly sessions
  • Sessions usually 50-60 minutes
  • Individual or group format
  • Homework between sessions is essential

Session Content

  • Education about pain and the mind-body connection
  • Identifying personal thought patterns
  • Learning and practicing new skills
  • Setting goals and tracking progress
  • Developing a long-term maintenance plan

Active Participation

CBT requires active involvement:

  • Completing homework assignments
  • Practicing skills between sessions
  • Tracking thoughts, emotions, and behaviors
  • Applying techniques in daily life

Benefits of CBT for Pain

Research shows CBT can help with:2,5

Pain-Related Outcomes

  • Reduced pain intensity
  • Decreased pain-related disability
  • Improved physical functioning
  • Better ability to cope with pain flares

Psychological Outcomes

  • Reduced depression and anxiety
  • Decreased catastrophizing
  • Improved self-efficacy
  • Better quality of life

Long-Term Benefits

  • Skills last beyond treatment
  • Continued improvement over time
  • Reduced healthcare utilization
  • Better management of future pain episodes

Related Approaches

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

Focuses on:6

  • Accepting pain rather than fighting it
  • Clarifying personal values
  • Taking committed action toward meaningful goals
  • Mindfulness and present-moment awareness

Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)

  • Systematic mindfulness training
  • Body scan meditation
  • Gentle yoga
  • Changing relationship to pain sensations

Pain Neuroscience Education

  • Learning how pain works
  • Understanding central sensitization
  • Reducing fear through knowledge
  • Often combined with CBT

Finding CBT for Pain

Providers

  • Psychologists specializing in pain
  • Licensed clinical social workers
  • Counselors with pain management training
  • Pain clinics and rehabilitation programs

Questions to Ask

  • Do you have training in CBT for chronic pain specifically?
  • How many chronic pain patients have you treated?
  • What is your approach to pain management?
  • Will there be homework between sessions?

Other Options

  • Online CBT programs for pain
  • Self-help books and workbooks
  • Group programs
  • Pain management programs

Self-Help Strategies

While working with a therapist is ideal, you can start applying CBT principles:

Thought Monitoring

  1. When pain increases, notice what you are thinking
  2. Write down the automatic thought
  3. Ask: Is this thought helpful? Is it accurate?
  4. Consider alternative perspectives

Activity Planning

  1. Schedule one enjoyable activity daily
  2. Plan activities in advance
  3. Start small and build gradually
  4. Notice the relationship between activity and mood

Relaxation Practice

  • Practice deep breathing for 5-10 minutes daily
  • Try progressive muscle relaxation
  • Use relaxation apps or recordings
