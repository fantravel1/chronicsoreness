Spinal Cord Stimulation

Electrical therapy for chronic pain

What Is Spinal Cord Stimulation?

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is a therapy that uses electrical impulses to interrupt pain signals before they reach the brain. A small device similar to a pacemaker is implanted under the skin, connected to thin wires (leads) placed near the spinal cord. It's typically considered when other treatments haven't provided adequate relief.1

How It Works2

  • Electrical impulses modify pain signals
  • Traditional SCS creates tingling (paresthesia)
  • Newer systems work without tingling
  • Different waveforms and frequencies available
  • Patient controls stimulation level
  • Can be turned on/off as needed

Conditions Treated

  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  • Chronic radicular pain
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Angina (refractory)
  • Some types of nerve damage

The Trial Period3

  • Temporary leads placed first
  • External device worn for 5-7 days
  • Tests effectiveness before permanent implant
  • 50% or greater pain relief = successful trial
  • Helps predict long-term success

Permanent Implant

  • Surgical procedure (usually outpatient)
  • Generator placed under skin (abdomen or buttock)
  • Leads positioned in epidural space
  • Programming session after healing
  • Multiple programming options

Types of SCS

  • Traditional low-frequency
  • High-frequency (10 kHz)
  • Burst stimulation
  • Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) stimulation
  • Each has different characteristics

Benefits

  • Can significantly reduce pain
  • May decrease medication use
  • Improves function in many
  • Reversible (can be removed)
  • Adjustable to changing needs

Risks and Considerations

  • Infection
  • Lead migration
  • Hardware failure
  • Need for revisions
  • MRI compatibility varies by device
  • Battery replacement needed
  • Not effective for everyone

Living with SCS

  • Regular follow-up appointments
  • Avoid certain activities initially
  • Carry device ID card
  • Inform medical providers
  • Security screening considerations
