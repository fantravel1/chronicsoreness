Spinal Cord Stimulation

Neuromodulation therapy for chronic pain

Key Takeaways

  • SCS uses electrical pulses to interrupt pain signals to the brain
  • Trial period allows testing before permanent implant
  • Best candidates have neuropathic pain not responding to other treatments
  • Can significantly improve quality of life for appropriate patients

What Is Spinal Cord Stimulation?

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is a neuromodulation therapy that uses mild electrical pulses to interrupt pain signals before they reach the brain. A small device (like a pacemaker) is implanted to deliver these pulses to the spinal cord through thin wires (leads).1

50,000+
SCS devices implanted annually in the United States2

How It Works

  • Electrodes placed in epidural space near spinal cord
  • Generator (battery) implanted under skin, usually in abdomen or buttock
  • Electrical pulses interfere with pain signal transmission
  • Patient controls stimulation with external remote

Types of Stimulation

Traditional (Paresthesia-Based)

  • Creates tingling sensation over painful area
  • Replaces pain with pleasant or neutral tingling
  • Traditional form of SCS

High-Frequency Stimulation

  • 10,000 Hz (10kHz therapy)
  • No tingling sensation
  • "Paresthesia-free"
  • May have broader pain coverage

Burst Stimulation

  • Intermittent bursts of electrical pulses
  • May work on different pain pathways
  • Often paresthesia-free

Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG) Stimulation

  • Targets specific nerve roots
  • Good for localized pain (foot, knee)
  • More precise targeting

Who May Benefit

Common Indications3

  • Failed back surgery syndrome (persistent pain after spine surgery)
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  • Diabetic neuropathy
  • Other peripheral neuropathies
  • Chronic radicular pain
  • Some cases of angina or peripheral vascular disease

Ideal Candidates

  • Neuropathic (nerve-related) pain
  • Conservative treatments have failed
  • No untreated psychiatric conditions
  • Realistic expectations
  • Successful trial stimulation
  • Not dependent on high-dose opioids

Not Good Candidates

  • Mechanical spine problems needing surgery
  • Primarily nociceptive (tissue-based) pain
  • Active infection
  • Untreated depression or psychological issues
  • Unrealistic expectations

The Process

Evaluation

  • Comprehensive pain evaluation
  • Review of previous treatments
  • Psychological evaluation (standard requirement)
  • Discussion of expectations

Trial Period

Testing before permanent implant:

  • Temporary leads placed through needle (no incision)
  • External generator worn for 5-7 days
  • Tests whether stimulation helps your pain
  • Most require 50%+ pain reduction for permanent implant
  • Leads removed after trial

Permanent Implant

  • Outpatient or short-stay procedure
  • Leads placed and anchored
  • Generator implanted under skin
  • Takes 1-2 hours
  • Usually done under sedation or light anesthesia

Recovery

  • Some soreness at incision sites
  • Restrict bending, lifting, twisting for 6-8 weeks
  • Programming sessions to optimize settings
  • Full benefit may take weeks to achieve

Outcomes

Expected Results4

  • Goal is significant pain reduction (often 50%+), not elimination
  • Improved function and quality of life
  • May reduce medication use
  • Results vary by individual

Success Rates

  • 50-70% of carefully selected patients have good outcomes
  • Success depends on proper patient selection
  • Trial helps predict response

Long-Term Considerations

  • Batteries need replacement or recharging
  • May need reprogramming over time
  • Small percentage need revision surgery
  • System can be removed if not helpful

Risks and Considerations

Possible Complications

  • Infection (1-3%)
  • Lead migration
  • Hardware problems
  • Pain at implant site
  • Need for revision surgery
  • Rare: spinal cord injury, paralysis

Lifestyle Considerations

  • MRI compatibility varies by device
  • Security screenings at airports
  • Some activities may need modification
  • Carry device ID card

Important Consideration

SCS is a significant intervention typically considered after other treatments have failed. Take time to understand the process, have realistic expectations, and ensure you're a good candidate through proper evaluation.

Questions to Ask

  • Am I a good candidate for SCS?
  • What type of stimulation do you recommend?
  • What pain reduction can I realistically expect?
  • How many SCS procedures have you performed?
  • What is the trial process like?
  • Is the device MRI compatible?
  • What is the battery life and replacement process?
  • What support is available after implant?
