Spinal Cord Stimulation

Using electrical signals to manage pain

What Is Spinal Cord Stimulation?

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is a treatment that uses electrical impulses to interrupt pain signals before they reach the brain. A small device (similar to a pacemaker) is implanted to deliver mild electrical pulses to the spinal cord. It's used for chronic pain that hasn't responded to other treatments.1

How It Works2

  • Electrical leads placed near spinal cord
  • Impulse generator implanted (usually in abdomen or buttock)
  • Electrical signals modify pain perception
  • May replace pain with tingling (traditional)
  • Newer devices may be undetectable
  • Patient controls stimulation level

Conditions Treated3

  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Chronic back and leg pain
  • Arachnoiditis
  • Angina (in some cases)

Types of SCS

  • Traditional (paresthesia-based)
  • High-frequency stimulation
  • Burst stimulation
  • Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) stimulation
  • Closed-loop/feedback systems

The Trial Process

  • Temporary leads placed first
  • Trial period typically 5-7 days
  • Test effectiveness before permanent implant
  • 50% or greater pain relief usually required
  • Track activities and pain levels during trial

Permanent Implantation

  • Outpatient or short hospital stay
  • Done under sedation or general anesthesia
  • Recovery: 2-4 weeks for most activities
  • Full recovery: 6-8 weeks

Expectations

  • Goal: 50% or greater pain reduction
  • Not a cureâ€”pain management tool
  • May reduce medication needs
  • Improves function for many
  • Not effective for everyone

Risks

  • Infection
  • Lead migration
  • Hardware failure
  • Need for revision surgery
  • Inadequate pain relief
  • Bleeding, nerve damage (rare)

Living with SCS

  • Regular follow-up appointments
  • Programming adjustments as needed
  • Battery replacement (rechargeable or non-rechargeable)
  • MRI compatibility varies by device
  • Airport security considerations

Who Is a Candidate?

  • Chronic pain for 6+ months
  • Other treatments haven't worked
  • No untreated psychological issues
  • No active infection
  • Realistic expectations
