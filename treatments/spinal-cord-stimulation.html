Spinal Cord Stimulation

Electrical therapy to interrupt pain signals

What Is Spinal Cord Stimulation?

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is a type of neuromodulation that uses mild electrical impulses to interrupt pain signals traveling to the brain. Electrodes placed near the spinal cord deliver stimulation that masks or modifies pain perception.1

How It Works

  • Electrodes placed in epidural space
  • Connected to implanted pulse generator
  • Electrical signals modify pain transmission
  • Patient controls stimulation level

Conditions Treated2

  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • CRPS (complex regional pain syndrome)
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Chronic radiculopathy
  • Angina (refractory)
  • Peripheral vascular disease pain

The Process

Trial Period

  • Temporary leads placed
  • Trial lasts 5-7 days typically
  • Assess pain relief (≥50% reduction usually required)
  • Determines if permanent implant appropriate

Permanent Implant

  • Outpatient surgery
  • Leads and generator implanted
  • Recovery takes several weeks
  • Programming and adjustments follow

Types of Stimulation3

  • Traditional - Paresthesia-based (tingling sensation)
  • High-frequency - No paresthesia (10,000 Hz)
  • Burst - Patterned stimulation
  • Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) - Targets specific nerves

Considerations

  • Reversible and adjustable
  • Battery replacement needed
  • MRI compatibility varies
  • Not suitable for all patients
