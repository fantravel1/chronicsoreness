Spinal Cord Stimulation

An advanced option for refractory chronic pain

What Is Spinal Cord Stimulation?

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) uses electrical impulses delivered by an implanted device to interrupt pain signals traveling to the brain. It's considered for chronic pain that hasn't responded to other treatments.1

How It Works

  • Electrodes placed in epidural space near spinal cord
  • Connected to implanted pulse generator (battery)
  • Delivers mild electrical pulses
  • Modifies pain signals before reaching brain
  • Traditional SCS creates tingling (paresthesia); newer systems may not

Conditions Treated2

  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  • Chronic neuropathic pain
  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Angina (refractory)
  • Some cases of chronic back/leg pain

The Process

Trial Period

  • Temporary leads placed
  • Test stimulation for 5-7 days
  • Must achieve significant pain relief (usually >50%)
  • Successful trial leads to permanent implant

Permanent Implant

  • Outpatient surgery
  • Permanent leads and generator implanted
  • Recovery over several weeks
  • Programming and adjustment of settings

Types of SCS3

  • Traditional (tonic) - Creates tingling sensation
  • High-frequency - No paresthesia
  • Burst stimulation - Intermittent pulses
  • Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) - Targets specific nerve areas

Effectiveness

  • 50% or greater pain relief in 50-70% of selected patients
  • Improved function and quality of life
  • May reduce medication use
  • Reversible - can be removed

Considerations

  • Major procedure with surgical risks
  • Battery replacement needed (rechargeable options available)
  • MRI compatibility varies by device
  • Not a cure - manages pain
  • Psychological screening required
