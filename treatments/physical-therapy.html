Physical Therapy for Chronic Pain

A cornerstone of chronic pain management

What Is Physical Therapy?

Physical therapy (also called physiotherapy) is a healthcare profession focused on restoring movement and function, reducing pain, and preventing disability. For chronic pain, PT goes beyond just treating symptomsâ€”it addresses the underlying causes of pain and teaches you how to manage your condition long-term.1

Physical therapists are movement experts who evaluate how you move, identify problems, and develop individualized treatment plans combining exercise, manual therapy, education, and other techniques.

Why PT Is Essential for Chronic Pain

  • Addresses deconditioning - Chronic pain often leads to reduced activity
  • Breaks the pain cycle - Movement reduces sensitivity over time
  • Builds self-efficacy - Learn to manage pain independently
  • Reduces fear of movement - Graded exposure builds confidence
  • Provides long-term tools - Skills that last beyond treatment

What Physical Therapy Includes

Evaluation

  • Detailed history of your pain and function
  • Assessment of movement, strength, flexibility
  • Posture and body mechanics analysis
  • Identifying contributing factors
  • Setting goals together

Therapeutic Exercise2

  • Strengthening - Building muscle support
  • Stretching - Improving flexibility
  • Aerobic conditioning - Improving endurance
  • Balance training - Stability and coordination
  • Core stability - Foundational support
  • Functional training - Practice real-life movements

Manual Therapy

  • Joint mobilization - Gentle movement of joints
  • Soft tissue mobilization - Massage techniques
  • Trigger point release - Targeting muscle knots
  • Myofascial release - Fascial manipulation
  • Manipulation - Some PTs perform thrust techniques

Education

  • Pain neuroscience education
  • Body mechanics and posture
  • Activity pacing
  • Self-management strategies
  • Flare management

Other Modalities

  • Heat and cold therapy
  • Electrical stimulation (TENS)
  • Ultrasound
  • Dry needling (in some states)
  • Aquatic therapy

Active vs. Passive Treatment

Research supports active treatments (exercise, movement) over passive treatments (heat, ultrasound, massage alone) for chronic pain. The most effective PT programs emphasize what you do yourself. Passive treatments can feel good and have a role, but shouldn't be the main focus.3

Research Evidence

Conditions with Strong Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain - First-line treatment; strong evidence4
  • Neck pain - Exercise and manual therapy effective
  • Osteoarthritis - Exercise is essential
  • Fibromyalgia - Aerobic exercise and strengthening help
  • Chronic shoulder pain - Exercise programs effective

What Studies Show

  • Exercise is one of the most effective treatments for chronic pain
  • Benefits are comparable to medications for many conditions
  • Improvements in pain, function, and quality of life
  • Effects can be long-lasting with continued self-management
  • Most effective when combined with education

Types of Physical Therapy

Orthopedic PT

  • Focuses on musculoskeletal conditions
  • Joint and muscle problems
  • Post-surgical rehabilitation
  • Most common type for chronic pain

Neurological PT

  • Conditions affecting the nervous system
  • Stroke, MS, Parkinson's
  • Some chronic pain conditions with neurological components

Specialized Approaches

  • Pain science-based PT - Incorporates pain neuroscience education
  • MDT (McKenzie Method) - Classification-based treatment
  • Pelvic floor PT - For pelvic pain conditions
  • Aquatic PT - Exercises in water

What to Expect

First Visit

  • Takes 45-60 minutes typically
  • Detailed evaluation
  • Discussion of goals
  • May begin some treatment
  • Home exercise program started

Ongoing Sessions

  • Usually 30-60 minutes
  • Exercise progression
  • Manual therapy as appropriate
  • Education and coaching
  • Home program updates

Frequency and Duration

  • Typically 1-3 times per week initially
  • Duration varies by condition (often 6-12 weeks)
  • May taper to less frequent visits
  • Periodic check-ins for maintenance

Home Exercise Is Crucial

The exercises you do at home matter more than what happens in clinic. PT gives you 30-60 minutes per week; you have the rest of the week to practice. Doing home exercises consistently is the biggest predictor of success. If you're not given a home program, ask for one.

Finding the Right PT

What to Look For

  • Licensed physical therapist - DPT or MPT degree
  • Experience with your condition
  • Evidence-based approach - Uses treatments supported by research
  • Emphasizes active treatment - Not just passive modalities
  • Good communication - Listens and explains
  • Sets measurable goals

Specializations

  • OCS - Orthopedic Certified Specialist
  • FAAOMPT - Manual therapy fellowship
  • NCS - Neurologic Certified Specialist
  • SCS - Sports Certified Specialist

Questions to Ask

  • What experience do you have with my condition?
  • What does treatment typically involve?
  • How will we measure progress?
  • What should I do on my own?

Making PT Work for You

  • Be honest - About pain levels, activity, concerns
  • Ask questions - Understand the why
  • Do your home exercises - Consistency matters
  • Track your progress - Note what helps
  • Communicate challenges - Let them know if something isn't working
  • Be patient - Progress takes time with chronic pain
