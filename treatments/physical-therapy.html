Physical Therapy

First-line treatment for most chronic pain conditions

Why Physical Therapy?

PT is strongly recommended by the American College of Physicians, NICE, and WHO for chronic low back pain, osteoarthritis, fibromyalgia, and many other pain conditions.1,2,3 It's often more effective than medications for long-term outcomes.

What Physical Therapists Do

Physical therapists (PTs) are movement experts who:

  • Assess how you move and identify dysfunction
  • Design individualized exercise programs
  • Use hands-on (manual) therapy techniques
  • Educate you about your condition
  • Help you gradually return to activities
  • Teach self-management strategies

Types of Physical Therapy Approaches

Therapeutic Exercise

Strengthening, stretching, aerobic conditioning, balance training. The foundation of PT. Evidence: Strong.4

Manual Therapy

Hands-on techniques: joint mobilization, soft tissue massage, manipulation. Evidence: Moderate.5

Pain Neuroscience Education

Understanding how pain works reduces fear and improves outcomes. Evidence: Growing.6

Modalities

Heat, cold, ultrasound, electrical stimulation, TENS. Usually adjuncts, not stand-alone treatments. Evidence: Variable.

Specific PT Approaches

McKenzie Method (MDT)

  • Focuses on directional preference (movements that reduce symptoms)
  • Strong evidence for low back pain7
  • Emphasizes self-treatment with repeated movements

Motor Control Exercises

  • Targets deep stabilizing muscles (core, multifidus)
  • Evidence for chronic low back pain8
  • Focus on quality of movement, not just strength

Graded Exercise Therapy

  • Progressive increase in activity based on time, not symptoms
  • Evidence for various chronic pain conditions
  • Note: Controversial for ME/CFS; avoid if you have PEM

Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

  • Specialized for pelvic pain, endometriosis, bladder issues
  • Internal and external techniques
  • Evidence for chronic pelvic pain9

Aquatic Physical Therapy

  • Exercises in warm water pool
  • Excellent for arthritis, fibromyalgia, back pain
  • See aquatic therapy guide

What to Expect

First Visit (Evaluation)

  1. Health history: Your pain, medical background, goals
  2. Physical exam: Posture, range of motion, strength, movement patterns
  3. Special tests: Specific to your condition
  4. Assessment: PT's findings and hypothesis
  5. Plan: Proposed treatment approach and goals
  6. Initial treatment: Often starts same visit

Duration: 45-60 minutes typically

Follow-Up Visits

  • Usually 30-45 minutes
  • Review of home exercises
  • Hands-on treatment and/or supervised exercise
  • Progression of exercises as appropriate
  • Education and self-management strategies

Typical Course

  • Frequency: Usually 1-2x/week initially
  • Duration: 6-12 weeks is common; varies by condition
  • Home program: Essential—PT works best with daily home exercises
  • Re-evaluation: Progress assessed every few weeks

Finding a Physical Therapist

Types of Access

  • Direct access: Many states allow PT without physician referral
  • Physician referral: Some insurances require it
  • Specialty clinics: Some focus on specific conditions

What to Look For

  • Experience with your condition: Ask if they treat chronic pain regularly
  • Specializations: OCS (orthopedic), WCS (women's health), NCS (neurologic)
  • Approach: Active (exercise-based) vs. passive (mainly modalities)—active is better for chronic pain
  • One-on-one time: How much direct PT contact vs. technician/exercise independently?
  • Communication style: Do they listen? Explain? Involve you in decisions?

Questions to Ask

  • How much experience do you have with [my condition]?
  • What approach do you typically use?
  • How much one-on-one time will I have with the PT?
  • What's the expected number of visits?
  • What should I do at home between visits?

Making PT Effective

Your Role

  • Do your home exercises: This is where most improvement happens
  • Communicate: Tell your PT what's working and what's not
  • Be honest about pain: Don't minimize or exaggerate
  • Ask questions: Understand why you're doing each exercise
  • Be patient: Chronic pain takes time to improve

Red Flags in PT

  • Only passive treatments (no active exercise program)
  • Never see the PT (only technicians or aides)
  • No home exercise program given
  • Treatment causes significant pain increase lasting >24-48 hours
  • No progress after 6-8 sessions with good compliance

Condition-Specific Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain: Strong evidence; ACP strongly recommends1
  • Osteoarthritis: Strong evidence for hip/knee OA10
  • Fibromyalgia: Aerobic and strengthening exercise recommended11
  • Chronic neck pain: Exercise and manual therapy supported12
  • Chronic pelvic pain: Pelvic floor PT has growing evidence

Cost and Insurance

  • Insurance: Most plans cover PT with referral; check visit limits
  • Medicare: Covers PT for approved conditions
  • Out-of-pocket: $75-200 per session typical
  • HSA/FSA: Usually eligible expense
