Physical Therapy

Movement as medicine for chronic pain

Key Takeaways

  • Physical therapy is a first-line treatment for most chronic pain conditions
  • PT addresses root causes, not just symptoms
  • Active treatments (exercise) are more effective than passive treatments alone
  • Finding the right PT who understands chronic pain is important

What Is Physical Therapy?

Physical therapy (PT) is a healthcare profession focused on evaluating and treating conditions that limit the ability to move and function. For chronic pain, PT uses exercise, manual therapy, education, and other techniques to reduce pain and improve quality of life.1

First-Line Treatment
Clinical guidelines recommend PT as first-line treatment for most musculoskeletal pain2

Goals of PT for Chronic Pain

  • Reduce pain
  • Improve strength and flexibility
  • Restore function and mobility
  • Educate about pain and self-management
  • Prevent future problems
  • Reduce fear of movement

Types of Physical Therapy Treatments

Exercise Therapy

The cornerstone of effective PT:

  • Strengthening: Building muscle to support joints
  • Flexibility: Improving range of motion
  • Aerobic conditioning: Building endurance
  • Motor control: Improving movement patterns
  • Balance training: Preventing falls, improving stability

Manual Therapy

Hands-on techniques:

  • Joint mobilization: Gentle movement of joints
  • Soft tissue mobilization: Working on muscles and fascia
  • Manipulation: Quick thrust techniques
  • Myofascial release: Releasing fascial restrictions
  • Trigger point therapy: Addressing muscle knots

Education

  • Pain neuroscience education
  • Body mechanics and posture
  • Ergonomics
  • Activity pacing
  • Self-management strategies

Modalities

Adjunct treatments (supportive, not primary):

  • Heat and cold therapy
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)
  • Ultrasound
  • Dry needling

Active vs. Passive Treatment

Research shows active treatments (exercise, movement) are more effective long-term than passive treatments (massage, heat, ultrasound). The best PT programs emphasize what you do, not what's done to you. Your participation is key.3

Conditions PT Helps

Strong Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain
  • Neck pain
  • Osteoarthritis (knee, hip)
  • Shoulder pain
  • Post-surgical rehabilitation

Good Evidence

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic headaches
  • TMJ disorders
  • Chronic pelvic pain
  • Complex regional pain syndrome

Specialized PT Areas

  • Orthopedic PT: Musculoskeletal conditions
  • Pelvic floor PT: Pelvic pain, incontinence
  • Vestibular PT: Dizziness, balance
  • Neurological PT: Nerve-related conditions

What to Expect

Initial Evaluation

  • Detailed history of your condition
  • Physical examination
  • Movement and strength assessment
  • Goal setting
  • Treatment plan development
  • Usually 45-60 minutes

Treatment Sessions

  • Typically 30-60 minutes
  • Mix of hands-on treatment and exercise
  • Education and self-care instruction
  • Progress monitoring
  • Home exercise program updates

Frequency and Duration

  • Often 1-3 times per week initially
  • Frequency decreases as you improve
  • Duration varies by condition (6-12 weeks typical)
  • Some benefit from periodic maintenance

  • Usually 15-30 minutes daily
  • Exercises should be manageable
  • Report if exercises increase pain significantly

Finding the Right PT

What to Look For

  • Licensed physical therapist (PT, DPT)
  • Experience with your specific condition
  • Understanding of chronic pain
  • Emphasizes active treatment, not just passive
  • Good communication style

Specializations

  • OCS: Orthopedic Certified Specialist
  • FAAOMPT: Fellowship in manual therapy
  • WCS: Women's Health Certified Specialist
  • Certifications in specific techniques (MDT, dry needling)

Questions to Ask

  • What is your experience with [my condition]?
  • What will treatment involve?
  • What should I expect for outcomes?
  • How long might treatment take?
  • Will I have a home exercise program?

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Mostly passive treatments (machines, heat)
  • No individualized assessment
  • No home program
  • Dependent relationship (keeps you coming indefinitely)
  • Doesn't listen to your concerns

Getting the Most from PT

Be an Active Participant

  • Do your home exercises consistently
  • Communicate openly about pain and progress
  • Ask questions when unsure
  • Report what helps and what doesn't

Expect Some Discomfort

  • Some soreness after PT is normal
  • Should not cause severe or lasting pain
  • "No pain, no gain" is NOT the goal
  • Communicate if pain is too much

Be Patient

  • Chronic pain takes time to improve
  • Progress may be gradual
  • Setbacks are normal
  • Long-term results require consistency

Access and Cost

Getting PT

  • Many states allow direct access (no referral needed)
  • Some insurance requires physician referral
  • Medicare covers with physician order

Insurance Coverage

  • Most insurance covers PT (may have visit limits)
  • Check copay and deductible
  • Ask about number of visits covered

If Cost Is a Barrier

  • Ask about sliding scale fees
  • Community health centers
  • Teaching clinics at PT schools
  • Focus on learning home program
