Myofascial Release

Soft tissue techniques for fascia and muscle pain

Key Takeaways

  • Myofascial release targets fascia and soft tissue restrictions
  • Can be performed by therapists or self-administered with tools
  • May help with muscle tightness, pain, and restricted movement
  • Often combined with stretching and exercise for best results

What Is Myofascial Release?

Myofascial release (MFR) is a manual therapy technique that focuses on releasing tension and restrictions in the fascia—the connective tissue that surrounds and connects muscles, bones, nerves, and organs throughout the body.1

Understanding Fascia

  • Three-dimensional web of connective tissue throughout the body
  • Surrounds every muscle, bone, nerve, blood vessel, and organ
  • Can become tight, restricted, or adhered due to injury, inflammation, or disuse
  • Fascial restrictions may contribute to pain and limited mobility

How MFR Works

Proposed mechanisms include:

  • Mechanical effects: Breaking up adhesions and restoring tissue mobility
  • Neurological effects: Reducing muscle tone through nervous system input
  • Fluid dynamics: Improving blood and lymph flow
  • Pain modulation: Decreasing sensitization of local tissues

Note on Evidence

While many people find myofascial release helpful, the scientific evidence is still developing. Some proposed mechanisms (like "releasing fascial adhesions") are debated among researchers. The benefits may come from multiple factors including touch, relaxation, and neurological effects.

Types of Myofascial Release

Direct (Active) MFR

Practitioner applies sustained pressure directly into the restriction:

  • Deeper, more intense pressure
  • Works to stretch and release tight tissue
  • May cause some discomfort during treatment
  • Common in sports massage and physical therapy

Indirect (Passive) MFR

Gentler approach following the tissue's path of least resistance:

  • Light, sustained pressure
  • Holds until tissue releases (3-5 minutes or longer)
  • Associated with John Barnes approach
  • Generally comfortable during treatment

Self-Myofascial Release (SMR)

Using tools to perform MFR on yourself:

  • Foam rollers
  • Massage balls (tennis balls, lacrosse balls)
  • Massage sticks and rollers
  • Specialized tools (Theragun, Hypervolt)

Conditions That May Benefit

MFR is commonly used for:2,3

  • Myofascial pain syndrome
  • Fibromyalgia
  • Chronic low back pain
  • Neck pain and headaches
  • Shoulder pain and stiffness
  • Plantar fasciitis
  • IT band syndrome
  • Post-surgical adhesions
  • Chronic muscle tightness
  • Limited range of motion
Emerging Evidence
supports MFR for several musculoskeletal conditions2

Self-Myofascial Release Techniques

Foam Rolling Basics

  1. Position body part on the foam roller
  2. Use body weight to create pressure
  3. Roll slowly over the muscle (about 1 inch per second)
  4. Pause on tender spots for 30-90 seconds
  5. Breathe deeply and try to relax
  6. Avoid rolling directly over bones or joints

Common Areas for Foam Rolling

  • Quadriceps: Front of thighs
  • Hamstrings: Back of thighs
  • IT band: Outer thigh (controversial—be gentle)
  • Calves: Lower legs
  • Glutes: Buttocks
  • Upper back: Thoracic spine area
  • Lats: Side of back

Using Massage Balls

For more targeted pressure:

  • Against a wall: Upper back, shoulders, chest
  • On the floor: Glutes, hips
  • Under feet: Plantar fascia
  • Can access areas foam roller cannot reach

Tips for Effectiveness

  • Start with less pressure and increase gradually
  • Aim for discomfort, not severe pain
  • Keep muscles relaxed while rolling
  • Follow with stretching for best results
  • Stay hydrated
  • Be consistent—regular use is key

Avoid Rolling These Areas

Do not foam roll directly over the lower back (lumbar spine), front of neck, bony prominences, or areas of acute injury or inflammation. For the low back, roll the muscles on either side of the spine, not the spine itself.

Professional MFR Treatment

What to Expect

  • Assessment of posture, movement, and areas of restriction
  • Treatment using hands, knuckles, elbows, or tools
  • Sustained pressure held for varying durations
  • May feel stretching, warmth, or mild discomfort
  • Sessions typically 30-60 minutes

Practitioners Who Offer MFR

  • Physical therapists
  • Massage therapists
  • Chiropractors
  • Occupational therapists
  • Athletic trainers

After Treatment

  • May feel soreness similar to post-exercise
  • Drink plenty of water
  • Some people feel tired or relaxed
  • Follow practitioner's home exercises

Research Evidence

Current state of evidence:2,3,4

Foam Rolling Studies

  • May temporarily increase range of motion
  • Can reduce delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS)
  • May improve arterial function
  • Effects often short-term

Manual MFR Studies

  • Some evidence for pain reduction in various conditions
  • May improve function and quality of life
  • Difficult to isolate effects from placebo
  • More high-quality research needed

Limitations

  • Many studies are small or of moderate quality
  • Difficult to create true placebo for manual therapy
  • Mechanisms not fully understood
  • Effects vary significantly between individuals

Choosing Tools for Self-MFR

Foam Rollers

  • Soft: Good for beginners or sensitive areas
  • Medium density: Standard choice for most people
  • Firm: For experienced users or deeper pressure
  • Textured: May provide more targeted pressure

Massage Balls

  • Tennis ball: Softer, good starting point
  • Lacrosse ball: Firmer, more intense
  • Specialized massage balls: Various sizes and densities
  • Peanut/double balls: For spine-adjacent muscles

Other Tools

  • Massage sticks: For calves, forearms, IT band
  • Percussion devices: Theragun, Hypervolt, etc.
  • TENS/vibration rollers: Combine vibration with rolling

Integrating MFR Into Your Routine

When to Do SMR

  • Before exercise: May increase range of motion for workout
  • After exercise: May help recovery
  • On rest days: General maintenance
  • Before stretching: May allow deeper stretches

Sample Routine

  1. 5-10 minutes of foam rolling target areas
  2. 30-60 seconds per muscle group
  3. Follow with stretching
  4. 2-3 times per week minimum

Combining with Other Treatments

  • Stretching: MFR then stretch for enhanced flexibility
  • Exercise: MFR as warm-up or cool-down
  • Heat: Can help relax tissues before MFR
  • Physical therapy: Often part of treatment programs
