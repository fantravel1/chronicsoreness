Pain Rehabilitation Programs

Comprehensive, intensive treatment for chronic pain

What Are Pain Rehabilitation Programs?

Pain rehabilitation programs (also called interdisciplinary or multidisciplinary pain programs) provide intensive, coordinated treatment from multiple specialists working together. Rather than focusing solely on pain reduction, these programs aim to improve function, quality of life, and self-management skills. They're considered the gold standard for complex chronic pain.1

The Approach2

  • Biopsychosocial model of pain
  • Addresses physical, psychological, social factors
  • Team works together (not in silos)
  • Function-focused, not just pain-focused
  • Active patient participation required

Team Members

  • Pain physician
  • Physical therapist
  • Psychologist
  • Occupational therapist
  • Nurse
  • Social worker
  • Sometimes vocational counselor

Program Components3

Physical

  • Exercise and conditioning
  • Stretching and strengthening
  • Body mechanics training
  • Aerobic conditioning
  • Pacing education

Psychological

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Stress management
  • Relaxation training
  • Coping skills
  • Group therapy

Educational

  • Pain neuroscience education
  • Sleep hygiene
  • Medication management
  • Flare-up planning

Occupational

  • Activity modification
  • Ergonomics
  • Return-to-work planning
  • Daily living skills

Program Formats

  • Inpatient (most intensive)
  • Day program (full day, go home evenings)
  • Outpatient (several hours, several days/week)
  • Typically 2-4 weeks duration

Who Benefits

  • Complex chronic pain conditions
  • Pain not responding to standard treatments
  • Significant functional impairment
  • High opioid use (often reduction goal)
  • Psychological factors contributing

Expected Outcomes

  • Improved function
  • Better quality of life
  • Reduced disability
  • Often reduced medication use
  • Pain may or may not decrease
  • Better coping and self-management

Finding a Program

  • Ask your pain doctor
  • Major medical centers often have them
  • CARF accreditation indicates quality
  • Insurance coverage varies
