Massage Therapy

Hands-on therapy for muscle tension and pain relief

Evidence Summary

Massage therapy may provide short-term pain relief for various chronic pain conditions. Evidence is strongest for low back pain and neck pain. Benefits tend to be temporary, so regular sessions may be needed for sustained relief.1,2

How Massage May Help Pain

  • Muscle relaxation: Reduces muscle tension and spasm
  • Improved circulation: Increases blood flow to tissues
  • Gate control: Touch sensations may block pain signals
  • Reduced cortisol: May lower stress hormones3
  • Increased serotonin/dopamine: May improve mood
  • Relaxation response: Activates parasympathetic nervous system
  • Fascial release: May address connective tissue restrictions

Types of Massage

Swedish Massage

Technique: Long strokes, kneading, circular movements

Best for: General relaxation, stress relief, gentle introduction to massage

Deep Tissue Massage

Technique: Firm pressure targeting deep muscle layers

Best for: Chronic muscle tension, specific problem areas. May cause temporary soreness.

Myofascial Release

Technique: Sustained pressure on fascia (connective tissue)

Best for: Fibromyalgia, chronic pain with fascial restrictions4

Trigger Point Therapy

Technique: Focused pressure on specific tight spots

Best for: Myofascial pain syndrome, localized muscle knots

Craniosacral Therapy

Technique: Very light touch on head, spine, sacrum

Evidence: Limited; some find it helpful for headaches, fibromyalgia

Lymphatic Drainage

Technique: Very gentle, rhythmic movements

Best for: Swelling, lymphedema; very gentle option for sensitive patients

Research Evidence by Condition

Chronic Low Back Pain

Evidence: Moderate. Cochrane review found massage may be beneficial for short-term pain relief, especially when combined with exercise and education.5

Neck Pain

Evidence: Low to moderate. May provide short-term relief. Multiple sessions typically needed.6

Fibromyalgia

Evidence: Low quality but promising. Myofascial release shows some benefit. Pressure must be appropriateâ€”too deep may worsen symptoms.7

Osteoarthritis

Evidence: Limited. Some studies show modest benefit for knee OA pain.8

Headaches

Evidence: Promising for tension-type headaches. May help reduce frequency.9

What to Expect

Before the Session

  • Communicate your pain conditions and sensitive areas
  • Discuss pressure preferences (light, medium, firm)
  • Mention any medications (blood thinners affect bruising risk)
  • Use the restroom beforehand
  • Remove jewelry if in treatment area

During the Session

  • Speak up: Tell therapist if pressure is too much/little
  • Breathe: Don't hold your breath during deep work
  • Draping: You'll be covered; only area being worked on is exposed
  • Duration: Typically 30-90 minutes

After the Session

  • Hydrate: Drink water
  • Mild soreness: Normal for 24-48 hours, especially with deep work
  • Rest: Don't schedule intense activity immediately after
  • Note effects: Track how long relief lasts

Finding a Massage Therapist

Credentials

  • Licensed Massage Therapist (LMT): State licensure requirements vary
  • Nationally certified: NCBTMB certification
  • Medical massage: Additional training for clinical conditions
  • Ask about: Experience with chronic pain conditions

Where to Find

  • Physical therapy clinics (often have massage on staff)
  • Integrative medicine centers
  • Chiropractic offices
  • Day spas (for relaxation; may have less medical focus)
  • Private practice massage therapists

Questions to Ask

  • Do you have experience with [my condition]?
  • What type of massage do you recommend for my symptoms?
  • How often do you recommend sessions?
  • Do you take insurance? (Some plans cover massage)

When to Avoid Massage

  • Acute injuries or inflammation (first 48-72 hours)
  • Blood clots (DVT) or risk of blood clots
  • Fever or acute illness
  • Open wounds, burns, or skin infections
  • Severe osteoporosis (fragile bones)
  • Over areas of cancer without oncologist approval
  • Directly over inflamed joints (RA flare)

Cost and Frequency

  • Cost: $60-150 per hour (varies by location and setting)
  • Insurance: Some plans cover; often need prescription
  • HSA/FSA: Often eligible
  • Frequency: Weekly initially, then maintenance as needed
  • Budget options: Massage schools offer discounted sessions

Self-Massage Options

Between professional sessions:

  • Foam rolling
  • Tennis ball or massage ball for trigger points
  • Massage guns (theraguns)
  • Self-massage techniques (hands, knuckles)
  • Partner massage
