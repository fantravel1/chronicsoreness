Massage Therapy for Chronic Pain

Understanding therapeutic massage as part of pain management

What Is Therapeutic Massage?

Therapeutic massage involves the manipulation of soft tissuesâ€”muscles, tendons, ligaments, and fasciaâ€”to promote healing, reduce pain, and improve function. Unlike relaxation massage, therapeutic massage specifically targets pain and dysfunction.1

Massage has been used for pain relief for thousands of years and is now one of the most commonly used complementary therapies for chronic pain. It can be part of a comprehensive pain management approach.

How Massage May Help Pain

  • Reduces muscle tension - Releases tight muscles and trigger points
  • Improves circulation - Increases blood flow to tissues
  • Decreases inflammation - Some evidence for anti-inflammatory effects2
  • Promotes relaxation - Activates parasympathetic nervous system
  • Gate control - Touch signals may reduce pain perception
  • Releases endorphins - Natural pain-relieving chemicals
  • Reduces stress hormones - Decreases cortisol levels
  • Improves sleep - Better sleep reduces pain

Types of Massage for Pain

Swedish Massage

  • Most common type; uses long strokes, kneading, friction
  • Lighter to moderate pressure
  • Good for general muscle tension and relaxation
  • Often a starting point for those new to massage

Deep Tissue Massage

  • Targets deeper muscle layers and connective tissue
  • Slower strokes with more pressure
  • Helpful for chronic muscle tension
  • May cause temporary soreness

Myofascial Release

  • Focuses on the fascia (connective tissue)
  • Sustained pressure to release restrictions
  • Often used for chronic pain conditions
  • Can be gentle or deep

Trigger Point Therapy

  • Targets specific trigger points (muscle knots)
  • Concentrated pressure on tender points
  • Especially useful for myofascial pain syndrome
  • Often combined with other techniques

Other Types

  • Craniosacral therapy - Light touch on head and spine
  • Lymphatic drainage - For swelling
  • Sports massage - For athletic injuries
  • Neuromuscular therapy - Addresses nerve-muscle relationship

Which Type Is Best?

There's no single "best" massage type for chronic pain. The right approach depends on your condition, preferences, and what your body tolerates. Many therapists combine techniques. Start with moderate pressure and adjust based on your response.

Research Evidence

Conditions with Evidence3

  • Chronic low back pain - Most studied; good evidence for short-term benefit
  • Neck and shoulder pain - Moderate evidence
  • Tension headaches - May reduce frequency
  • Fibromyalgia - May help some people4
  • Osteoarthritis - Some evidence for knee and hand OA
  • Myofascial pain - Commonly used, some evidence

What Studies Show

  • Generally provides short-term pain relief
  • Effects may last days to weeks
  • Regular sessions may provide cumulative benefit
  • Works best as part of comprehensive approach
  • Quality of evidence is often moderate

What to Expect

Before Your Session

  • Complete health intake form
  • Discuss your pain condition and goals
  • Mention any areas to avoid
  • Share relevant medical history

During the Session

  • Typically 60-90 minutes for therapeutic massage
  • Communicate about pressureâ€”speak up if it's too much or too little
  • Some discomfort is normal; pain that makes you tense is too much
  • Breathe deeply and try to relax

After the Session

  • Mild soreness is common (like after exercise)
  • Drink plenty of water
  • Rest if needed
  • Benefits may be felt immediately or over following days

When to Avoid Massage

Avoid massage or consult your doctor first if you have: blood clots or high risk of clots, bleeding disorders or on blood thinners, fractures, severe osteoporosis, open wounds or skin infections, fever or acute illness, or cancer (without oncology clearance). Certain areas should be avoided during pregnancy.

Finding a Massage Therapist

Qualifications to Look For

  • Licensed massage therapist (LMT) - State licensure required in most states
  • Relevant training - Additional training in techniques for your condition
  • Experience with chronic pain - Ask about their experience
  • Professional setting - Clean, appropriate environment

Questions to Ask

  • What is your experience with my condition?
  • What techniques do you use?
  • How many sessions might I need?
  • What should I expect after the session?

Where to Find Therapists

  • Referral from healthcare provider
  • Physical therapy clinics (may have massage therapists on staff)
  • Professional associations (AMTA, ABMP directories)
  • Pain management centers

Practical Considerations

Frequency

  • For acute issues: 1-2 times per week initially
  • For maintenance: every 2-4 weeks
  • Adjust based on response and budget

Cost and Insurance

  • Typically $60-120 per session
  • Some insurance covers massage (especially with PT)
  • HSA/FSA may be used with prescription
  • Some therapists offer package discounts

Self-Massage Options5

  • Foam rolling - For large muscle groups
  • Massage balls - For trigger points
  • Massage guns - Percussion devices
  • Self-massage techniques - Your therapist can teach you
