Massage Therapy

Hands-on treatment for pain and tension

Key Takeaways

  • Massage can reduce pain, muscle tension, and stress
  • Several types of massage suit different conditions
  • Benefits are usually short-term—regular sessions may help maintain relief
  • Works best as part of a comprehensive treatment plan

How Massage Helps Pain

Massage therapy involves manipulation of soft tissues to reduce pain, relieve muscle tension, and promote relaxation. Research supports its use for various chronic pain conditions, though benefits are often short-term.1

Recommended
Clinical guidelines support massage for chronic low back pain2

Proposed Mechanisms

  • Reduces muscle tension and trigger points
  • Improves circulation
  • Activates relaxation response
  • May affect pain signaling pathways
  • Promotes release of endorphins
  • Psychological benefits (relaxation, touch)

Types of Massage

Swedish Massage

  • Most common form
  • Long, flowing strokes
  • Kneading and circular movements
  • Good for relaxation and mild tension
  • Light to moderate pressure

Deep Tissue Massage

  • Targets deeper muscle layers
  • Slower, more forceful strokes
  • For chronic muscle tension
  • Can be uncomfortable during treatment
  • May cause soreness afterward

Trigger Point Therapy

  • Focuses on tight muscle knots
  • Sustained pressure on trigger points
  • For myofascial pain
  • Can refer pain to other areas during treatment

Myofascial Release

  • Targets connective tissue (fascia)
  • Gentle sustained pressure
  • For widespread tension and restriction
  • See our detailed guide

Other Types

  • Sports massage: For athletes and active individuals
  • Lymphatic drainage: For swelling, fluid retention
  • Craniosacral therapy: Very gentle head and spine focus
  • Thai massage: Combines stretching with pressure

Conditions with Evidence

Research supports massage for:3,4

Good Evidence

  • Chronic low back pain
  • Neck pain
  • Tension-type headaches
  • Osteoarthritis (particularly knee)

Moderate Evidence

  • Fibromyalgia
  • Shoulder pain
  • Cancer-related pain
  • Anxiety and stress

Managing Expectations

While massage can provide relief, benefits often fade within days to weeks. Regular sessions may help maintain improvement. Massage typically works best combined with exercise, stretching, and other active approaches.

What to Expect

Before Your Session

  • Complete health intake form
  • Discuss your pain and goals
  • Mention any medical conditions
  • Inform about medications, especially blood thinners
  • Communicate pressure preferences

During the Session

  • Sessions typically 30-90 minutes
  • Undress to comfort level (draping provided)
  • Communicate if pressure is too much or too little
  • Some discomfort with deep work is normal
  • Sharp pain is NOT okay—speak up

After the Session

  • Drink plenty of water
  • Some soreness normal (like after exercise)
  • Avoid strenuous activity for rest of day
  • Benefits may develop over 24-48 hours

How Often

  • Acute issues: 1-2 times per week initially
  • Chronic pain: Every 1-4 weeks for maintenance
  • Depends on response and budget

Finding a Massage Therapist

Credentials to Look For

  • Licensed Massage Therapist (LMT)
  • State licensure requirements vary
  • Additional certifications in specific techniques
  • Experience with your condition

Questions to Ask

  • What experience do you have with [my condition]?
  • What type of massage do you recommend?
  • What should I expect after the session?
  • How often should I come?

Where to Find Therapists

  • Referral from healthcare provider
  • Professional association directories
  • Physical therapy clinics (some have massage therapists)
  • Integrative medicine centers

Safety Considerations

When to Avoid or Modify Massage

  • Blood clots or risk of blood clots
  • Bleeding disorders or on blood thinners
  • Fractures or severe osteoporosis
  • Open wounds or skin infections
  • Recent surgery
  • Certain cancers (consult oncologist)
  • Fever or acute illness

Areas to Avoid

  • Over blood clots
  • Directly over tumors
  • Areas of acute inflammation
  • Over healing fractures

Communicate Medical Conditions

Always inform your massage therapist about medical conditions, medications, and areas of concern. A good therapist will adapt their approach to your needs and limitations.

Self-Massage Techniques

Tools for Self-Massage

  • Tennis balls, lacrosse balls
  • Foam rollers
  • Massage canes (like Theracane)
  • Percussion massagers

Basic Techniques

  • Apply pressure to tender spots
  • Hold pressure for 30-60 seconds
  • Use circular motions
  • Don't press so hard it causes significant pain
  • Focus on common tension areas (neck, shoulders, back)

Cost and Access

  • Sessions typically $60-150 for 60 minutes
  • Some insurance covers with prescription
  • FSA/HSA often applicable
  • Massage schools offer discounted sessions
  • Some community clinics offer sliding scale
