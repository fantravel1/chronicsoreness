Cold Therapy

Using ice and cold for pain relief

How Cold Helps Pain

Cold therapy (cryotherapy) uses cold temperatures to reduce inflammation, numb pain, and decrease blood flow to an area. While often associated with acute injuries, cold can also be helpful for certain chronic pain situations, particularly after activity or during inflammatory flares.1

How It Works2

  • Constricts blood vessels (reduces swelling)
  • Numbs the area (reduces pain signals)
  • Decreases inflammation
  • Reduces muscle spasm
  • Slows metabolic activity

When to Use Cold

  • Acute injuries (first 48-72 hours)
  • After exercise that causes pain
  • Inflammatory flares (RA, gout)
  • Swelling
  • Migraines (some people)
  • After overactivity

Cold for Chronic Pain

  • After activity that increases pain
  • During inflammatory flares
  • Some prefer cold to heat
  • May be combined with heat
  • Personal response varies

Types of Cold Application3

  • Ice packs
  • Gel packs (reusable)
  • Frozen vegetables (peas work well)
  • Ice massage
  • Cold water immersion
  • Cooling sprays

How to Apply

  • Always use barrier (cloth) between ice and skin
  • Apply for 15-20 minutes
  • Allow skin to return to normal before reapplying
  • Don't apply to areas with poor circulation

Safety

  • Never apply ice directly to skin
  • Don't use on areas with numbness
  • Watch for signs of frostbite
  • Avoid with Raynaud's disease
  • Limit application time

When NOT to Use Cold

  • Cold sensitivity or cold urticaria
  • Poor circulation
  • Over areas with no sensation
  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Some heart conditions
