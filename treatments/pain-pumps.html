Intrathecal Pain Pumps

Implanted drug delivery for severe chronic pain

What Is an Intrathecal Pain Pump?

An intrathecal pump (also called a pain pump or implanted drug delivery system) is a surgically implanted device that delivers pain medication directly to the fluid around the spinal cord. This allows much smaller doses than oral medications while providing effective relief.1

How It Works

  • Pump implanted under skin (usually abdomen)
  • Catheter runs to spinal fluid
  • Programmable, controlled delivery
  • Medication delivered 24/7
  • Refilled periodically (office procedure)

Candidates2

  • Failed other treatments
  • Severe, intractable pain
  • Cancer pain
  • Chronic non-cancer pain
  • Spasticity (baclofen pump)
  • Passed psychological screening
  • Successful trial first

Medications Used

  • Morphine (most common)
  • Hydromorphone
  • Ziconotide
  • Baclofen (for spasticity)
  • Combinations

The Process

  • Trial period first (temporary catheter)
  • If successful, permanent implant
  • Surgical procedure
  • Programming and dose adjustments
  • Regular refills (every 1-6 months)

Benefits3

  • Very small medication doses needed
  • Fewer systemic side effects
  • Continuous pain relief
  • Programmable/adjustable
  • Reversible (can be removed)

Risks and Considerations

  • Surgical risks
  • Infection
  • Catheter problems
  • Granuloma formation (rare)
  • Medication side effects
  • Requires regular follow-up
  • Battery replacement (years)
