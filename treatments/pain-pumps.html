Intrathecal Pain Pumps

Targeted drug delivery for severe pain

What Is an Intrathecal Pain Pump?

An intrathecal pain pump is an implanted device that delivers pain medication directly into the fluid around the spinal cord. Because the drug goes directly to pain receptors, much smaller doses are needed compared to oral medications, potentially reducing side effects.1

How It Works

  • Small pump implanted under skin (usually abdomen)
  • Catheter delivers medication to spinal fluid
  • Programmable to adjust dose
  • Pump refilled periodically through skin
  • Battery lasts several years

Who May Benefit2

  • Severe chronic pain uncontrolled by other treatments
  • Cancer pain
  • Failed back surgery syndrome
  • Complex regional pain syndrome
  • When oral medications cause intolerable side effects
  • Must pass psychological evaluation
  • Must have successful trial first

Medications Used3

  • Morphine: Most common, FDA-approved
  • Ziconotide: Non-opioid option, FDA-approved
  • Baclofen: For spasticity
  • Combinations: Sometimes used off-label

The Trial

  • Temporary test before permanent implant
  • May be single injection or temporary catheter
  • Evaluates pain relief and tolerability
  • Usually requires 50%+ pain relief to proceed

The Surgery

  • Pump placed under skin in abdomen
  • Catheter threaded to spinal area
  • Done under anesthesia
  • Usually 1-2 hour procedure
  • Hospital stay may be required

Living with a Pain Pump

  • Regular refills needed (every 1-6 months)
  • Dose adjustments as needed
  • MRI restrictions (depends on pump model)
  • Medical alert identification
  • Regular follow-up appointments

Risks and Complications

  • Infection
  • Catheter problems (kink, movement, blockage)
  • Drug-related side effects
  • Pump malfunction
  • Spinal headache
  • Need for revision surgery
  • Granuloma formation (rare)

Important Considerations

  • Requires commitment to ongoing care
  • Expensive (usually covered by insurance)
  • Not a cureâ€”manages pain
  • May need dose increases over time
  • Can't be "turned off" immediately in emergencies
