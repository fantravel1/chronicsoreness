Prolotherapy

Injection therapy for musculoskeletal pain

What Is Prolotherapy?

Prolotherapy (proliferative therapy) involves injecting an irritant solutionâ€”usually dextrose (sugar water)â€”into damaged ligaments, tendons, or joints. The goal is to stimulate the body's natural healing response. It's been used for decades, though evidence is still evolving.1

How It Works

  • Injection causes localized inflammation
  • Inflammation triggers healing response
  • Body produces new collagen
  • May strengthen weakened structures
  • Multiple treatments typically needed

Solutions Used2

  • Dextrose (most common): Safe sugar solution
  • Sodium morrhuate: Less commonly used
  • Phenol: Rarely used now
  • Often mixed with local anesthetic

Conditions Treated3

  • Chronic low back pain
  • Sacroiliac joint pain
  • Knee osteoarthritis
  • Tendinopathy
  • Ligament injuries
  • Joint hypermobility/instability

The Procedure

  • Multiple injections at one session
  • May use ultrasound or fluoroscopy guidance
  • Brief procedure
  • Some pain at injection sites expected
  • Usually 3-6 sessions, weeks apart

What to Expect After

  • Pain and stiffness for several days
  • Avoid anti-inflammatories (they block healing response)
  • Rest, then gradually increase activity
  • Full response takes weeks to months
  • Improvement often gradual

Evidence

  • Some positive studies, especially for back pain and OA
  • Quality of evidence is moderate
  • Not universally accepted in mainstream medicine
  • More research needed
  • Generally safe with few serious risks

Risks

  • Temporary increased pain
  • Stiffness
  • Bruising
  • Infection (rare)
  • Allergic reaction (rare)

Finding Treatment

  • Sports medicine physicians
  • Physiatrists
  • Some pain specialists
  • Osteopathic physicians
  • Usually not covered by insurance

Considerations

  • Out-of-pocket cost typically
  • Requires multiple sessions
  • Results variable between individuals
  • Reasonable option when other treatments fail
